Why did Kuhn call them revolutions? Because the politics is exact. A political revolution begins when institutions stop solving the problems they created, and it proceeds outside the rules, because the rules themselves are what's in dispute. Paradigm change works the same way. Each side judges by its own standards, defines the problems its own way, and finds itself perfectly persuasive. The debate is circular by nature. There is no higher court. And here Kuhn drops his most famous bomb. Incommensurability. The new paradigm isn't just incompatible with the old. It changes what the words mean. Einstein's mass is not Newton's mass with corrections. Deriving Newton's equations from relativity changes the meaning of every symbol in them. The comfortable story that new science extends old science is, at the revolutionary moments, simply false. That's why brilliant people talk past each other in paradigm debates. They're not being stubborn. They're speaking different languages while using the same words. Here's the practice. In your next deep organizational disagreement, check for circularity. List each side's success metrics. If each camp scores itself by its own criteria, uptime versus velocity, rigor versus speed, no memo will ever win. Stop writing proofs. Design a demonstration that recruits, a pilot whose results the other camp can feel. Revolutions are persuasion campaigns. Plan accordingly.
