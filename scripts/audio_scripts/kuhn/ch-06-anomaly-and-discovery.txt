Who discovered oxygen? Priestley isolated it, but called it dephlogisticated air, a better version of ordinary air. Lavoisier saw a new element, but only because his weight experiments had primed him to expect trouble. The honest answer is that discovery isn't a moment. It's a process, and it has a structure. It begins when nature violates a paradigm-induced expectation, and it ends only when the conceptual categories have been rebuilt so the anomaly becomes the expected. Here's the paradox Kuhn loved. Rigidity enables surprise. Roentgen noticed X-rays because a screen glowed where his training said nothing should glow. Without precise expectations, there is no anomaly, just noise. The paradigm's very strictness is the discovery instrument. Psychology backs this up. Show people a red six of spades briefly and they'll see a normal card. Expectation assimilates the anomalous, until repeated exposure forces the new category out. Scientists are no different. Here's the practice, in two moves. First, make your expectations precise. Forecast, predict, set error bands, because a team that predicts precisely gets surprised early, and early surprise is competitive advantage. Second, when the needle trembles outside the band, don't patch it and move on. Budget real time. The glimpse is free. The discovery is in the three weeks of follow-up.
