Everything you were taught about how science works is a tidy lie. Not a malicious one. A useful one. The story goes: scientists gather facts, theories improve, knowledge accumulates, brick by brick, from Aristotle's errors to today's truths. Thomas Kuhn opened his nineteen sixty-two masterpiece by pulling that wall down. His conversion came as a young physicist assigned to teach Aristotle. He expected to find bad Newton, primitive fumbling toward mechanics. Instead, one afternoon, the text snapped into focus. Aristotle wasn't doing Newton badly. He was doing something else entirely, and doing it with rigor and coherence. Different questions, different concepts, a different world. That shock carries the whole book. If discarded frameworks were real science, then science doesn't grow by accumulation. It grows by displacement, by revolutions that swap one way of seeing for another. And if that's true, the confident textbook story is manufactured history. Here's the practice. Find the story your own field tells about its past, the inevitable march to the present, and go read something from the losing side, in its own terms. Chronological snobbery dies fast on contact with primary sources. What replaces it is humility, and a sharper eye for which of today's certainties is next.
