If revolutions tear science apart every few generations, why does science look so smooth? Because the victors write the textbooks. After every revolution, the textbooks are rewritten, and they rewrite history backwards. Past scientists appear to have been working on today's problems with today's standards, marching in single file toward the present. Dalton's law of fixed proportions gets taught as a careful experimental discovery. Historically, chemists had decades of good evidence against it, and Dalton's atomism won by redefining what counts as a compound until the law became true by category. The revolution vanishes into a footnote of steady progress. Kuhn's comparison is brutal. He says the trained scientist, reading this history, is like a character from Orwell's nineteen eighty-four, the victim of a past rewritten by the powers that be. And the strangest part: the distortion is functional. Normal science works better when practitioners believe the tradition is continuous. Useful for training. Corrosive for judgment. Here's the practice. Your organization does this too. The founding myth, the inevitable pivot, the postmortem sanitized into a straight line. Fine for onboarding. Dangerous for decisions. So keep the raw record, the original memos, the options that died, the argument as it actually ran, and reread it before the next big bet. If your only history is the polished deck, you have institutionalized amnesia.
