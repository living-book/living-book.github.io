Here's a fact that should have killed falsificationism. Every theory, at every moment in history, faces unsolved anomalies. If failed predictions refuted theories, everything would be refuted always. So why isn't it? Kuhn's answer became the chapter's iron law. Scientists never abandon a paradigm because of failures alone. They abandon it only when a rival exists to jump to. To reject a paradigm without substituting another is to reject science itself. The choice is never theory versus nature. It's always theory versus theory versus nature. Watch the responses inside a real crisis. Most practitioners rationally keep patching, because patching has always worked before. Some drift to philosophy, debating first principles physics normally ignores. Einstein described the years before relativity as feeling like the ground had been pulled out from under him. And the wild new options usually come from the young or the newcomers, the people least invested in the old commitments. Here's the practice, and it will change how you argue. Stop cataloguing what's wrong with the incumbent system, process, or strategy. A complaint is not a decision. Build the working alternative first, even a small one, then let the comparison do the killing. And if you want bold options generated, ask the people with the least tenure. They can see options the veterans literally cannot afford to see.
