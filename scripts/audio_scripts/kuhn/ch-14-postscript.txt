Seven years after the book exploded, Kuhn sat down to repair it. Critics had counted twenty-one different meanings of the word paradigm in the text. The postscript of nineteen sixty-nine is his answer, and it's where the theory gets its final form. Paradigm splits in two. The disciplinary matrix is everything a community shares: the symbolic generalizations deployed without debate, the models and metaphysical commitments, the values, and the exemplars. And it's the exemplars, the concrete worked problems that train a practitioner's sense of similarity, that Kuhn calls the deepest and least understood idea in the book. Incommensurability gets tamed too. Rival paradigm speakers are like speakers of different languages. Communication is partial, translation is laborious but possible, and translating is not converting. You can understand a framework perfectly and still not live in it. And to the charge of mob psychology, Kuhn answers with the five values. Accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness. Shared by all, weighted differently by each, and the variance is functional. It spreads the community's bets. Here's the practice. Map your team's matrix. What formulas do we use without debate? What models are we committed to? Which values do we all cite but weight differently? Which exemplars trained us? Do this and most so-called logic fights become legible as what they really are: different layers of the matrix, arguing.
