New theories don't arrive when someone clever has a good idea. They arrive on schedule, and the schedule is set by the old paradigm's failures. Kuhn's evidence is striking. Copernicus didn't overthrow a healthy astronomy. He inherited one that astronomers themselves called a monster, patched with epicycles for centuries, unable even to fix the calendar. The crisis was public knowledge before the cure existed. Same pattern everywhere. Phlogiston chemistry drowning in weight anomalies before Lavoisier. The ether problem gnawing at physics before Einstein. Crisis first, theory second. This also explains one of science's oddest facts, simultaneous discovery. When the same breakthrough appears independently in three places at once, it's because the breaking point was public. Many minds converged on the same visible wound. The crisis was the real author. Here's the practice. If you're carrying a heterodox idea, don't pitch it as interesting. Map it directly onto your field's most publicly acknowledged failure, the problem everyone complains about at conferences. That's the only door novelty enters through. No crisis, no listeners, and rationally so. And watch for the signal in your own market. When three competitors ship the same original idea in the same quarter, don't ask who copied whom. Ask what just broke.
