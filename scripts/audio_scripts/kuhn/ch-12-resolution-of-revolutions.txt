Max Planck, who started quantum physics, left the most cynical line in the philosophy of science. A new truth does not triumph by convincing its opponents, he wrote. It triumphs because its opponents eventually die. Kuhn takes the line seriously. If paradigm debates are circular, how do they actually end? Not by proof. By conversion, and by demography. The sequence runs like this. A few early adopters commit while the evidence is still against the new paradigm, for reasons that are often frankly aesthetic. It's neater. It feels right. Their work makes the new framework concrete and fruitful. Visible wins accumulate. Arguments multiply. The profession tips, and the holdouts, never refuted, simply become irrelevant. Priestley went to his grave rejecting oxygen. Kuhn refuses to call him irrational. The field had moved, that's all. Rationality, Kuhn insists, lives in good reasons, accuracy, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness, weighed differently by different people. That variance isn't a bug. It's how a community spreads its bets. Here's the practice. If you're driving change, stop trying to convert the most invested skeptics with memos. Recruit early adopters, arm them to produce visible wins, and let time do the demographic work. And audit yourself honestly. Of the positions you defend, which are reasons, and which are just tenure?
