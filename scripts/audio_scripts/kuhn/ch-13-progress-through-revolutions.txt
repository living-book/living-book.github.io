The whole book builds to one uncomfortable question. If revolutions replace worldviews rather than refining them, and no neutral standard exists, why does science so obviously work? Kuhn's answer is the book's quietest bombshell. Science progresses from, not toward. The analogy is Darwin. Before eighteen fifty-nine, evolution meant progress toward a goal. Darwin removed the goal, and biology survived, better than ever. Kuhn does the same for knowledge. Later science solves more problems, with more precision, than earlier science. That progress is real and measurable. What's optional is the destination, the idea that we're approaching final truth. Closer to the truth, Kuhn argues, does no explanatory work at all. Puzzle-solving power explains everything the miracle story explained. What actually drives the progress is community structure. Shared exemplars. Peer-only judgment. Insulation from outside demands. Fresh generations trained and turned over. It reads like a design specification for any field that wants to move. Here's the practice. Notice how much energy your team spends chasing the right architecture, the final org design, the correct strategy, as if a destination existed. Reframe the metric. Does this change solve more of what currently blocks us? That's progress, and it's the only kind on offer. One warning though. Today's fitness is fitted to today's environment. Environments shift. Nothing about progress-from guarantees you're fit for what's coming.
