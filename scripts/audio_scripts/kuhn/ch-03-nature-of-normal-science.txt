Most working scientists spend their entire careers on what Kuhn cheerfully calls mop-up work. And he means it as a compliment. Once a paradigm wins, the community stops asking whether it's true and starts making it precise. The paradigm is a promise, and normal science is the long, disciplined actualization of that promise. Newton's Principia claimed all celestial motion follows universal gravitation, but Newton left the moon's orbit crude and the planetary perturbations unsolved. It took a century of Europe's best minds, Euler, Lagrange, Laplace, to make the heavens actually obey the book. None of them was testing Newton. All of them were completing him. That restriction of vision, the freedom to stop debating foundations and drill, is exactly what makes mature science so productive. Nobody builds a precision instrument for a framework they might abandon on Thursday. Here's the practice. Look at your backlog and sort it honestly. Which work extends the framework, and which challenges it? Both matter, but confusing them wrecks teams. Demanding disruption from every quarter starves the deep, unglamorous precision work that progress actually rides on. Somewhere in your field, someone's Laplace work, decades of refinement nobody celebrates, is quietly enabling the next revolution. Maybe fund it.
