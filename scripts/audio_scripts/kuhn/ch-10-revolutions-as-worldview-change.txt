After a revolution, Kuhn says, scientists work in a different world. Not a metaphor he uses lightly. Where Aristotle saw a heavy body struggling toward its natural place, Galileo saw a pendulum, near frictionless, periodic, timeable. Same swinging stone. Different phenomenon. Different measurable quantities. Different science. The gestalt psychologists had the image, the duck that becomes a rabbit, but Kuhn sharpens it. The scientist has no neutral stimulus underneath to fall back on. Perception itself is trained by the paradigm. You don't observe and then interpret. Your training decides what you see in the first place. Modern cognitive science largely proved him right. Chess masters don't look at boards harder than novices. They see different boards. Expertise is restructured perception, and so, inevitably, is expert blindness. Here's the practice, in two rules. First, staff important reviews with differently trained eyes, not for diversity of opinion, but because a security engineer and a designer looking at the same screen are literally seeing different objects. You want all the objects seen. Second, when you switch frameworks, expect a disoriented stretch where you can't see what used to be obvious. That's not incompetence. That's the retraining. The lenses invert, the world swims, and then, one day, the rabbit is just there.
