Ask ten physicists to define force and you'll get hesitation and disagreement. Give them a problem and they'll converge in seconds. That gap is the heart of this chapter. Kuhn argues that shared examples run deeper than shared rules. A research tradition coheres not because everyone memorized the same definitions, but because everyone ground through the same exemplars. The inclined plane. The pendulum. The orbit. Solving them, again and again, until new problems started looking like old ones. The knowledge lives in trained similarity perception, in the eye, not in the rulebook. That's why historians hunting for the rules of an era find chaos at the level of definitions while the science itself hums along coherently. Like Wittgenstein's games, the tradition is a family resemblance, no essence required. Here's the practice, and it's immediately usable. Stop writing rulebooks and start curating exemplars. If you want a team to share judgment, hand them three gold-standard examples of the craft, a model document, a model decision, a model solution, and make people work through them. And here's the diagnostic. If your team recites the same definitions but diverges on concrete cases, your practice is fragmenting, whatever the handbook says. If they can't define the terms but converge on cases, you're healthy. Cases are the curriculum.
