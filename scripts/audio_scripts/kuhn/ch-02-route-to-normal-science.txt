Why do some fields race ahead while others argue about foundations forever? Kuhn's answer is one word. Paradigm. Before a field has one, it's a battlefield of schools. Optics before Newton had particle theorists, medium theorists, emanation theorists, all rigorous, all rebuilding the subject from scratch in every book, all addressing the general public because no shared foundation existed. Then comes an achievement compelling enough to pull practitioners away from the rival schools, and open-ended enough to leave everyone work to do. Newton's Opticks. Franklin's account of electricity, born from the puzzle of the Leyden jar. The foundation wars end, not because anyone won the argument, but because the community found something better to do. The symptoms of maturity follow: specialist journals, professional societies, papers no outsider can read. Losing the lay audience, Kuhn notes, is perversely a sign of health. Here's the practice. Diagnose your own team or field. If every meeting relitigates first principles, you're pre-paradigm, and the cure isn't more debate. It's a working exemplar, a first success concrete enough to build on. Find it or make it, declare it the foundation, and let the accumulation begin. Consensus on fundamentals isn't intellectual surrender. It's what makes depth affordable.
