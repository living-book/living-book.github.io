No one will ever tell you they envy you. That's what makes this law dangerous. Envy is the one emotion too shameful to admit, so it converts into something wearable. Criticism. Coldness. Backhanded praise. Sudden joy at your stumble. Greene's advice is to stop watching what people say and start watching micro-signals. The friend whose congratulations arrive a beat too late. The colleague who copies your work and then competes with you. Mary Shelley lived it. Her circle of brilliant friends celebrated her, until Frankenstein made the teenager more famous than all of them, and the warmth curdled into subtle sabotage from people who genuinely believed they loved her. Envy grows best in closeness. It needs comparison, and comparison needs proximity. Here's the practice, in two directions. Outward, when you sense the signals from someone close, don't confront, deflect. Downplay the success, share the credit, even ask them for help. Envy deflates when the gap feels smaller. Inward, be honest about your own envy. When someone's win stings you, name it, then convert it. Envy is just admiration plus a wound. Studied honestly, it's the most precise map you have of what you actually want.
