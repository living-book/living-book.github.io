This chapter is a dialogue, Deutsch's homage to Plato, and his correction of him. The god Hermes visits the sleeping Socrates and teaches him, by relentless questioning, the book's whole epistemology. Knowledge comes not from authority, not from revelation, not from the senses, but from conjecture corrected by criticism. And here's the sharp twist, all communication is itself conjectural. When you listen, you guess the speaker's meaning, and you often guess wrong, productively. Even a god cannot pour knowledge into a mind. Hermes can only help Socrates create it for himself. Then comes the sly frame. Socrates tells the dream to his students, and young Plato, taking notes, misunderstands it systematically. He hears knowledge cannot be transmitted and writes down the opposite, doctrines of authoritative Forms and philosopher-kings. Deutsch stages the birth of authoritarian philosophy as history's most consequential productive misunderstanding, proving the dialogue's own thesis in the very act of corrupting it. Here's the practice. Treat every handoff, every spec, every brief, every lecture, as a conjecture-generator, not a pipe. The receiver will build something different, guaranteed, so create feedback loops that surface the difference early, teach it back, red-team the spec, show worked examples. And harvest productive misunderstanding. When someone gets your idea wrong in an interesting way, they may have created something new. Plato did, catastrophically well.
