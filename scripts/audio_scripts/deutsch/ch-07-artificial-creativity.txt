An artificial general intelligence, Deutsch argues, would be a person. A universal explainer, with all the rights and moral status that word carries. And here's his provocation, we cannot build one by stacking up tricks, or training on examples, or imitating human output. It requires a working explanation of creativity, of how new explanations get created, and as of his writing, nobody has one. Nobody even seems to miss it. The Turing test, he says, aims at the wrong thing entirely. Judging a machine by its outputs can never distinguish genuine explanation-creating from an enormous, ever-growing lookup table of mimicry. He revives Lady Lovelace's objection to Babbage's engine, it has no pretensions to originate anything, it does only what we know how to order it to perform. Turing thought machines would eventually surprise us and refute her. Deutsch sides with Lovelace on the deep point. Surprise is not origination. A recording can surprise you. Here's the practice for the AI age, two disciplines. First, for any system you evaluate, ask what would actually distinguish understanding from very good mimicry in this domain, and then design that test, not a vibe check. Second, hold humility in both directions. Don't declare personhood because the output is fluent. Don't declare it impossible on principle either. And watch for Deutsch's real tell, the system that disobeys well, that generates criticism, new problems, and explanations nobody asked it for.
