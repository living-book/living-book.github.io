Some systems, built for a small parochial purpose, suddenly cross a threshold and become capable of everything in their domain. Deutsch calls it the jump to universality, and it's the most consequential pattern in technology and biology. Alphabets, a few dozen letters, can spell every possible word. Positional numerals can write every number. The computer can run every computation. And the jump is almost always unintended, and unnoticed by the very people who make it. Charles Babbage designed a universal computer out of brass gears, a full century early. Neither he nor his government grasped what universality meant, they funded it, barely, as a better machine for printing navigation tables. Meanwhile the genetic code was universal for billions of years, capable of specifying life-forms that wouldn't exist for eons, its reach just waiting, unnoticed, in the chemistry. And the counter-example, Roman numerals, deliberately kept non-universal, parochialism as a choice. Here's the practice. Judge your tools and skills by jump-status. A tool that does one more thing per release is parochial. A tool that becomes programmable has jumped, and its future value detaches completely from its roadmap. Same for careers, skills with universality, writing, programming, modeling, compound across every domain. And remember Babbage's blindness. When you've built something universal, your own imagination is the bottleneck. Stop asking what you built it for. Ask what it can do.
