When you look at a distant galaxy through a telescope, processed by computers into a false-color image on a screen, are you seeing something less real than a table in front of you? Deutsch says no, you're seeing something more directly. Here's his argument. All perception is already indirect. Your brain builds the visual world from nerve impulses, using theories, inborn and learned. You never touch reality raw. You live, always, inside a kind of virtual-reality chamber your own brain renders. A telescope and its software are just a better inference chain, built from better theories. Deutsch describes a night at an observatory, looking at a galaxy. Not one photon that hit his retina came from the galaxy, they all came from the display. Yet the observation was more faithful than any naked-eye glance, corrected for the atmosphere, amplified beyond what the eye can register. What makes an observation good is not how direct it feels, but how good the theories are in the chain from reality to your awareness. Here's the practice, and it cuts both ways. Trust your dashboards over your gut, if the pipeline's theories are sound, so audit them, what does each stage assume, and when was it last criticized. But give I saw it with my own eyes the very same scrutiny, because the brain's pipeline has famous bugs, bias, salience, memory that quietly rewrites itself.
