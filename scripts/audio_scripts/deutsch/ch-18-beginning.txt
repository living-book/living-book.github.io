Everything in the book converges here, on one claim. We are at the beginning of infinity. The Enlightenment, barely a few centuries old, began the first sustained tradition of criticism, the first dynamic society, and with it the open-ended, unlimited growth of knowledge. This is not the culmination of history. It's the infancy. Everything achieved so far is negligible against what's possible, because there is no bound, except the laws of physics, on what knowledge can achieve. Deutsch is fighting a pervasive feeling, that the great discoveries are behind us, that we live in a mature, nearly finished civilization tidying up the details. The whole book is marshaled against it. Good explanations have unlimited reach. People are universal constructors. Infinity is our working environment. All evils are just gaps in knowledge. Problem-solving has no end. So our era is not an ending. It's the fragile, precious start of an infinite process, and, crucially, one that could still be snuffed out. The dynamic society is rare and fragile. Static cultures are the historical norm, and the tradition of criticism survives only if we keep transmitting and defending it. Here's the practice, and it's the book's final gift. Treat your field, your product, your knowledge as early, not mature. That reframes ambition and humility at once. And guard the conditions of criticism, dissent, error-correction, teaching people how to question rather than what to conclude, because those are the fragile source of all progress, and the first thing to decay. The choice is civilizational, and it's yours.
