Two comfortable ideas need killing, Deutsch says, and one argument kills both. The first is the Principle of Mediocrity, we're nothing special, just chemical scum on a typical planet. The second is Spaceship Earth, the biosphere is our precious life-support system, perfectly tuned to sustain us. Both are factually backwards. Here's why. People are universal explainers and constructors. Anything not forbidden by the laws of physics is achievable, given the right knowledge. That makes knowledge-creators the most significant entities in the cosmos, not insignificant scum. And the biosphere? A typical place in the universe is intergalactic vacuum, near absolute zero. Earth's biosphere never comfortably supported humans either. Early people in the Great Rift Valley, the supposed cradle, died of cold, hunger, and parasites in what pastoral imagination calls paradise. What changed wasn't the biosphere. It was knowledge, fire, clothing, agriculture, medicine. Life support was built, not given. The Earth is not our spaceship. Knowledge is. Here's the practice. Look at your organization's self-image. Mediocrity culture says we're a small player, we just adapt. Spaceship culture says protect the legacy system, it sustains us. Both cap ambition at the status quo. The Deutschian question is different. Any outcome not forbidden by the laws of your domain is a knowledge problem. So the real question isn't what does our environment allow. It's what knowledge would make it allow more.
