Here's a genuine puzzle. Human creativity is the engine of change. Yet it evolved in societies that were almost entirely static, hostile to new ideas, punishing anyone who deviated. If innovation got you killed, why did evolution give us the power to innovate? Deutsch's answer is a beautiful inversion. Creativity didn't evolve to innovate. It evolved to transmit existing culture faithfully. Think about what a child must do to absorb the complex, mostly unspoken rules of a tribe. Not just copy movements, but guess the meanings and rules behind other people's behavior, conjecture them, test them, correct them. That's an act of creativity, and a purely imitative animal could never do it. You literally cannot acquire a human culture without the same explanatory creativity later used for science. So creativity's original survival value was high-fidelity conformity. And then, tragically, static societies bent this magnificent faculty toward reproducing their anti-rational memes, an immense engine of mind turned against itself, used to enforce sameness. Only the Enlightenment, rarely and recently, redirected it toward criticism and progress. Here's the practice. Recognize that acquiring a rich practice is itself a creative act, which reframes onboarding and apprenticeship entirely. Learning your craft deeply isn't passive absorption. It's conjecturing the hidden rules behind the masters' choices. And the organizational lesson, the same creative faculty your people use to learn how we do things can be turned to improving them, but only if your culture rewards criticism instead of conscripting creativity into pure conformity.
