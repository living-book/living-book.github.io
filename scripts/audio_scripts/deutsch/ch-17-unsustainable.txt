Sustainability, as most people mean it, reaching a stable state that endures unchanged, is not just unattainable, Deutsch argues. It's the wrong ideal entirely. It is the aspiration of a static society, and static societies fail, precisely because problems are inevitable and only new knowledge solves them. The right ideal is the opposite, not a fixed sustainable state but continual problem-solving, a dynamic society that expects unknown problems and keeps creating the knowledge to meet them. The appeal of living within our means, reaching balance with nature, is a category error. No past civilization survived by achieving stasis. The ones that tried were the most fragile, undone by the first problem their frozen knowledge couldn't handle. Sustainable prescribes exactly the posture that has always preceded collapse, stop creating knowledge, and trust that no new problem arrives. Take Easter Island. The usual moral is they exhausted their resources, they should have consumed less. Deutsch inverts it, they lacked the knowledge, and the knowledge-creating culture, to solve their emerging problems. Meanwhile every Malthusian ceiling in history, food, energy, materials, was shattered not by restraint but by new knowledge, the Haber process, the Green Revolution. Here's the practice, and one honest caveat. Reframe your stable-state goals as problem-solving-capacity goals. Durability comes from the ability to detect and solve emerging problems faster than they arrive, reserves of adaptability, not just resources. The caveat, some thresholds are irreversible, so weigh where betting on future knowledge could actually bite.
