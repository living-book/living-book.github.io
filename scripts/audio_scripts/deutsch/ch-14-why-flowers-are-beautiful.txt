Why do we find flowers beautiful? Deutsch uses the question to defend a startling claim, that some beauty is objective, not cultural, not arbitrary, not merely in the eye of the beholder. Here's the argument, and it runs through the flower and the insect that pollinates it. A flower must signal to a bee across an enormous gap, two species with no shared genes and therefore no shared code, no secret handshake they could have agreed on. For the flower to be recognized as attractive by a completely different kind of creature, the signal must meet criteria that neither party can cheaply fake. It must embody objective standards of beauty. And human attraction to flowers, which crosses every culture, taps that same objective beauty. Camouflage and mimicry can exploit cheap channels within a species. But advertising across the species gap, where arbitrariness can't survive, requires something real, hard to fake, hard to vary. Here's the practice. In design and communication, learn to separate parochial appeal from objective quality. When a signal has to land across a gap, a different culture, a different discipline, a different kind of user, the elements that survive that crossing are your candidates for real quality, not just local fashion. So to find what's genuinely good rather than merely trendy, test it across the widest audience gap you can reach. Whatever still works is closer to objective, hard-to-vary beauty.
