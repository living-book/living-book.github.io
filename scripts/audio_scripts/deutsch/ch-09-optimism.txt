Here is David Deutsch's principle of optimism, and it's a factual claim, not a mood. All evils are caused by insufficient knowledge. Every failure, every disaster, every suffering, is due to some knowledge we don't yet have, and knowledge can be created by criticizable conjecture. Optimism, properly defined, is just that. And its opposite, pessimism, is always a form of prophecy, claiming to know the unknowable, the future growth of knowledge that hasn't happened yet. From Malthus to modern prophets of the final century, doom-sayers extrapolate today's knowledge into a future where problems keep coming but knowledge freezes, systematically ignoring the one variable that has smashed every previous ceiling. Consider an asteroid on a collision course. For most of history, a natural disaster, pure fate. For a civilization that funds sky surveys, a preventable failure of knowledge. Deutsch contrasts two ancient societies, Athens, alive with criticism, and Sparta, engineered for stasis, a society built to prevent change and therefore unable to correct its own mistakes. Here's the practice. Take your team's three most persistent problems, outages, churn, burnout, and rewrite each as a sentence beginning we don't yet know how to. Suddenly it's a research agenda, not the weather. And ban prophecy from your planning. Scenarios, error budgets, yes. Confident declarations of what can't be done or can't go wrong, no. And fund your Spaceguard, the survey watching the problems you'd rather not think about.
