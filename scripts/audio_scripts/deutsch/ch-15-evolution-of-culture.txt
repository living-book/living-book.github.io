Cultures evolve, Deutsch says, by the variation and selection of memes, ideas that get copied. But he splits memes into two kinds with opposite survival strategies, and the split explains the whole shape of human history. Rational memes survive because they're useful and true. They help the people who hold them, so they get copied for their content, and they can withstand a critical, questioning mind. Anti-rational memes survive by the opposite trick. They disable the criticism that would otherwise kill them. They propagate by suppressing their holders' ability to reject them, through taboo, through terror, through the sanctification of tradition. And which kind dominates decides everything. It explains why almost every society in history was static, millennia of near-zero progress, and why a rare few became dynamic. Static societies aren't knowledge-poor by accident. They're actively structured, by anti-rational memes, to prevent new ideas and crush the criticism of old ones. Their stability is an achievement, purchased by disabling the curiosity of every new generation. Here's the practice. Diagnose your organization's meme-ecology. Do ideas survive here because they're true and useful, or because they're protected from criticism, by hierarchy, by taboo, by we've always done it this way, by fear of the messenger? The tell of an anti-rational meme, it punishes the act of questioning it, rather than answering the question. Dynamic teams transmit the practice of criticism. Static ones transmit conclusions and police dissent, then mistake the resulting quiet for health.
