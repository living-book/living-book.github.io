There's a famous set of mathematical results, Arrow's theorem, Condorcet's paradox, that seem to prove democracy is incoherent, that no fair way exists to turn individual preferences into a group decision. Deutsch says the math is real but wildly misapplied, because it inherits a deep error. It assumes choosing means aggregating fixed preferences, weighing given options by a rule. But rational decision-making, individual or collective, isn't aggregation. It's problem-solving, creating new options and criticizing away the errors. The paradoxes dissolve because their premise is false. He leans on Popper's reframing of the founding question of politics. Not Plato's who should rule, but how can we remove bad rulers and bad policies without violence. The right question for any decision system isn't does it faithfully mirror the will of the people. It's how well does it find and fix mistakes. A system optimized to represent opinion perfectly can be worse at correcting errors than a messier one that produces decisive change and clear accountability. Here's the practice. Redesign your decisions around error-correction, not consensus-measurement. The best team process isn't the one that most accurately polls everyone's preferences. It's the one that surfaces bad calls fastest and reverses them cheapest, favoring reversibility, clear ownership, rapid feedback. Ask of any governance scheme, how does it remove a bad policy. If the answer is slowly and painfully, no amount of fairness at the input will save it.
