Philosophy, Deutsch says, is never harmless, and some of it is actively poisonous. Bad philosophy is philosophy that prevents the growth of other knowledge, and it can capture an entire science. His prime exhibit, the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics, which taught two generations of physicists that seeking an account of quantum reality was naive, that the theory is only a tool for predictions, that foundational questions were career poison. Instrumentalism didn't stay modest. It became a doctrine that enforced its own immunity from criticism. Watch the mechanism. Facing genuinely strange results, the quantum pioneers adopted a positivist philosophy, only the observable is meaningful, as a shield. The shield hardened into curriculum, shut up and calculate. And dissenters were marginalized, not by argument, but by the doctrine that their questions were meaningless. When Everett derived the multiverse from the bare equations in nineteen fifty-seven, he met not refutation but shrugs and career exile. He left physics. Bad philosophy's signature, it doesn't win debates. It prevents them. Here's the practice. Audit your own field for question-forbidding doctrines. That's not measurable, so don't ask. That's philosophy, we ship product. The metric is the goal, discussion closed. Each may have started as useful discipline. Check whether it now functions as immunity. The test is cheap and symmetrical, what question would this doctrine punish me for asking seriously, and who benefits from the silence?
