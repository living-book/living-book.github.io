Knowledge does not come from your senses. That's the first shock of David Deutsch's book. It comes from conjecture criticized, guesses about what's really out there, tested against reality and against each other. And the guesses that win share one property Deutsch makes the foundation of everything. Good explanations are hard to vary. Every detail plays a necessary role, so you can't change any part without wrecking the whole thing. Take the seasons. The ancient Greek myth blamed Persephone's journey to the underworld. But every detail is arbitrary. Swap the goddess, swap grief for anger, and the myth works just as well, which is exactly why it explains nothing. It even predicted winter would grip the whole world at once, blind to the fact that Australia has summer when Greece has winter. Now the real explanation, the tilt of Earth's axis. The geometry forces every detail. It predicts opposite seasons in opposite hemispheres. You cannot vary it without breaking it. And it has reach, it explains seasons on planets no one has ever visited. That over-delivery is the signature of truth. Here's the practice. Test any claim, a market thesis, a postmortem, with one question. Could this story explain the opposite outcome just as smoothly, with a small tweak? If yes, it explains nothing. Prefer accounts where every piece is load-bearing, and prize the ones that reach beyond what they were built for.
