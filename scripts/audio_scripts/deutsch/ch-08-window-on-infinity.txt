Infinity isn't mystical, Deutsch says. It's the ordinary working environment of anyone who takes unlimited progress seriously. And to train your intuition, he checks you into Hilbert's Infinity Hotel. The hotel has infinitely many rooms, and every one is full. A new guest arrives. No problem, everyone moves up one room, and room one is free. Infinitely many new guests arrive? Everyone moves to double their room number, and all the odd rooms open up. Even infinitely many coaches, each carrying infinitely many passengers, can be absorbed. In an infinite hotel, full never means no vacancy. But it isn't anything-goes either. The hotel generates waste faster than it can dispose of it, and some tasks stay impossible even there. Infinity has exact structure. The moral runs through the whole book. Where knowledge is concerned, we are always at the beginning of infinity. Every achievement so far is negligible against what remains, and every argument that we're running out quietly assumes a finitude it hasn't earned. Here's the practice. Sort your constraints honestly. Teams treat idea-space as scarce, defending one concept as if it were the last, while treating real physical limits, schedules, attention, energy, as negotiable. That's exactly backwards. Conjecture-space is the Infinity Hotel, there's always another room for a rival explanation. But compute budgets and attention obey conservation laws. Optimism about the unbounded. Arithmetic about the bounded.
