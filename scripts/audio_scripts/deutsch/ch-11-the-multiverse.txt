Quantum theory, Deutsch argues, is not a mystery to be endured. It's an explanation to be believed. And read as an explanation rather than a recipe for predictions, it describes a multiverse, an immense number of parallel histories, differentiating as physical systems interact. He builds the picture carefully, fungible instances that are genuinely identical, histories that split apart, interference as the fleeting reunion of histories that only just diverged. This isn't an interpretation bolted onto the math. It's what the unvarnished math says. Consider the scandal it solves. For a century, quantum theory delivered magnificent predictions wrapped in official meaninglessness, shut up and calculate. Single-universe stories need a mysterious collapse, triggered by an ill-defined act of measurement. The multiverse needs no collapse at all. Measurement is just ordinary differentiation of histories. A single photon seeming to take two paths at once is really fungible instances in briefly-diverged histories, recombining to make a pattern no single-history story explains without magic. Here's the transferable skill, explanatory literalism. When your best model says something uncomfortable, try believing it, and see what follows, instead of adding epicycles to protect your intuition. Teams do quantum quietism all the time, using models whose implications they refuse to state out loud. It's just a tool is often a way of avoiding what the tool is telling you.
