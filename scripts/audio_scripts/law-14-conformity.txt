You in a group are not the same person as you alone. That's the uncomfortable finding at the heart of this law. Groups generate a field, a shared mood that rewards agreement, punishes difference, and quietly replaces your judgment with the group's. You don't notice it happening. Nobody does. That's the mechanism. Philip Zimbardo proved how fast it works. His Stanford prison experiment took ordinary, healthy students, labeled half guards and half prisoners, and within six days the pretend roles produced real cruelty, so real he had to shut it down. And at My Lai, ordinary soldiers committed an atrocity while one helicopter pilot, Hugh Thompson, landed between them and the villagers and said no. Same field, opposite outcome. The difference was a mind that stayed his own. Here's the practice. First, map your groups' unwritten rules, what can't be said at work, in your family, online. Just seeing them loosens their grip. Second, voice one small dissent this week, politely, in a low-stakes moment. Not to rebel, to exercise the muscle. Conformity compounds silently, and so does independence. The pull of the group is constant. Your awareness of it is the only variable you control.
