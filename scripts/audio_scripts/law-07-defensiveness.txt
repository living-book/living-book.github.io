You cannot argue someone into changing. Every person walks around defending three quiet beliefs about themselves. I am independent, I make my own decisions. I am intelligent. And I am fundamentally good. Push against any of those, even accidentally, and the walls go up. The argument is over, no matter how right you are. Lyndon Johnson built a career on knowing this. As a young congressional aide, then as the master of the Senate, he didn't push colleagues. He studied them. He made stubborn old senators feel wiser, more needed, more in charge, and then watched them talk themselves into exactly what he wanted. People never felt steamrolled by Johnson. They felt enhanced. That was the trick. There was no trick. He confirmed their self-opinion, and their resistance simply never activated. Here's the practice. Before your next difficult ask, do two things. First, genuinely affirm the other person's judgment, ask their advice and mean it. Second, frame your idea so the final step is theirs to take, not yours to win. People will fight your conclusion but defend their own to the death. Influence isn't about pressure. It's about making resistance unnecessary.
