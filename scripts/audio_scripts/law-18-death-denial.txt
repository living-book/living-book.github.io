You are going to die, and you spend astonishing energy pretending otherwise. Greene saves this law for last because it powers all the others. Push death out of awareness and life goes flat, days blur, decisions defer, and everything waits for a someday that isn't guaranteed. The denial doesn't protect you. It sedates you. William James, the great psychologist, spent his young adulthood paralyzed by depression and thoughts of death, close enough to the edge that existence itself felt optional. Then came the turn. He decided that if life was finite and free will uncertain, he would act as if his choices mattered, and find out. The urgency that had terrified him became fuel. He produced the work of several lifetimes, and preached to his students that the feeling of being fully alive sits right next to the awareness of the deadline. That's the paradox. Mortality, faced directly, doesn't darken life. It sharpens it. Here's the practice. Tonight, name one thing you've been postponing, the conversation, the project, the trip, and do the first concrete piece of it tomorrow. Not because life is short, but because that's what makes it vivid. Death is the deadline. Deadlines are why anything gets finished.
