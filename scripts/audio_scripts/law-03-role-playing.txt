Everyone you meet is wearing a mask. Polished, practiced, and presented for effect. People show you what they want you to see. The real information leaks out elsewhere. In the flash of a micro-expression. In a tone that doesn't match the words. In what happens to someone's face the moment they think no one is watching. Milton Erickson, the great psychiatrist, was a master at reading these leaks. Polio paralyzed him as a teenager, and confined to a bed, all he could do was watch people. He learned that bodies constantly broadcast what mouths conceal. A patient would say yes while their voice fell, and Erickson heard the no underneath. Decades of that observation made him seem like a mind reader. He wasn't. He was just actually looking. Here's the practice. In your next meeting, pick one person and watch the mismatch. When their words say agreement, check the jaw, the hands, the pace of the nod. One mismatch tells you more than an hour of talk. And remember, this law cuts both ways. You're on stage too. Play your role well, present your best mask deliberately, and never confuse anyone's performance, including your own, with the person underneath.
