There is a version of you that you refuse to meet. Greene calls it the shadow. The aggression, envy, greed, and strangeness you edited out to become a presentable adult. Repression doesn't kill any of it. It just moves it out of sight, where it gains pressure. Then it leaks. In sudden outbursts, in harsh judgments of people who do what you won't let yourself do, in secret behaviors that would shock your public. Richard Nixon is the American case study. Disciplined, controlled, publicly virtuous, and privately corroded by resentments he never faced. The shadow didn't stay buried. It wiretapped, it schemed, it raged onto tape, and it destroyed him at the height of his power. What you repress does not disappear. It waits. Here's the practice. Notice what you judge most harshly in others this week. That heat is usually a signpost to your own denied material. Then find one safe, controlled outlet for it. Competitiveness into sport, aggression into hard problems, darkness into creative work. Greene's paradox is worth keeping. People with an integrated shadow don't become worse. They become more real, more forceful, harder to manipulate. The danger was never the darkness. It was pretending it wasn't yours.
