The crowd that cheers you today will saw through your pedestal tomorrow. Greene calls it fickleness, and every leader lives with it. People want to be led, and they resent being led, both at once. Demand loyalty, act entitled to your position, and the resentment wins. Authority, real authority, is never owed. It's earned, and re-earned, daily. George Marshall earned it as well as anyone in the twentieth century. He never campaigned for rank, never grabbed credit, never postured. He took the blame, gave away the glory, told presidents hard truths, and delivered, decade after decade. When he declined to command the D-Day invasion, the fame went to Eisenhower, and Marshall's stature somehow grew. Congress, generals, and rival politicians followed him because following him never felt like submission. It felt like good judgment. Here's the practice. Take your next visible success and hand the credit away, publicly, specifically, by name. Then take the next failure in your orbit and absorb your share of it out loud. Do both consistently and something odd happens. You stop demanding followership, and it starts arriving on its own. Fickle crowds test every leader the same way. They watch what you do when there's nothing in it for you.
