This is the heart of Lakatos, his answer to the oldest question in the philosophy of science. What separates science from pseudoscience? Not truth. Not testability. Trajectory. A programme is progressive if each new version predicts novel, surprising facts, and some of them come true. It's degenerating if its adjustments only explain things after the fact, forever patching, never predicting. Science versus pseudoscience isn't about content. It's about whether you're running ahead of the facts or panting behind them. His two exhibits are unforgettable. Einstein's programme predicted that starlight would bend around the sun, an outrageous, improbable claim, before Eddington measured it and found it true. Stunning novel content. Progressive. Now Marxism. Lakatos asks bluntly, did it ever predict a novel fact that came true? Its famous predictions failed, workers getting poorer, revolution in the most industrial nations, and each failure was met with a patch that predicted nothing further. Same structure as Newton, hard core, belt, heuristic. Opposite trajectory. And crucially, degenerating is not dead. A programme behind the facts can recover, as Prout's did. The verdict is always provisional, always dated. Here's the practice. Audit your frameworks quarterly. List the last five adjustments to your strategy or product thesis. For each, did it predict something new that came true, or explain away a miss? Five explanations in a row is degeneration, whatever the sunk cost says.
