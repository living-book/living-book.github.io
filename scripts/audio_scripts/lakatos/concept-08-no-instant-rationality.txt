Here's the most honest, and most unsettling, thing Lakatos ever said. His methodology can tell you a programme is degenerating. It cannot tell you the rational moment to abandon it. Appraisal is not advice. The score is objective, but it's announced in retrospect, sometimes decades late. It is not irrational to stick with a degenerating programme, it might recover. It's not irrational to gamble on a young one still behind its rivals. Rationality works, but slowly, and fallibly. The dream of instant rationality, a rulebook that delivers a verdict at every moment, is a myth. His example cuts deep. The Michelson-Morley experiment is taught as the crucial experiment that killed the ether. In reality, it was performed in eighteen eighty-seven and treated for decades as a puzzling anomaly inside the ether programme. It became crucial only after Einstein's programme succeeded, a quarter-century later. The execution happened long before anyone knew a crucial experiment had occurred. Hindsight wrote the caption. Here's the practice, and it demands two disciplines. First, humility. The verdict on today's contested bets belongs to the future. Treat confident real-time obituaries and coronations as fiction. Second, honesty. You may rationally stay with a struggling approach or leap to an immature one, but you must say which, out loud, and show the real score. The sin isn't the bet. It's cooking the books.
