Lakatos ends with a dizzying move. He turns his method on itself. Methodologies of science, he says, are themselves research programmes, and history is the ground where they're tested. His meta-criterion, the best methodology is the one that reconstructs the largest part of great science as rational, leaving the smallest residue for psychology and sociology to mop up. Inductivism fails this test. So does naive falsificationism. Each has to call Newton or Copernicus or Einstein irrational at their finest moments, which means the methodology is degenerating as a theory. History becomes the ocean of anomalies in which methodologies sink or swim. His provocation was legendary. He proposed writing history of science as rational reconstruction in the main text, with the actual, messier history down in the footnotes, and the footnotes noting exactly where real scientists deviated from rationality's script. Critics gasped, this is caricature, not history. And Lakatos grinned, because the caricature is the point. It shows precisely how much of the record reason can claim, and prices what's left for sociology to buy. Here's the practice. Apply the meta-criterion to your own decision frameworks. A good framework should reconstruct most of your best past decisions as rational applications of it. If your prized methodology would have forbidden the three best calls your team ever made, the methodology is degenerating, not the calls. And keep honest footnotes, where reality departed from the method. The size of those footnotes is your framework's real test score.
