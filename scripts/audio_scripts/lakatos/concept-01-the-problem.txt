Imre Lakatos wrote in the middle of a war. On one side, Karl Popper: science is rational because it refutes its theories. On the other, Thomas Kuhn: history shows scientists clinging to refuted theories for decades, changing their minds by something that looks like conversion, which Popper called mob psychology. Lakatos saw both sides were half right, and half fatal. Popper's clean logic can't survive contact with real history. Kuhn's real history seems to throw logic overboard. His life's project was to save scientific rationality while fitting what scientists actually did. His key example is devastating. Newton's theory of gravitation, the most successful research programme in history, was born refuted and stayed refuted. The moon's orbit misbehaved for decades. Mercury never quite fit. On Popper's naive rules, Newton should have been abandoned at birth. Instead the anomalies became fuel. Any methodology that condemns Newton condemns itself. So Lakatos changes the question. Stop asking, is this theory falsified? Everything is falsified, always, from birth. Start asking, is this sequence of theories growing? Here's the practice. Whenever you catch yourself asking is this technology dead, is this strategy finished, you're asking a snapshot question. Ask the trajectory question instead. Not where is it now, but is the series still producing new successes.
