Around the hard core sits the protective belt, and it's where all the real work happens. The belt is everything adjustable, the auxiliary assumptions, the instrument theories, the initial conditions. When a prediction fails, the belt takes the hit, by design. You refine an assumption, you remeasure, you add a correction factor. The core stays untouched. Day-to-day science is belt engineering. But here's the crucial distinction Lakatos draws. Not all belt adjustments are equal. Some are content-increasing, they predict new facts. Some are merely ad hoc, they only explain backwards, saving the theory while predicting nothing. The difference is everything. Return to Neptune. Uranus misbehaves, and Newtonians adjust an auxiliary assumption, the number of planets, predicting a new one at an exact location. Gloriously content-increasing. Now the mirror image. The same move for Mercury, postulating a planet Vulcan inside its orbit, never found, re-postulated, never found. Identical maneuver, opposite verdict, because one predicted something new and one just made excuses. Here's the practice. Run your postmortems with belt discipline. List the assumptions between your core system and the failure, config, data quality, third parties, and route blame there first. But demand each fix predict some new improvement. A fix that only explains the incident backwards is ad hoc debt. And watch for your Vulcan, the patch you've applied three times.
