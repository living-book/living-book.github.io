Why aren't scientists paralyzed by the ocean of anomalies surrounding every theory? Lakatos's answer is the positive heuristic, the programme's battle plan. It's a partly articulated agenda telling researchers which problems to attack, in what order, which idealizations to relax next. And it lets them work through a planned sequence of increasingly realistic models, largely ignoring the actual anomalies, because the plan already anticipates them. Newton's march is the showpiece. First a point-sun with a single point-planet. Then multiple planets, adding their mutual pull. Then planets as extended balls. Then spinning, bulging spheroids. Then wobbling axes and tides. Newton knew every early stage was false before he calculated it. The anomalies each crude stage would face were foreseen and queued. That's not scrambling to answer refutations. That's a curriculum. This is why great programmes feel inevitable in hindsight. The whole sequence of papers was implicit in the founding idea, waiting to be unrolled. Here's the practice. The difference between a roadmap and a backlog is exactly the positive heuristic. A backlog reacts to whatever broke. A roadmap embodies a planned lifting of assumptions, version one assumes one region, benign users, version two relaxes one, version three the next. If every incoming anomaly reshuffles your priorities, you don't have a programme. You have a complaint queue.
