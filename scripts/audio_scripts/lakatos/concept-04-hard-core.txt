Every research programme has a center its defenders simply refuse to question. Lakatos calls it the hard core, and the rule protecting it the negative heuristic. When anomalies arrive, you are forbidden to blame the core. You deflect the blame outward, to the adjustable assumptions around it. This sounds like pure dogmatism. Lakatos insists it's rational, and necessary. If scientists renegotiated their foundations at every anomaly, they'd never derive the deep consequences that show a theory's real power. The programme would die of doubt before its strength appeared. Fixing the foundations by decision is a division of labor across time. His example is beautiful. In eighteen fifteen, Prout proposed that all atomic weights are whole-number multiples of hydrogen's. Measurements disagreed constantly. Chlorine came out at thirty-five and a half. Proutians didn't touch the core. For a century they attacked the belt instead, questioning their purification methods, their instruments. Vindication came absurdly late, when isotopes explained the anomalies and the core stood essentially right. A hundred years of dogmatism, paid off. Here's the practice. Name your hard core out loud, the two or three commitments your team won't renegotiate this year, and route all criticism to the belt around them. But mark a date to review the core. Protection is a policy, and policies should expire on evidence.
