You don't want what you have. You want what's just out of reach. The moment you possess something, it goes quiet. The thing across the fence keeps glowing. Greene calls this covetousness, and it runs your desires more than you'd like to admit. It also runs everyone else's, and that's the strategic part. Coco Chanel understood it completely. She never chased the crowd. She withdrew, created scarcity, released little, explained nothing. The less available she made herself and her work, the more ferociously the world wanted both. Her mystery wasn't shyness. It was architecture. She built desire out of absence. Here's the practice, in two directions. First, on yourself. When you ache for the new job, the new city, the new person, remember the ache is partly an illusion of distance. Whatever you obtain will become ordinary within months. Learn to want the thing in front of you. Second, on how you show up. Stop overexplaining. Stop being endlessly available. Leave gaps, in your time, in your story, in your presence. People's imaginations will fill those gaps with something better than you could ever pitch. Presence is nice. Absence, used deliberately, is power.
