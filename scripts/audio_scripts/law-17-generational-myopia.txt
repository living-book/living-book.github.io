You think the way you see the world is just realism. It's actually a birthmark. Every generation is shaped by the crisis and mood of its formative years, and then mistakes that shaping for objective truth. Depression children hoard. Boom children spend. Each cohort scoffs at the last one and is blind to itself. Greene calls it generational myopia, and the people who escape it gain an almost unfair advantage, they can see the present while everyone else argues with the past. Benjamin Franklin kept escaping it, for eighty years. Printer, scientist, revolutionary, diplomat, he read each era's rising spirit and moved with it while contemporaries defended expired worlds. In Paris, aged seventy, surrounded by powdered wigs and formality, he showed up as the plain-spoken frontier philosopher, exactly what that moment craved, and charmed France into funding a revolution. That wasn't luck. It was zeitgeist literacy. Here's the practice. Ask someone twenty years younger what's changing, and actually listen, without correcting them. Their world isn't a worse version of yours. It's the draft of the next one. Then ask what your own generation can't see. History doesn't repeat for the alert. It hands them the timing everyone else misses.
