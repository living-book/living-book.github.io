Success is a drug, and the dose that kills judgment is smaller than you think. Greene calls it grandiosity. Win a few rounds, collect some praise, and a quiet inflation begins. You start crediting outcomes entirely to your own genius. Luck, timing, and other people's work fade from the story. Your sense of what you can control swells past reality, and reality is patient about collecting. Michael Eisner ran the pattern at Disney. His first years were spectacular, and the spectacle convinced him the brilliance was all his. He sidelined dissent, made ever-grander bets, launched a disastrous theme park, hired and destroyed a president in months, and drove away the partners who had balanced him. The board finally forced him out of the company he had saved. The higher the altitude, the thinner the honesty. Here's the practice. After your next win, write down every factor that wasn't you. The timing, the market, the colleague's assist, the plain luck. Not as false modesty, as calibration. Then let your ambitions grow one deliberate size at a time, each bet slightly beyond the last, none of them fatal. Know your limits isn't advice to shrink. It's how you keep compounding instead of combusting.
