The most dangerous aggression doesn't look like aggression. It smiles, shakes your hand, and calls itself partnership. Greene's law says a current of hostility runs under human affairs, and the sophisticated aggressors long ago learned to disguise it, as friendliness, as generosity, as reasonable requests that keep arriving until you've given everything away. John D. Rockefeller built Standard Oil that way. Rarely a raised voice, rarely an open threat. Instead, warm offers to struggling rivals, quiet control of the railroads their businesses depended on, and a squeeze so gradual that surrender always looked like a fair deal by the time it came. His competitors kept reading the smile. They should have been reading the pressure. Here's the practice, in two directions. Outward, with anyone new to your interests, weigh actions and ignore words. Watch for boundary tests, small encroachments floated to see what you tolerate, and answer the first one calmly and firmly. Aggressors sort the world into resistance and prey, and they sort early. Inward, stop pretending you have no aggression of your own. You do. Channeled, it's ambition, persistence, the force to finish things. Denied, it leaks out sideways as passive hostility. See the wolf in others. Harness the one in yourself.
