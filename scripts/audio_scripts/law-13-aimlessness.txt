Most people are not living a life. They're reacting to one. Drifting between jobs, trends, and other people's definitions of success, busy every hour and aimless every year. Greene calls this the law of aimlessness, and its cure is what he calls a sense of purpose, not a slogan, but an organizing force that decides what matters and what's noise. Martin Luther King Junior didn't start with one. He was a reluctant young pastor, pushed to the front of the Montgomery bus boycott almost by accident. Then one night, threatened and terrified in his kitchen, he found it, a calling larger than his fear. From that moment, every decision had a compass. Strategies changed, setbacks came, but the direction never wobbled. Purpose didn't make him fearless. It made fear irrelevant to the question of what to do next. Here's the practice. Write one sentence, what I am building toward is, and finish it honestly. Not what sounds impressive. What pulls you. Look for the clues in what fascinated you as a child, in work that doesn't feel like work. Then use the sentence as a filter for one week. Watch how many urgent things stop mattering. A drifting boat works as hard as a sailing one. Only one of them arrives.
