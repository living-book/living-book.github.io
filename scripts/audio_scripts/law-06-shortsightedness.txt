In 1720, the smartest man alive lost a fortune in a stock bubble. Isaac Newton sold his South Sea shares early, banked a solid profit, then watched the price keep climbing. The fever got him. He bought back in near the top and was wiped out, losing the equivalent of millions. He reportedly said he could calculate the motions of heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people. That's the law of shortsightedness. Under excitement or panic, the present moment swells until it blocks the entire future. Everyone around you reacts to today's noise, today's trend, today's emergency, and their urgency is contagious. Greene's counsel is to elevate your perspective. Pull back until you can see consequences, and the consequences of consequences. Here's the practice. Take the thing that feels most urgent this week, and ask, will this matter in five years? Then ask what the five-year version of you would want done about it now. Most urgent things dissolve under that lens. The few that survive are your real work. The crowd lives in the next five minutes. Almost nobody competes with you in the next five years. That's the emptiest, most profitable ground there is.
