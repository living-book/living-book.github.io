Your attitude is not a reaction to your life. It's the thing creating it. Greene's law of self-sabotage says each of us looks at the world through a lens ground in childhood, hostile, fearful, resentful, or open, and then the lens manufactures the evidence. Expect betrayal and you act guarded, and guarded people attract distance, and distance looks like betrayal. The prophecy fulfills itself and calls itself realism. Anton Chekhov is the counterexample. Raised poor, beaten by a violent father, he had every right to a bitter lens. Instead, as a young man, he made a deliberate decision. He chose to understand his father rather than hate him, to treat generosity as a discipline. The bitterness lifted, and one of literature's most humane voices emerged from one of its harshest childhoods. The lens was the choice. Here's the practice. Catch one hostile interpretation this week. A curt message, a meeting you weren't invited to, and hold it up. Write down two harmless explanations. You'll usually find you were about to sabotage a relationship over fiction. Circumstances are stubborn, but the lens is yours. Change the attitude, and the circumstances begin, slowly and strangely, to follow.
