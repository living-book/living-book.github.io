# Concept 6 — The Positive Heuristic

## Core Thesis

The **positive heuristic** is the programme's battle plan: a partially articulated agenda that tells researchers which problems to tackle in which order, how to modify the belt, which idealizations to lift next. It lets scientists work in a planned sequence of increasingly realistic models — largely *ignoring* actual anomalies, because the plan already anticipates the refutations of each crude stage.

## The Problem It Solves

Two mysteries at once. Why aren't scientists paralyzed by the ocean of anomalies? Because the heuristic pre-schedules them: today's model is known-false by design, and its failures are next quarter's agenda. And what fills Kuhn's "puzzle-solving" with direction? Not the paradigm's gravity but a plan — the relative autonomy of theoretical science, where the sequence of problems is chosen by the programme, not dictated by nature's complaints.

## Key Episode

Newton's planned march, Lakatos's showpiece: first a point-sun and single point-planet; then multiple planets (mutual perturbations); then planets as extended balls; then spinning, bulging spheroids; then wobbling axes and interplanetary tides. Newton knew each stage was false before calculating it. The anomalies each stage would face were foreseen and queued. That's not response to refutation — that's a curriculum.

## The Shift

From reactive science (anomaly arrives → scramble) to programmed science (models improve on schedule → anomalies met when the plan reaches them). The heuristic also explains why great programmes feel inevitable in hindsight: the sequence of papers was implicit in the founding idea, waiting to be unrolled.

## Critiques & Rivals

How much of this is retrospective tidying? Historians doubt Newton possessed the plan as cleanly as the reconstruction shows. And heuristics run out — Lakatos concedes programmes can exhaust their positive heuristic and drift. Kuhn's rejoinder: what Lakatos calls a plan is exactly what exemplars-plus-training produce, no separate entity needed.

## Modern Application

The difference between a roadmap and a backlog is the positive heuristic. A backlog reacts; a roadmap embodies a planned relaxation of idealizations — v1 assumes one region, one currency, benign users; v2 lifts one assumption; v3 the next. Teams with a real heuristic ignore most incoming anomalies *rationally*, because the plan already schedules them. If every anomaly reorders your priorities, you don't have a programme; you have a complaint queue.

## Key Terms

- **Positive heuristic** — the planned agenda for developing the belt
- **Relative autonomy of theory** — problem choice driven by plan, not anomaly
- **Sequence of models** — deliberately false approximations, refined on schedule

## Key Quotes

> "The positive heuristic consists of a partially articulated set of suggestions or hints on how to change, develop the 'refutable variants' of the research programme."

> "Which problems scientists working in powerful research programmes rationally choose, is determined by the positive heuristic of the programme rather than by psychologically worrying (or technologically urgent) anomalies."

## Reflection Questions

1. Write your project's model sequence: which idealization does each next version lift?
2. Which anomalies can you now *rationally* ignore because the plan schedules them?
3. Is your heuristic running out — when did the plan last generate a problem, rather than the bug tracker?
