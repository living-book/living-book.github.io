# Concept 7 — Progressive vs Degenerating Programmes

## Core Thesis

The heart of the methodology — the demarcation criterion itself. A programme is **theoretically progressive** if each new version predicts novel, unexpected facts; **empirically progressive** if some of those predictions are corroborated; **degenerating** if its adjustments only accommodate facts after the fact, explaining yesterday while predicting nothing. Science versus pseudoscience is not a property of content but of *trajectory*: is the programme running ahead of the facts, or panting behind them?

## The Problem It Solves

Demarcation, at last, with history intact. Marxism and Freudianism aren't unscientific because they're false or untestable — they're degenerating because every adjustment has been retrospective patchwork ("the workers' rising living standard? false consciousness!"). Meanwhile Newton lived in an ocean of anomalies yet stayed gloriously scientific: each Newtonian repair predicted something new. The criterion judges programmes the way markets judge companies — on the growth curve, not the balance-sheet snapshot.

## Key Episode

Lakatos's twin exhibits. Einstein's programme: predicted light bending before Eddington measured it — stunning novel content, empirically progressive. Marxism, in Lakatos's harsh audit: did it ever predict a novel fact successfully? Its famous predictions failed (workers' immiseration, revolution in the most industrial nations), and each failure was met by belt-patches predicting nothing further. The structure — hard core, belt, heuristic — is identical in both. The trajectory is opposite.

## The Shift

From "is it falsifiable?" to "is it fertile?" — the demarcation question becomes dynamic. And crucially: degenerating is not dead. A programme behind the facts can recover (Prout's did). The verdict is always provisional, always dated, always revisable — which is exactly why no algorithm ever tells you the moment to abandon ship.

## Critiques & Rivals

Feyerabend pounced: if degeneration doesn't dictate abandonment, the methodology gives no advice, so anything goes. "Novel fact" itself proved slippery — must it be temporally new, or merely not-used-in-construction? (The debate refined it toward the latter: use-novelty.) And measuring progress requires deciding what the programme's adjustments were *for* — historians can contest every scoring.

## Modern Application

Audit your frameworks quarterly like a Lakatosian: list the last five adjustments (to strategy, model, product thesis). For each — did it predict something new that came true, or did it explain away a miss? Five explanations in a row = degeneration, whatever the sunk investment says. The same audit exposes hype: a technology whose every iteration merely re-explains its unmet promises is a degenerating programme wearing a roadmap.

## Key Terms

- **Progressive problemshift** — adjustments with corroborated novel content
- **Degenerating problemshift** — retrospective accommodation only
- **Novel fact** — improbable, unexpected, not built into the theory

## Key Quotes

> "A research programme is said to be progressing as long as its theoretical growth anticipates its empirical growth... it is stagnating if its theoretical growth lags behind its empirical growth."

> "What really count are dramatic, unexpected, stunning predictions: a few of them are enough to tilt the balance."

## Reflection Questions

1. Score your main framework's last five adjustments: predictions or excuses?
2. Which celebrated project in your orbit is degenerating behind a confident roadmap?
3. What novel fact — improbable in advance — has your current strategy actually called?
