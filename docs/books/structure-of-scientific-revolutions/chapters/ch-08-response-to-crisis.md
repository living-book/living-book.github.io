# Chapter 8 — The Response to Crisis

## Core Thesis

Even in deep crisis, scientists do not treat anomalies as Popperian refutations, and they do not abandon a paradigm until an alternative candidate is available. **To reject one paradigm without substituting another is to reject science itself.** Crisis loosens the rules, multiplies competing articulations, and drives some practitioners to philosophy — but the decision is always a comparison between paradigms, never between paradigm and nature alone.

## The Problem It Solves

The falsificationist puzzle: every theory, at every moment, faces unsolved anomalies — so why isn't everything refuted always? Kuhn's answer: anomaly only becomes decisive when (a) it resists the field's best attacks, (b) it strikes fundamentals, and (c) a rival framework can claim it as a solved problem. Until then, it's just another puzzle on the pile.

## Key Episode

The response to the failing Ptolemaic system: most astronomers patched epicycles for generations — rationally, because patching had always worked. Others, like Copernicus, turned to philosophical first principles and to alternatives (Neoplatonic sun-worship, ancient heliocentric hints). Kuhn also cites Einstein's testimony that, before relativity, it was "as if the ground had been pulled out from under one" — and notes the flight to first-principles debate that marks physics in every deep crisis.

## The Shift

From refutation-logic to comparative judgment: the unit of scientific choice is never "theory vs. evidence" but "theory vs. theory vs. evidence." Crisis behavior also gets a typology: some scientists redouble normal efforts, some quietly leave the field, some — usually young or newcomers — chase the wild options that the loosened rules now permit.

## Critiques & Rivals

Popper conceded scientists behave this way but insisted they *shouldn't* — dogmatism as vice. Lakatos reframed the point as method: never abandon a programme merely for anomalies; abandon it for a *better programme*. Feyerabend pushed further: even proliferation before crisis is good. The empirical claim — no rejection without replacement — has held up remarkably well.

## Modern Application

Never kill a system, strategy, or process on failure alone; kill it on comparison. "This is broken" is a complaint; "this alternative solves what breaks here" is a decision. Conversely, if you want an old approach retired, don't catalogue its failures — build the working alternative that makes the failures fatal. And expect the boldest option-generation from people least invested in the old commitments.

## Key Terms

- **Paradigm comparison** — choice is triadic: two paradigms and nature
- **Rule-loosening** — crisis licenses speculation normal science forbids
- **Extraordinary science** — the transitional research mode inside crisis

## Key Quotes

> "To reject one paradigm without simultaneously substituting another is to reject science itself."

> "Once it has achieved the status of paradigm, a scientific theory is declared invalid only if an alternate candidate is available to take its place."

## Reflection Questions

1. What failing approach are you attacking without offering a replacement?
2. In your last big pivot, was the trigger the failure — or the appearance of a viable alternative?
3. Who in your organization is free enough of legacy commitments to generate wild options?
