# Chapter 13 — Progress through Revolutions

## Core Thesis

Science progresses — but **from**, not **toward**. Kuhn's closing move is an evolutionary analogy: as Darwin removed the goal from biological evolution, Kuhn removes the goal from scientific development. Later science is better at puzzle-solving than earlier science — more problems solved, more precision — without needing to be closer to a final truth. Progress is real, directional, and goalless.

## The Problem It Solves

The nagging worry the whole book creates: if revolutions replace rather than extend, and no paradigm-neutral standard exists, why does science so obviously *work*? Kuhn's answer separates two things everyone had fused: instrumental progress (undeniable — puzzle-solving power grows) and teleological progress (unnecessary — "closer to the truth" does no explanatory work). Science can be the most progressive human enterprise without approaching anything.

## Key Episode

Part of the answer is sociological: science looks uniquely progressive partly because the victors write the textbooks (Chapter 11), but mostly because of the community's peculiar structure — insulated from lay demands, sharing rigorous training, judging work only by peers. Kuhn's list of what makes a field progress reads today like a design spec: shared exemplars, autonomy from external pressure, a communication network, and generational turnover through education.

## The Shift

From truth-approach to fitness-increase. The evolutionary analogy: paradigms are selected by competition for puzzle-solving capacity within an environment of anomalies, the way organisms are selected by reproductive fitness — no telos required, and irreversibility guaranteed. Kuhn ends by asking what knowledge *of* the world could mean under this picture — the question his critics, and his own later work, chased for decades.

## Critiques & Rivals

Scientific realists find this the book's deepest error: the no-miracles argument (science's success would be miraculous unless theories approximately track truth) directly targets this chapter. Structural realists offer the compromise — structure accumulates across revolutions even as ontologies churn. Kuhn held the line: he saw no coherent sense in "closer to the truth," and his final work recast development as speciation of scientific lexicons.

## Modern Application

Measure progress as capability, not destiny. Teams chasing "the right architecture" or "the final org design" mistake the game: there is no destination, only increasing problem-solving fitness under changing environments. This liberates: you don't need to justify a change as The Truth, only as solving more of what currently blocks you. And it warns: today's winner is fit for today's anomalies — environments shift, and fitness expires.

## Key Terms

- **Progress from** — development away from primitive beginnings, toward no fixed goal
- **Evolutionary analogy** — paradigm selection as goalless fitness competition
- **Instrumental vs. teleological progress** — puzzle-solving power vs. truth-approach

## Key Quotes

> "We may, to be more precise, have to relinquish the notion, explicit or implicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to the truth."

> "The developmental process described in this essay has been a process of evolution from primitive beginnings... But nothing that has been or will be said makes it a process of evolution toward anything."

## Reflection Questions

1. What would change in your work if you optimized "solves more problems" instead of "approaches the right answer"?
2. Which fitness of yours — skill, product, strategy — is adapted to an environment that's shifting?
3. Does your team's definition of progress smuggle in a destination nobody has justified?
