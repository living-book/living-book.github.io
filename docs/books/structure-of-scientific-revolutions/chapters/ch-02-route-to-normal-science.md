# Chapter 2 — The Route to Normal Science

## Core Thesis

A field becomes a science when it acquires its first **paradigm** — an achievement so compelling that it draws practitioners away from competing schools and so open-ended that it leaves plenty of problems to solve. Before the paradigm: chaos of competing schools, each rebuilding foundations. After: a community that agrees on fundamentals and gets to work on details.

## The Problem It Solves

Why do some fields (physics, chemistry) show rapid consolidated progress while others endlessly re-argue their foundations? Kuhn's answer isn't intelligence or method — it's paradigm acquisition. Pre-paradigm fields waste their energy on foundational debates; every author starts from scratch, addressing the general educated public rather than fellow specialists.

## Key Episode

Optics before Newton. Epicureans, Aristotelians, and Platonists each had theories of light — light as particles, as modification of the medium, as interaction between medium and eye. All were scientific in method; none commanded the field. Newton's *Opticks* provided the first near-universal paradigm for physical optics. Similarly, electricity before Franklin was a babel of competing effluvium theories; Franklin's account of the Leyden jar unified the field.

## The Shift

From school-versus-school debate to a shared foundation nobody has to re-defend. The signs of arrival: specialized journals, professional societies, textbooks replacing original works, and research reports that no layperson can read. Losing the general audience is, perversely, a sign of maturity.

## Critiques & Rivals

Social scientists have argued their fields are multi-paradigm sciences rather than pre-paradigm ones. Kuhn's own criteria for "having a paradigm" were attacked as vague — Margaret Masterman famously counted 21 senses of the word in the book. The Postscript concedes and refines this.

## Modern Application

Diagnose any field or organization: is it pre-paradigm (every meeting relitigates foundations) or paradigmatic (shared assumptions, cumulative work)? Startups often die in pre-paradigm mode — endless strategy debates, no accumulation. The first "good-enough exemplar" — a working product, a repeatable playbook — plays the role of paradigm: it ends foundational war and lets normal work begin.

## Key Terms

- **Paradigm** — a concrete scientific achievement that supplies a community's models of problems and solutions
- **Pre-paradigm period** — competing schools, foundational debate, random fact-gathering
- **Normal science** — research firmly based upon a shared paradigm

## Key Quotes

> "To be accepted as a paradigm, a theory must seem better than its competitors, but it need not, and in fact never does, explain all the facts with which it can be confronted."

> "In the absence of a paradigm... all of the facts that could possibly pertain to the development of a given science are likely to seem equally relevant."

## Reflection Questions

1. Which parts of your work are pre-paradigm — where does every discussion restart from foundations?
2. What was the founding exemplar of your discipline or team, and what problems did it declare solvable?
3. What did your field stop debating when it professionalized — and was anything valuable lost?
