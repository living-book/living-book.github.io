# Theory-Laden Perception

## Definition

Kuhn argues paradigm change is like a gestalt switch — a duck becomes a rabbit — except that unlike the classic gestalt image, the scientist has no access to a neutral, stimulus-level description to fall back on afterward. "What were ducks in the scientist's world before the revolution are rabbits afterwards." The claim is not merely that scientists interpret the same data differently; it is that the categories of observation themselves — what counts as a pendulum versus constrained fall, a planet versus a wandering star — are supplied by the paradigm, so the data change too.

## In the Book

The signature case: where Aristotle saw a heavy body straining toward its natural resting place, Galileo saw a pendulum — near-frictionless periodic motion, a different phenomenon with different measurable quantities. Kuhn extends this to post-Copernican astronomers who "saw" sunspots and a mutable heavens in a sky that had not physically changed, and to chemists after Dalton who saw fixed proportions where their predecessors had seen continuous solutions. He draws on the Bruner-Postman experiment with anomalous playing cards and on gestalt psychology generally, but sharpens the point: trained perception, once restructured, does not coexist with the old way of seeing — it replaces it.

## Why It Matters

This dissolves the assumption that disagreements can always be settled by "just looking at the data," because looking is itself trained and partisan. It predicts a specific, disorienting cost of switching frameworks — a period where you literally cannot see what you used to, which is retraining rather than incompetence — and it argues for deliberately staffing decisions with differently-trained eyes, since experts don't just hold different opinions about a shared object, they perceive different objects. The idea is echoed by later cognitive science findings that expert perception (a chess master's board, a radiologist's scan) is genuinely restructured by training, not just differently interpreted.
