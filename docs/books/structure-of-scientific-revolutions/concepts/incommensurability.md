# Incommensurability

## Definition

Two paradigms are incommensurable when there is no common measure between them: not just different answers, but different questions, different standards for what counts as answering, and often different meanings attached to the same words. Kuhn's sharpest example is perceptual rather than verbal — where Aristotle saw constrained fall (a heavy body struggling toward its natural place), Galileo saw a pendulum (near-frictionless periodic motion). Same swinging stone, different phenomenon, different measurable quantities, different science.

## In the Book

Chapter 9 establishes the logical form: paradigm debates are circular because each side's criteria for a good theory are themselves paradigm-dependent, so "neither may hope to prove his case" to the other. Chapter 10 grounds this perceptually, drawing on the Bruner-Postman experiment with anomalous playing cards (a red six of spades) and gestalt psychology's duck-rabbit to argue that scientists, unlike lab subjects, have no neutral stimulus-level description to fall back on — the paradigm supplies the phenomenal world itself. Facing the "irrationalism" charge this provoked, Kuhn's 1969 Postscript walks the claim back from mysticism toward mechanism: rival paradigm-speakers are like speakers of different languages, communication is partial rather than impossible, and laborious translation is achievable even where full conversion is not.

## Why It Matters

Incommensurability names a specific failure mode in disagreements that looks like stubbornness or bad faith but is actually a translation problem: two parties using the same words to mean structurally different things, each internally consistent, neither reducible to the other by adding more data. Recognizing it changes the remedy — from "present better evidence" to "do the translation work" or "wait for demonstrations that recruit converts" — and it applies wherever expert communities built on different foundational commitments talk past each other, from science to organizational or ideological conflict.
