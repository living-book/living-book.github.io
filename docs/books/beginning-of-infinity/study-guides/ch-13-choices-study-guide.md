# Study Guide: Chapter 13 — Choices

## Core Idea
Arrow/Condorcet impossibility results are real but misapplied: they assume choice means aggregating fixed preferences. Rational decision-making is problem-solving — creating options and criticizing errors. Good institutions optimize error-correction, not representational fidelity.

## Key Terms
Arrow's theorem · error-correction (political) · choice as problem-solving · Popper's question

## Case Summary
Popper's reframing: not "who should rule?" but "how to remove bad rulers/policies without violence?" Plurality systems may out-correct proportional ones.

## Application Checklist
- [ ] Ask if your decision process measures preferences or removes bad decisions fast
- [ ] Stop treating preferences as fixed inputs — they're improvable conjectures
- [ ] Find your cheapest mechanism to reverse a wrong decision

## Self-Test
1. Why do the impossibility theorems misfire on democracy?
2. What is Popper's reframed founding question of politics?
3. Why can "fair representation" lose to error-correction?
