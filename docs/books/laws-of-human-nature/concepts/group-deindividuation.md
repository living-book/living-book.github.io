# Group Deindividuation

## Definition

Being in a group changes what a person thinks, feels, and is capable of doing through three specific, well-documented mechanisms: diffusion of responsibility (when everyone is accountable, no one feels individually accountable, and the effect grows with group size), deindividuation (people begin thinking and feeling as "members" rather than as individuals with personal judgment), and emotional contagion (moods and fears spread through a group faster than rational deliberation can check them). The insidious part is that this transformation operates below conscious awareness — the person swept into a group's emotional current experiences themselves as arriving at clearer conclusions, not as losing independent judgment.

## In the Book

Law 14's central case is the My Lai Massacre (1968): American soldiers who were, by every account, ordinary young men before the war killed hundreds of unarmed civilians after months of dehumanizing training, mounting combat losses, and a chain of command that made disobedience feel illegitimate. The book traces the mechanisms step by step — dehumanizing language, authority pressure from Lieutenant Calley, diffusion of responsibility across dozens of participants, and normalization (once a few soldiers fired, not firing became the deviant act) — while noting that Hugh Thompson, the pilot who intervened to stop the killing, was isolated and treated as a traitor by his own peers afterward. The book also cites Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment, where undergraduates randomly assigned as "guards" internalized the role into genuine cruelty within six days, and Zimbardo himself, as supervisor, became so absorbed in the institutional role that he let the experiment run past the point he should have stopped it.

## Why It Matters

This replaces "groups can go wrong" with a specific, checkable mechanism list — you can ask, of any group you're in, which of these three forces (diffusion of responsibility, deindividuation, contagion) is currently active, rather than relying on trust in your own moral stability. It also supplies the corrective structurally rather than as willpower: because the distortion is invisible from inside the group, the fix has to come from outside it — delaying group-made decisions, consulting an independent reference point, or building the habit of low-stakes dissent before high-stakes situations require it.
