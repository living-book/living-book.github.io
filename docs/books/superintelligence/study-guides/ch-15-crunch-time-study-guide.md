# Study Guide: Chapter 15 — Crunch Time

## Core Idea
A discovery's value is the value of having it earlier, not the value of the fact itself — so scarce brilliance is best spent on urgent, elastic, robustly positive-value problems rather than famously hard but non-time-sensitive ones. Two objectives survive all three filters: strategic analysis (finding "crucial considerations" that could overturn our whole direction) and capacity-building (an early, truth-seeking support base). The book closes with humanity as children each holding an independent trigger to an undetonated bomb — no adult in the room, no way to simply back away.

## Key Terms
Deferred gratification (philosophical) · crucial consideration · elasticity (of a problem) · robust positive value · common good principle (see Ch. 14)

## Case Summary
The samurai vs. the octopus: a project with years of sunk investment in an almost-safe design discovers a crucial consideration showing a different approach is safer. Does it abandon its progress (samurai) or rationalize the finding away (octopus, puffing ink)? Building institutions that reliably choose the samurai path is a distinct challenge from discovering the consideration in the first place.

## Application Checklist
- [ ] Value a discovery by how much earlier it arrives, not by how impressive or difficult it was to find
- [ ] Filter potential work by urgency, robust positive value, and elasticity — not just importance
- [ ] Watch for "worried octopus" rationalization when a costly finding threatens sunk investment
- [ ] Prioritize gaining strategic clarity (crucial considerations) over rushing into any specific fix under deep uncertainty

## Self-Test
1. Why does Bostrom argue that some famously difficult, "important" academic problems may actually have low value to have solved right now?
2. What are the three filters (urgency, robust positive value, elasticity) for choosing which problems to prioritize, and why does "encouraging kindness" fail one of them?
3. What does the "samurai vs. octopus" dilemma illustrate about the difference between discovering a crucial consideration and actually acting on it?
