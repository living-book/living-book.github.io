# Decisive Strategic Advantage

## Definition

"A level of technological and other advantages sufficient to enable it to achieve complete world domination." A project or agent that obtains one is positioned to suppress competitors and form a **singleton** — a single global decision-making agency, which could be a democracy, tyranny, unified AI, or enforced norm-set. Whether a decisive strategic advantage arises depends on the interaction between takeoff speed (Ch. 4) and the size of any head start between competing projects.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 shows this can arise even without a fast takeoff: historical technology races (nuclear fission, satellites, ICBMs) show typical leader-laggard gaps of months to a few years — shorter than a slow takeoff but longer than a fast one — and because growth right after the crossover point (Ch. 4) is explosive, even a six-month head start timed near that inflection can produce total, unrecoverable separation between projects. Chapter 6 shows how little capability is actually needed to convert such an advantage into concrete world-shaping power — Yudkowsky's mail-ordered DNA scenario requires only one human accomplice as an "actuator," since a superintelligence's power resides in its brain, not its hands. Bostrom also compares the scenario to America's 1945-49 nuclear monopoly, when the US could have (but didn't fully) leveraged its advantage into a permanent singleton via either a first-strike threat or the 1946 Baruch Plan.

## Why It Matters

Whether the machine intelligence transition produces a single dominant power or many competing ones determines almost everything downstream — whether the control problem needs to be solved once (Ch. 9-13) or repeatedly under competitive pressure (Ch. 11's multipolar scenarios), and whether humanity's future gets decided by one motivation system or negotiated among many.
