# Mental Models

## Definition

Chris Argyris's distinction anchors the discipline: people behave consistently with their **theory-in-use** (what their actions actually imply), not their **espoused theory** (what they say they believe). Mental models are dangerous not because they're wrong — all models simplify — but because they're tacit: unexamined, they get mistaken for fact, and reality can drift far from them before anyone notices.

## In the Book

Detroit's Big Three ran for decades on an unexamined assumption set ("styling matters more than quality") that had once been true and was mistaken for permanent — losing US market share to imports (near-zero to 38% by 1986) as a result. The counter-case is Royal Dutch/Shell: planner Pierre Wack discovered in 1972 that handing managers accurate forecasts of coming oil-market discontinuity changed nothing ("our scenarios would be like water on a stone") until scenarios were redesigned to force managers to see which assumptions their own trouble-free future required — after which Shell went from weakest of the seven oil majors (1970) to arguably strongest (1979). Two techniques operationalize the discipline: the **left-hand column** (writing what you're really thinking beside what you actually said, to expose untested assumptions steering a conversation) and **balancing inquiry and advocacy** (stating your reasoning while genuinely probing others', replacing "winning the argument" with "finding the best argument").

## Why It Matters

Good ideas fail in practice not from weak will but because they collide with mental models nobody has surfaced. Senge's closing metaphor: systems thinking without mental models is the DC-3's engine without wing flaps — necessary, insufficient.
