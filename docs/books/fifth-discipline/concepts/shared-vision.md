# Shared Vision

## Definition

Not one person's (or one group's) vision imposed on others, which "at best commands compliance, not commitment." A vision is truly shared when people have a similar picture and are committed to one another having it. Senge's hologram metaphor: cut a photograph in half and each half shows only half the image; cut a hologram in half and each half still shows the whole image, from a different angle — a shared vision doesn't fundamentally change as more people join it, it intensifies.

## In the Book

Spartacus's army choosing crucifixion over betraying a shared vision of freedom opens the chapter; MIT's Draper Laboratory asking NASA to restart a flawed Apollo guidance design rather than patch it, because their whole reason for being was "a man on the moon by the end of the decade," is the real-world parallel. Senge distinguishes **enrollment** (choosing freely to join) from **compliance** (going along) and from **selling** (persuading without full disclosure) — and estimates that 90% of what organizations call "commitment" is actually compliance. He also distinguishes **intrinsic** visions (Kyocera's Inamori: aim for "perfection," an internal standard) from **extrinsic** ones (Pepsi vs. Coke), warning that extrinsic visions risk collapsing into defensive "protect what we have" once the rival is beaten.

## Why It Matters

Shared vision gives generative learning something to strive toward and supplies the long-term orientation systems thinking alone couldn't argue people into — cathedral builders and Kyocera's "perfection" standard show that long-term commitment is a byproduct of genuine vision, not evidence or fear-based urgency.

## Connections

- **tempers** [orthogonality-thesis](/books/superintelligence/concepts/orthogonality-thesis.md) — Ch. 11's enrollment/compliance distinction shows that stated goal clarity (a "vision statement") does not by itself produce aligned commitment or capability; the orthogonality thesis's claim that intelligence and goals vary independently says nothing about whether a goal is genuinely *shared* versus merely announced, which is where Senge locates almost all the actual difficulty.
- **parallels** [network-effects](/books/platform-revolution/concepts/network-effects.md) — the hologram metaphor (value intensifying, not dividing, as more people hold the same picture) is structurally close to network effects (value increasing as more users join a platform), though Senge's version runs on shared meaning rather than usage volume.
