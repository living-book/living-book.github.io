# Feedback Loops

## Definition

The three building blocks of systems language: **reinforcing feedback** (an amplifying loop — the engine of all growth and all accelerating decline), **balancing feedback** (a goal-seeking loop underlying all stability and all "mysterious" resistance to change), and **delays** (time lags between action and consequence that produce overshoot and oscillation). Every feedback loop treats causality as circular: "my hand on the faucet controls the water level" is only half the story; "the water level controls my hand" is the other, equally true half.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 introduces all three via the water-glass example, then the arms race: each side's individually linear self-story closes into a reinforcing loop neither side controls alone. Reinforcing loops explain compounding growth (word-of-mouth sales, the Pygmalion effect) and accelerating collapse (bank runs); balancing loops explain why a manager's memos couldn't reduce his staff's 70-hour weeks — an unwritten norm was the real, hidden balancing goal. Delays turn balancing loops into oscillation (an old shower's ten-second lag makes aggressive knob-turning worse, not better) and are what let reinforcing loops like the arms race run for years before anyone perceives the pattern.

## Why It Matters

Feedback-loop thinking is Senge's antidote to blame and guilt: in a feedback system, everyone shares responsibility for what the system produces, though not everyone holds equal leverage to change it. It's also the direct predecessor of the systems archetypes (Chapter 6), which are simply named, recurring combinations of these three elements.
