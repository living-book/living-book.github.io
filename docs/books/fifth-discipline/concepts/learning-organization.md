# Learning Organization

## Definition

An organization "continually expanding its capacity to create its future" — not merely surviving (adaptive learning) but generating new capability (generative learning). Senge frames it as an emerging engineering "innovation," requiring five component disciplines — systems thinking, personal mastery, mental models, shared vision, team learning — to converge as an ensemble, the way five separately-invented technologies had to converge before the DC-3 could start commercial aviation.

## In the Book

Introduced in Chapter 1 via the DC-3 analogy: the disciplines are invented but not yet "innovated" into reliable, repeatable practice. Chapter 2 supplies the negative case — seven learning disabilities (I am my position, the enemy is out there, the illusion of taking charge, fixation on events, the boiled frog, the delusion of learning from experience, the myth of the management team) that keep ordinary organizations from becoming learning organizations, "despite the best efforts of bright, committed people." The rest of the book is effectively the anatomy of the five disciplines this concept depends on.

## Why It Matters

Senge's claim is structural, not moral: even organizations staffed with capable, well-intentioned people default to poor collective learning because of how they're designed and how people are taught to think — a third of the 1970 Fortune 500 was gone by 1983, and Shell estimated the average large-industrial-enterprise lifespan at under forty years. The learning organization is offered as the systematic alternative to hoping individual brilliance is enough.
