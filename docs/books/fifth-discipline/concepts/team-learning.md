# Team Learning

## Definition

Teams, not individuals, are the fundamental learning unit in organizations. The discipline rests on **alignment** — individual energies harmonizing like laser light instead of cancelling like scattered bulb-light (Bill Russell's Celtics) — and on distinguishing two modes of conversation: **discussion** (a "ping-pong game," advocacy aimed at winning) and David Bohm's **dialogue** (a group accessing "a larger pool of common meaning" no individual holds alone, via three conditions: suspending assumptions, seeing each other as colleagues, and a facilitator who holds the context).

## In the Book

DataQuest Drives' two-day dialogue session, run under explicit ground rules (suspend assumptions, act as colleagues, spirit of inquiry), surfaces a decade-long rift between R&D ("artists, designers, creators") and marketing ("the great unwashed") that neither side could name alone — R&D head Joe Grauweiler admits live that the firm's split make/buy product strategy is "insane," a confession the team's normal discussion mode had never allowed. Chris Argyris's **defensive routines** (habitual self-protective moves — smoothing over conflict, or "the abstraction wars" of winner-take-all opinion) get worse specifically when teams attempt systems thinking, because its core message — "our actions create our reality" — is itself threatening. Senge's reframe: defensiveness is a locked safe, and its intensity signals how important the buried issue is.

## Why It Matters

A group of talented individual learners doesn't automatically produce a learning team, any more than talented athletes automatically produce a great sports team — and most management teams, unlike sports teams or orchestras, have no "practice field" at all: every real decision is made once, under time pressure, final the moment it's made.
