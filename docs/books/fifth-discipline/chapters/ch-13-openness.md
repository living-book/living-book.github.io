# Chapter 13 — Openness

## Core Thesis

Internal politics — a "who" over "what" environment where power gets wielded arbitrarily — is a form of authoritarianism, even when the powerful hold no official title. Bill O'Brien's opening image: organizational politics is like a paper-mill smell you stop noticing after two weeks in town. Shared vision (Ch. 11) and **openness** are the twin antidotes, but openness itself splits into two distinct, easily-confused halves: **participative openness** (freedom to speak) and **reflective openness** (willingness to have one's own thinking challenged).

## The Problem It Solves

Participative openness alone — everyone states their view, formal "open communication" procedures — produces what Senge calls "open closedness": people talk more, nothing changes, because no one's actual thinking is at risk. A senior executive at an ostensibly "open" company found himself, unknown to him, "in the barrel" — colleagues privately agreed he was a self-serving "free spender" and voiced sharp criticism of his individual decisions in meetings, but never once stated the underlying generalization, a textbook **leap of abstraction** (Ch. 10) reinforced every time it was repeated behind his back. Both sides were still playing games — his "defend the decision," theirs "name the fault" — until a consultant got them to state the pattern to his face; the perception (partly right, partly wrong) was finally tested rather than assumed.

## Key Episode

A Boston tech firm's leadership team wrote "honesty and forthrightness in all communications" into a vision session, until a salesperson said "of course, we don't mean honest to our customers" — since none of the firm's competitors admitted true delivery delays. The president held firm: "I simply don't want to be part of an organization that sanctions lying." After an hour's debate, the team committed to real honesty with customers despite an expected near-term booking hit; six years later the firm held a preeminent niche position, built partly on a reputation for reliability competitors didn't have.

**The "Wall" exercise**: a workshop group maps every feedback relationship in a real problem (e.g., balancing work and family) on a wall of blank paper — within half an hour the wall is covered in circles and arrows and everyone recognizes the problem is genuinely "un-figure-out-able." Donella Meadows ran this on Third World malnutrition with international experts; one experienced lobbyist, previously certain someone somewhere had the answer, realized live: "nobody knows the answer. Not us, not them, not anybody." The realization is liberating, not defeating — it dissolves the authoritarian assumption, carried over from childhood (parents, teachers, "the boss"), that someone above you always has the answer.

## The Mechanism

E.F. Schumacher's distinction (from *A Guide for the Perplexed*): **convergent problems** have a right answer that intelligent study converges on (the fastest route to Albany); **divergent problems** have no single correct answer — the more intelligent people study them, the more their answers diverge ("why do you want to go to Albany?" "how do you best educate children?"). Divergent problems "offend the logical mind, which wishes to remove tension by coming down on one side or the other" — and most of the important problems managers face (how to develop people, what to invest in, how to satisfy customers) are divergent. Only genuine openness — not more analysis — lets people work productively with them.

## The Shift

Openness, like Bohm's dialogue, is a property of *relationships*, not individuals — "I am an open person" doesn't quite make sense; the same person is open with some people and not others. O'Brien names the underlying disposition *agape*: not romantic love, but unconditional commitment to another person's completion, "ruthless compassion." Freedom is likewise reframed: "freedom from" external control (the beer-game players' freedom to order however much they want) can still produce helplessness if the underlying way of thinking traps you; "freedom to" create the results you truly want is the freedom personal mastery and generative learning actually pursue.

## Key Terms

- **Participative vs. reflective openness** — freedom to speak vs. willingness to have one's own thinking challenged
- **"Open closedness"** — freely talking that produces no real change in anyone's thinking
- **Convergent vs. divergent problems** (Schumacher) — problems with a right answer vs. problems where intelligent study only produces contradictory answers
- **"Freedom to" vs. "freedom from"** — freedom to create desired results vs. mere absence of external constraint
