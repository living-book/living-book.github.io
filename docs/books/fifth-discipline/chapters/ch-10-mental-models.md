# Chapter 10 — Mental Models

## Core Thesis

Good ideas fail to reach practice not from weak will but because they collide with tacit mental models — deeply held internal images that shape both what we do and, more insidiously, what we're even able to see. Chris Argyris's distinction is the spine of the chapter: people behave consistently with their **theories-in-use**, not their **espoused theories** — what they actually do, not what they say they believe.

## Key Episode

Detroit's Big Three operated for decades on an unexamined assumption set (per consultant Ian Mitroff): styling matters more than quality, the US market is isolated from the world, workers don't affect productivity. The assumptions had been true — for a limited period — and were mistaken for permanent fact; German and Japanese automakers "educated" American consumers otherwise and took the US market from near-zero to 38% share by 1986. Royal Dutch/Shell went the opposite direction: planner Pierre Wack, foreseeing OPEC-style discontinuity in 1972, discovered that handing managers accurate forecasts changed nothing — "our scenarios would be like water on a stone" — until Group Planning redesigned scenarios to force managers to see which assumptions their own "trouble-free future" required, then walked them toward a new mental model. Shell went from "Ugly Sister," weakest of the seven oil majors in 1970, to arguably strongest by 1979 — the only major that responded to the 1973 embargo by slowing refinery investment and decentralizing rather than centralizing control.

## The Mechanism

Two reflection skills anchor the discipline. **Leaps of abstraction**: the mind moves from concrete observation to sweeping generalization almost instantly and then treats the generalization as fact — "Laura doesn't care about people" (built from: rarely praises, stares off, cuts people off) becomes unquestionable, and nobody ever asks Laura, who turns out to have an undisclosed hearing impairment. **The left-hand column**: writing out an actual dialogue on the right of a page and what you were really thinking but didn't say on the left exposes the private, untested assumptions ("he lacks confidence... he lacks initiative") steering a conversation nowhere — Senge's own worked example with a colleague named Bill shows both people talking around a failed presentation instead of naming it.

**Balancing inquiry and advocacy** is the corrective: pure advocacy between two people escalates exactly like an arms race (Ch. 5) — each side's forceful case pushes the other to argue more forcefully, positions harden, nothing is learned. Reciprocal inquiry — stating your view *and* the data/reasoning behind it, then actively inviting others to find the gaps — replaces "winning the argument" with "finding the best argument."

## Institutional Practice

Shell institutionalized mental-model work through scenario planning treated explicitly as "planning as learning" (Arie de Geus): the value of a plan isn't its accuracy but the institutional learning generated by making it. Hanover Insurance built a network of "internal boards" — two to four senior managers advising local general managers, explicitly not to control decisions but to test and extend the GM's own mental model — governed by a ten-point credo whose second principle warns against imposing "the authorized mental model": the goal is not congruency, though congruency often results when the process works. Hanover's three-day "Merit, Openness, and Localness" seminar, built with Argyris and Lee Bolman, videotapes managers role-playing — "it is often hilarious to see how much our own behavior deviates from what we say we do."

## The Shift

From treating mental models as either invisible or as unchangeable "facts" to treating them as testable, examinable, improvable working assumptions. Senge's closing metaphor: systems thinking without mental models is the DC-3's engine without wing flaps (Ch. 1) — necessary but insufficient. Mental models expose the assumptions; systems thinking restructures them once exposed.

## Key Terms

- **Mental model** — deeply held, often tacit internal image that shapes perception and action
- **Espoused theory vs. theory-in-use** (Argyris) — what we say we believe vs. what our actions actually imply
- **Leap of abstraction** — untested jump from observed particulars to treated-as-fact generalization
- **Left-hand column** — technique exposing unspoken assumptions steering a real conversation
- **Balancing inquiry and advocacy** — stating your reasoning and data while genuinely probing others' reasoning, to find the best argument rather than win
