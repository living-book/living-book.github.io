# Chapter 15 — A Manager's Time

## Core Thesis

Learning requires time to think and reflect, individually and collaboratively — and American management culture treats sitting still as evidence of doing nothing. A manager from a Japanese firm observes the cultural inversion directly: in Japan, a person sitting quietly is assumed to be thinking and left undisturbed; move around, and colleagues feel free to interrupt. In American firms, it's the reverse.

## The Problem It Solves

Donald Schön traces the reflex to the schoolroom: a teacher managing thirty students has no time to really listen to any one of them, so learning becomes synonymous with absorbing information as fast as possible — a habit carried straight into the "chain gang" model of management (Bill O'Brien's phrase: "the speed of the boss sets the speed of the gang"). MIT microworld experiments (Ch. 17) found this isn't just external pressure — even given ample time and full information in a simulated environment, managers running a simulated four-year business cycled through three to six different strategies without ever once articulating why the current one was failing or what a new one was meant to accomplish. "Ready, fire, aim" is internalized, not merely imposed.

## The Mechanism

Bill O'Brien's discipline: he doesn't schedule short meetings — "if it isn't a subject worthy of an hour, it shouldn't be on my calendar" — and treats a "big year" as one with roughly twelve real decisions, because senior managers should only be handling complex, dilemma-like **divergent** problems (Ch. 13), not the **convergent** problems (how to run this promotion, how to close this one sale) that belong lower in the organization. A senior manager processing twenty decisions a day is a diagnostic signal that management work is being done poorly — either too much convergent work is being pulled upward, or too little time is going to the genuinely hard problems. Ed Simon at Herman Miller committed his management team to spend 25% of work time on "learning the work of organizational architects" — mastering reflection and inquiry skills and applying them to the firm's most strategic issues.

## The Shift

Incisive action gets confused with incessant activity. The fix isn't less action — it's separating the two, and building in real time for reflection, conceptualizing, and examining complex issues as a recognized part of managerial work, not something squeezed into the margins or treated as evidence of idleness.

## Key Terms

- **Reflection-in-action** (Schön) — thinking about what you're doing while you're doing it, characteristic of successful professional learners
- **Chain gang model of management** (O'Brien) — activity valued for its own sake; the boss's pace sets everyone's pace
- **Convergent vs. divergent problems** (recap, Ch. 13) — routine problems that belong locally vs. complex dilemmas that deserve senior time
