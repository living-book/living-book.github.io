# Chapter 11 — Shared Vision

## Core Thesis

A shared vision is not an idea, and not one person's vision imposed on others — it's a force in people's hearts, palpable once enough personal visions genuinely connect. It answers "what do we want to create?" and gives generative learning (Ch. 1) something to strive toward; without it, adaptive learning has no direction and the forces of the status quo are overwhelming.

## Key Episode

*Spartacus*: Crassus offers a thousand captured slaves mercy if they surrender Spartacus. One by one, each man stands and says "I am Spartacus," choosing death. Their loyalty was never to Spartacus the man but to the shared vision he'd inspired — freedom — so compelling that no one could bear to renounce it even by silence. Senge's real-world parallel: MIT's Draper Laboratory, lead contractor on Apollo's guidance system, discovered mid-project that their design specifications were wrong after millions had already been spent — and asked NASA to let them start over rather than jerry-rig a fix, because their entire reason for being was "a man on the moon by the end of the decade." Bart Bolton, a middle manager with no authority, pitched Digital Equipment Corporation's "networked organization" vision in a 1981 staff paper when interconnecting even a hundred machines seemed implausible; by 1987, over 10,000 computers were on line — Digital became one of the first fully-networked corporations, eventually 43,000+ machines across 600+ facilities in 50+ countries.

## The Mechanism

The **hologram metaphor**: cut a photograph in half and each half shows only half the image; cut a hologram in half and each half still shows the whole image, from a slightly different angle. When a group truly shares a vision, each person's vision is the whole, seen from their own vantage — adding people doesn't change the vision, it intensifies it. This is why "vision statements" written by top management alone and handed down usually fail — Hamel and Prahalad's finding that "strategic plans reveal more about today's problems than tomorrow's opportunities" applies directly: a vision built on one or two people's picture, without the personal visions of everyone else feeding into it, generates compliance, not commitment. John Kryster's story: bringing a peer (Harriet Sullivan, 15 years his senior) *data and charts* to argue his distribution-system vision produced only resistance; coming back with just his reasoning and a proposal to test it small-scale — no charts — won her genuine partnership: "Now you're willing to test your idea... maybe we'll learn something."

## The Shift

**Enrollment vs. compliance vs. selling**: enrollment means "placing one's name on the roll" by free choice; selling means getting someone to act without full possession of the facts; commitment goes further than enrollment — feeling fully responsible for making the vision happen, not just wanting it to occur. Senge's estimate: 90% of what passes for organizational "commitment" is actually compliance. The leadership shift required: from announcing a vision to asking "will you follow me?" — genuinely vulnerable, especially for leaders used to setting goals unilaterally.

## Critiques & Rivals

Senge distinguishes **intrinsic** visions (Kyocera's Inamori: aim for "perfection," not just "best" — an internal standard) from **extrinsic** ones (Pepsi vs. Coke, Avis vs. Hertz) — extrinsic visions risk collapsing into defensive "protect what we have" once the competitor is beaten, and rarely sustain the creativity a genuine vision requires. He also argues, against the standard "you must create urgency/crisis to drive change" playbook, that long-term commitment cannot be argued into people rationally ("shifting the burden" warnings alone never produced lasting change in his experience) — it has to be wanted, which only a genuine vision supplies. Medieval cathedral builders, working toward a completion date a century past their own lifetime, are his proof that long-term orientation is a byproduct of vision, not evidence or fear.

## Key Terms

- **Shared vision** — a common picture people are committed to one another having, not merely each individually holding
- **Hologram metaphor** — each person's version of a shared vision is the whole, seen from a different angle
- **Enrollment** — choosing to join a vision, distinct from being sold or complying
- **Intrinsic vs. extrinsic vision** — an internal standard of excellence vs. a goal defined by beating a rival
