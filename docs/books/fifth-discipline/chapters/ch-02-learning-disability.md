# Chapter 2 — Does Your Organization Have a Learning Disability?

## Core Thesis

A third of 1970's Fortune 500 firms were gone by 1983 (Shell survey); average lifespan of a large industrial enterprise is under forty years — half a human lifetime. Not bad luck: seven specific learning disabilities, built into how organizations are designed, managed, and how people are taught to think, guarantee that even "excellent" companies underperform their potential.

## Seven Disabilities

1. **"I am my position"** — Detroit engineers, each owning "their" component, used three different bolts (three wrenches, three inventories) for one engine mount the Japanese did with one bolt design and one owner. Each group's piece "worked fine"; the system didn't. Displaced steelworkers refused retraining not from stubbornness but identity: "I am a lathe operator."
2. **"The enemy is out there"** — marketing blames manufacturing, manufacturing blames engineering. People Express slashed prices and bought Frontier Airlines to fight "aggressive competitors" while its real problem — collapsed service quality — went untouched, and the company died anyway.
3. **The illusion of taking charge** — an insurance VP nearly gave a speech vowing to fight litigious lawyers by taking more cases to trial; systemic modeling (Ch. 17's microworlds) showed the firm couldn't win enough cases to offset costs. He tore up the speech. Aggression at an external enemy is reactiveness wearing a proactive mask.
4. **Fixation on events** — organizational conversation runs on last month's sales, last quarter's earnings; newspapers explain the Dow's 16-point drop by yesterday's profit news. Real threats (arms race, environmental decay, eroding education, declining product quality relative to competitors) are slow processes invisible to event-thinking.
5. **The boiled frog** — Detroit didn't register Japan's rising US market share (4% in 1962, under 10% in 1967, 15% in 1974) as existential until the 1980s, by which point it hit 21.3%, headed to ~30% by 1989. Sudden-threat detection, tuned by evolution, misses gradual ones entirely.
6. **The delusion of learning from experience** — real learning is trial-and-error, but the highest-leverage organizational decisions (R&D bets, capital investment, leadership promotions) have consequences stretching years past anyone's "learning horizon." You never get to try again with feedback.
7. **The myth of the management team** — Chris Argyris's "skilled incompetence": teams trained to defend positions, not inquire, that maintain an appearance of unity while squelching real disagreement — proficient at avoiding exactly the learning they exist to produce.

## Key Episode

Barbara Tuchman's *The March of Folly* supplies the historical register: Valois France's currency devaluations (I-am-my-position blindness to their own effect), Britain's decade of non-engagement with the American colonies before 1776 (boiled frog), Renaissance cardinals maintaining a pious facade of agreement while backstabbing produced the popes whose abuses triggered the Reformation (myth of the management team).

## The Shift

These are not personality flaws of bad managers — they are structural, present in every organization to some degree, and they operate *despite* the best efforts of bright, committed people. The five disciplines (Ch. 1) are framed explicitly as antidotes; naming the disability precisely is the diagnostic step before any of them can work.

## Key Terms

- **Learning disability** — structural pattern of thought/interaction that blocks organizational learning, independent of individual competence
- **Boiled frog** — failure to register gradual, non-event threats
- **Learning horizon** — breadth of time/space within which one can observe the consequences of one's own actions
- **Skilled incompetence** (Argyris) — proficiency at protecting oneself from appearing uncertain, at the cost of collective inquiry
