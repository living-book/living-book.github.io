# Study Guide: Chapter 13 — Openness

## Core Idea
Openness splits into participative (freedom to speak) and reflective (willingness to have your own thinking challenged). Without the reflective half, "openness" becomes talking more while nothing changes. Divergent problems (no single right answer) require genuine openness, not more analysis.

## Key Terms
Participative vs. reflective openness · open closedness · convergent vs. divergent problems (Schumacher) · freedom to vs. freedom from

## Case Summary
Executive unknowingly "in the barrel" — colleagues criticized his decisions but never named the underlying (untested) generalization about his character. Boston tech firm commits to real customer honesty despite short-term booking risk; thrives 6 years later. The "Wall" exercise — mapping every feedback loop in a real problem — reveals that "un-figure-out-able-ness" is liberating, not defeating.

## Application Checklist
- [ ] Check whether a team's "openness" is participative only (talk) or also reflective (willingness to be wrong)
- [ ] Name a generalization about a colleague that's circulating without ever being tested with them directly
- [ ] Sort a current hard problem into convergent (has a right answer) vs. divergent (doesn't) before debating it

## Self-Test
1. What's the difference between participative and reflective openness, and why can you have one without the other?
2. Why is "someone in the barrel" a leap-of-abstraction problem, not just a personality conflict?
3. What is a divergent problem, and why does more expert analysis make it worse, not better?
