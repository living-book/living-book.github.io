# Study Guide: Chapter 5 — A Shift of Mind

## Core Idea
Systems thinking replaces linear cause-effect chains with circles of causality, built from three elements — reinforcing feedback, balancing feedback, delays — and targets dynamic complexity (subtle, distant cause-effect) rather than detail complexity (many variables).

## Key Terms
Detail vs. dynamic complexity · reinforcing feedback · balancing feedback · delay · Pygmalion effect

## Case Summary
US-USSR arms race: each side's linear "we respond to their threat" story closes into a reinforcing loop neither side controls alone. Lily-pond riddle: exponential growth invisible until nearly complete. Manager's memos fail to cut overwork because an unwritten "heroes work 70 hours" norm is the real (hidden) balancing goal.

## Application Checklist
- [ ] Separate a "complex" problem into detail complexity (many variables, tools can handle it) vs. dynamic complexity (delayed, distant effects, needs systems view)
- [ ] When you meet unexplained "resistance to change," look for the hidden balancing loop and implicit norm it protects
- [ ] Check whether a self-fulfilling-prophecy loop (Pygmalion effect) is running on your team, for better or worse

## Self-Test
1. Why couldn't sophisticated Cold War systems analysis stop the arms race?
2. What's the difference between a reinforcing loop and a balancing loop — give one business example of each.
3. Why does an old shower's ten-second delay make temperature oscillate worse the more aggressively you adjust it?
