# Study Guide: Chapter 2 — Does Your Organization Have a Learning Disability?

## Core Idea
Seven built-in learning disabilities — I am my position, the enemy is out there, illusion of taking charge, fixation on events, boiled frog, delusion of learning from experience, myth of the management team — explain why even bright, committed people in "excellent" organizations underperform their real potential.

## Key Terms
Learning disability · boiled frog · learning horizon · skilled incompetence · fixation on events

## Case Summary
Detroit's three-bolt engine mount (three fiefdoms, no systemic owner) vs. Japan's one-bolt design. Japan's US auto market share crept from 4% (1962) to 21.3% (early 1980s) before Detroit reacted — classic boiled frog. People Express fought "aggressive competitors" while ignoring its real, self-inflicted collapse in service quality.

## Application Checklist
- [ ] Ask whether a "competitor problem" is actually a self-inflicted quality/service problem in disguise
- [ ] Check your team's last big disagreement: was it surfaced, or smoothed into a watered-down compromise?
- [ ] Look for a slow-moving trend (market share, quality gap, attrition) your org is normalizing instead of reacting to

## Self-Test
1. How does "I am my position" produce the three-bolt engine mount?
2. Why is "taking charge" against an external enemy often reactiveness in disguise?
3. What is skilled incompetence, and why does it make management teams worse learners, not better?
