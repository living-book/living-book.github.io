# Study Guide: Chapter 4 — The Laws of the Fifth Discipline

## Core Idea
Eleven laws describe why obvious fixes in complex systems backfire or relocate the problem, and why real leverage is nonobvious, delayed, and rarely close in time or space to the symptom.

## Key Terms
Compensating feedback · shifting the burden to the intervenor · leverage · trim tab · principle of the system boundary

## Case Summary
Rug merchant stamping down a bump (snake underneath) · Boxer's "I will work harder" masking the pigs' exploitation · Buckminster Fuller's trim tab turning opposite the desired direction to leverage a huge ship · 1960s inner-city aid programs offset by inbound migration · blind men and the elephant (ear/trunk/leg ≠ elephant).

## Application Checklist
- [ ] When a fix "worked" short-term, ask what compensating response it may have triggered elsewhere
- [ ] Look for where you're "shifting the burden" to a helper/consultant instead of building the host system's own capacity
- [ ] Before accepting a stated either-or dilemma, ask if it dissolves once you add a timeline

## Self-Test
1. What is compensating feedback, and why does the 1960s inner-city housing example illustrate it?
2. Why is the trim tab a good metaphor for leverage — what makes it nonobvious?
3. What does "dividing an elephant in half does not produce two small elephants" warn against organizationally?
