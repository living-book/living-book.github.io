# Study Guide: Chapter 14 — Localness

## Core Idea
Localness pushes decisions to where the information and responsibility live, but raises two problems: senior managers' emotional grip on control, and the risk of myopic local decisions depleting shared resources (Tragedy of the Commons). Real control is distributed, not centralized.

## Key Terms
Localness · illusion of being in control (roller-skate metaphor) · Tragedy of the Commons (Hardin) · water-line principle (Bill Gore) · manager as researcher/designer

## Case Summary
Kollmorgen's roller-skates-on-springs metaphor for why top-down control of a complex org is illusory. The Sahel's desertification from rational individual herd-expansion. Bill Gore's water-line principle for risk-taking. James Burke's "congratulations on the mistake" and Captain Asoh's full-responsibility landing inquiry as models of organizational forgiveness.

## Application Checklist
- [ ] Identify a shared "commons" (support staff time, customer goodwill, reputation) at risk of Tragedy-of-the-Commons depletion in your org
- [ ] Ask whether a decision is "above" or "below the water line" before acting unilaterally
- [ ] Check whether central management's real remaining job is designing learning processes, not issuing orders

## Self-Test
1. Why does the roller-skate metaphor show top-down control of a complex organization is an illusion?
2. What two conditions define a Tragedy of the Commons, and what's a business example besides the Sahel?
3. What is the "water-line principle," and what does it let an organization avoid?
