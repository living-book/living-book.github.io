# The Authenticity Trap

## Definition

The authenticity trap is what happens when "being true to yourself" gets defined by your past self, so that any behavior stretching beyond your comfort zone feels like a betrayal rather than growth. Ibarra distinguishes "chameleons," who adapt style to fit new situations without feeling fake because their core self is anchored in values and goals rather than mannerisms, from "true-to-selfers," who treat stylistic demands as threats to authenticity and so hold back. As Harvard's Amy Cuddy puts it, the way out is to "fake it till you become it."

## In the Book

Chapter 4 opens with Ibarra's own failure as a new Harvard MBA instructor: she was "too much herself" — academic, nervous, distant — until a senior colleague's advice to physically dominate the classroom (which she found horrifying) actually worked, loosening her up until the performance became authentically hers. She then reports her study of investment bankers and consultants transitioning from analytical work to client advising: chameleons borrowed liberally from successful colleagues' demeanor and got more mentoring as a result (illustrated by Michael Lewis's account of imitating star salesman "Alexander" in *Liar's Poker*, and by Barack Obama's code-switching across audiences), while true-to-selfers clung to "substance over form," concluded their more theatrical peers were "all talk," and learned more slowly. Ibarra dismantles four popular definitions of authenticity — true to which self (past, present, or future "possible self"), sincerity ("without wax"), true to values/purpose, and social recognition — showing each one breaks down precisely when you're trying to become someone you aren't yet.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates two things people conflate: comfort and authenticity. Discomfort during a genuine stretch is evidence of growth, not evidence of inauthenticity — but only the chameleon reading of authenticity (anchored in values, not mannerisms) allows that distinction. It applies to any change effort — organizational, personal, creative — where "this doesn't feel like me" is being used to justify reverting to the old, familiar, already-obsolete way of operating.
