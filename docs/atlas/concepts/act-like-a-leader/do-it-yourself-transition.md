# The Do-It-Yourself Transition

## Definition

A do-it-yourself transition is a leadership step-up that isn't triggered by a promotion, a new title, or explicit organizational sponsorship — instead, subtle shifts in the business environment quietly change what's expected of you while your formal role stays the same. Because no one hands you a signal that it's time to change, you have to notice the gap and drive the transition yourself.

## In the Book

Ibarra grounds the concept in Jacob's situation: after his food-manufacturing company was bought by a private investor, his title didn't change, but what his boss and peers expected of him changed substantially — more strategic thinking, more cross-functional collaboration, less firefighting. Her 2013 survey of 173 INSEAD executive alumni found that among managers reporting major changes in what was expected of them, only 47 percent had actually been promoted in the prior two years. The rest, like Jacob, were expected to step up while sitting in the same job. She cites the near-universal agreement (79 percent) with the maxim "what got you here won't get you there," paired with people's persistent difficulty reinventing themselves because new demands clash with existing self-concepts. The chapter includes a ten-item self-assessment (industry change, leadership turnover, company size shifts, new competitors, technology change, stakeholder growth, tenure, etc.) to help readers score how strongly their own environment is signaling a do-it-yourself transition.

## Why It Matters

This reframes a common source of quiet frustration — "I'm doing the same job but it feels harder and less rewarded" — as a legible pattern rather than a personal failing or organizational neglect. It applies wherever role scope drifts faster than role titles: shifting market conditions, restructurings, or technology change that raise the bar on an unchanged job description. Recognizing the pattern turns a vague sense of falling behind into a concrete diagnostic question: has my environment changed enough that I need to change first, without waiting to be told?
