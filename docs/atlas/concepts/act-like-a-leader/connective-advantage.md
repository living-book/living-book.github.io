# Connective Advantage

## Definition

Connective advantage is the deliberate cultivation of three distinct kinds of professional networks — operational (getting today's work done), personal (career development and support), and strategic (sensing the future and building coalitions for change) — rather than letting a network accumulate passively around convenience and chemistry. Ibarra measures network quality along three dimensions she calls BCD: the breadth of contacts, the connectivity between them, and the network's dynamism (how actively it evolves).

## In the Book

Chapter 3 argues that most competent managers have strong operational networks but personal networks disconnected from their leadership growth, and strategic networks that are "nonexistent or underutilized." Returning to Jacob from chapter 1, Ibarra notes his reliable internal relationships were excellent for solving functional problems but didn't reach outside his existing mind-set, leaving him unprepared for the future. Her survey of 173 INSEAD alumni found the biggest sources of help in stepping up to leadership were external to the organization, with bosses and predecessors ranking only fourth — and just 10 percent of respondents reported having a mentor or sponsor actively looking out for their career. She reframes networking as a relational task at the heart of strategic work, not a distraction from it, and recommends building the strategic network outward from the periphery of one's current network: seeking outside expertise and eliciting perspectives from peers in different functions.

## Why It Matters

This distinguishes networking-as-socializing from networking-as-infrastructure: three different network types serve three different functions, and a person can be well-connected on one axis (getting daily work done) while being isolated on the axis that actually enables the next career step. The BCD framing gives a portable way to audit any network — a team's, a company's, an individual's — for whether it's built for maintaining the status quo or for sensing and mobilizing around change.
