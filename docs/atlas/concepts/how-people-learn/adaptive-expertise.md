# Adaptive Expertise

## Definition

There are two fundamentally different types of expertise. Routine expertise efficiently executes well-practiced procedures with speed and accuracy. Adaptive expertise flexibly approaches novel situations, learns continuously, and questions whether the problem as presented is the best way to begin. Adaptive experts continually push beyond their current competence; they view themselves as accomplished novices rather than answer-filled experts.

## In the Book

The book contrasts two types of sushi experts: one excels at following fixed recipes (routine expertise); the other prepares sushi creatively and adapts to varying circumstances (adaptive expertise). It describes these as "artisans" versus "virtuosos" and notes these distinctions appear across diverse fields—from information systems design to surgical practice.

The NRC report emphasizes that adaptive expertise depends on metacognition—the ability to monitor one's approach and question current understanding. Adaptive experts "don't simply attempt to do the same things more efficiently; they attempt to do things better." This requires a particular mindset: viewing expertise not as a state of knowing all answers but as a continual process of learning. The book argues that "a major challenge for theories of learning is to understand how particular kinds of learning experiences develop adaptive expertise or 'virtuosos.'"

The concept of the "accomplished novice" contrasts with the mental model of the "answer-filled expert." An accomplished novice is skilled and proud of achievements but realizes that "what they know is minuscule compared to all that is potentially knowable." This model "helps free people to continue to learn even though they may have spent 10 to 20 years as an 'expert' in their field."

## Why It Matters

This distinction explains why efficient performers sometimes fail when circumstances change. It suggests that training for routine competence—accuracy and speed in known tasks—differs fundamentally from developing adaptive capacity. In organizations and professions, this concept reveals that hiring or promoting routine expertise may be suboptimal if future success requires flexibility and continuous learning. The concept applies wherever change is inevitable: technical fields, leadership, complex problem-solving. It implies that the right mindset about expertise—as incomplete and evolving—may be as important as the competence itself.
