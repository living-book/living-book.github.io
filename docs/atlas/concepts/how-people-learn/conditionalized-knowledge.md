# Conditionalized Knowledge

## Definition

Experts do not just possess more knowledge than novices; their knowledge is "conditionalized"—it includes specifications of the contexts in which it is useful and applicable. Knowledge without such conditions is "inert," remaining unused even when relevant. Conditionalized knowledge enables selective retrieval: experts can rapidly identify which tools, principles, or procedures apply to a novel problem without exhaustively searching everything they know.

## In the Book

The NRC report states: "Many forms of curricula and instruction do not help students conditionalize their knowledge. Textbooks are much more explicit in enunciating the laws of mathematics or of nature than in saying anything about when these laws may be useful in solving problems." 

The book illustrates the problem through word problems. Students who can solve a set of practice problems from a chapter often fail when similar problems appear on cumulative tests where the chapter source is hidden. This happens because students learned "if this is a Chapter 5 problem, use the Chapter 5 formula," rather than learning the conditions that trigger the formula's use. Similarly, students can state that "he who hesitates is lost" or "many hands make light work," but not know when to apply each maxim—when one is appropriate versus its opposite.

In contrast, effective instruction helps students learn when, where, and why knowledge applies. The book describes how chess masters and physics experts condition their knowledge through extensive practice with feedback, gradually building associations between problem features and applicable strategies.

## Why It Matters

This concept explains a pervasive failure mode in education and training: learners pass assessments yet cannot apply knowledge outside the training context. It highlights that passive coverage is insufficient; learning must include opportunities to encounter multiple contexts and develop reliable associations between contexts and solutions. In organizational learning, this suggests that knowledge transfer fails when practices are documented without their conditions of applicability—when "how we do it" lacks "when and why we do it this way." The concept applies wherever knowledge must transfer to novel situations: design instruction and assessment to make conditions of applicability explicit and practiced.
