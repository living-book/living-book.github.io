# Affirmative Topic Choice

## Definition

Affirmative Topic Choice is the deliberate practice of selecting what an organization will study through the lens of possibility and strength rather than problems and gaps. Based on the Poetic Principle—that organizations are like open books with endless interpretations—practitioners consciously reframe problems as affirmative questions. Instead of studying "high employee turnover," the topic becomes "retention in magnetic work environments." The choice of topic is fateful: it determines not just what the organization discovers but what it creates.

## In the Book

The book illustrates the fateful power of topic choice through the partnership between University of California, Berkeley and the city of Berkeley. Rather than address "town-gown conflict" or "mutual distrust," a twelve-person planning team deliberately chose the affirmative topic of "partnership." At a seventy-five-person retreat, participants explored points of effective partnership already existing between the two organizations. One project leader later reflected: "By selecting the topic of partnership, we opened the door for a different kind of conversation—and a different kind of experience... we discovered that there were places where our similarities intersected. This helped us to begin seeing one another as allies rather than adversaries."

The reframing generated concrete outcomes: a law enforcement collaborative, "town and gown" informal meetings, joint housing and financing initiatives, a Southwest Berkeley Health Initiative, and joint procurement exploration—all outcomes that would not have emerged from a deficit-focused inquiry into barriers or conflicts.

The book emphasizes that reframing problems into affirmative topics is "central to Appreciative Inquiry." Practitioners identify the underlying human aspiration or value hidden within a problem statement and make that aspiration the explicit topic. The Cathedral Foundation shifted its focus question from "How do we care for the elderly?" to "How do we create a positive experience of aging?"—a change that cleared the way for significant service diversification and rekindled staff enthusiasm.

## Why It Matters

This concept reveals that inquiry is never neutral—the frame you choose predetermines the landscape of possibilities you'll discover. Unlike abstract problem definition (which feels objective), affirmative topic choice is transparently value-laden, forcing organizations to name what they actually care about and want more of. This makes it a powerful lever for strategy and culture: it externalizes the choice of direction and makes it discussable. The practice also surfaces a deeper truth applicable across domains: that what we call "problems" are often inverted strengths. High turnover is the inverse of retention and belonging; low morale is the inverse of engagement and meaning. Studying the inverse directly rather than the problem indirectly often generates more actionable and energizing insights.
