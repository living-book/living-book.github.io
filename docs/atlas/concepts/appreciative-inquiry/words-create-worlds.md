# Words Create Worlds: The Constructionist Principle

## Definition

The Constructionist Principle holds that meaning is not discovered but created through human relationship and communication. Organizations first emerge through language, reside in stories, and come to life in conversations. Since organizational reality is constructed through dialogue, the practice of Appreciative Inquiry—which brings stakeholders together to collaboratively discover, dream, and design—literally constructs new organizations and new futures.

## In the Book

The book draws on social construction theory: "The terms by which we understand our world and our self are neither required nor demanded by 'what there is.' Our modes of description, explanation, and/or representation are derived from relationship. As we describe, explain, or otherwise represent, we also fashion our future." Words matter not just as tools for communication but as world-builders. According to the authors, "Words matter—they not only make a difference, they literally bring things to life, creating the world as we know it."

A concrete example illustrates this principle starkly: Leaders in Nevada sought to merge state and county child welfare services. Their goal was to build positive relationships among people who had previously competed for resources and to design an integrated service delivery system. They engaged all stakeholders—social workers, foster parents, grandparents, youth, lawyers, judges, adoptive parents—through a series of AI Summits. As people who seldom talked to each other connected through appreciative interviews, "the path to integration became ever more apparent." As they shared their dreams, "the image of one organization and an integrated service delivery system became clear." As they designed together, they created an integrated system based on shared values of family preservation, child well-being, and one-stop service delivery. In the case narrative, the authors note: "Words created worlds. They made a positive difference in the lives of thousands of children and their parents."

The power of language choice emerges in organizational naming: organizations "first emerge through language. They reside in stories and come to life in conversations." Change "occurs through language, storytelling, and human communication." Each step of Appreciative Inquiry—topic selection, interviews, meaning-making, dream dialogues, provocative propositions, and inspired actions—is fundamentally a linguistic act that constructs new organizational reality.

## Why It Matters

This principle reveals that organizational culture and reality are not fixed entities to be analyzed from the outside but are actively co-created through internal dialogue. It empowers practitioners: rather than being victims of organizational culture, people who understand this principle can deliberately reshape it by changing the conversations they have. This has profound implications for leadership, strategy, and change management: the narrative choices leaders make—what stories they tell, what metaphors they use, what conversations they facilitate—literally construct the organization's future. It also surfaces why affirmative topic choice and appreciative inquiry matter so much: they ensure that the conversations co-creating the organization are generative and life-giving rather than deficit-focused and constraining. This applies across domains: in education, the language teachers use to describe students' potential shapes student identity and achievement; in healthcare, the language providers use shapes patient hope and healing; in communities, the narratives told about collective possibility shape civic engagement.
