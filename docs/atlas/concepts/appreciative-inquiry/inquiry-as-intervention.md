# Inquiry as Intervention: The Simultaneity Principle

## Definition

The Simultaneity Principle asserts that organizational change happens the moment an inquiry begins, not after analysis is complete and solutions are implemented. Questions function as interventions: they shape perception, direct attention, spark imagination, and set trajectories in motion. The moment someone asks, "What are we at our best?" the organization begins moving in that direction, even before any formal change initiative is launched.

## In the Book

The book draws on therapist Marilee Goldberg's observation: "The moment of questioning is also the moment of choice, which usually holds the greatest leverage for effective action and positive change." Viktor Frankl's experience in a concentration camp illustrates this starkly. While others asked "Will we survive?"—a question that confined them to a world of life and death—Frankl held the question "Has all this suffering, this dying around us, a meaning?" Despite identical circumstances, he inhabited a fundamentally different world of meaning and possibility.

A corporate example: the Cathedral Foundation, a social service organization caring for the elderly, had lost enthusiasm for its work. Rather than diagnose the problem, they simply shifted their strategic planning question from "How do we care for the elderly?" to "How do we create a positive experience of aging?" That single reframing opened the possibility space. The organization began diversifying services in new directions, and enthusiasm rekindled—not from implementing a solution but from inhabiting a different question.

The book emphasizes that questions are "intrinsically related to action"—they "spark and direct attention, perception, energy, and effort." Gutenberg's invention of the printing press emerged from his persistent question: "How can sacred texts be produced in large numbers for the masses?" The question haunted him until the wine press analogy provided the answer. In organizations, the same principle holds: the questions that are habitually asked create the organization's trajectory, often invisibly.

## Why It Matters

This concept disrupts the conventional change management sequence (diagnose → design → implement → measure). It reveals that the diagnosis itself changes the patient. The power lies in recognizing that no inquiry is neutral—every question is an intervention that begins reshaping culture, attention, and identity immediately. This has immediate practical implications: changing the questions asked in meetings, strategy sessions, and performance reviews can shift organizational direction without requiring formal restructuring. It also suggests a counterintuitive strategy: the most effective interventions may be the simplest—a well-crafted question asked widely. This applies across domains: in therapy, education, leadership coaching, and community development, the quality and direction of inquiry often matters more than the comprehensiveness of the solution.
