# MECE Structuring

## Definition

MECE (pronounced "mee-see") stands for Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. It means breaking a problem into distinct, non-overlapping categories (mutually exclusive) while ensuring no relevant issue is overlooked (collectively exhaustive). This structuring discipline underpins the McKinsey problem-solving process.

## In the Book

At McKinsey, MECE is not just a tool but "a way of life." One alumnus summarized his McKinsey experience as "Structure, structure, structure. MECE, MECE, MECE." The book emphasizes that structure comes *before* facts in the problem-solving process. When a team defines the boundaries of a problem and breaks it into component elements, they enable rapid hypothesis generation and focused analysis.

The book provides concrete examples: In framing whether Acme Widgets can lower manufacturing costs, the team breaks the problem into non-overlapping options (reduce supplier costs, reduce labor, reduce process time) with no category appearing twice. This allows them to quickly eliminate dead-end options using the Quick and Dirty Test. The MECE framework gives the issue tree—the visual map of what must be analyzed—its underlying logic.

## Why It Matters

MECE is transportable across domains. It prevents the ad hoc, muddled thinking prevalent in many organizations. By forcing yourself to define what belongs in each bucket and what doesn't, you surface hidden assumptions, avoid analyzing the same thing twice, and ensure you're not leaving key options on the table. It works equally well for product strategy, cost reduction, organizational design, or any other domain where a decision tree is needed.
