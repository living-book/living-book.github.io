# Prewiring: No-Surprises Stakeholder Management

## Definition

Prewiring means presenting your findings to relevant decision makers one-on-one or in small groups *before* the formal presentation. This practice bridges information and trust gaps by letting stakeholders understand your logic, voice concerns, and align before public commitment. A good presentation contains no shocking revelations for the audience because they've already been through the findings.

## In the Book

At McKinsey, prewiring is doctrine: "Prewire everything." The benefits compound: it prevents blindsiding (people hate surprises that force them to change plans), builds consensus by letting stakeholders "put their mark" on the solution early, exposes errors in your analysis before the high-stakes meeting, and handles political realities by letting you learn which decision makers are blockers and which are allies.

Naras Eechambadi's First Union example is exemplary: he spent two months interviewing people across the company to understand their concerns and expectations before presenting his business case to the president. This let him calibrate his message, identify where expectations were misaligned, and work out compromises with skeptics privately. He finished the two-hour presentation in ninety minutes with funding approved—a feat colleagues said never happened because of prewiring's credibility. Paul Kenny's experience shows even disagreement becomes manageable: when he had to kill a product, prewiring let him surface objections beforehand so the formal presentation debated ideas, not facts.

## Why It Matters

Prewiring is a trust and alignment multiplier. In any context where multiple stakeholders must agree—corporate decisions, cross-functional initiatives, community projects—the cost of handling objections one-on-one is far lower than resolving them in public where face-saving becomes part of the resistance. It also generates better solutions by incorporating diverse perspectives early. The mechanism is portable: any complex recommendation benefits from private socialization before public commitment.
