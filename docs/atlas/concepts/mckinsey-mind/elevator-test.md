# The Elevator Test

## Definition

The elevator test asks: can you explain your solution—its core claim and three key supporting points—in the time it takes an elevator ride (roughly 30 seconds)? If not, either you don't understand your own recommendation well enough or your structure isn't clear enough. Passing the test is not just about brevity; it's a diagnostic for coherence.

## In the Book

McKinsey teaches that knowing your solution thoroughly means passing the elevator test. You don't have to be in an actual elevator; the test is metaphorical. The book shows how the first slide of a presentation serves as the elevator test answer: the main recommendation plus three bullet-pointed supporting points. Your boss, a potential investor, or your CEO should instantly grasp your position.

The book includes testimonials from alumni. Brad Farnsworth noted that many senior executives fail the elevator test—they can't focus on two or three key points and articulate them well, even those with decades of experience. Roger Boisvert of CTR Ventures said starkly: "If I am not able to do the elevator test, I shouldn't be talking with anyone." Deborah Knuckey added that the test is fundamentally about whether your idea is compelling enough to move someone to action. If the message isn't clear in 30 seconds, the underlying issue probably isn't compelling.

## Why It Matters

The elevator test is a clarity forcing function that works across domains. It catches muddled thinking, reveals when you're hiding behind jargon or complexity, and prepares you for time-constrained contexts (board meetings, investor pitches, chance encounters with decision makers). It's also audience-agnostic: if you can pass it for a sophisticated listener, you can tailor downward for others.
