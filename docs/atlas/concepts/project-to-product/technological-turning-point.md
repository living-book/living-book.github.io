# The Turning Point

## Definition

Borrowing Carlota Perez's model of technological revolutions, Kersten frames every
major shift in the means of production (steam, steel, mass production, software) as a
two-phase cycle: an **Installation Period** where new technology and financial capital
combine to spawn a "Cambrian explosion" of disruptive startups, followed by a
**Deployment Period** where the production capital of new industrial giants takes
over. Between them sits the **Turning Point**, historically marked by financial crashes
and recoveries — the moment when existing businesses either master the new means of
production or become relics of the last age.

## In the Book

Kersten opens the book by placing today's enterprises at the Turning Point of the Age
of Software: fifty years after the 1968 NATO software engineering conference, "the
pace of change feels relentless because we are passing through the Turning Point," and
at the current rate of disruption, half of S&P 500 companies will be replaced within
ten years. The book's diagnosis is that established organizations are trying to survive
this transition using managerial frameworks — Taylorist project management,
managerial accounting, organizational hierarchies — imported from the *previous*
technological revolution (the Age of Mass Production), the same way canals, railways,
and assembly lines were the infrastructure of past cycles. Kersten's own field
evidence for being mid-Turning-Point is Nokia (a hardware-era master that never built
software-era infrastructure) and a $1 billion "LargeBank" transformation that was
project-managed to cost reduction rather than business outcome. The entire Flow
Framework is positioned as the new managerial infrastructure — analogous to the
electrical grid for the Age of Mass Production — needed to get organizations through
this Turning Point rather than be swept away at it.

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes "digital transformation" from a vague mandate into a dated,
survivable event with historical precedent: every technological revolution has had
losers who kept using the last era's management tools and winners who built the new
era's infrastructure in time. It gives a domain-neutral lens for any moment when an
organization's dominant means of production changes underneath it — the diagnostic
question becomes "are we still running Deployment-Period playbooks in an Installation
Period," which applies as readily to AI adoption, energy transitions, or any sector
facing a step-change in its core production technology.
