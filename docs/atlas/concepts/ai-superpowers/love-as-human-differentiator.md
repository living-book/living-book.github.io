# Love as the Human Differentiator

## Definition

If a society keeps equating a person's worth with their economic productivity,
mass AI-driven job displacement will devastate not just incomes but identity and
meaning. Lee's proposed reorientation is to treat love, compassion, and human
connection — activities that require no data, no optimization, and cannot be
executed by a machine — as the domain a person's worth should anchor to instead,
freeing economic value and human value to be judged by different yardsticks.

## In the Book

Lee tells this as a personal conversion narrative. He describes running his
pre-diagnosis life as an "optimization algorithm" maximizing influence and
minimizing anything that didn't serve it — including, on the day his first
daughter was born by emergency C-section, calculating whether attending a
Sculley presentation at Apple had higher expected value than staying in the
delivery room. A stage IV lymphoma diagnosis in 2013 broke that logic: facing
mortality, none of his professional achievements offered comfort, only the
"warmth and love" from family he had responded to "on the basis of cold
calculations." He writes that he had "turned into a person that thought like a
machine" while trying to build machines that thought like people. The chapter
explicitly links this personal reckoning to the book's economic argument: the
Human Veneer quadrant of the risk-of-replacement grid and the Social Investment
Stipend are both, in Lee's framing, mechanisms for redirecting freed-up human
time toward exactly this compassionate work.

## Why It Matters

This supplies the psychological half of an argument that is otherwise purely
economic: automation displacing labor is a crisis of meaning as much as income,
and the two require different remedies — a stipend can replace income, but only
a cultural shift in what confers worth can replace meaning. It generalizes to
any domain where a measurable proxy (output, productivity, score) has quietly
become the thing people use to judge their own worth, and asks what happens to
identity when the proxy stops requiring a human to produce it.
