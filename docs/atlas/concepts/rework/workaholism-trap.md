# The Workaholism Trap

## Definition

Culture treats all-nighters and burning the midnight oil as a badge of honor, but the book calls this "not only... unnecessary, it's stupid. Working more doesn't mean you care more or get more done. It just means you work more." Workaholics try to "fix problems by throwing sheer hours at them," substituting brute force for the harder work of an elegant solution — and the pattern is often self-perpetuating, because some workaholics unwittingly create the crises that justify the extra hours.

## In the Book

The chapter lists compounding costs: burnout eventually crashes and hits harder for having been deferred; long hours make judgment worse, not better ("If all you do is work, you're unlikely to have sound judgments... No one makes sharp decisions when tired"); and the visible sacrifice pressures colleagues who keep reasonable hours into feeling inadequate, creating "an ass-in-seat mentality" where people stay late out of obligation rather than output. The chapter's closing line reframes who the real hero is: "Workaholics aren't heroes. They don't save the day, they just use it up. The real hero is already home because she figured out a faster way to get things done."

## Why It Matters

This separates effort from output as distinct variables that a culture can easily confuse — rewarding visible hours instead of the quieter, harder skill of finding a shorter path to the same result. Anywhere long hours are treated as a proxy for commitment or competence, this concept names the substitution being made and predicts its costs: degraded judgment, resentment among people who don't play along, and self-inflicted crises that "justify" the very hours being logged.
