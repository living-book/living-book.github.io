# Embrace Constraints

## Definition

"Constraints are advantages in disguise." Limited resources leave no room for waste and force creative solutions that abundance never would have produced. The book treats this as a general law, not a startup consolation prize — it reaches for examples across art and industry to show constraints actively generating quality, not just limiting damage.

## In the Book

The chapter cites Shakespeare working within the strict fourteen-line, iambic-pentameter form of the sonnet, and Hemingway and Raymond Carver forcing themselves into simple, clear language for maximum impact. It points to *The Price Is Right* running for thirty-plus years on one constrained formula ("how much does this item cost?"), and to Southwest Airlines flying only Boeing 737s so every pilot and part is interchangeable — lower cost by design, not by cutting corners. The authors then turn the lens on their own company: building Basecamp with a design firm still running client work, a seven-hour time difference between the two co-founders, a small team, and no outside funding — constraints that "forced us to keep the product simple." Notably, the practice didn't stop once resources grew: 37signals now deliberately caps each product team at one or two people and keeps features to a minimum, manufacturing artificial constraints on purpose.

## Why It Matters

This reframes scarcity from a problem to be solved before "real" work can begin into raw material the work runs on. It licenses a specific move available in any domain — impose an artificial limit even when you don't have to — because the limit itself is what forces the wasteful options off the table and the creative ones into view.
