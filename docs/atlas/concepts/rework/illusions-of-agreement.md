# Illusions of Agreement

## Definition

"The problem with abstractions (like reports and documents) is that they create illusions of agreement. A hundred people can read the same words, but in their heads, they're imagining a hundred different things." The fix is to remove the layer of abstraction entirely: "Instead of describing what something looks like, draw it. Instead of explaining what something sounds like, hum it."

## In the Book

The chapter opens by naming the cost directly — "The business world is littered with dead documents that do nothing but waste people's time. Reports no one reads, diagrams no one looks at, and specs that never resemble the finished product." As a case study it cites Alaska Airlines building its Airport of the Future not from blueprints and sketches but from cardboard-box mock-ups of podiums, kiosks, and belts in a warehouse, then a real prototype tested in Anchorage with actual passengers and employees — a getting-real process the book credits with cutting wait times and raising agent productivity. It also quotes furniture craftsman Sam Maloof, who found it impossible to draw all the intricate detail a chair needs and instead discovered the design with tools in hand: "Many times I do not know how a certain area is to be done until I start working with a chisel, rasp, or whatever tool is needed."

## Why It Matters

This exposes a specific failure mode of any planning artifact — agreement that exists only in the room, not in the heads of the people who signed off — and it's invisible until the real thing gets built and everyone discovers they'd pictured something different. Moving to something concrete as early as possible (a mock-up, a sketch, a working prototype) converts contested imagination into a shared, checkable object before the cost of disagreement compounds.
