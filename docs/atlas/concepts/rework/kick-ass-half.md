# A Kick-Ass Half, Not a Half-Assed Whole

## Definition

Trying to build every good idea at once turns a pile of great ideas into a mediocre product, because "you have limited time, resources, ability, and focus." The book's move is not to trim quality uniformly across a full scope but to sacrifice entire ideas so what remains can be done well: "cut your ambition in half... You're better off with a kick-ass half than a half-assed whole."

## In the Book

The chapter treats cutting as a craft skill borrowed from other media: "Directors cut good scenes to make a great movie. Musicians drop good tracks to make a great album. Writers eliminate good pages to make a great book." The authors apply the principle to *Rework* itself, reporting they cut the manuscript from 57,000 words to about 27,000 between drafts — "Trust us, it's better for it." The chapter also offers a reassurance against loss aversion: most cut ideas won't look as good once you have distance from them, and the genuinely great ones can always be built later — nothing forces them to be built now.

## Why It Matters

This separates two very different responses to too much ambition: spreading effort thinner (which degrades everything) versus removing whole pieces (which protects what remains). It gives permission to treat "later" as a legitimate holding place for good ideas rather than a euphemism for abandoning them, which is what makes aggressive cutting psychologically survivable in any project with more good ideas than capacity.
