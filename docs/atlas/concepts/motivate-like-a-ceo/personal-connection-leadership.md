# Personal Connection Leadership: Making Genuine Human Connections with People

## Definition

People are motivated by leaders who know them, value them, and show it through attention and action. Personal connection is not optional relationship-building; it's a primary mechanism of motivation. It works because it signals respect, creates psychological safety, and shifts the relationship from transactional to relational. Leaders build these connections through small, consistent actions: asking questions about people's work and lives, listening carefully, following up, taking time for coffee or lunch, and remembering what matters to them.

## In the Book

Bates makes personal connection a core principle (Principle 4 of eight). She illustrates it through Bill Swanson (Raytheon CEO) who visited a manufacturing plant and was confronted by a woman who said he should see the problems on the floor. Swanson initially dismissed her, claiming he was too busy. But a blizzard delayed his flight, and he decided to visit the plant unannounced the next morning—without fanfare, so people wouldn't stage-manage.

When he arrived at her station, she said: "What are you doing here?" He replied: "Well, you asked me to come. Can you show me the problem?" She showed him a circuit card assembly issue. Swanson, drawing on his engineering background, helped her solve it. Then she showed him a harness problem, and he rolled up his sleeves and worked through that too. By the end, "many workers in the plant were standing around watching. They started to clap and then to cheer."

The impact was organizational: "That visit changed the attitude of the entire plant. Because Swanson made a personal connection, problems were addressed, and morale started to improve dramatically. The factory became exceptionally productive as team members addressed their issues."

Bates underscores the simplicity: "When people come to expect this from their leaders, they feel a sense of pride in and loyalty to the organization." She notes: "Making personal connections isn't just good for other people—it is very good for you, too. Reaching out and extending yourself to others every day is the path to greater professional fulfillment."

She provides practical methods: "There are so many ways that you can make a personal connection with employees and customers. You can pick up the phone, stop and talk to them, send notes, e-mail or small gifts, make time for breakfast, lunch or coffee. One interaction like that can have an impact for years. A simple acknowledgment is one of the most powerful motivators in the world."

Bates also highlights Principle 5: "Make the Conversation about Them." She contrasts a CEO who held court for 30 minutes talking about golf courses, restaurants, and private jets—and never asked a single question—with leaders who turn conversations into genuine exchanges. "If you don't make a real effort to get to know other people and turn the conversation to them, you are virtually guaranteed to miss an opportunity." People want to be asked about and seen, not lectured at.

She references Evelyn Murphy's campaign for lieutenant governor of Massachusetts. Murphy's advisor told her: "They know you're qualified. This is really about whether they like you enough to vote for you." When Murphy stopped focusing on her qualifications and started listening to people, sitting with them at picnics, and dressing casually, she became authentic and relatable. "I was more physically present and at ease and laughing at myself. That, more than anything else, was a turning point in my career." She won the election.

## Why It Matters

Personal connection addresses a fundamental human need: to be seen and valued. In organizations where leaders remain distant, people perform transactionally—they do what's required, no more. Where leaders know and value them, people go the extra mile, stay in tough times, and defend the organization fiercely. Conversely, personal connection reveals information leaders can't get any other way—people confide insights, concerns, and ideas when they trust the leader cares about them as humans. Finally, personal connection is the antidote to the "too busy to lead" trap; leaders often feel overwhelmed and retreat, but small moments of connection—a conversation, a note, a visit—pay enormous dividends in motivation and retention.
