# Purpose-Driven Leadership: Discovering and Communicating Your Mission

## Definition

A leader's effectiveness in motivating others depends first on the leader discovering and genuinely committing to their own sense of purpose. This is not an epiphany but an evolution—a journey of paying attention to what excites you, what you believe matters, and how your work connects to a larger contribution. Only when you have clarified this for yourself can you authentically communicate it to others.

## In the Book

Bates opens by arguing that purpose is essential to human fulfillment. She cites research from Costa Rica's "Blue Zones" (where people live to 100+) showing that longevity correlates with having a *plan de vida*—a sense of purpose. She makes this universal: "We are happiest when we have the opportunity to apply our talents and skills to something meaningful."

For leaders, the process works differently than many expect. Bates profiles Jeff Taylor (Monster.com, Eons) who describes discovering purpose as "an evolution that becomes more intense the further you go." He didn't start with a grand epiphany; he paid attention to what excited him—big, global, "impossible" ideas—and that pattern revealed his mission. Taylor says: "Your own interests have so much to do with your success. It's about your outlook, passions, and voice."

Vikki Pryor (SBLI CEO) discovered purpose through lived experience: struggling with poverty, discovering discrimination in hiring, and gradually connecting the dots between financial freedom and human potential. This realization drove her entire career and now shapes her mission: "If you have financial freedom, it frees you to do the things you are here in this life to do."

Bates emphasizes that once you've discovered your purpose, you must articulate it—not as a corporate slogan, but as a genuine statement of what matters to you and why. The book argues that leaders who work *with* purpose radiate it, and others are "attracted like magnets."

## Why It Matters

Leaders who skip this step inevitably fail to inspire. They may execute strategy, but they don't energize people. Conversely, leaders who have done this work internally can communicate with authenticity that employees sense immediately. This distinction explains why some organizations have "pockets of happy, productive people" while others do not—the variance often traces to individual leaders' clarity about purpose. In a knowledge economy where intrinsic motivation drives performance, a leader's capacity to model and communicate purpose becomes a core business skill, not a nice-to-have.
