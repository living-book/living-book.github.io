# Chief Motivational Officer: Making Motivation Your Primary Leadership Responsibility

## Definition

Most leaders see motivation as something that happens alongside operational work. Bates proposes a reframe: *Motivation is the work.* Specifically, leaders should adopt the mindset of "Chief Motivational Officer" (CMO). This means that every day, one of the primary roles is connecting people with purpose, communicating mission and values, and inspiring people to bring their full selves to work. It is not a side effort or a "nice-to-have"; it is core to the leader's job and directly impacts business results.

## In the Book

Bates introduces the CMO concept in Chapter 1: "Think of your job as a leader as CMO—chief motivational officer. What does this mean? It means you decide that every day one of your primary roles is inspiring people and connecting them with purpose."

She frames the stakes clearly: leaders who don't embrace this role "are not only less effective, but they also can do real damage to the organization." When leaders are too caught up in day-to-day execution to communicate purpose and inspire, they send a message that people and purpose don't matter. Employees told her: "They are too busy 'doing' to lead. They are caught up in the day-to-day and neglect to communicate."

Bates frames it as a business imperative, not just feel-good leadership: "A motivated, energized workforce is essential to accomplishing your goals. A good strategy and motivated people always accomplish amazing things. Motivated people will overcome the obstacles, defy the odds, and get more done."

She points to results. Rich Krueger (DynamicOps CEO) told her: "We don't have time or money enough to be successful without motivated people. If they treat it just like a job, we will fail." In startup and innovation contexts especially, a motivated team is the difference between success and failure.

Bates also notes the personal payoff. Greg Case (Aon): "I was fortunate to have the opportunity to travel to over 500 Aon offices across the globe, working to connect people with the mission of becoming one global company... [he] felt energized by the mission to change how they saw themselves and their company... many leaders become animated when they discuss the rewards of connecting people to a purpose."

The business outcome was tangible: "A global client is worth eight or nine times what a regional client is worth. The economics are staggering; a $1 million client becomes a $10 million client."

## Why It Matters

The CMO mindset solves the "too busy to lead" trap. Leaders who wait for gaps in their schedule to communicate and inspire never do it. By making motivation a primary role—not a luxury add-on—leaders allocate time and energy accordingly. Additionally, this mindset distinguishes leadership from management. Managers can oversee operations; leaders inspire direction and meaning. In knowledge work, this distinction is survival: people have options. They stay for leaders who help them feel their work matters. The CMO mindset acknowledges this reality and acts on it systematically.
