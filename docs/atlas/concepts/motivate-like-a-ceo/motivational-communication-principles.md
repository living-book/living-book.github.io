# Eight Principles for Motivating Through Communication

## Definition

Bates identifies eight core principles that appear across leaders who successfully motivate others. These are not innate talents but learnable practices: (1) discover your own purpose first; (2) communicate a clear, powerful mission; (3) learn what motivates your people (not assuming they're motivated by what motivates you); (4) make personal connections; (5) make conversations about them, not you; (6) praise, recognize, and reward; (7) walk the talk (act consistently with your words); and (8) empower people. Each principle reinforces the others, and leaders who practice all eight create a multiplier effect in motivation.

## In the Book

Bates frames these principles as the bridge between discovering your own purpose and actually moving people. She notes they are not rules but "common threads" she found by "talking to and observing very effective leaders." Each principle receives a full section with detailed examples.

**Personal connection** (Principle 4) illustrates the mechanism. Bill Swanson (Raytheon CEO) visited a manufacturing plant unexpectedly and found a woman complaining that he wouldn't visit the floor. When a blizzard delayed his flight, he went to the plant unannounced and, by rolling up his sleeves to solve her assembly-line problems, transformed morale. The factory became "exceptionally productive" with "the highest on-time delivery and some of the highest quality statistics in the company." One personal connection shifted the entire facility's attitude.

**Walk the talk** (Principle 7) shows how consistency creates trust. At Baystate Medical Center, Mike Daly required his leadership team to resolve disagreements openly and transparently—if anyone disagreed with a decision, they had to "red light" it and resolve it before proceeding. Leaders who couldn't tolerate this openness had to leave. Over time, this created a shared value system so powerful that Baystate transformed from a demoralized post-merger disaster into a nationally recognized teaching hospital.

**Empower people** (Principle 8) works because people are motivated when trusted. Ellyn McColgan (Morgan Stanley) learned to ask: "Is that really what you want from me?" and shift from problem-solving to enabling. This respect accelerates progress and builds loyalty.

The principles reinforce each other: you can't communicate a mission powerfully without knowing your people; you can't make conversations about them without genuine connection; you can't empower without walking the talk.

## Why It Matters

These principles translate abstract concepts like "purpose" into observable, repeatable behaviors. A manager who doesn't naturally think about storytelling or personal connection can learn these practices and see measurable results. Because they're grounded in psychological research and leadership practice, they work across industries and team sizes. More fundamentally, they reframe leadership: not as command-and-control, but as the active work of creating conditions where people choose to bring their full selves to work.
