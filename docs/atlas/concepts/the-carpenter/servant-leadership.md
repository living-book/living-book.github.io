# Servant Leadership

## Definition

Leadership is not a position from which to accumulate power. It is a commitment to serve those you lead—employees, customers, team members. The greatest leaders earn influence through sacrifice: giving time, energy, focus, and love to help others succeed. Those who serve in small ways earn the right to serve in larger ways. Power flows to those who give it away.

## In the Book

The Carpenter demonstrates this through a subtle lesson: he asks Michael to fetch wood, water, and tools throughout the day. Later, he reveals the teaching: "Because I wanted to demonstrate the second greatest strategy of all to you. Love then leads us to the second greatest strategy of all and it is to serve." Michael served without keeping score, thinking only of helping. The Carpenter then explains that real leaders—Gandhi, Lincoln, MLK, Jesus—are honored not for what they took but for what they sacrificed. He names the mechanism: "When you love, you serve, and when you serve, you sacrifice. Service requires a sacrifice of something. Whether it's time, energy, money, love, effort, or focus, serving others always costs you something, but with service and sacrifice, you gain so much more."

The Carpenter also notes that authenticity is non-negotiable: "You can't fake it. You can't pretend to serve your way to power." People know who would "run into a burning building" to save them. Real service is visible and builds genuine trust.

## Why It Matters

This inverts the common zero-sum view of power and influence. Most organizations struggle because leaders protect themselves and their position. This concept shows that organizations become powerful when leaders prioritize the growth and wellbeing of those they lead. It explains why certain companies and leaders command loyalty that others cannot buy. It also clarifies the distinction between compliance (fear-based) and commitment (love/trust-based)—one is fragile, the other endures.
