# The Symbolic Frame

## Definition

The symbolic frame treats organizations as cultures rather than machines: what matters most is not what happens but what it means, and activity is only loosely coupled to meaning — the same event can carry multiple, equally valid interpretations for different people. The book distills this into five suppositions, including that in the face of ambiguity, symbols (myths, heroes, ceremonies, rituals, stories) arise to anchor hope and direction, and that "culture forms the superglue that bonds an organization, unites people, and helps an enterprise to accomplish desired ends."

## In the Book

Chapter 12 opens with Siena, Italy's 800-year-old palio horse race — a 75-second event neighborhoods live for all year, complete with clubs, hymns, and costumes — as the template for organizational culture-building, then draws a direct line to Zappos's "Culture of Happiness": themed departments, office parades with cowbells, and new hires offered $2,000 to quit during training (95 percent decline), all of which the book credits with driving both employee loyalty and business results. The chapter's other running examples are how simple visual symbols carry outsized cultural weight — GEICO's gecko, Target's bullseye, McDonald's golden arches unifying franchises "as much... by corporate values, and the legend of Ray Kroc as by sophisticated control systems" — and how founding myths anchor identity, as with Southwest Airlines' origin story, sketched by Rollin King and Herb Kelleher on a cocktail napkin planning routes between three Texas cities.

## Why It Matters

The symbolic frame explains why interventions that are functionally identical can succeed or fail entirely based on what they signal — a ceremony, a ritual, a founding story does real organizational work even when it changes no formal process or incentive. It reframes "irrational" behavior (elaborate rituals, brand attachment, resistance to a technically superior redesign) as evidence of a meaning-system operating on its own logic, not a failure of clear thinking to be corrected. Any collective that must sustain commitment through ambiguity and uncertainty — where the rational payoff of showing up isn't self-evident — runs partly on this symbolic layer.
