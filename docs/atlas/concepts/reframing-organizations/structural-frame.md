# The Structural Frame

## Definition

The structural frame treats an organization as a rational architecture: roles, goals, hierarchy, and coordination mechanisms designed to fit its environment and technology. Its central move is naming a set of enduring structural dilemmas — differentiation versus integration, gap versus overlap, clarity versus creativity, autonomy versus interdependence, too loose versus too tight, goal-less versus goal-bound — each a permanent tension to be managed and rebalanced, never a problem with a final fix.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 walks through these dilemmas with paired real-world failures on both sides of each trade-off. On "clarity versus creativity," McDonald's over-defined roles serve customers who want predictability, while an airline gate agent's rigid "that's not my job" script alienates a passenger — same structural choice, opposite payoff depending on context. On "too loose versus too tight," Andersen Worldwide's decentralized structure let client-facing partners overrule its internal audit team, which fed directly into the Enron accounting scandal, while managed-care insurers who centralize treatment-approval authority in clerks far from the patient's bedside produce the opposite failure — a denied hospitalization the book links to a subsequent assault. The chapter opens with two restructuring attempts that backfired: the BBC's post-2004 pile of new editorial-approval layers, meant to prevent another scandal, instead produced the conditions for two worse ones (the Jimmy Savile cover-up and a false pedophilia accusation); and Larry Summers's push to centralize authority at a famously decentralized Harvard, which cost him the presidency within four years.

## Why It Matters

The structural frame reframes "our reorg didn't work" from a design mistake to be corrected next time into evidence that some trade-offs cannot be solved, only continuously rebalanced as circumstances shift. It gives a vocabulary for diagnosing why a fix that worked in one part of a system (tighter approval processes, more autonomy, clearer roles) produces the opposite of its intended effect elsewhere — because the same lever pushes a different dilemma past its tolerance. Any coordinated system that divides labor and then has to reintegrate it faces this same family of trade-offs.
