# Multiframe Thinking (Reframing)

## Definition

A frame is "a mental model — a set of ideas and assumptions — that you carry in your head to help you understand and negotiate a particular territory." Because no frame captures the whole of an organization, competent diagnosis means deliberately viewing the same situation through more than one frame — structural, human resource, political, symbolic — and comparing the pictures that result, rather than locking onto the first plausible read. Bolman and Deal call the failure mode "cluelessness": misreading a situation and not knowing you're misreading it, so you do more of what you already know even when it isn't working.

## In the Book

The opening chapter grounds cluelessness in Volkswagen's emissions-cheating scandal and Wells Fargo's fake-accounts scandal — in both, leadership held a narrow, self-protective picture of what was happening and kept "steady on course" for years despite mounting contrary evidence, including internal whistleblower reports Wells Fargo repeatedly punished rather than heard. The book contrasts this with Cristina "Cha Cha" Rowan, who broke frame during an armed home invasion by offering the intruder a glass of wine, redefining the situation from "robbery" to "social occasion" and ending it without violence or loss. The book's running example of frame-switching in practice is the FBI–CIA rivalry: the same decades-long dysfunction reads differently as a structural problem (the agencies report through entirely separate chains of command), a political problem (turf and funding battles rooted in the personal enmity between J. Edgar Hoover and "Wild Bill" Donovan), and a symbolic problem (the FBI's public, headline-driven culture versus the CIA's culture of secrecy) — no single frame explains the failure, but together they do.

## Why It Matters

Multiframe thinking replaces the question "what's the right answer?" with "what am I even looking at?" — treating diagnosis, not solution-finding, as the place skill actually lives. It explains why smart, experienced people running well-resourced organizations still miss disasters in plain sight: not a deficit of intelligence but a single, unexamined lens applied to a problem that needed several. Because the same underlying situation supports genuinely different, non-overlapping readings, this is a general habit for any complex system where confident action on one interpretation forecloses seeing the others.
