# The Political Frame

## Definition

The political frame views organizations as coalitions of individuals and groups with enduring differences in values, interests, and perceptions of reality, in which most important decisions allocate scarce resources and therefore run on bargaining and power rather than on a single rational calculus. The book states five propositions: organizations are coalitions; coalition members have lasting differences; important decisions are about who gets scarce resources; those differences plus that scarcity put conflict and power at the center of daily life; and goals and decisions emerge from negotiation among stakeholders pursuing their own interests, not from unitary organizational rationality.

## In the Book

Chapter 9 opens with the Columbia and Challenger space shuttle disasters as its central case, walking all five propositions through the same event. NASA was not an isolated technical actor but part of a coalition of contractors, Congress, the White House, and the media — which is why teacher Christa McAuliffe was aboard Challenger despite her expertise being irrelevant to the mission: her presence built public and congressional support for a program under funding pressure. Thiokol, the solid-rocket-booster contractor, had won its sole-source contract through disputed political maneuvering years earlier and was reluctant to jeopardize that billion-dollar relationship by halting flights to fix a known O-ring flaw; NASA, having promised Congress the shuttle would be commercially self-funding through a launch schedule it was badly behind on, responded to engineers' last-minute delay request with "My God, Thiokol, when do you want me to launch? Next April?" The Columbia Accident Investigation Board later found the same institutional dynamics unresolved seventeen years after Challenger, concluding the failure could not be fixed "simply by retirements, resignations, or transferring personnel" — i.e., it was structural to the coalition, not a matter of replacing individuals.

## Why It Matters

The political frame reframes conflict and turf-fighting from individual character flaws (selfishness, incompetence) into the predictable output of interdependence, scarcity, and divergent interests — which means the fix is not better people but better handling of bargaining, coalitions, and power. It explains why swapping out the individuals involved in a political failure so often reproduces the same failure: the underlying coalition structure and resource scarcity are untouched. Any system where multiple stakeholders with real, legitimate, non-aligned interests must share a fixed resource pool exhibits this same dynamic, whether the stakeholders are federal agencies, corporate divisions, or nations.
