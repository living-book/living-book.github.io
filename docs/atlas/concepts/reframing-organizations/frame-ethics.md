# The Four Frames' Ethics

## Definition

Bolman and Deal map each organizational frame onto a distinct ethical mandate and a corresponding leadership gift: the structural frame (factory) demands excellence, given through authorship — trusting people with real creative latitude in their work; the human resource frame (family) demands caring, given through love; the political frame (jungle) demands justice, given through the responsible use of power; and the symbolic frame (temple) demands faith or belief, given through significance. The claim is not that one of these is the "real" ethics of organizations — it is that all four operate simultaneously, and a leader who satisfies only one is not thereby ethical.

## In the Book

Chapter 19 opens with Volkswagen and Wells Fargo (revisited from Chapter 1) and adds Siemens, which routinely paid more than $1 billion in bribes across a dozen countries and built a compliance program specifically to hide the bribery rather than stop it — until a former midlevel executive, Reinhard Siekaczek, was arrested and the deception collapsed. It contrasts these with Medtronic under CEO Bill George, whose founder had created a "Mission and Medallion Ceremony" personally inducting every new employee (George continued it for thousands of employees, including at 2 a.m. for night-shift workers); when a top-performing executive was found running a secret Swiss account to pay doctors, George forced an immediate resignation and made the incident public rather than treat it as a cost of doing business in a different market — during his tenure Medtronic's market capitalization rose from $1 billion to $60 billion while ethically similar fast-growth peers like Enron and WorldCom collapsed. The book uses Google's 70/20/10 time-allocation policy as its example of the structural ethic of authorship in practice, and Southwest Airlines' explicit, repeated use of the word "love" in its culture (Love Field, ticker symbol LUV, "Heroes of the Heart" ceremony) as its example of the human resource ethic of caring.

## Why It Matters

This concept blocks a common failure mode: an organization can be scrupulously ethical on one axis — legally compliant (justice), operationally excellent, or symbolically inspiring — while being corrupt or callous on another, and "we're ethical" claims usually only audit the axis that's convenient. It gives a checklist for a fuller ethical audit of any institution: is it excellent, does it care for the people inside it, is it just in how it allocates scarce goods, and does it earn genuine faith rather than manufacture appearance — four separable questions that can each be answered differently even for the same organization at the same time.
