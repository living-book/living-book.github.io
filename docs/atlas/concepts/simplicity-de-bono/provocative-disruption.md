# Provocative Disruption

## Definition

De Bono distinguishes between "shedding" (challenging elements to justify their existence) and "provocative amputation" (removing elements arbitrarily without justification to see what happens). Provocative methods are designed to break the trance of taken-for-granted assumptions and open up new design space. The goal is not to find the "right" solution but to trigger movement and escape from local equilibrium.

## In the Book

De Bono explains the distinction: "With the shedding process, or historical review, we look to see if something is really necessary. If it is not really necessary we drop it... With the 'amputation' procedure there is no such examination. We look at all things, one at a time, and then see what would happen if we were to throw out (amputate) that element. There is no need for any justification. Even the most obvious things are 'thrown out'."

The book gives examples. If air passengers did not need tickets, this could lead to electronic check-in at departure: "The passenger would simply key in an identifying code... and the computer would let the passenger through." If restaurants did not have chairs, people would spend less time dining, leading to the idea of charging by time instead of by meal, making food cheap with high turnover. If supermarkets did not have checkout points, there could be automatic price readers and an "honour system" with spot checks.

De Bono also describes "wishful thinking" as a provocation. The question is not "is this possible" but "wouldn't it be nice if...?" For example, "Wouldn't it be nice if people had exact change when paying for parking?" This could lead to the idea of pre-charged cards for parking. The wishful thought does not require you to know a solution beforehand.

He emphasizes: "The first idea that comes to mind may not be so interesting but the second and third ideas that flow from it can be very interesting."

## Why It Matters

Provocative methods are essential because rational analysis alone keeps you trapped in the existing system. You cannot think your way out of a system while remaining within its logic. By arbitrarily removing constraints or imagining ideal states, you force new combinations and open design space that incremental improvement would never reach. This is particularly useful when the existing system is so entrenched that "that's not possible" feels like a fact rather than a choice.
