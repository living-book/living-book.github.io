# Cascading Communication

## Definition

The most effective way to align an organization is for leadership team members to leave their own meetings, immediately tell their direct reports what was decided in their own words, and have each layer repeat the process downward — deliberately spreading, as Lencioni puts it, "true rumors." This beats formal channels (email blasts, all-hands decks, videoconferences) because employees judge a message less by its content than by whether multiple people they trust are saying the same consistent thing. It requires message consistency, timeliness, and live delivery, and depends on a step called "commitment clarification" — leaders explicitly asking, at the end of a meeting, "what are we going to go back and tell our people?"

## In the Book

Lencioni tells of a global software company whose offices felt disconnected despite heavy formal communication; once executives started leaving meetings and repeating the same points to their direct reports, an HR leader in Australia calling her counterpart in Germany and hearing the identical message created more felt alignment than any produced communication had. He contrasts this with a "postmeeting confusion" story from early in his career: after a staff meeting decided on a hiring freeze, the head of HR sent a company-wide email announcing it — only to have two peers who'd been in the same meeting storm her office minutes later, because they'd walked away with a different understanding of what was actually decided. The gap wasn't in the messaging technology; it was that the room never verified they agreed on what to say.

## Why It Matters

This reframes "communication problems" as often being commitment problems in disguise: a message that sounds inconsistent as it moves down an organization is usually evidence that the people at the top were never actually aligned, not that the channel used to spread it was wrong. It generalizes to any hierarchy trying to propagate a decision — informal, repeated, person-to-person reinforcement earns more trust than a single well-produced broadcast, because trust in a message tracks the perceived sincerity of the messenger more than the polish of the medium.
