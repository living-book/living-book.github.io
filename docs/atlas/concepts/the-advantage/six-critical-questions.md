# The Six Critical Questions

## Definition

A leadership team creates organizational clarity by explicitly answering six questions together, as a set, rather than in isolation: Why do we exist? How do we behave? What do we do? How will we succeed? What is most important, right now? Who must do what? None can be skipped without leaving a gap that undoes the alignment the others create. Crucially, there is no "right" answer to find — only a directionally correct one the whole team can genuinely commit to.

## In the Book

Lencioni contrasts this with the standard corporate answer to alignment: the mission statement. He quotes a real (if satirical — it's the fictional Dunder Mifflin's, from *The Office*) mission statement full of "quality," "expertise," and "long-term professional relationships" to show how buzzword-laden declarations fail to create any actual shared understanding, and are typically produced by a small group in isolation rather than argued through by the full team. He names "Perfection Paralysis" as the trap that follows: teams stall trying to find the objectively correct answer, when what actually creates alignment is simply *having* an answer the team can rally behind — he cites the military maxim "a plan is better than no plan." Achieving this requires the cohesion built in Discipline 1, because reaching genuine answers demands "passionate, messy dialogue," not a facilitated wordsmithing session.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates two things people conflate: the intellectual quality of an answer and the social fact of alignment around it. It's useful anywhere a group is stuck debating the "right" framing of a problem when the actual blocker is that no one has forced a real, binding decision — the fix is not more analysis but a structured, exhaustive pass through a fixed checklist of questions until the group owns a common, if imperfect, answer to each.
