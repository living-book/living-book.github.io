# The Four Meetings Rhythm

## Definition

Lencioni argues the fix for bad meetings is not fewer or shorter meetings but more of them, each scoped to one kind of content: a daily administrative check-in (5-10 minutes, no agenda, just logistics), a weekly tactical staff meeting (45-90 minutes, built around a real-time agenda rather than a pre-set one), a monthly or quarterly strategic topical meeting (2-4 hours, for a single deep issue), and a quarterly or off-site developmental review (1-2 days, for team health and long-range direction). "The human brain isn't meant to process so many disparate topics in one sitting" — mixing administrative, tactical, and strategic content in one meeting is what makes meetings feel unfocused and interminable, not the number of meetings itself.

## In the Book

For the weekly tactical meeting, he replaces the traditional pre-built agenda with a "real-time agenda": the first minutes are spent going around the room with thirty-second priority reports and reviewing a one-page scorecard tied to the organization's thematic goal, and only then does the team decide what to actually discuss that day — because "you don't know what you need to discuss until you've come together and assessed the situation," much like a marriage counselor wouldn't set the agenda for a couple before meeting them. For the daily check-in, he tells of a Silicon Valley leadership team that stopped doing it during a stretch of vacations and only then realized, once they felt "strangely disconnected," how much low-level friction the ten-minute daily habit had been quietly absorbing.

## Why It Matters

This reframes meeting fatigue as a categorization failure rather than a quantity failure: forcing operational status, tactical problem-solving, and deep strategic debate into the same sixty-minute block guarantees that none of them get done well, and the instinct to compress meetings to save time usually makes this worse, not better. It generalizes to any recurring coordination ritual — matching the cadence and format of a meeting to the timescale of the decision it serves (minutes-scale operational noise vs. hours-scale strategic depth) is what makes the ritual sustainable rather than dreaded.
