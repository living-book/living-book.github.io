# The Thematic Goal (Rallying Cry)

## Definition

The thematic goal is the single, qualitative, time-bound priority that answers "what is most important, right now?" for the whole organization. It must be singular — one thing has to be most important even when several things matter — because a list of five or seven "top priorities" gives every department license to pick whichever one serves them, which is precisely how departmental silos form. The thematic goal is not a permanent mission; it changes as the organization moves through different seasons.

## In the Book

Lencioni traces the idea back to a search for organizations that don't suffer from silos, which led him to emergency responders: firefighters, ER staff, soldiers on a rescue mission. "You never see two firefighters standing in front of a burning building and arguing about which one of them should be responsible for climbing up and saving a child's life based on the departmental jurisdiction of their respective divisions." What crisis gives these groups is a single rallying cry with no room for competing interpretation. He argues ordinary organizations can manufacture the same clarifying effect, without needing an actual crisis, by naming one thematic goal per period — a concept he first developed in his fable *Silos, Politics, and Turf Wars* — and lets a small number of supporting "defining objectives" cascade under it.

## Why It Matters

This concept names the specific failure mode of "everything is a priority": a long list of co-equal goals doesn't fail because any one goal is wrong, it fails because it hands every subgroup a legitimate excuse to optimize for the priority that serves them, and coordination collapses. It applies anywhere resource-constrained groups need to move together — a single ranked focus, even a temporary and imperfect one, does something a balanced portfolio of goals cannot: it removes the ambiguity that silos exploit.
