# Human Systems as Reinforcement

## Definition

Every recurring process that touches people — recruiting, interviewing, performance reviews, compensation, promotion, termination — should be judged by one criterion: does it keep people focused on what the organization has already decided is important, even when no leader is in the room to say so out loud? Lencioni's claim is that the best human systems are deliberately simple and customized, not sophisticated or benchmarked against what a larger, unrelated company does, because "no generic systems can be downloaded from the Internet" and fit someone else's clarity, not yours.

## In the Book

He argues leaders often abdicate this discipline in the name of efficiency, adopting a performance review or compensation plan because "it's good enough for General Electric or PepsiCo" — missing that the point of the system is to reinforce *this* organization's specific answers to the six critical questions, not to look professional. On hiring specifically, he stakes out a position between two failure modes: pure "gut feel" hiring, where leaders trust unstructured judgment and quietly forget their bad hires, and over-structured hiring, where HR or legal departments layer on so much process that judgment about cultural fit gets crowded out. His prescribed middle ground is a one-page, front-and-back hiring guide — one side listing the organization's specific behavioral values as observable interview criteria, the other side blank for notes — consistent across departments for cultural fit even where technical requirements differ.

## Why It Matters

This concept turns "culture" from a stated value into an audit question you can apply to any recurring process: strip away the sophistication of a system and ask whether it still reinforces the organization's actual priorities, or whether it has become an end in itself (avoiding lawsuits, matching a benchmark, looking rigorous). It's a general antidote to process bloat — the right amount of structure is whatever amount keeps the system pointed at the organization's stated priorities, and no more, because added structure past that point substitutes for judgment rather than supporting it.
