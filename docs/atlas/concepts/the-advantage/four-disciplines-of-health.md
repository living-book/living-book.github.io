# The Four Disciplines Model

## Definition

Organizational health is produced by four disciplines taken on in order and then maintained together on an ongoing basis: (1) build a cohesive leadership team, (2) create clarity — align the team intellectually around the answers to a small set of critical questions, (3) overcommunicate that clarity to everyone in the organization, and (4) reinforce clarity through the nonbureaucratic human systems (hiring, performance management, rewards) that touch every employee. Each discipline is a precondition for the next to hold; skipping one leaves the ones after it unstable.

## In the Book

Lencioni states the model isn't linear or tidy in practice — "like building a strong marriage or family, it's a messy process that involves doing a few things at once" — but the ideal build order is clear: without behavioral cohesion at the top (Discipline 1), a leadership team cannot have the passionate, honest debate needed to reach real clarity (Discipline 2); without clarity, there's nothing coherent to communicate (Discipline 3); and without communication, there's no shared message for the reinforcing systems (Discipline 4) to reinforce. He argues the model is close to foolproof: cohesive, aligned, well-communicated, reinforced leadership creates an environment "in which success is almost impossible to prevent," because cohesive teams catch their own catastrophic strategic mistakes before those mistakes compound — the model doesn't replace smart decisions, it protects the organization from the worst version of them.

## Why It Matters

This is a template for diagnosing where a change effort is actually stuck: a new strategy that isn't landing might be failing not at the strategy layer but three disciplines upstream, in an incohesive leadership team that never truly agreed on it. It generalizes to any effort to align a group of people around anything — the failure mode is rarely "we announced this wrong," it's usually that the announcement (Discipline 3) was covering for missing agreement (Discipline 2) that was itself covering for a team that couldn't have an honest fight (Discipline 1).
