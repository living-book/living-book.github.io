# Kata as Disciplined Improvement

## Definition

A kata (derived from Japanese martial arts) is a detailed pattern of movement or thinking that becomes habitual through repeated practice. In Scrumban, kata refers to establishing disciplined, repeated routines for continuous improvement—such as A3 Thinking (a structured problem-solving and hypothesis-testing method on a single page) or the Cynefin framework (a decision-making kata for different types of problems). The goal is to create value-adding habits that reinforce important values, so that when employees and managers seek improvements day after day, "without having to think about it, then high performance becomes sustainable."

## In the Book

Reddy draws on Mike Rother's concept from *Toyota Kata* that Toyota's success derives not from specific tools but from "invisible routines of thinking and acting, particularly in management, that differ significantly from those found in most companies." A3 Thinking exemplifies this: it is both a disciplined approach to problem-solving and a kata—a teaching protocol for instilling common vocabulary and thinking patterns throughout an organization. The book emphasizes that adopting a kata (or several katas) across an organization represents an ideal, but adopting some habitual discipline is a key ingredient to accelerating individual and organizational learning. For early Shu-level learners especially, katas provide the concrete steps and structure they need before abstract principles can take hold. Scrumban "naturally supports many different katas"—the framework doesn't mandate one; it enables teams to discover and institutionalize whichever works best for their context.

## Why It Matters

This concept transforms continuous improvement from an aspirational goal ("we want to improve") into an executable, transmissible practice. Without kata, improvement depends on individual brilliance or episodic consulting engagements; with kata, it becomes a self-sustaining organizational capability that new team members can be trained into. It also explains why Scrumban succeeds where other frameworks stall: the framework itself is less important than the embedded thinking routines it enables. A team with deep A3 Thinking discipline will improve faster and more sustainably than a team with a perfect process design and no improvement discipline. Practically, it offers a bridge between abstract principles (systems thinking, empiricism) and concrete daily action—the kata is the mechanism that makes principles operational.
