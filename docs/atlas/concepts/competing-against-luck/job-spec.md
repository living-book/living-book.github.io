# The Job Spec

## Definition

A job spec is a structured document that captures everything a solution must
address to genuinely satisfy a customer's job: the functional, emotional, and
social dimensions of the progress the customer wants; the tradeoffs the
customer will and won't accept; the full set of competing solutions the new
offering must beat; and the obstacles and anxieties that must be overcome
before the customer will switch. It is the design brief that follows once a
job has been deeply understood, translating "the richness and complexity of
the job into an actionable guide for innovation."

## In the Book

Chapter 6 argues that because a job "has a richness and complexity to it,
your solution must, too" — a solution built only from the functional
requirement misses the point, since real jobs almost always carry social and
emotional weight as well. The chapter's central claim is that a complete
solution to a job is never just the core product; it must also include the
"experiences of purchase and use" that remove the obstacles a customer faces
in hiring the new solution and firing the old one, "which means that
ultimately all successful solutions to jobs can be thought of as services,
even for product companies." A recurring illustration in the notes is a
restaurant that earned strong Yelp reviews and, as a result, attracted
foodies seeking a "hidden gem" experience its food was never designed to
deliver — a mismatch between what the job spec should have said and what the
business actually optimized for, producing disappointed customers and bad
reviews despite being, by its own account, "a decent local place."

## Why It Matters

Treating a job statement as sufficient for design skips the step where most
failures actually happen — the tradeoffs, competitors, and friction that
decide whether a customer chooses you over every other way of getting the
job done. A job spec forces those specifics onto the table before
engineering begins, and it makes explicit that a product's real competition
often lies outside its own product category.
