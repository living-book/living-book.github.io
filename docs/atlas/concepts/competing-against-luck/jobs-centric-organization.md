# Building a Jobs-Centric Organization

## Definition

Delivering on a job spec consistently requires processes that deliberately
coordinate functions a company's org chart normally keeps siloed — product,
marketing, sales, and service each optimize for their own narrow efficiency
by default, not for the customer's end-to-end experience. The book's
prescription is not to reorganize around jobs (large-scale reorgs rarely
work) but to build and integrate the right cross-functional processes and to
measure and manage against metrics tied to what customers actually need from
the experience, letting jobs-centricity settle into the culture over time
rather than being installed by structural fiat.

## In the Book

Chapter 7 states plainly that job-centric process integration "does not come
naturally to most companies" because existing processes are "aimed at
improving efficiency or achieving a narrow outcome within a specific
function," not at delivering a complete cross-functional experience. It
identifies a further gap: "most organizations do not have one person who is
the 'steward' ensuring the company consistently delivers against the
customer's job," and argues that traditional organizational structures and
silos have real value and are likely to endure — so the practical path is to
"carefully set up and integrate the right processes, measure the right
things, and over time embed jobs centricity in the culture," rather than
attempt disruptive restructuring. The notes cite a 2010 Bain & Company study
of fifty-seven major reorganizations in which fewer than a third produced any
meaningful improvement, and some destroyed value — direct evidence against
the reorg-first instinct. The chapter also points to Edgar Schein's work on
organizational culture and Atul Gawande's checklist-driven patient-safety
gains as models for how process discipline, not restructuring, changes
outcomes.

## Why It Matters

The instinct when a company loses focus on its customers' jobs is to redraw
the org chart; the evidence the book cites says that mostly fails, and
destroys value more often than it helps. Building jobs-centricity through
process integration and job-aligned metrics is slower and less dramatic, but
it is the lever that actually works because it changes what gets measured
and coordinated day to day, not just who reports to whom.
