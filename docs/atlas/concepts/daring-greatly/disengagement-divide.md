# The Disengagement Divide

## Definition

Brown's theory is that disengagement is the issue underlying most of the dysfunction she sees in families, schools, and organizations, and that it grows from one specific mechanism: the widening space between a group's *practiced values* (what leaders actually do, think, and feel) and its *aspirational values* (what they claim to stand for). She calls this space the "value gap" or "disengagement divide." Her governing line is "we can't give people what we don't have" — who leaders *are* matters immeasurably more than what they know or say they want to be. People don't disengage because leadership names the wrong values; they disengage when leadership fails to live the values it names.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 illustrates the divide with paired family vignettes. In the failure cases: a mother preaches honesty but shrugs off an undercharged grocery bill ("wasn't my fault, they're making a mint anyway"); a father preaches respect and accountability but is too absorbed in his BlackBerry to make his sons address a broken toy; parents demand gratitude while calling each other names and never saying please or thank you; and a father with a zero-tolerance drug policy gets called a hypocrite by his daughter over his own well-documented high-school drinking. In the success case, a couple who profess a "feelings first" ethic actually enact it — when their son comes home upset from basketball practice, they follow him upstairs, sit on his bed, and share their own high-school struggles rather than lecturing, producing (in the father's own account) a moment where "we were all crying" and the relationship visibly opened. Brown extends the mechanism beyond the family to politics (laws legislators exempt themselves from) and religion (leaders who convert faith into "compliance and consequences" rather than modeling how to sit with uncertainty), arguing both are large-scale instances of the same divide: "faith minus vulnerability equals politics, or worse, extremism."

## Why It Matters

This reframes disengagement — in a team, a classroom, a congregation, a country — as a measurable structural gap rather than a motivational deficiency in the disengaged party. It gives leaders a specific, checkable lever: disengagement isn't fixed by better slogans or stricter enforcement of stated values, but by narrowing the daylight between the values an authority publishes and the ones it actually enacts under pressure — a diagnostic that applies as cleanly to corporate culture and civic trust as it does to parenting.
