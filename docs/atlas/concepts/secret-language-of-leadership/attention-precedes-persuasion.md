# Attention Precedes Persuasion

## Definition

Getting attention isn't about transmitting information — it's about clearing space in a distracted listener's mind so something new can land there at all. Denning sets out six principles governing what reliably captures this "front-of-mind" attention: the unexpected (routine events get relegated to automatic, back-of-mind processing), the emotional and specific (abstractions don't move people; one burned child does what "tens of thousands killed" cannot), the personal ("what's in it for me"), the relevant (attention pointed somewhere other than the leader's actual theme backfires), effort proportionate to how distracted and how large the ask is, and — most reliably of all — the negative, because human attention carries an evolved bias toward threats over rewards.

## In the Book

Chapter 8 opens by treating attention as a scarce resource competing against a "kaleidoscope" of stimuli, and distinguishes front-of-mind attention (conscious, focused, the kind used to solve a problem or watch a film) from back-of-mind, automatic attention that handles routine input without ever surfacing. Denning illustrates the specificity principle with Tolstoy's Anna Karenina noticing, for the first time, how large her husband's ears are once she has begun her affair — a small concrete detail carrying disproportionate emotional weight. He uses Al Gore's 2000 debate performance as a cautionary case for the relevance principle: Gore's stories drew attention, but to problems under the administration he'd served, focusing the audience on the wrong question entirely. The chapter then ranks specific communication tools — telling a story about the audience's own problems, telling a story of the speaker's own adversity, asking a question, using a striking metaphor — by how reliably they achieve this attention-capturing effect, with the negativity bias offered as the reason accentuating a problem or threat consistently outperforms leading with the positive.

## Why It Matters

This treats "getting heard" as a distinct, prior problem from "being convincing" — a message can be true, well-argued, and completely ignored because it never cleared the attention gate. It generalizes past leadership speeches to any competition for a distracted audience's mind (marketing, teaching, writing a subject line, opening a meeting): specificity, threat-framing, and personal relevance aren't rhetorical decoration, they're the mechanism by which a message gets a hearing before its content is even evaluated.
