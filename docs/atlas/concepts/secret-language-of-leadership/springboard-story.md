# The Springboard Story

## Definition

A springboard story describes a single, concrete instance of a change already succeeding somewhere, told with minimal detail so it leaves "mental space" for listeners to generate their own analogous story in their own context — with themselves as protagonist. Because listeners arrive at the idea themselves, they don't need to be argued into it: "What a great idea! I've just had a great idea!" To work, the story's ending must be both credible (it has to ring true given what the audience already knows) and happy specifically for that audience, not just for the organization or the leader.

## In the Book

Denning traces the concept to his own experience at the World Bank in 1996 (the story is reproduced in Appendix 1), where a simple account of a health worker in Zambia using the internet to solve a malaria question, told to skeptical staff, did more to spark the knowledge-sharing initiative than any direct pitch. Chapter 9 sets the mechanism against direct, "well-told" storytelling: a fully immersive story fills the listener's mind with the speaker's sights, sounds, and details, leaving no room for the listener's own story to form. The springboard story deliberately does the opposite — it is told sparingly so the audience's own imagination does the persuading. Denning invokes William Gibson's line, "the future is already here, it's just not very evenly distributed," to frame the story as evidence the change is already real elsewhere, then names the two failure modes: an incredible ending (e.g., citing one successful corporate acquisition when most acquisitions fail) breeds skepticism instead of enthusiasm, and a happy ending that's happy only for the company (not for the "workers who will be laid off") fails to generate the audience's own version of the story.

## Why It Matters

Springboard stories work by getting out of the way of the audience's own reasoning rather than overriding it — the opposite move from a direct pitch, which the audience must accept or reject as-is. Anywhere a change requires buy-in rather than compliance (a manager persuading skeptical staff, a founder pitching an unproven model, an activist trying to shift public opinion), this reframes the goal of the message from "communicate content completely" to "leave room for the listener to finish the thought themselves" — ownership of the conclusion, not the polish of its delivery, is what produces durable enthusiasm.
