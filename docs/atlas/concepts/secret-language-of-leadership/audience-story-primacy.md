# Audience Story Primacy

## Definition

Every audience already carries its own ongoing story about who it is and what a given change means for it; a leader's message succeeds only to the extent it's compatible with, or reshapes, that existing story — not according to how sincere or logically sound the message is. Denning names the standing obstacle to seeing this the "fundamental attribution error": the tendency to explain other people's resistance by their character or motives ("they're difficult," "they don't get it") rather than by the situation and story they're actually operating inside.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 opens with Howell Raines, who took over as executive editor of the New York Times in 2001 with a coherent, well-resourced change strategy, the backing of the publisher, hiring and firing authority, and the good fortune of 9/11 landing six days into his tenure as a chance to prove his "overwhelming force" approach — yet was dismissed after nineteen months. Denning argues the tactical missteps often cited (his aggressive demeanor, military metaphors, isolation in the "DMZ" of his corner office) were secondary; the deeper failure was that Raines never grasped or worked with the newsroom staff's own story about who they were and what mattered to them, so his management tools — however forcefully applied — never converted into the staff's desire to change. The chapter states directly that "neither the sincerity of their intentions nor the soundness of their ideas" can guarantee managers like Raines that their actions will be read as constructive, because listeners interpret action through their own story, not the actor's intent.

## Why It Matters

This displaces the default diagnosis for failed persuasion or change efforts — "they resisted because of their character or self-interest" — with a testable alternative: the message never engaged the story the audience was already living inside. It applies anywhere one party assumes their intent is self-evident to another (management edicts, product pitches, policy rollouts, even personal arguments): the question to ask isn't "was my case sound," but "what story is the other side already in, and does what I said fit inside it."
