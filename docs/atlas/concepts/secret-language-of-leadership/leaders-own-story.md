# The Leader's Own Story

## Definition

Before a leader can inspire a goal in others, they must inhabit it themselves — not as a mental position they hold but as a commitment of "mind, body, and soul." Denning distinguishes a goal argued for instrumentally, on pragmatic or legal grounds, from a goal a leader has fully internalized as worthwhile in itself; only the latter generates the intensity of language and presence that moves other people, because a half-committed leader "will find themselves beating around the bush, avoiding issues, saying things they don't really mean."

## In the Book

Chapter 3 traces Abraham Lincoln's transformation across three moments. In his 1861 inaugural address, Lincoln argued instrumentally and legally for preserving the Union, explicitly disclaiming any intent to abolish slavery. By his December 1862 Annual Message to Congress, he had shifted to explicitly moral language ("in giving freedom to the slave, we assure freedom to the free"). By the Gettysburg Address in November 1863, he called for "a new birth of freedom" — reframing the war from defending an "old Union" that contained slavery to proclaiming a "new Union" that would fulfill the promise of liberty. Denning reads this sequence as Lincoln's own commitment deepening before his public language could carry it; he notes the Gettysburg Address had no discernible effect on the audience physically present that day (dazed by a two-hour preceding oration, the speech ended before the photographer was ready) — its power came later, once reprinted, from the moral commitment embedded in the words themselves, not from delivery. The chapter frames committing to lead as a real, costly choice — citing the risks Lincoln, Gandhi, and Martin Luther King Jr. each accepted — that most people hesitate over, as Hamlet and Odysseus did.

## Why It Matters

This locates the source of inspiring communication in the communicator's own prior conviction rather than in technique — a leader arguing a position they haven't personally settled into produces detectable hedging, regardless of how well-crafted the words are. It generalizes to any situation where someone is trying to move others toward a change (a manager selling a reorg, a founder pitching a vision, an activist recruiting allies): the audience is responding to the depth of the messenger's own commitment as much as to the content of the message.
