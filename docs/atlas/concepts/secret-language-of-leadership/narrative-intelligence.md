# Narrative Intelligence

## Definition

Narrative intelligence is knowing which narrative pattern produces which effect on which audience — the ability to "think narratively" about the world, distinct from Howard Gardner's multiple intelligences (linguistic, logical, interpersonal, and so on) because none of those cover navigating "a world of interacting narratives." Denning treats it as a skill with identifiable components: familiarity with different story patterns and their effects, the ability to overcome the fundamental attribution error and read the audience's own story, and the capacity to anticipate, in real time, whether a given communication tool will actually generate a new story in a particular listener's mind.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 first raises the idea while arguing that all leadership communication — not just stories but questions, metaphors, offers, challenges — succeeds or fails by whether it "generates a new story in the mind of a listener." Chapter 5, "Cultivating Narrative Intelligence," develops it fully: Denning cites studies showing stories are remembered faster and more accurately than abstract claims, and that once an audience is absorbed in a compelling narrative, the perceived credibility of the source matters less — narrative can bypass source-credibility problems that sink direct argument. He is explicit that the research base is thin (psychology long treated the brain as an "abstract information processor" and ignored storytelling) but argues enough is known to act on now, and provides a self-assessment quiz in Appendix 3. Crucially, Denning pairs the concept with a warning it can't stand alone: narrative intelligence divorced from truthfulness produced Al Gore's exaggerations and Ronald Reagan's factual slips — skill in narrative without integrity becomes manipulation.

## Why It Matters

Narrative intelligence names persuasion skill as a form of pattern recognition rather than raw charisma or eloquence: it can be studied, practiced, and gotten wrong in specific, correctable ways (wrong pattern for the situation, ignoring what the audience already believes, mistaking a well-told story for an effective one). This reframes any influence problem — pitching, teaching, organizing, negotiating — as a question of pattern-fit between message and listener's existing mental model, not just of message quality in the abstract.
