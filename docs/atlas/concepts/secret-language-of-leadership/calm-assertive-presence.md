# Calm Assertive Presence

## Definition

Leadership presence — "the ability to connect authentically with the thoughts and feelings of others" — has a physical dimension that Denning treats as separable from message content: body language that listeners read, often unconsciously, as either calm assertiveness or aggression. Because audiences trust nonverbal signals over words when the two conflict, incongruent body language undermines an otherwise sound message, while a short list of specific, masterable behaviors (steady eye contact, no notes, unhurried pace) reliably signals a leader is "there for the audience" without threatening them.

## In the Book

Chapter 7 draws its central case from Cesar Millan's television program The Dog Whisperer, where dog owners learn "calm assertiveness" — energy that is present and unmistakable but not threatening — and dogs instantly accept them as a leader once they display it. Denning explicitly ties this back to Howell Raines (introduced in Chapter 4): the "look of an angry hawk" that crossed Raines's face when addressing his staff registered as aggression regardless of his actual intent, and triggered an emotional, "reptilian brain" reaction in listeners rather than a reasoned one. He also cites Vicki Hearne's Adam's Task, which distinguishes self-absorbed "Hollywood types," over-analytical "researchers" who get bitten by frustrated dogs, and animal trainers who exhibit "soft, acute, 360-degree awareness" — the disposition Denning maps onto effective leaders. The concrete basics he prescribes are narrow: direct eye contact with everyone in the room (not sideways, which reads as shifty), discarding notes because reading is incompatible with sustained eye contact, and unhurried, uncluttered physical movement — a short list he argues moves most speakers from "boring" to "extraordinary" even without years of stagecraft training.

## Why It Matters

This separates the content of a message from the channel that determines whether it's trusted at all — a technically correct argument delivered with incongruent body language will be read through the body language, not the words. It generalizes past formal speeches to any face-to-face influence moment (a difficult one-on-one, a negotiation, a first meeting with a new team): presence is not a fixed trait some people have and others lack, but a short, specific, practicable set of behaviors.
