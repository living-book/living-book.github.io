# Seeking Disconfirmation

## Definition

McGrath argues that most corporate decision-making processes are built to surface
information that confirms an existing strategy is correct, when the more valuable
information — especially in a fast-changing environment — is disconfirming: evidence
that pinpoints exactly where the current strategy is most likely to be wrong. She pairs
this with a shift from precise-but-slow, prediction-oriented planning toward "fast and
roughly right," discovery-driven, options-oriented decision making, on the reasoning
that speed and correction beat precision and prediction once advantages are transient.

## In the Book

Chapter 6's central case is Kodak. In 1979, incoming researcher Wolfgang Gunther (the
author's father) told a Kodak executive, Emulsion Research head Tom Whiteley, that
"8-mm movies have already had it" because of video cassette cameras — and was berated
for not appreciating how entrenched Kodak's technology was. Five years later, a 1984
Forbes article, "Has the World Passed Kodak By?," was met not with reflection but with
a seven-page, single-spaced rebuttal document flatly denying the criticisms ("Kodak
does not have poor management"). Kodak filed for bankruptcy roughly three decades
later. McGrath contrasts this with the candor practiced inside the growth-outlier
firms: Cognizant's former CEO Lakshmi Narayanan describes how CEO Francisco D'Souza
interrogates every recommendation — "the conclusion will be challenged, the reasoning
behind the conclusion will be challenged, the data that supports the reasoning will be
challenged" — and HDFC Bank's Aditya Puri tells staff to "treat the CEO visit as a
dentist visit... there will be pain," precisely to keep disconfirming information
flowing upward instead of being filtered out.

## Why It Matters

An organization's information-gathering habits determine how early it can see a wave
ending. Confirmation-seeking cultures generate a false sense of security right up until
the numbers force the issue — by which point, as McGrath's other Kodak-vs-Fuji material
shows, recovery is far more expensive or impossible. Building disconfirmation-seeking
into leadership practice (structured challenge, rotating adversarial review, inviting
technologists and outsiders into strategy conversations) converts a psychological bias
that's costly at any scale into an early-warning system.
