# Arenas, Not Industries

## Definition

McGrath argues that "industry" is too coarse a unit of strategic analysis once
advantages are transient, because it assumes competitors are firms offering close
substitutes within the same category. She proposes the **arena** — a granular
combination of a specific customer segment, a specific offer, and a specific
geography, organized around the "job to be done" rather than a product category — as
the real unit at which competitive battles are actually fought and won.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 catalogs how within-industry thinking blindsides incumbents: no money-center
bank saw Merrill Lynch's cash management account coming in the 1980s because Merrill
wasn't a bank; Google's move into phone operating systems and video, Walmart's move
into health care, and mobile carriers, internet credit firms, and card networks all
converging on payments are framed as arena collisions rather than industry rivalries.
McGrath contrasts industry-based strategy with chess — a fight for checkmate within
one bounded game — against arena-based strategy with the game of Go, where the goal is
territorial capture across many simultaneous local contests. The book's running example
is language-training firm Berlitz: in Brazil, Berlitz competed for a fierce mass market
but a nearly uncontested upper-income segment, so it positioned itself there as an elite
brand; in the United States, with a broad middle-income customer base, it instead
competed on convenience and flexibility — two different arena strategies executed
under one company, both feeding a longer-term evolution toward becoming a cultural
consultancy.

## Why It Matters

Mapping arenas instead of industries surfaces threats and openings that industry
classification hides by construction — a competitor who doesn't share your SIC code but
serves the same job-to-be-done for the same customer will not show up in a five-forces
analysis until it's too late. It also legitimizes running genuinely different strategies
for different slices of what looks, at the industry level, like a single market.
