# Mapping Altitude

## Definition

Work has different degrees of granularity, from the whole value stream down to individual keystrokes. Value stream mapping deliberately operates at the macro level — the full request-to-receipt cycle across every function that touches it — before anyone is allowed to design micro-level, process-specific fixes. The book's central warning is "suboptimization": improving one component while ignoring its effect on the rest of the system, which "could cause the overall work system to perform more poorly."

## In the Book

Chapter 1 opens with Figure 1.1 ("Granularity of Work") to make the point explicit: value stream mapping is macro-level work meant for leadership to set strategic direction, while process-level mapping is micro-level work meant for the people who do the work to design tactical improvements. Martin and Osterling argue this is precisely backward in most organizations — teams get "tangled up in the weeds," designing micro-level improvements before anyone understands the macro picture, which the book identifies as a leading cause of failed improvement efforts. A recurring example: reducing one department's turnaround time can simply push a bigger queue onto the next department, making the overall system worse even though the local metric improved — faster patient discharge from an ER means nothing if there's no bed to admit the patient into. During future-state design (Chapter 4), the book notes an "interesting phenomenon": when teams hold the macro perspective, process-time reductions become a *by-product* of fixing macro-level barriers (IT disconnects, handoffs, batching) rather than the direct target — the facilitator's job is to keep pulling the team back up out of the weeds.

## Why It Matters

Choosing the wrong altitude to analyze a problem is a silent failure mode: the fix works exactly as designed and still makes things worse, because the boundary drawn around "the system" excluded the part that absorbs the consequence. Before optimizing any single component, you have to be able to answer "the system that contains what I'm about to change — have I actually seen all of it?"
