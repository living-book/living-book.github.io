# Rudy's Rutabaga Rule

## Definition

"Once you eliminate your number one problem, number two gets a promotion." Solving the worst problem in a system does not leave it problem-free — it simply advances whatever was previously the second-worst issue into the top slot. The naive fantasy of consulting (or of self-improvement, or management) is a final state with no problems left; the rule says that state doesn't exist, because there is always a next-worst item waiting.

## In the Book

The rule comes from Weinberg's own first taste of consulting, at thirteen, as a relief stock boy in Hillman's Supermarket. Standing with produce manager Rudy, trying to fit vegetables into limited counter space, young Weinberg notices that rutabagas never sell and suggests clearing their space for something else — Rudy calls it a great idea, sweeps them into a box, then immediately asks, "Now what's the least popular vegetable?" Weinberg generalizes this decades later to a dripping faucet he fixes at night, only to lie awake as a previously unnoticed antenna wire, now the loudest remaining problem, taps against the window — he chooses to leave it rather than repeat the cycle. He also applies it to teaching: removing one difficult student from a class doesn't produce a trouble-free class, it promotes the second-most-difficult student into the top complaint.

## Why It Matters

This corrects a specific, recurring expectation-management error: judging an intervention's success by whether problems stopped occurring, rather than by whether the problem list improved. It's directly useful anywhere someone measures progress by "are we problem-free yet" — engineering, health, organizational change — since that framing guarantees perceived failure. The more honest question the rule points to is whether the new top problem is less costly than the old one, not whether a top problem still exists.
