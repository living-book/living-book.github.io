# Self-Perpetuating Problems

## Definition

Asking "WHERE DOES THIS PROBLEM COME FROM?" usually turns up a person, a process,
or a motivation you can act on — far better than blaming vague "Nature" or "human
nature," which the book calls "the world's number one excuse for inactivity." But
some problems trace back to nothing external at all: "the ultimate source of the
problem may be nowhere at all... IN THE VALLEY OF THE PROBLEM SOLVERS, THE PROBLEM
CREATOR IS KING, OR PRESIDENT, OR DEAN." A memo-generating bureaucracy, a standing
army after the war ends, or a disarmament conference can become an institution that
perpetuates its own reason for existing, because the people who staff it have no
incentive to see it disappear.

## In the Book

Janet Jaworski, stalled by a Polish visa clerk demanding an eighth notarized copy,
resists blaming impersonal "bureaucracy" and instead traces the clerk's behavior to
a plausible source — selection processes (the book invokes the Peter and Paul
Principles) that promote functionaries chosen precisely for being intractable
enough to shield superiors from clients — and by treating him as a person (learning
his name, Mr. Matczyszyn, instead of "Mr. Grayface") she resolves the visa problem
directly. A "Utilization of Commas in Weekly Punctuation Reports" memo, by
contrast, illustrates a problem that comes from itself: bureaucratic activity, "full
of sound and fury, signifying nothing," is self-perpetuating because its
originators — safely separated in offices "on the top floor of the tallest
building" — profit from generating it. The chapter's sharpest example is the
International Disarmament Conference: the very people whose careers, meals, and
patisserie visits depend on the arms race persisting have every reason not to
solve it, and problem-solving institutions whose original problem vanishes (the
March of Dimes after polio, standing armies after a war) routinely go find another
problem rather than disband.

## Why It Matters

An institution, procedure, or role that was created to solve a problem can quietly
become dependent on that problem's continued existence, at which point no amount
of well-intentioned effort from within it will resolve the problem — the incentive
runs the other way. Asking who benefits from a problem's persistence, and whether
the answer includes the very people or offices tasked with fixing it, exposes a
class of problems that ordinary troubleshooting can't touch because the trouble is
the point.
