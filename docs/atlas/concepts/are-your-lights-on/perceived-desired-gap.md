# Problem as Perceived-Desired Gap

## Definition

"A PROBLEM IS A DIFFERENCE BETWEEN THINGS AS DESIRED AND THINGS AS PERCEIVED." This
definition has a practical edge: since a problem is a gap between two mental states,
not an objective property of the world, it can be closed by changing the actual
situation (things as perceived) or by changing what is wanted (things as desired).
Crucially, the book insists "PHANTOM PROBLEMS ARE REAL PROBLEMS" — a discrepancy
felt but not measurable by outside observers is still a genuine problem for the
person feeling it.

## In the Book

Peter Pigeonhole, tasked with the Brontosaurus Tower elevator complaints, arms
himself with this definition and realizes the wait can be shortened either by
actually reducing wait time or by making the wait feel shorter. He recalls a case
where employees were getting hurt rushing down stairs, solved not by slowing
anyone down but by installing mirrors on each landing — vanity made people slow
themselves. The book grounds "phantom problems" with the example of feeling cold
in a 25°C room: the thermostat reading doesn't resolve the problem because the
problem is the gap between what you feel and what you want to feel, not the
number on the dial. When Peter's mirror fix backfires (graffiti), and the graffiti
fix backfires (crayons), and the actual rat-in-the-control-box fix backfires
(elevators now run too well and overload the subway), the chapter uses this
recurring collapse to show that each perceived-desired gap, once closed, tends to
open a new one elsewhere.

## Why It Matters

Defining a problem as a gap between two mental states, rather than as a broken
object or a wrong number, opens up a second axis of intervention that's easy to
miss: you can resolve a problem by managing perception or expectation instead of
only by changing the underlying reality. It also legitimizes problems that have no
external, measurable cause — a felt discrepancy is enough to justify action — which
matters anywhere people dismiss complaints as "not a real problem" because no
instrument can detect them.
