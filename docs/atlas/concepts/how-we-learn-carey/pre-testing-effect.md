# Pre-Testing Effect

## Definition

Pre-testing is administering a test on material before instruction occurs. Counterintuitively, bombing a pretest improves subsequent learning of that material—students who answer wrong on a pretest about scientific explanations later score about 10% higher on related questions on a cumulative exam than students who were never pretested. The pretest doesn't teach the answer directly; rather, it activates relevant prior knowledge, orients attention to the kinds of distinctions and concepts that matter, and primes the brain for what to listen for in the upcoming lecture.

## In the Book

Bjork and Soderstrom tested UCLA students with pretests on three lectures about scientific reasoning. Students tanked the pretests. Then they attended the lectures a day or two later, effectively getting feedback and the correct answers. The cumulative exam two weeks after the last lecture showed that pretest-related questions scored about 10% higher than unrelated ones—proving that even failed tests boost learning. Bjork explains that the pretest provides vocabulary exposure and a preview of what kinds of distinctions are important. The student who bombs a pretest on "What are the characteristics of scientific explanations?" walks into the lecture primed to recognize relevant contrasts with belief-based reasoning. This is different from practice tests, which are typically taken after learning to assess knowledge; pretesting flips the sequence and harnesses testing's power as a *primer* for learning.

## Why It Matters

This finding inverts the conventional sequence where learning comes first, then testing measures it. It suggests that testing can *enable* learning, not just measure it—a subtle but powerful shift that means introducing questions and uncertainty before providing information can deepen understanding. For instructional design, it implies that opening a course or chapter with difficult questions, mystery, or apparent confusion isn't a bug; it's a feature that primes deeper learning of subsequent material.
