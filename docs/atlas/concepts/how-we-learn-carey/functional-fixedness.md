# Functional Fixedness

## Definition

Functional fixedness is the cognitive bias that makes us perceive an object or concept only according to its conventional use. We see a box as a container, not as a platform. A screwdriver is for screws, not as a pry bar. A doctor must be male. When objects are "fixed" this way, we literally cannot perceive them as suitable for alternative purposes, even when those alternatives would solve our problem. The brain has made an automatic, unconscious assumption that predetermines how we interpret the available information—until something forces us to reconsider.

## In the Book

Duncker's candle problem perfectly demonstrates fixedness: subjects given materials including tacks, matches, and candles in *boxes* struggle to mount candles on a wall because they see the boxes only as containers. When the boxes are emptied and sit on the table, success rates soar—the boxes are now visible as platforms. Duncker found that objects not fixed functionally are almost twice as easily reused as fixed ones. The pencil problem similarly tricks people: they assume pencils must lie flat (in a plane) because geometry is traditionally done on paper. Fixedness infects everyday life: we search drawers for scissors when keys in our pocket would open a package just as well. Mystery novels exploit it, subtly prompting readers to rule out suspects. The key insight is that fixedness happens *automatically*—we're not even aware we've made the assumption until someone or something breaks it.

## Why It Matters

Functional fixedness reveals why expertise can sometimes hinder—experts have strong, automatic assumptions about how things work. It explains why fresh perspectives solve stuck problems and why advice like "think outside the box" or "see it differently" points at something real: the box is real, and it's in our assumptions. For problem-solving and innovation, this concept is permission to question every fixed assumption about what something "is" or what role someone "plays."
