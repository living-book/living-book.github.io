# Extended Mind

## Definition

The extended mind is the philosophical insight that human cognition is not contained within the brain but distributed across the person, tools, and environment. We think partly with our hands (writing, drawing), partly with our instruments (calculators, search engines), and partly with others (conversation, collaboration). The tools we use to offload and reorganize thinking become part of the thinking process itself.

## In the Book

Thompson invokes the extended mind theory early to ground the entire book's argument. The theory counters the cultural ideal of genius—Rodin's Thinker, sitting alone, reasoning purely within the skull. In reality, humans have *always* outsourced cognition. Books amplified memory. Inexpensive paper and reliable pens let us externalize thoughts rapidly. When we do long division on paper, our eyes zip around the written digits as if they were external short-term memory; the paper is part of the cognitive act, not just a record of it.

Thompson illustrates through physicist Richard Feynman's pushback against historian Charles Weiner. Weiner called Feynman's notebooks "a record of his day-to-day work." Feynman corrected him: "It's not a record. It's the working. You have to work on paper and this is the paper." The act of writing *was* the thinking; the notebook wasn't secondary documentation.

This applies universally. As philosopher Andy Clark writes, external resources "enable us to pursue manipulations and juxtapositions of ideas and data that would quickly baffle the un-augmented brain." We are augmented creatures by nature; every new tool shapes what we can think about and how we think.

Thompson argues that three modern digital biases have intensified extension: infinite external memory (smartphones record everything), connection-making tools (search, hyperlinks reveal relationships invisible before), and explosive publishing (tools that make broadcasting trivial amplify everything we do).

## Why It Matters

The extended mind dissolves the question "Are computers making us smarter or dumber?" The question presupposes that intelligence lives inside us. But if thinking is distributed, then better tools = better thinking. It also reframes the imperative for digital literacy: we must understand how to think *with* tools, not *against* them or in spite of them. For education, it means teaching not just knowledge but fluency with the cognitive tools of the age—search, visualization, collaboration platforms. For psychology, it suggests that the boundary between self and tool is permeable; technologies aren't external to our minds but extensions of them. The deeper implication: humans have always been creatures who think through our tools; the digital age just makes that dependency visible and intensifies it.
