# Public Thinking

## Definition

Public thinking is the practice of externalizing incomplete thoughts, questions, and early ideas to an audience—sharing thinking in progress rather than polished conclusions. This visibility triggers serendipitous connections with others puzzling over the same problems and accelerates the resolution of simultaneous, independent discoveries (what sociologists call "multiples"). The key mechanism is not fame or broadcasting; it is connection among dispersed minds working on similar challenges.

## In the Book

Thompson traces this through sociological research on "multiples"—the historical pattern where major inventions and discoveries occur independently to different people within years or weeks of each other. Oxygen, sunspots, logarithms, radio, penicillin: breakthrough ideas seem to bubble up simultaneously across the globe. Sociologist Robert Merton explained that when inventive people aren't aware of each other's work, innovation slows; mathematicians waste time duplicating work their colleagues were already doing.

The Internet, Thompson argues, is now history's most powerful engine for resolving multiples at scale. His primary example is Ushahidi in Kenya. During the 2007 post-election violence, activist Ory Okolloh posted publicly about her need for a tool to map violence incidents using Google Earth. She didn't have the technical skills to build it. But by thinking out loud, she triggered connections: web developer Erik Hersman saw her post, realized his friend David Kobia (a Kenyan programmer in Alabama) could make it real. Within days, Ushahidi—"testimony" in Swahili—was live, crowdsourcing incident reports via SMS and Twitter. Within years, it became globally essential for disaster response and human rights documentation.

Thompson emphasizes that public thinking works best when people aren't competing for credit or profit. Everyday thinkers online are "thrilled to discover someone else with the same idea." Scientists and entrepreneurs, by contrast, often compete rather than collaborate once they learn others are pursuing the same concept.

## Why It Matters

Public thinking inverts the Romantic ideal of the solitary genius and reveals cognition as fundamentally social and networked. For psychology and learning, it suggests that externalizing rough ideas (not polished arguments) is a power move: it recruits collaborators, attracts criticism that sharpens thinking, and dramatically accelerates problem-solving. For society, it shows how visibility—the ability to broadcast questions and early-stage work—can unlock latent collaboration at scale. The implication: tools and norms that make thinking visible are infrastructure for collective intelligence.
