# Office as Interruption Factory

## Definition

Offices have become interruption factories that chop workdays into fragments. A busy office is like a food processor—it cuts the day into fifteen-minute segments here, ten-minute segments there, twenty minutes somewhere else, with each segment filled with a meeting, a call, or an unnecessary interruption. Meaningful work—creative, thoughtful, important work—requires extended stretches of uninterrupted time to reach flow state. The modern office makes such uninterrupted stretches nearly impossible to find.

## In the Book

The book opens with a telling observation: when asked where they go to get real work done, almost no one answers "the office." If they mention the office at all, they qualify it: "super early in the morning before anyone gets in," "late at night after everyone's left," or "on the weekend." The reason is that offices have become interruption factories. The day gets shredded into work moments—meetings, conference calls, unnecessary interruptions.

Fried and Hansson use the food processor metaphor explicitly: "A busy office is like a food processor—it chops your day into tiny bits. Fifteen minutes here, ten minutes there, twenty here, five there." Each segment contains an institutionalized unnecessary interruption.

The book contrasts this with remote work. When you work away from the office, you can settle into your own productive zone—the same work you couldn't get done at work becomes completable. The interruptions at home or a coffee shop are passive and controllable (TV, background noise), not active and human-initiated (a tap on the shoulder, a meeting request).

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes the fundamental constraint on productivity. It's not about working harder or longer—it's about protecting uninterrupted time. The office structure itself, regardless of individual effort, makes deep work architecturally impossible. This challenges the assumption that physical presence correlates with productivity and suggests that organizational effectiveness depends more on work environment design than on supervision or co-location. It applies across knowledge work: if you're doing work that requires thinking, the default office is a contraption that prevents it.
