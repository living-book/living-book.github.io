# Asking Different Questions

## Definition

Most people ask questions to confirm hypotheses they already hold, not out of genuine curiosity — and when the answer doesn't match the expectation, they often don't even register the mismatch. Asking different questions means noticing the pattern of questions you habitually ask, recognizing the mindset underneath it, and deliberately substituting a different kind of question to shift that mindset. The relationship runs both ways: your questions reveal and reinforce your mindset, and changing the question changes what you're able to see.

## In the Book

The book introduces this as the first of three "habits of mind" (alongside taking multiple perspectives and seeing systems) through Yolanda, a state child-services director in crisis after a string of child deaths, and Doug, her deputy. Stuck repeating the same questions — "Who is responsible? Where in the system is the problem?" — Yolanda finds herself getting nowhere. Doug's insight is that the questions themselves are the trap. The authors map this onto a 2x2 of mindsets (Figure 1.1): narrowing-to-decide versus opening-to-explore, crossed with threat versus opportunity, each quadrant generating characteristically different questions ("Who is at fault?" versus "What are other ways of looking at this?"). Yolanda is diagnosed as stuck in threat/narrowing — legitimate mindsets in isolation, but ones that foreclose innovative solutions to a genuinely complex problem.

The authors are explicit that this is a learnable, fast skill, not a personality trait, and that the biggest barrier is simply remembering to try it, followed by the fear that unfamiliar questions will look naive or waste time ("If I do that, won't everyone think I'm stupid?").

## Why It Matters

Any group facing a hard problem can get stuck re-running the same debate because everyone is quietly asking the same category of question. This gives a concrete lever for the stuck-ness: instead of trying to answer harder, name the mindset the current questions are protecting (threat, scarcity, judgment, narrowing) and swap in a question from a different quadrant. It converts a general prescription like "think outside the box" into a specific, repeatable diagnostic move.
