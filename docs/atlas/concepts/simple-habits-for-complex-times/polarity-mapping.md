# Polarity Mapping

## Definition

A polarity (Barry Johnson's term, adopted by the authors) is a tension between two poles that mutually create each other and can never be permanently resolved — only managed — because you cannot do without either side. Long-term versus new employees, centralized control versus local autonomy, and short-term versus long-term thinking are all examples: pick a mutually acceptable name for each side, then map, in a two-by-two, the positive contribution and the negative "shadow side" of each pole. A well-formed map has roughly equal richness in all four boxes; a lopsided map signals you haven't gathered enough perspectives yet.

## In the Book

The book introduces polarity mapping in Chapter 4 as an extension of taking multiple perspectives, applied to paradox rather than to people. It uses a government call-center case study: hold-time targets were imposed to fix slow service, employees responded by rushing callers off the phone and rationing questions, customer satisfaction fell, and call volume rose — the target-driven "fix" pushed the organization in exactly the opposite direction from the actual goal, because it collapsed a polarity into a single-sided solution. The book's running example, Yolanda's child-services agency, is diagnosed as caught in a "local autonomy versus centralized control" polarity that has already survived more than a dozen organizational reviews attempting to solve it once and for all. The authors describe the resulting failure pattern precisely: leaders swing hard toward one pole to fix a problem, fall into that pole's shadow side after a few years, then swing back — an oscillation that looks like fresh reform each time but is really "déjà vu all over again."

## Why It Matters

Most change efforts implicitly assume every organizational tension is a problem with a correct, permanent answer, and burn credibility cycling between "solutions" that each work for a while before their shadow side reasserts itself. Recognizing a tension as a polarity rather than a problem changes the target of leadership work from finding the right answer to designing ongoing, purposeful movement between two poles you can never fully leave behind — a distinction that applies anywhere resources, attention, or values are genuinely and permanently contested (stability vs. change, individual vs. collective, growth vs. profitability).
