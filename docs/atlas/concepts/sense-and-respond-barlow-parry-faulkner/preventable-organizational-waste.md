# Preventable Organizational Waste

## Definition

Preventable organizational waste is any activity within an organization that does not contribute to customer value creation. Unlike obvious manufacturing waste (scrap, downtime), organizational waste in service environments is hidden in pristine offices and disguised as normal work. It becomes "institutionalized" when organizations fail to see it as preventable, treating it instead as a permanent feature of operations. The authors define this waste specifically as work that doesn't address what truly matters to customers.

## In the Book

Barlow, Parry, and Faulkner introduce a striking finding: between 40 and 90 percent of service demand in ineffective organizations is entirely preventable. Much of this comes from the organization failing to deliver fit products or services in the first place, creating a cascade of corrective demand. They highlight that preventable work is often "hidden and processed under the illusion that it creates value." Organizations fail to see the actual nature of preventable work, treating failures as normal operations that justify investment in automation or process speed.

The authors emphasize that in their experience introducing Customer Value Principles, the journey to responsiveness requires first learning to "make waste visible." They distinguish between waste that is locked in by original organizational design and waste created through process mutation over time. Many improvement efforts, they note, simply create "neat waste"—making preventable work move faster without removing it. For example, a call center that streamlines how it handles broken-product returns is optimizing preventable demand rather than removing it by fixing what causes the breakage in the first place.

## Why It Matters

Recognizing preventable waste reframes the productivity question from "How fast can we handle volume?" to "Should we be doing this work at all?" This distinction is foundational to competitive advantage. Organizations competing on cost efficiency in preventable work face inevitable extinction as competitors either remove the waste entirely or find lower-cost labor sources. The acknowledgment that 40–90% of work may be preventable creates urgency around process redesign and reveals the true origin of organizational costs. For leadership, it shifts the focus from people performance metrics to system performance, since most "poor performance" is actually a symptom of work that the system itself creates through poor design.
