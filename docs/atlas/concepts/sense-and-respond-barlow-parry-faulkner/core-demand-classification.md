# CORE Demand Classification

## Definition

CORE is a framework that categorizes all incoming customer demand into four types. Creation demand (positive) comes from customers seeking to optimize or extend existing services. Opportunity demand (positive) is when customers ask for something not currently offered, signaling market gaps. Restorative demand (negative) occurs when the organization has failed to deliver properly, forcing corrective work. External demand (negative) originates from failures or gaps in other organizations or systems. Each type requires fundamentally different operational responses.

## In the Book

The authors demonstrate that most organizations treat all demand uniformly as "units of work to be processed efficiently," missing critical distinctions in the nature of that work. Using a telecommunications company example with 300,000 monthly inquiries, they show: 57% was restorative demand (supplier failures), 38% was external demand (customer equipment issues), and only 4% was creation demand. This profile reflects poor system design rather than customer interest.

The book establishes a "golden rule": never automate restorative demand, as it locks in frustration for both customers and staff. Instead, restorative demand signals where root causes must be fixed to eliminate the demand entirely. Opportunity demand, by contrast, should trigger innovation processes to explore new revenue streams. Creation demand should be optimized through better automation and access. External demand requires rethinking the environment or developing new solutions. The authors emphasize that changing a demand profile from 4% creation to 50%+ creation requires fundamentally redesigning the organization around customer purpose.

## Why It Matters

This framework transforms how organizations think about volume and efficiency. Instead of "process more work faster," it asks "what kind of work are we doing and should we be doing it at all?" It reveals that organizations with healthy demand profiles generate dramatically different financial outcomes than those dominated by restorative work. Understanding demand type makes visible the hidden costs of organizational failure and creates accountability for fixing root causes rather than perpetually managing symptoms. For frontline staff, this framework provides a shared language for distinguishing value-creating work from waste, enabling them to drive improvement rather than just execute transactions.
