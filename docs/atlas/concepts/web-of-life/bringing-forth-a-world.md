# Bringing Forth a World

## Definition

The Santiago theory of cognition, developed by Maturana and Varela, holds that mind is not a thing but a process — cognition — and that this process is identical to the process of life itself: "living systems are cognitive systems, and living as a process is a process of cognition." Because a system's structural changes are triggered by, but not a copy of, the environment, cognition cannot be representation of a pre-given external world; instead, by specifying which perturbations trigger which internal changes, a living system "brings forth a world." As Maturana and Varela put it: "To live is to know."

## In the Book

Chapter 11 traces the collapse of the earlier "computer model" of cognitive science — the assumption that intelligence is symbol manipulation over internal representations of an external world — citing evidence that neurons studied in awake, freely behaving animals respond to entire contexts rather than fixed features, and that biological pattern recognition is far faster and more damage-tolerant than sequential symbol processing could explain. Capra shows the Santiago theory replacing representation with structural coupling: since the environment only triggers, never specifies, an autopoietic system's structural changes, and the system itself determines which perturbations count as triggers, perception is a "continual bringing forth of a world" rather than a picture of one. He extends this radically to organisms without nervous systems: bacteria sensing chemical gradients, heat, and magnetic fields are said to bring forth "a world of warmth and coldness, of magnetic fields and chemical gradients," with Francisco Varela's phrase "embodied action" capturing how, in this view, perception and action are inseparable rather than sequential stages of an information pipeline.

## Why It Matters

This concept displaces the picture of knowing as accurately mirroring an outside reality with knowing as an ongoing, structure-dependent act of engagement — two systems with different histories of structural coupling will, by construction, bring forth different worlds from the identical environment. It is the sharpest available tool for explaining persistent, good-faith disagreement between competent observers: not as one side seeing correctly and the other distorting, but as two enactments built from different structural histories, neither a passive readout of "the facts."
