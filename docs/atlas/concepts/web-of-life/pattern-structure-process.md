# Pattern, Structure, Process

## Definition

Capra proposes that any living system can be fully described along three interdependent dimensions: its **pattern of organization** (the configuration of relationships that determines the system's essential identity — what makes it "a bicycle" or "a cell"), its **structure** (the specific physical components that embody that pattern), and its **process** (the ongoing activity that continually links pattern to structure). A pattern can be recognized only once it's embodied in a structure; in living systems, that embodiment never stops, which is why life shows the flux of process rather than the fixed assembly of a machine.

## In the Book

In Chapter 7, "A New Synthesis," Capra assembles the book's central thesis by mapping each of his three criteria onto the science he has surveyed: autopoiesis (Maturana and Varela) is the *pattern* of life, dissipative structure (Prigogine) is the *structure* of life, and cognition (Bateson, then Maturana and Varela) is the *process* of life. He illustrates the pattern/structure distinction with a bicycle: the functional relationships among frame, pedals, and wheels are its pattern, while the specific tubing, rubber, and shape of a touring versus racing bike are its structure — the same pattern can be embodied in many structures. Because a bicycle's components are fixed once assembled, it needs no third criterion; but because a living cell continually synthesizes and dissolves its own structures, life requires "process" as the link that keeps embodying pattern in structure moment to moment. Capra credits Alexander Bogdanov and the cyberneticists with first separating pattern of organization from physical structure, and treats the synthesis of these three strands — after decades in which biology, cybernetics, and complexity theory developed largely in isolation — as the book's main intellectual contribution.

## Why It Matters

The three-way split gives you a way to diagnose why an intervention on one dimension fails: changing structure (swapping people, tools, or parts) without changing pattern (the relationships that define the system) leaves the system's essential character intact, while changing pattern without sustaining process (the ongoing activity of upkeep) produces a design that never gets embodied. It is a general diagnostic for any self-maintaining system — organizational, biological, or technical — asking not just "what is it made of" but "what relationships make it what it is" and "what keeps re-making those relationships."
