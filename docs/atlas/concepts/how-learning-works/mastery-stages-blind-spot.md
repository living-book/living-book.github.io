# The Four Stages of Mastery and the Expert Blind Spot

## Definition

Skill development runs along two dimensions that don't track together: competence (how much you can actually do) and consciousness (how aware you are of what you know or don't know). Crossing them yields four stages — unconscious incompetence (don't know what you don't know), conscious incompetence (aware of the gap), conscious competence (skilled but effortful and deliberate), and unconscious competence (skilled and automatic). Competence rises roughly linearly through the four stages, but consciousness first rises then falls — so novices and experts are both relatively unaware, for opposite reasons.

## In the Book

Drawing on the Sprague and Stuart (2000) model (Figure 4.2), the book explains why deep expertise is a liability, not just an asset, for teaching. Experts organize knowledge into large conceptual "chunks," recognize patterns instantly, skip steps without noticing they've skipped them, and link specifics to deep principles — all hallmarks of unconscious competence. This produces "expert blind spot": instructors underestimate how long tasks take novices, make argumentative leaps students can't follow, and give instructions like "sauté until done" or "add spices to taste" that are perfectly clear to a chef and useless to a beginner, because the expert's knowledge of what "done" means has become invisible to the expert themselves. The chapter frames this as a structural mismatch, not a personality flaw: the same automaticity that makes someone an expert makes them a worse-than-expected teacher of novices unless they deliberately compensate for it.

## Why It Matters

This explains a specific, recurring failure of experts trying to teach or document what they know: they aren't withholding information, their fluency has made the information disappear from their own conscious awareness. It reframes "why can't they just explain it clearly" — the person who could once explain it clearly (when they were in conscious competence) is usually not the person doing the explaining now. Any domain with an expertise gap between teacher and learner — onboarding, documentation, mentorship — inherits this same asymmetry.
