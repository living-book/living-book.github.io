# The Practice-Feedback Cycle

## Definition

Practice and feedback only produce learning when they are coordinated into a cycle: goal-directed practice produces observed performance, targeted feedback evaluates that performance against the goal, and the feedback then redirects the next round of practice. Large amounts of either practice or feedback, delivered on their own or misaligned with each other, can leave performance flat.

## In the Book

The chapter's opening cases are both instructors doing large amounts of work with no payoff. Professor Cox writes extensive margin comments on three writing assignments, but each assignment is a different genre (policy brief, memo, editorial), so students never get a chance to apply his feedback to the same skill twice — the feedback and the next practice opportunity are decoupled. Professor Strait's anthropology students spend most of their practice time polishing PowerPoint visuals — the part they're already good at — rather than the argument-construction skills she actually wants, because they were never given practice specifically targeting that weaker skill. The book states the principle directly: "Goal-directed practice coupled with targeted feedback are critical to learning," depicted as a cycle in Figure 5.1 (practice → observed performance → targeted feedback → guides further practice, all embedded in learning goals). It contrasts effective feedback to a GPS rather than a simple "you are wrong": a GPS says how far off you are and what to do next, and research shows feedback works best when it is formative (aimed at improving the next attempt) rather than only summative (a final grade), and delivered while students can still act on it — illustrated by two students with the identical misconception, one who gets it back as an unexplained "C" a week later and concludes he's "totally lost," the other who gets it flagged after two practice problems and corrects it immediately.

## Why It Matters

This separates two questions people usually collapse into one: "is there enough practice/feedback" and "are they aimed at the same thing, at a time the learner can use it." A team, student, or system can be swimming in both effort and review comments and still not improve, because the review isn't reaching the same unit of practice that produced it. It's a diagnostic for stalled improvement in any coaching, code-review, or training loop: check alignment and timing before assuming the volume is insufficient.
