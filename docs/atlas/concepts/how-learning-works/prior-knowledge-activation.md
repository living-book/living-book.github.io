# Prior Knowledge Activation

## Definition

Students interpret everything new through the lens of what they already know, believe, and assume — new knowledge "sticks" better when it has prior knowledge to stick to. But prior knowledge only helps when it is accurate, sufficient, appropriate to the context, and actually activated; students do not spontaneously bring relevant knowledge to bear on a new situation just because they possess it.

## In the Book

Ambrose et al. open with recall research: people with more soccer knowledge recalled more scores from unfamiliar matches, and students given unfamiliar facts about well-known people learned them at twice the rate of students given the same facts about unfamiliar people. But possessing relevant knowledge isn't enough — it has to be triggered. In Gick and Holyoak's classic convergence-problem study, most students who had just solved one problem failed to apply the analogous solution to a second, structurally identical problem; a simple instructor prompt to "think about the second problem in relation to the first" raised the solve rate to 80 percent. Martin and Pressley found the same effect with "elaborative interrogation" — asking readers "why" questions (e.g., "Why would Ontario have been the first place baseball was played?") forced them to connect new material to Canadian history they already knew, improving retention. A statistics class redesigned around having students generate their own examples of high- and low-variability situations from their own lives outperformed a traditionally taught baseline two to one. The chapter also names the failure modes: prior knowledge can be accurate but insufficient (declarative "knowing what" without procedural "knowing how," as when Professor Won's students know what statistical tests are but can't select or execute them), or inappropriate — everyday meanings of words like "random" or "positive" distorting technical understanding, or an analogy (skeletal vs. cardiac muscle) applied past the point where it breaks down.

## Why It Matters

Any teaching, onboarding, or persuasion problem is really two problems: whether the relevant prior knowledge exists, and whether it gets activated at the moment it's needed. Treating both as the same problem is a common mistake — an audience can hold the exact fact you need them to use and still fail to apply it unless something in the moment points them back to it. This reframes "they should already know this" complaints: the fix is often not more content but a deliberate retrieval cue.
