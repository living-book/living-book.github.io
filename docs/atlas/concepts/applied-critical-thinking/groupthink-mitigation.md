# Groupthink Mitigation

## Definition

Groupthink occurs when the members of a cohesive group prioritize unanimity and harmony over realistic appraisal of alternatives. The result is defective decision-making: limited exploration of alternatives, neglect of risks, dismissal of contrary information, and self-appointed "mindguards" who shield the group from adverse views. Groupthink mitigation is the practice of deliberately introducing structural and procedural safeguards—at the organizational level and during decision-making—to preserve the group's capacity for critical thinking despite its social pressures toward conformity.

## In the Book

The handbook describes seven defects of groupthink decision-making: discussion limited to two alternatives; failure to examine objectives and values; neglect of risks in the chosen course; dismissal of initially rejected alternatives; little input from outside experts; interest only in supporting information; and failure to plan for contingencies. Symptoms include overestimation of group power/invulnerability, closed-mindedness, pressures toward uniformity, self-censorship, "mind-guards" protecting the group from bad news, and stereotyped enemy views.

Mitigation happens at two levels. Organizationally: senior leaders must set tone by encouraging objections, avoiding stating their preference up front, chartering competing teams to examine the same problem, bringing in outside expertise, and assigning devil's advocates. During decision-making: pre-commit individuals' ideas in writing before group deliberation; use anonymous feedback (e.g., weighted voting); employ structured techniques like 1-2-4-Whole Group and Brainstorming that give every member voice; and hold a "second chance" meeting where each member restates reservations after reaching consensus. The handbook emphasizes that tools like Liberating Structures intercede group dynamics that would otherwise derail good thinking.

## Why It Matters

Groups are vulnerable to conformity pressure precisely because cohesion and trust are valuable for cooperation. But those same dynamics can suppress the dissenting voice that catches the flaw before the plan runs. Groupthink mitigation decouples the preservation of team trust from the suppression of critical feedback—making it safe to challenge the group's direction without threatening belonging. This is especially critical in hierarchical, cohesive teams (military units, executive leadership groups) where informal dissent often gets silenced by respect for authority or desire to maintain relationships.
