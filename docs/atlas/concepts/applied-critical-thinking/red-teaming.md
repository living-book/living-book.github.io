# Red Teaming: Institutionalized Dissent

## Definition

Red teaming is a function that provides decision-makers with independent capability to explore alternatives in plans, operations, and concepts from the perspectives of partners, adversaries, and others. It is diagnostic, preventative, and corrective—designed to help organizations escape the patterns of failure they fall into predictably according to their own mindsets, biases, and culture. Red teaming works best behind the scenes, assisting leaders in a non-critical, helpful manner.

## In the Book

The U.S. Army's UFMCS treats red teaming as an institutional answer to organizational groupthink. A Red Team performs three types of tasks: support to operations and decision-making, critical review of existing plans, and intelligence analysis. The handbook emphasizes that effective red teaming requires top cover—permission to challenge conventional wisdom without retaliation. It is not easy work: Red Teamers must be credible communicators, able to explain why something is being challenged and offer alternative framings, not just criticism. The handbook describes red teaming as analogous to medicine: diagnostic (finding the problem), preventative (catching issues before they cause harm), and corrective (treating active failures). A standing Red Team, ad hoc team, or individual red teamers might all fulfill this function, but the critical element is independence and the organizational culture's willingness to listen when challenges arise.

The book centers red teaming in planning cycles, especially at problem-framing and operational-design stages where the team's ability to expose unstated assumptions has the most impact. Red Teams challenge facts, problem frames, and assumptions; develop targeted cultural questions; and propose alternative perspectives.

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes organizational challenge as a legitimate, structured function rather than as interpersonal conflict or disloyalty. It shifts the burden from individual heroes who "speak truth to power" to systemic mechanisms that make dissent safe and expected. Organizations that institutionalize red teaming separate the idea being challenged from the person offering the challenge—and make space for the "second voice" before decisions lock in. This applies far beyond military contexts: any organization making consequential decisions in the face of uncertainty can benefit from a practice that legitimizes testing the plan before commitment.
