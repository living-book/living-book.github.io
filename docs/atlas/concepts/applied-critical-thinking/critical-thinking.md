# Critical Thinking

## Definition

Critical thinking is a deliberate, hard process by which a thinker improves the quality of their thinking by recognizing the structures inherent in thinking, imposing intellectual standards upon them, and overcoming native egocentrism and sociocentrism. It is fundamentally about what to believe and what to do, grounded in awareness that our perceptions are constructions filtered through mental models, values, and frames we don't always recognize.

## In the Book

The handbook emphasizes that critical thinking is not a single skill but a constellation of habits and practices: awareness of what one knows and doesn't know; challenging assumptions and exploring alternatives; searching for hidden assumptions; questioning and arguing logically; adopting a skeptical state of mind while remaining open-minded; gathering and assessing relevant information; questioning one's own assumptions as rigorously as others'; considering multiple points of view; and deferring judgment until adequate reflection. The book grounds the necessity for critical thinking in several failures of human cognition. Most people operate on "cognitive autopilot"—running routines without conscious deliberation. Our brains are full of unconscious biases and mental models—simplified frameworks that help us cope with complexity but often misrepresent reality. We perceive what we expect to perceive, filtered through our frames and values. We are drawn to confirming evidence and dismiss contradicting evidence. We reason both deductively (from premises to conclusions) and inductively (from observation to probability), and both modes are prone to error when premises are unstated assumptions rather than facts. We confuse correlation with causation. We think in linear cause-and-effect chains when systems are complex and interdependent.

Critical thinking, in the handbook's view, is the counter-weight to these failures. It is a discipline—a habit that requires practice and structure, not talent. The handbook provides "Critical Thinking Traits"—a list of how critical thinkers think and act: they are habitually inquisitive, well-informed, trustful of reason, open-minded, flexible, fair-minded in evaluation, honest in facing biases, prudent in judgment, willing to reconsider, clear about issues, orderly in complex matters, diligent in seeking information, and persistent in precision.

## Why It Matters

This concept treats thinking itself as a skill and discipline, not as something everyone does equally well. It recognizes that decision-makers are not neutral observers but subjects of their own biases, frames, and values. Critical thinking is not cynicism or contrarianism—it's rigorous self-examination paired with willingness to revise conclusions in the face of evidence. For organizations, it means treating critical thinking not as something smart people naturally do, but as a capability to be built, trained, and institutionalized through tools, practices, and permission to slow down and ask questions before deciding.
