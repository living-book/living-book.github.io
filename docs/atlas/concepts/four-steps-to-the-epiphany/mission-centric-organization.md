# The Mission-Centric Organization

## Definition

As a startup grows, Blank argues it should pass through three distinct organizational stages: team-centric (the Customer Development team during Discovery, Validation, and Creation), mission-centric (Company Building, where departments form around a written, shared mission rather than a "cookie-cutter" template of Sales/Marketing/Business Development), and only later process-centric (large-company stability and repeatability). Skipping straight from founder-driven chaos to imported "big company" process — hiring experienced executives who impose order before the mission itself is validated — is, in his framing, "the beginning of the end for a promising startup."

## In the Book

Chapter 6 tells this through BetaSheet: after early visible success, the board replaced the founding CEO with an experienced pharmaceutical-industry executive, who cut the sales staff and, one by one, swapped in traditional sales, marketing, and Product Development leaders from larger companies. Sales recovered for eighteen months, but underneath, competitors and even BetaSheet's own customers were building in-house alternatives, the most innovative engineering and sales talent began leaving because "not going by the book" had become career-limiting, and infighting between Sales, Marketing, and Product Development intensified — the company shut down by its fifth year. Blank contrasts this with his own early experience as VP of Marketing at SuperMac, where department heads described their "mission" purely as their job title's daily tasks (the trade-show team's mission was "to set up trade show booths") until he rewrote the department's mission as four concrete outcome statements the whole team recited aloud — turning scattered activity into a coordinated function without yet imposing rigid process.

## Why It Matters

It offers a third option between "stay scrappy forever" and "import corporate process wholesale," which is the false binary most founders and investors default to when a startup needs to grow. The mechanism — write the shared mission down, organize departments to serve it rather than to justify their own existence, and only add rule-based process once the mission itself is proven — applies to any group scaling past the size where everyone can coordinate by proximity alone, well beyond startups.
