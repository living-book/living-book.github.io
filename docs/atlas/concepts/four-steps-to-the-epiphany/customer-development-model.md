# The Customer Development Model

## Definition

Customer Development is a four-step, iterative process — Customer Discovery, Customer Validation, Customer Creation, Company Building — that runs alongside Product Development and treats a founder's beliefs about customers, problems, and markets as hypotheses to be tested with real people, not facts to be assumed. It exists because "startups that survive the first few tough years do not follow the traditional product-centric launch model" — they invent a parallel discipline of customer learning.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 diagnoses why the standard linear Product Development model (concept → first customer ship → roll-out) fails startups: it has "no iterative loop that says, 'If our assumptions are wrong, maybe we need to rethink,'" and it assumes customer adoption follows the same fixed schedule as engineering milestones. Blank walks through named disasters — Webvan's $612.7 million deficit built by scaling infrastructure and marketing on unvalidated demand assumptions, versus Tesco's profitable UK online-grocery build-out on top of existing stores — to show the same industry producing both outcomes depending on whether customer learning preceded execution. Chapter 2 lays out the four-step model as a series of recursive circles rather than a line, each with an explicit "Iterate or Exit" checkpoint, and contrasts it directly against the Technology Life Cycle Adoption Curve (Rogers/Moore's "chasm"), arguing that curve seduces early-stage founders into fixating on execution and mainstream-market glory before they've even confirmed a market exists.

## Why It Matters

It reframes the earliest phase of any new venture — not just tech startups — as a research problem before it is an execution problem: the goal is to convert founder hypotheses into verified facts by leaving the building, rather than to build faster or market harder on an untested plan. Once you see planning documents (a business plan, an MRD, a launch calendar) as bundles of unverified guesses, the natural next move in any domain is to ask which guesses are cheapest to falsify first, and to build a process — not just a single validating conversation — around finding out.
