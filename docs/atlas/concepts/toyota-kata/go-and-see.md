# Go and See

## Definition

"Go and See" is Toyota's addition to the center of the PDCA cycle
(Figure 6-7): at every step of Plan-Do-Check-Act, someone verifies the
actual condition in person rather than relying on secondhand reports or
calculated data. Rother's summary of the underlying reasoning: "No matter
how confident you are, you must always go and see the actual condition for
yourself... because the situation is always changing as you move forward."
A single observation goes stale — "if you were to only go and see one
time... you would become progressively more removed and distant from the
real situation."

## In the Book

Chapter 6 illustrates the point with an assembly process in Portugal:
engineers had calculated from machine-capacity data exactly which
workstation was the bottleneck, but on the shop floor a completely
different workstation was the actual bottleneck. Rother uses this to draw
his distinction between facts and data — "Toyota makes a distinction
between facts and data, and prefers facts over data wherever possible" —
and notes that "the words spoken most often at Toyota may well be, 'Show
me.'" The same instinct drives the turbine-blade example earlier in the
chapter, where engineers proposed countermeasures for coating damage
before anyone had gone to directly observe the process; the engineer who
did go and watch found the real cause the others had assumed away.

## Why It Matters

Data is always a compressed, time-delayed, and interpreted stand-in for a
situation; it can be internally consistent and still describe a world that
no longer exists or never quite existed. Treating "go and see" as a
required, repeated step — not a one-time due-diligence check — is a
general corrective for any decision process that has drifted toward
managing the model of reality instead of reality itself, whether the
"process" in question is a factory floor, a codebase, a market, or an
organization.
