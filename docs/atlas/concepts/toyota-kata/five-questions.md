# The Five Questions

## Definition

Once a target condition is set, Toyota's coaches move people forward with
a fixed sequence: (1) What is the target condition? (2) What is the actual
condition now? (3) What obstacles are now preventing you from reaching the
target condition, and which one are you addressing now? (4) What is your
next step (the start of the next PDCA cycle)? (5) When can we go and see
what you have learned from taking that step? Rother calls this a
"minikata" — a small, teachable device that distills the improvement kata
down to something "accessible and usable by anyone."

## In the Book

Chapter 6 presents the five questions (Figure 6-21) as the summary
distillation of everything the chapter has built: target conditions, the
gray zone, PDCA, and go-and-see. Rother notes the questions are
cumulative — "the better you've defined the target condition, the better
you'll be able to assess the current condition," and so on down the chain
— and that before a target condition exists, questions 1 and 2 run in
reverse order. Chapter 8 shows the questions in use as the backbone of the
mentor/mentee "coaching kata": a coach asks them of a mentee at the
process, repeatedly, turning the abstract improvement kata into a concrete
habit through sheer repetition — Rother reports asking them "any time I
visit a process."

## Why It Matters

A complex practice becomes teachable only when it can be compressed into a
short, memorizable sequence that someone can run without supervision. The
five questions work because they force a strict order: you can't skip to
"what should we do" before establishing what you're aiming at and what's
actually true right now. That ordering — target, actual, obstacle, step,
checkpoint — is a domain-neutral template for turning open-ended problem
solving into a routine, applicable anywhere a coach wants a trainee to
internalize a way of thinking rather than just receive an answer.
