# Coaching Kata

## Definition

The coaching kata is Toyota's own routine for teaching the improvement
kata: a mentor assigns a mentee a purposely vague challenge, the mentee
proposes an analysis or plan, the mentor pushes back ("please think about
this some more," or simply "why?"), and the exchange repeats — "like a
game of catch" — until the mentee's understanding is sharp enough to act.
Every person at Toyota has a mentor, and that mentor has a mentor,
independent of the org chart — "the buddy system, or two-man rule, with
one buddy being the mentor."

## In the Book

Chapter 8 opens by rejecting classroom training as sufficient for
behavioral change — "explaining a different way of doing something...
will [not] be sufficient for altering behavior" — on the grounds that
people cannot objectively assess their own performance or perceive their
own habits, the same reason athletes still need a coach no matter how
skilled they become. Rother describes the mentor/mentee dialogue (Figure
8-2) as rooted in a Buddhist master/apprentice tradition, deliberately
starting vague so the mentor can "see how the mentee is approaching the
situation" before giving input, with the goal of the mentee "figuring
things out for him- or herself under guidance." He notes team leaders and
group leaders spend more than 50 percent of their time coaching this way,
that it can take roughly ten years of practice before someone is ready to
be a good mentor, and that Toyota's historical reluctance to hire senior
managers from outside stems directly from this — the routine has to be
lived, not briefed.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates two things usually bundled together: transmitting
information and changing behavior. Explaining a method can transmit
information in minutes; changing what someone actually does under
pressure requires repeated, observed practice on real problems with
feedback from someone who can see what the learner cannot see in
themselves. Any domain trying to scale a skill or a way of thinking —
not just a checklist — runs into this same wall, and the coaching kata's
answer (vague prompt, guided struggle, repeat) is a transferable template
for how to teach judgment rather than just facts.
