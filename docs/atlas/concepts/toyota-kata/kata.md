# Kata

## Definition

A kata is a repeated behavioral routine — a "way of keeping two things in
alignment or synchronization with one another" — that lets an organization
stay in harmony with dynamic, unpredictable conditions it cannot control.
Rother distinguishes kata from a principle: a principle helps you choose
between options but doesn't tell you how to proceed step by step, whereas
"a kata guides you into" concerted, repeated action. The routine is
prescribed; the content it's applied to is not.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 builds the concept from the martial-arts origin of the word — a
choreographed sequence of movements handed down from master to student —
and argues Toyota's success rests less on its tools (kanban, andon, jidoka)
than on two underlying behavior patterns: the "improvement kata" and the
"coaching kata" that teaches it (Figure 1-5). Rother lists the properties
such a method would need: it must operate at the process level (small
detail changes accumulate into big shifts), be embedded in daily work
rather than bolted on as a special initiative, be content-neutral so it
applies to any situation, rely on facts over opinion, and outlast any one
leader. He contrasts this with the "action-item list" approach documented
in Chapter 2 — the scattershot practice of listing observed problems,
prioritizing them, and assigning due dates — which he calls "unscientific
and ineffective" because it substitutes motion for a routine of inquiry.

## Why It Matters

A kata reframes management's job: instead of asking "what should we do,"
you ask "what routine should everyone practice so the right things get
discovered and done." This distinction — a trained procedural habit versus
a one-off decision or a stated principle — travels far beyond manufacturing:
any domain where conditions are too unpredictable to plan around in
advance (research, software, coaching, personal habits) benefits from
separating "the fixed procedure for finding out what to do" from "the
content of what gets done," because the former can be taught and practiced
while the latter cannot.
