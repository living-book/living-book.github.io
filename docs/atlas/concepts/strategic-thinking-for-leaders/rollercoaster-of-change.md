# The Rollercoaster of Change

## Definition

The Rollercoaster of Change is a five-stage emotional arc — shock, denial, "hang in
there," hope, rebuilding — that the book claims underlies every kind of change a
person or organization goes through, regardless of the change's content. The stages
are not optional or skippable: a leader has to move through them personally,
managing their own reaction first, before they are credible or effective at guiding
anyone else through the same arc.

## In the Book

Chapter III introduces the model as a synthesis: "This one Model integrates and
synthesizes over 20 other theories of change, all of which have this same
Rollercoaster Foundation." Each stage gets a matching leadership response: Stage 1
(shock and denial) calls for advance notice and clear expectations; Stage 2
(depression and anger) requires listening, then empathizing, and only then
explaining why the change is necessary — "this a, b, c order is key"; Stage 3 (hope
and adjustment) means clarifying each person's new role and their WIIFM ("What's In
It For Me"); Stage 4 (rebuilding) means empowering committed people and then
stepping back to monitor. Tip #8 walks the model through a first-person internal
monologue at the "Self" level — from "It's my boss's fault... it's them, not me!"
(denial) through "I do have the ability to respond... it's within my power to
respond positively or negatively" (hope) to "I am 'response-able' for my life's
destiny" (rebuilding) — showing the same arc applies to a leader's own attitude, not
only to organizational change.

## Why It Matters

Naming the stages turns a confusing, demoralizing slide into anger or resistance
into an expected, temporary phase of a known sequence rather than a sign that the
change is failing or that people are being unreasonable. It also flips the usual
leadership instinct to explain the rationale for a change immediately: the model
insists listening and empathizing have to come first, because a person in shock or
denial cannot absorb a rationale yet. Any change effort — technological,
organizational, personal — can be diagnosed by asking which stage people are
actually in, rather than which stage the plan assumes they should be in.
