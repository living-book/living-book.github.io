# Life's Task

## Definition

Every person is born with a "primal uniqueness" — a specific mix of inclinations, tastes,
and latent skills that "wants to grow, transform itself, and flower to its full potential."
Greene calls the work that expresses this uniqueness a person's Life's Task: not a goal
chosen from outside, but a destiny recovered from inside. Realizing it happens in three
stages — reconnecting with the inclination, choosing or redirecting a career path around
it, and expanding the very idea of "work" so it stops being a compartment separate from
meaning.

## In the Book

Greene opens the book's first strategic section with this concept and returns to it as the
foundation everything else in the book stands on: apprenticeship, mentors, and mastery only
work if aimed at the right target. The central case is Leonardo da Vinci's restless early
drifting between apprenticed trades before his visual, mechanical fascinations converged
into a life's work. Greene generalizes this into named strategies with their own examples:
the "primal inclination strategy" (Einstein's fascination with the compass needle as a
child prefigures his lifelong pull toward invisible forces; similarly Marie Curie, Ingmar
Bergman, Martha Graham), the "Darwinian strategy" of finding an unoccupied niche that fits
one's temperament, the "rebellion strategy" against a parent's chosen path (Mozart), and the
"adaptation strategy" of redirecting a task when the original path closes (boxing trainer
Freddie Roach). Greene is explicit that what blocks the Life's Task is a counterforce —
social pressure to conform, parents steering toward a "lucrative and comfortable" career —
that can sever a person from their own uniqueness so completely they no longer recognize it.

## Why It Matters

The concept reframes career choice as a diagnostic problem rather than a preference
problem: the right vocation already exists in a person's biography as a pattern of
childhood fascinations, and the task is excavation, not invention. Applied outside
individual careers, it names a more general failure mode — an organization, a product
line, or a research program built to satisfy an external pressure (investor expectation,
parental script, market fashion) rather than its own actual comparative advantage will
underperform even with equal effort, because energy and attention naturally concentrate
where the underlying fit is real.
