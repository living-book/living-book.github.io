# The Naive Perspective

## Definition

Greene names the "Naïve Perspective" as a distorting lens formed in childhood, when a
person's survival depended on how parents and other emotionally significant figures felt
about them — a habit of viewing others through the question "how does this implicate me?"
rather than "what does this person actually want?" Carried unexamined into adulthood, it
causes people to misread colleagues' motives, project their own feelings onto bosses and
mentors, and swing between idealizing and demonizing the people around them. Social
intelligence, in Greene's definition, is nothing more than the deliberate process of
discarding this lens and replacing it with realistic, outward-focused observation.

## In the Book

The chapter's central case is Benjamin Franklin, who as a charming, favored younger child
in a large family learned to get his way through wit and charm — a strategy Greene argues
was rooted in narcissism, not real understanding of other people, and one that left Franklin
vulnerable to being exploited until he confronted his own naivety and began deliberately
studying people from the outside in. Greene pairs the Naive Perspective with a companion
taxonomy, the "Seven Deadly Realities" — Envy, Conformism, Rigidity, Self-obsessiveness,
Laziness, Flightiness, and Passive Aggression — universal, largely disguised human traits
that people rarely display openly but that surface destructively once triggered; envy in
particular is flagged by the tell that "people who praise you too much or who become overly
friendly in the first stages of knowing you are often envious." Greene is explicit that the
antidote to the Naive Perspective is not cynicism but "supreme acceptance": treating people's
dark tendencies as a fact of the species to be worked around, not a personal betrayal to
react to emotionally.

## Why It Matters

The concept locates a specific failure mode in professional relationships — reading a
colleague's or superior's behavior through the emotional stakes it carries for you rather
than through what actually motivates them — and gives it a developmental origin, which
makes it something to catch and correct rather than a fixed personality trait. It applies
anywhere accurate modeling of other people's incentives determines outcomes: negotiation,
management, coalition-building, and reading adversarial behavior in any competitive
environment.
