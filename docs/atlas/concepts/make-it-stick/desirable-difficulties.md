# Desirable Difficulties

## Definition

Coined by psychologists Elizabeth and Robert Bjork, "desirable difficulties"
are impediments introduced into practice — spacing sessions apart,
interleaving different skills, varying the conditions of practice, requiring
retrieval instead of review — that slow performance and increase effort
*during* learning but produce stronger, more precise, more enduring learning
afterward. The word "desirable" is doing real work: these difficulties only
help up to the point a learner has enough foundational knowledge to
productively struggle; imposed on a true beginner with nothing to retrieve,
the same friction just obstructs.

## In the Book

Chapter 4, "Embrace Difficulties," is framed by the story of Marine Corps
logistics officer Mia Blundetto, who had a lifelong fear of falling but was
assigned to command an air-delivery platoon requiring her to jump from
aircraft — a difficulty she chose to embrace rather than avoid, which the
book uses as an emotional register for the chapter's cognitive claim: the
same kind of friction that makes jump training frightening and effortful is
what the earlier chapters showed made spaced, interleaved, and
retrieval-based practice more effective than their easier alternatives. This
chapter names and unifies those earlier findings under one umbrella term and
extends it to generation (attempting to solve a problem or produce an answer
before being shown the solution) and varied practice conditions, arguing that
short-term impediment and long-term strength are correlated across all of
them.

## Why It Matters

It reframes "hard" as a signal to interpret rather than avoid: difficulty
that arises from *the right kind of friction* (retrieval, spacing,
variation) is a sign learning is being consolidated, not a sign the method is
failing. It also draws the boundary that keeps the idea from becoming a
platitude — difficulty helps only when the learner has the foundation to
struggle productively; the same obstacle imposed too early is just an
obstacle. Any process that trades a comfortable, fast signal now for a
stronger outcome later — training, hiring, product design, negotiation — can
be examined for which of its frictions are desirable and which are merely
costly.
