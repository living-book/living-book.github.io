# Structure Building

## Definition

Psychologists call it "rule learning" and "structure building": the habit of
extracting the underlying principle behind a specific experience — rather
than banking the experience itself as an isolated fact — and linking the
resulting principles into a connected structure that can be applied to new,
different situations later. Learners who single out the salient, generative
lessons from what they encounter and connect those lessons to each other
outperform learners who take experiences at face value and fail to infer
anything transferable from them.

## In the Book

Chapter 6, "Get Beyond Learning Styles," uses this concept to displace the
popular "learning styles" theory (that people learn best when instruction
matches a fixed visual/auditory/kinesthetic preference), which the book
states is not supported by empirical research. In its place, the chapter
identifies structure building as a real and consequential difference between
learners. It is illustrated at length through Bruce Hendry, a self-made
investor who, from a string of disconnected ventures — a childhood firework
resale scheme, building and selling a house, managing a rental fourplex,
selling microfilm and then stocks — repeatedly extracted general rules ("high
demand and short supply," "you have no control of the downside") and
assembled them into a structure of investing principles he later applied to
size up complex, unfamiliar opportunities. The chapter frames this as a
skill that can be taught and practiced, not a fixed trait — closely tied to
interleaved, varied practice, which the book shows earlier is what builds
the capacity to detect what's common across superficially different
problems.

## Why It Matters

It distinguishes learning that stays trapped in the specific case from
learning that generalizes. Someone who "takes charge of their own education"
by relentlessly asking what a given experience teaches beyond itself
compounds knowledge across unrelated ventures, while someone who treats each
experience as a one-off never builds a transferable structure. This matters
anywhere expertise needs to transfer to unfamiliar situations — investing,
diagnosis, design, strategy — because the raw volume of experience matters
far less than whether the underlying rules were ever extracted from it.
