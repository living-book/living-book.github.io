# Spaced Practice

## Definition

Distributing study or practice sessions across time — with gaps that allow
some forgetting to set in between them — produces stronger, longer-lasting
learning than massing the same amount of practice into one concentrated
block (cramming). The mechanism is memory consolidation: embedding new
learning durably takes hours to days of neural processing, and the added
effort of retrieving partly-forgotten material re-triggers and strengthens
that consolidation. Massed practice feels more productive because its gains
are immediate and visible; spaced practice feels less productive because the
forgetting it requires you to work through is also visible.

## In the Book

Chapter 3, "Mix Up Your Practice," presents a study of thirty-eight surgical
residents learning microsurgery — reattaching severed vessels — across four
short lessons. Half completed all four lessons in a single day (the normal
in-service schedule); the other half completed the same four lessons spaced
a week apart. Tested a month later on live rats, the spaced group beat the
massed group on every measure: elapsed time, number of hand movements, and
surgical success. Sixteen percent of the massed-practice residents damaged
the rats' vessels beyond repair and could not complete the surgery. The book
uses this to debunk what it calls the "myth of massed practice" — the
pervasive belief, held by teachers, athletes, and corporate trainers alike,
that focused repetition until a skill is "nailed" is the most effective way
to learn it.

## Why It Matters

It exposes a mismatch between what practice *feels* like while it's
happening and what it *produces* later: massed practice generates fast,
satisfying, visible improvement that evaporates, while spaced practice
generates slow, effortful, unsatisfying improvement that lasts. Anywhere
performance is being built for later use rather than for an immediate
demonstration — training, skill acquisition, habit formation, even
organizational rollout of new practices — the same tradeoff between visible
short-term gains and durable long-term capability applies.
