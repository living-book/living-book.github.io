# Interleaving

## Definition

Practicing several distinct but related skills, problem types, or categories
in mixed, unpredictable sequence — rather than drilling one type
repeatedly before moving to the next (blocked or "massed" practice) — builds
a different and more valuable skill: recognizing *which* solution or
approach a given situation calls for. Interleaved practice performs worse
during the practice session itself but produces better performance later, on
new, unlabeled problems.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 opens the idea with a study of eight-year-olds tossing beanbags
into buckets: children who practiced on buckets two and four feet away (never
three feet) outperformed children who drilled only on the three-foot bucket
when both groups were later tested on the three-foot target. Chapter 3
develops the mechanism with a study of college students learning to compute
the volumes of four obscure geometric solids (wedge, spheroid, spherical
cone, half cone). Students who practiced problems blocked by type scored 89
percent correct during practice versus 60 percent for students given the
same problems in mixed order — but on a test a week later, the interleaved
group outperformed the blocked group substantially. The book also cites
interleaved practice at identifying bird species or painters' styles as
building the capacity to extract what distinguishes categories, not just
memorize examples of each.

## Why It Matters

Blocked practice optimizes for the feeling of fluency during the exercise —
each repetition is easier than the last because context doesn't change — but
that fluency is an artifact of predictability, not of understanding. Real-world
problems rarely arrive pre-sorted by type; the skill that transfers is
recognizing which of several tools a novel, unlabeled situation calls for.
Wherever the true task is later discrimination among options rather than
execution of one known routine, training in mixed, unpredictable order builds
the capability that blocked training only appears to.
