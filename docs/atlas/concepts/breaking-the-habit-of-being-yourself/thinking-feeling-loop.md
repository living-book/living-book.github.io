# The Thinking-Feeling Loop

## Definition

In the Book's model, thoughts and feelings are neurochemically linked in a feedback loop. When you think certain thoughts, your brain releases neurotransmitters and neuropeptides that trigger hormonal responses in the body. Once you feel the way you were thinking, your body sends signals back to your brain, which then generates more thoughts that match the bodily feeling—creating a self-reinforcing cycle Dispenza calls a "state of being."

## In the Book

Dispenza describes the mechanism through three chemical messengers: neurotransmitters (chemical signals from the brain), neuropeptides (bridges between brain and body that trigger hormones), and hormones (chemicals in the body that produce feelings). He gives the example of thinking about a confrontation with your teenager: as you think angry thoughts, your brain fires specific neural circuits, releasing neurotransmitters that trigger neuropeptides to signal your adrenal glands to release adrenaline and cortisol. Now you feel angry. But the moment your body begins feeling riled up, it sends feedback to the brain, causing you to think angrier thoughts, which produce more stress chemicals—a feedback loop.

The book argues this cycle becomes problematic when repeated thousands of times. After decades of the same thinking-feeling pattern, the body "memorizes" that state of being. Dispenza claims that by midlife, about 95 percent of behavior is subconscious habit controlled by these memorized emotional states, with the conscious mind accounting for only 5 percent.

## Why It Matters

Understanding this loop reveals why change is hard: you cannot think yourself out of an emotion because your body is already signaling the brain to generate matching thoughts. To break the cycle requires interrupting it deliberately—observing it without acting from it, or shifting your internal state through attention and intention rather than waiting for external circumstances to change.
