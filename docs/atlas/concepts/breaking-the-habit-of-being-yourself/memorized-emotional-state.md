# Memorized Emotional State

## Definition

The body develops a "memory" of emotional states through repetition. When you think and feel the same way thousands of times over decades, the neural circuits involved fire together so often that they become hardwired. Dispenza calls this Hebb's law in action: "nerve cells that fire together, wire together." Eventually the emotional state becomes automatic and unconscious—your body "remembers" how to feel that way without your brain's conscious involvement.

## In the Book

Dispenza illustrates this through an example of someone who has mentally rehearsed suffering for 20 years. The person no longer needs to think about the original painful event; the body has been chemically conditioned to automatically produce the feelings of suffering. When the person tries to change, the body "talks back" to the brain: *You don't have the will or persistence to do what I've been doing. I've modified my receptor sites over the years. I'm influencing you more than you know.*

The book emphasizes that the emotions are so deeply practiced—rehearsed internally thousands of times—that the body's chemical state becomes independent of current circumstances. Someone carrying grief or betrayal may physically relive that emotional chemistry 24/7 without realizing they are voluntarily perpetuating it. Dispenza claims that mastering any emotion (whether joy or suffering) requires the same neurochemical conditioning: practicing the feeling so repeatedly it becomes second nature, independent of external triggers.

## Why It Matters

This concept reveals that emotions feel unchangeable not because they are biologically fixed, but because they are highly practiced habits of the nervous system. It implies that changing your emotional baseline is possible through deliberate practice in a new direction—but requires understanding that you are fighting an opponent (the habituated body) that has had years of training.
