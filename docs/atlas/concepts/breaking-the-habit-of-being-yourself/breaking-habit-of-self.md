# Breaking the Habit of Being Yourself

## Definition

The central claim of the book is that most people are imprisoned in unconscious habit patterns—automated identities and emotional states—that were formed early in life and have been rehearsed thousands of times. To change, one must "break the habit of being yourself": stop being your old identity, observe it without judgment, release the memorized emotions attached to it, and consciously cultivate a new identity through deliberate practice (primarily meditation).

## In the Book

Dispenza argues that by midlife, the majority of a person's identity is subconsciously programmed. A psychologist cited in the text claims that by the mid-30s, personality and habits are completely formed. The person then repeats the same patterns unconsciously: same thoughts, same feelings, same behaviors, same outcomes. This is why external circumstances tend to reproduce themselves—the person's consciousness has not changed, so they attract and create the same situations.

Breaking the habit requires several steps. First, become aware of the old self through metacognitive observation. Second, decide consciously that you no longer want to be that person—admit and declare your desire to change. Third, observe and become familiar with the old patterns until they lose their power (what Dispenza calls "unlearning"). Fourth, imagine and mentally rehearse a new way of being until the new state becomes as automatic and familiar as the old one.

The book frames this not as self-improvement but as self-transcendence. You cannot become a new person while holding the identity of the old one. Real change requires that you step outside your sense of self altogether—what Dispenza calls becoming "nobody, no thing, no time"—and reorganize yourself from that empty, creative space.

The book claims evidence for this possibility: Dispenza's workshops have produced what he calls "unexplainable healings" and "spontaneous remissions" when people changed their internal states. His core assertion is that consciousness and emotion directly affect the body and external circumstances through mechanisms he describes as quantum physics, epigenetics, and neuroscience.

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes personal transformation as possible and accessible to anyone willing to undertake the internal work. It suggests that your current identity is not your destiny but a habit you learned and therefore can unlearn. It also places responsibility for change squarely on your own consciousness rather than on external circumstances.
