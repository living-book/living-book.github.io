# Elevated Emotion

## Definition

Dispenza distinguishes between "low-frequency" emotions associated with survival mode (anger, fear, shame, guilt, victimization) and "elevated" or "high-frequency" emotions associated with creation mode (gratitude, love, compassion, joy, peace, wholeness). Elevated emotions correspond to a different neurobiological state—the parasympathetic nervous system is dominant, the body is in coherence, immune function is strong, and the person is open to novel possibilities and connection.

## In the Book

The book frames elevated emotions not as arbitrary or less "real" than negative emotions but as distinct physiological states with specific neurochemical and genetic signatures. When you are in gratitude, compassion, or love, your heart coherence increases, your immune function improves, your stress hormones decrease, and different genes are activated than when you are in fear or anger.

Dispenza describes a crucial insight: when you stop "being" the old identity (bound to survival emotions), the emotional energy that fed that identity becomes available to move elsewhere. That energy, he writes, "transmutes" into higher-frequency emotion—what you experience as joy, expanded awareness, or love. This is not a replacement imposed from outside but a liberation of energy that was previously bound up in maintaining the old identity.

The book also argues that you can "master" elevated emotions just as easily as people master suffering. If someone can repeatedly practice the chemistry of suffering until it becomes automatic, the same nervous system can practice the chemistry of joy until joy becomes ordinary and unconditional. The key is repeated internal rehearsal—thinking, feeling, and imagining from that emotional state until it memorizes into a new state of being.

## Why It Matters

This concept suggests that emotional states are not merely psychological—they are embodied, measurable, and have direct effects on health and what the book calls your "electromagnetic broadcast" into the world. It implies that cultivating elevated emotions is not self-indulgence but a neurobiological choice with practical consequences.
