# Metacognition

## Definition

Metacognition—literally, thinking about thinking—is the ability to step back from your own mind and observe it as if from outside. In Dispenza's framework, this is the frontal lobe's first vital function for change. By becoming self-aware enough to notice your own automatic thoughts, feelings, and behaviors, you create a space between the observer (conscious you) and the observed (the old habits). From that space, you gain the power to choose differently.

## In the Book

The book frames metacognition as the first task when breaking the habit of being yourself. Dispenza explains that by the time most people reach their mid-30s, 95 percent of their behavior is subconscious programming running automatically. These programs operate "below the level of conscious awareness, much like automatic software programs." To change them, you must first make the unconscious conscious.

Dispenza uses athletic examples: golfers and swimmers change their technique by watching videotapes of themselves so they can see what the old form looks like and what the new form should look like. Similarly, you must observe your own unconscious thoughts, reflexive behaviors, and automatic emotional reactions "under observation to determine if they are true and whether you want to continue to endorse them with your energy."

The book argues that as you develop metacognitive skill—becoming familiar with all aspects of your old self—you become more conscious, and the act of observing the old pattern neurologically separates you from it. When you consciously inhibit a habitual state, "nerve cells that no longer fire together, no longer wire together," and you prune away the neural hardware of the old self.

## Why It Matters

Metacognition is the gateway to change. Without the ability to observe your own patterns, you remain trapped inside them. Once you develop metacognitive awareness, you are no longer passively identified with your habits—you are the conscious witness to them, which is the first step toward rewiring them.
