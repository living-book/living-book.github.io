# The Availability Heuristic

## Definition

The availability heuristic is "the process of judging frequency by 'the ease with which instances come to mind.'" It is a substitution heuristic: faced with the hard question "how frequent is this category?", System 1 answers the easier question "how easily can I retrieve examples?" Kahneman and Tversky's key discovery was that the number of instances retrieved doesn't even need to be large or the retrieval conscious — an impression of ease is enough, as demonstrated by tasks like judging at a glance which of two letter sets could form more words.

## In the Book

Kahneman developed the heuristic with Amos Tversky and Paul Slovic during their 1971–72 year in Eugene, Oregon, originally asking what people actually do when estimating frequencies like "people who divorce after 60." He lists concrete sources of availability bias: salient events (Hollywood divorces, political sex scandals) get overestimated because they're easy to recall; a single dramatic event (a plane crash, a burning car on the roadside) temporarily inflates perceived risk in that category; and personal experience is weighted far more heavily than statistics or others' accounts. A well-known study he cites asked spouses to estimate their own percentage contribution to household chores like tidying and taking out the garbage — each partner's own actions were simply more available in memory, inflating self-estimates on both sides.

## Why It Matters

The availability heuristic explains a specific and consequential kind of miscalibration: risk perception distorted by media coverage and memorability rather than base rates — people fear flying more right after a covered crash, fear crime more after a vivid local incident, and misjudge relative danger across categories that get uneven media attention. It generalizes to any estimation task built on "what comes to mind" rather than systematic counting — performance reviews weighted by the most memorable incident, project retrospectives dominated by the most recent failure — and points to the corrective: deliberately seek base rates and systematic counts instead of trusting the fluency of recall.
