# The Peak-End Rule and the Two Selves

## Definition

Kahneman distinguishes the experiencing self, which answers "does it hurt now?", from the remembering self, which answers "how was it, on the whole?" — and shows the remembering self is systematically biased in two ways: duration neglect (how long an experience lasted has almost no effect on how it's remembered) and the peak-end rule (the remembered evaluation is well predicted by the average of the worst moment and the final moment, regardless of everything in between). Because "memories are all we get to keep from our experience of living," it is the remembering self's distorted account — not the experiencing self's real-time record — that we consult when we decide, which Kahneman calls "the tyranny of the remembering self."

## In the Book

The grounding study, run with physician Don Redelmeier, tracked two colonoscopy patients who rated momentary pain every 60 seconds: patient A's procedure lasted 8 minutes and ended at pain level 7; patient B's lasted 24 minutes (three times longer, objectively more total suffering) but ended at pain level 1. Patient A remembered the procedure as worse, exactly as the peak-end average predicts (7.5 vs. 4.5), even though duration and total suffering both favored B having the worse memory. Kahneman follows this with the cold-hand (cold-pressor) experiment: participants held a hand in painfully cold water for 60 seconds, or for 90 seconds where the last 30 seconds were slightly less cold — objectively more total pain — and when offered a choice of which to repeat, 80% chose the longer trial, voluntarily signing up for more pain because it was remembered as less bad. He also relates a listener's account of a symphony ruined, in his memory, by a scratch near the end — even though "the experience was not actually ruined, only the memory of it," 40 minutes of musical enjoyment notwithstanding.

## Why It Matters

The peak-end rule and duration neglect matter anywhere a designed experience will be judged retrospectively rather than lived through in real time: a customer service call, a hospital stay, a vacation, a course, an onboarding flow. It gives a concrete, testable design lever — worry less about shortening an unpleasant experience and more about how it peaks and how it ends — while flagging a real ethical tension, since optimizing for the remembering self's verdict can mean deliberately choosing a design that involves more total lived suffering (or less total lived pleasure) than an alternative.
