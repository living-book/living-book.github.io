# The Craving Brain

## Definition

A cue-routine-reward sequence, on its own, is just conditioning — it doesn't yet explain why habits feel compulsive. What converts a loop into an automatic habit is craving: the brain starts anticipating the reward as soon as it registers the cue, and that anticipation itself becomes an uncomfortable, motivating state if left unsatisfied. The craving, not the reward, is what actually drives the routine. This is why simply removing a reward doesn't kill a habit and why habits persist even when their original payoff stops feeling good.

## In the Book

Duhigg grounds the mechanism in neuroscientist Wolfram Schultz's monkey experiments: a monkey named Julio learned that a shape on a screen preceded a lever-pull that delivered blackberry juice. Once the habit was fully learned, Schultz began withholding or delaying the juice — and recorded a distinct neurological signature of craving and frustration firing the instant the cue appeared, well before any routine was even executed. Monkeys with a fully formed habit ignored open doors and food offered nearby, glued to the lever by craving alone. Duhigg's central human case is Claude Hopkins's early-1900s campaign to sell Pepsodent toothpaste: almost nobody brushed their teeth in America at the time, so Hopkins didn't sell dental health — he engineered a craving, by having his ads describe a filmy coating on teeth that only brushing removed, giving people a tingling, minty sensation immediately after each use that they came to crave whether or not it improved dental hygiene. The chapter also cites Cinnabon's deliberate practice of locating stores away from other food stalls, so the smell of cinnamon travels unobstructed down mall corridors and builds a craving before a shopper even sees the store.

## Why It Matters

This concept locates the actual lever of habit formation: not the reward itself, but the anticipation of it, which means new habits can be engineered (as marketers do) or diagnosed and interrupted (as in addiction treatment) by manipulating what the brain has learned to expect from a given cue. It explains the felt experience of compulsion — why an unmet habitual urge produces real discomfort rather than mere absence of a bonus — and why habit design in any domain (product, workplace ritual, personal routine) succeeds or fails based on whether it builds a genuine anticipatory craving around the cue, not just a nominally attractive reward at the end.
