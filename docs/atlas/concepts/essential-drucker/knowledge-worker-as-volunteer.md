# The Knowledge Worker as Volunteer

## Definition

Knowledge workers are formally full-time employees, but functionally they must be managed as if they were volunteers: they are paid, but they "own their means of production" — their knowledge — and have the mobility to walk away with it. Because money satisfaction is mainly a "hygiene factor" that prevents demotivation rather than a driver of performance, what actually motivates knowledge workers is what motivates unpaid volunteers: challenge, belief in the organization's mission, continual training, and visible results.

## In the Book

In "The Dimensions of Management," Drucker builds this from the mismatched-competence problem inside organizations: a hospital administrator cannot do the clinical lab technician's job, and a marketing vice president who rose through sales cannot tell market-research specialists how to do market research, yet remains formally responsible for their performance. He likens this relationship to a conductor and an orchestra's instrumentalists — the conductor cannot play the tuba, but the orchestra depends on the conductor for the score, the standards, and the direction, and "can easily sabotage even the ablest" one. From this he draws the volunteer analogy directly, citing Frederick Herzberg's 1959 finding that money dissatisfaction demotivates sharply while money satisfaction is merely a hygiene factor, and concludes that "employees" increasingly have to be managed as "partners" — a term whose defining feature is that all partners are equal.

## Why It Matters

This identifies a structural shift in leverage: when the worker owns the actual asset (knowledge, expertise, reputation) rather than renting it to the employer for a wage, traditional command-and-control management loses its grip because the person can simply take the asset elsewhere. It generalizes to any relationship where the party with formal authority does not hold the party with real expertise's leverage — open-source maintainers, freelance specialists, academic researchers inside institutions — and explains why mission and autonomy substitute for hierarchy as the operative motivational lever in those settings.
