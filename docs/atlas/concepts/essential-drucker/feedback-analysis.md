# Feedback Analysis

## Definition

Most people believe they know their own strengths and are usually wrong. The only reliable corrective Drucker offers is feedback analysis: whenever you make a key decision or take a key action, write down what you expect will happen, then check the actual result against that written expectation nine to twelve months later. Done consistently over two or three years, it reveals where your strengths genuinely lie, what habits waste them, where you are merely competent, and where you have no strength at all.

## In the Book

Chapter 15 situates this inside a larger claim: knowledge workers will increasingly outlive their employers (a fifty-year working life against a thirty-year average corporate lifespan) and so must learn to manage themselves rather than be managed into a fixed career track, the way "the peasant's son became a peasant" once required no self-knowledge at all. Drucker reports having run feedback analysis on himself for fifteen to twenty years and being surprised every single time, and states everyone who does it reports the same. From it he draws several specific action conclusions beyond "know your strengths": concentrate resources where the analysis shows real strength; work to convert competence into strength through targeted skill-building; watch for intellectual arrogance — contempt for knowledge outside one's specialty — as a disabling and detectable blind spot; and fix the "bad manners" that quietly undermine otherwise strong performers, since manners are "the lubricating oil" of an organization.

## Why It Matters

This turns self-knowledge from an introspective guess into a measurement procedure with a built-in error-correction cycle — written prediction, followed by a comparison against an actual outcome, on a discipline of expectation-vs-result. That mechanism is domain-neutral: it is the same structure as a forecaster keeping a calibration record, or a scientist keeping a lab notebook of predicted versus observed results, applied to the question of where a person's abilities actually lie rather than where they assume they lie.
