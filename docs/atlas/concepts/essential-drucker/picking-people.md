# Picking People Is a Discipline, Not a Gift

## Definition

Executives typically bat only .333 on promotion and staffing decisions — one-third right, one-third barely adequate, one-third outright failures — a performance no other area of management would tolerate. Drucker argues this is fixable: people decisions can approach perfection not through a rare gift for judging character, but through a deliberate, repeatable process.

## In the Book

Chapter 9 grounds the claim in two contrasting exemplars: General George C. Marshall, who personally chose every general officer for World War II from an overage, untested peacetime army and produced "the largest corps of competent general officers any army has ever had," and Alfred P. Sloan Jr., who personally placed every General Motors executive down to master mechanics for four decades with a near-flawless record. Drucker extracts what these very different men shared: four basic principles (mistakes in placement are the placer's fault, not the placed person's; subordinates have "a right to competent command"; people decisions are the highest-leverage decisions an executive makes; never give a stretch assignment to an unproven newcomer) and five decision steps (think through the assignment itself, since job descriptions can go decades unrevised; look at several qualified candidates, not one; scrutinize candidates against the assignment's actual demands rather than personal comfort; discuss candidates with people who have worked with them; confirm the appointee understands the new job's real requirements).

## Why It Matters

This reframes a decision usually treated as an art (having "an eye for talent") as a process with identifiable steps whose omission predictably produces failure — useful anywhere selection decisions are made under uncertainty about a person's future performance in a role they haven't yet held (hiring, board appointments, delegating a project to a junior team member). It also isolates a specific accountability rule — a bad placement is the placer's mistake, not the placed person's failure — that changes who is expected to learn from a bad hire.
