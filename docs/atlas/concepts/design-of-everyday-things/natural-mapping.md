# Natural Mapping

## Definition

Mapping is the relationship between controls and their effects. A natural mapping exploits spatial analogy and cultural convention — turning a steering wheel clockwise turns the car right, moving a lever up raises volume — so the correct action is obvious rather than memorized. Poor mapping forces people to learn and recall an arbitrary correspondence, and recall fails exactly when it matters most.

## In the Book

Chapter 3's central case is the four-burner kitchen stove (Figure 3.2): with controls arranged in a single line and burners arranged in a rectangle, there is no way to infer which knob operates which burner without reading labels, and Norman notes all four possible wrong-mapping layouts are actually sold on commercial stoves. He shows the fix is purely geometric — stagger the burners to match a linear control layout, or arrange the controls in the same rectangle as the burners — after which "no labels would be needed. Learning would be easy; errors would be reduced." He deliberately reframes this as a design failure rather than a user failure: people blame themselves for turning on the wrong burner, but "it is not their fault... even as simple a device as the everyday kitchen stove is frequently badly designed, in a way that guarantees the errors." The same idea appears in Chapter 1 as one of the tools (alongside signifiers and constraints) that bridges the Gulf of Execution, and again with car-seat and light-switch panel examples where physical layout mirrors the space being controlled.

## Why It Matters

Natural mapping converts a memory problem into a perception problem: instead of requiring someone to learn and recall an arbitrary rule, you let them read the answer directly off the spatial arrangement in front of them. It generalizes to any system where an operator must translate an intention into one of several controls — dashboards, control panels, org charts mirroring communication paths, even the layout of a form mirroring the sequence of a process. When errors cluster around "people keep hitting the wrong one," the mapping between controls and outcomes is usually the place to look before blaming training or attention.
