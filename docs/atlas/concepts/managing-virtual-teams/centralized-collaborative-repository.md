# Centralized Collaborative Repository

## Definition

Virtual teams require a centralized, accessible-anytime repository for project information—a wiki, intranet, or team portal where everyone can access schedules, project plans, decision records, and collaborative documents without needing to email copies back and forth or wait for synchronous meetings. This repository becomes the source of truth and enables asynchronous, timezone-independent work.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 recommends: "Provide a centralized repository for project information. If you have team members in Asia, Europe, the South Pacific, and North America, you have maybe an hour or two of overlap each day. Place project-related information on an intranet site (also called a 'team room') where everyone can access it 24/7." The book notes that this repository should "incorporate other collaboration technologies, such as instant messaging, wikis, and blogs."

Chapter 3 discusses the practical problem with email distribution of documents: "When you email a document, you are actually creating a copy of the document for each recipient. Once people receive the document, you cannot be sure how they will store it or whether or not they keep it updated." A wiki or intranet repository solves this by hosting a single source of truth. The book notes that "most wikis and blogs have a file repository, archive, or gallery system of some kind. Team members can list the files and download copies as needed, but the original remains available to all on the wiki."

Chapter 4 describes the sample project (the book's own authorship) where "all three authors have full wiki permissions to read, write, and edit. We granted our publisher read-only rights so that he could view our progress whenever he wanted to. We also set the wiki up to send notifications to all of us when any pages were changed." This enabled asynchronous collaboration where each author could make progress without waiting for a meeting.

Chapter 17 on wikis details how wikis function as this repository: they maintain revision history, allow multiple people to edit the same page, support file galleries, and provide search and notification features that turn distributed information into shared knowledge.

## Why It Matters

This concept solves a fundamental problem of distributed work: without a central repository, team members either live in their email inboxes managing versions, or they waste synchronous meeting time on status sharing and information transfer that could happen asynchronously. The repository also creates an audit trail—team members can see what was decided when and by whom. It supports async work across time zones by eliminating the need to wait for a synchronous meeting to access information or make progress on work. The psychological shift is also important: moving from "check your email for the latest document" to "go to the repository for the current version" changes how teams think about information flow.
