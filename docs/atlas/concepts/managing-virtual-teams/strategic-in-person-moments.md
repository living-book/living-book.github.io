# Strategic In-Person Moments

## Definition

Virtual teams should budget for critical in-person meetings at project start and periodic intervals throughout the project lifecycle, not because they want to, but because the upfront investment in synchronous, colocated interaction creates shared context, relationship, and trust that enables more efficient asynchronous work afterward. The book emphasizes this investment should happen at the forming stage, not only when problems arise.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 strongly recommends: "It is best if this [initial team] meeting can take place in person. The cost of getting everyone together for a few days at the beginning of a big project will be saved many times over with fewer conflicts and better communication. Shared experiences and goals are the fastest ways to build rapport within a team."

The chapter also advises managers to "Budget for periodic in-person meetings. If the project is going to last for more than a year or is highly complex, budget for periodic in-person meetings for at least the functional leads. In addition, the project manager should plan to visit each of the other locations at least once during the project." Critically, "These visits should be part of the project plan and not scheduled only to discuss problems. If the only time the team meets in person is when there is a problem, it has a significant dampening effect on the entire team, and the project manager will not be very popular."

The book notes the psychological importance: "From a psychological and sociological standpoint, it is important for people to be able to visualize their teammates so that they can 'hear' and 'see' the human being on the other side of the email, instant message (IM), or telephone. There is a tendency for people to be more blunt, to say things in email that they would never say to someone's face, and for issues to escalate more quickly when most of the interaction is via email or IM if the people involved have never met in person or via telephone."

## Why It Matters

This concept challenges the assumption that distributed work means no travel or that every meeting must be virtual. Instead, it recognizes that face-to-face time has unique properties: it enables informal communication, builds personal relationships that sustain teams through conflict, allows non-verbal communication that prevents misunderstandings, and accelerates decision-making. The investment in a few days of in-person time at the beginning can be justified by the reduction in conflict, slower decision cycles, and lower morale that plague purely virtual teams. The key insight is timing: invest early in the forming stage to build a foundation, then rely on async work for the bulk of the project.
