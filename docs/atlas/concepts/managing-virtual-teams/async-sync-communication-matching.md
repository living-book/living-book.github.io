# Async-Sync Communication Matching

## Definition

Virtual teams must match the communication mechanism to the urgency and nature of the information being shared. Synchronous tools (conference calls, instant messaging, chat rooms) ensure all team members receive the same information at the same time, suitable for urgent or decision-critical messages. Asynchronous tools (email, wikis, message boards, blogs) allow team members to access information at their convenience and are better for reference material, project plans, and schedules.

## In the Book

The book identifies two primary communication categories in Chapter 4. Synchronous tools are reserved for high-urgency situations—"think fire alarm"—where immediate coordination is necessary. Examples include VoIP, instant messaging, chat rooms, and conference calls. In contrast, asynchronous tools like email, wikis, intranets, and message boards are designed for information that can be accessed repeatedly and needs to be referenced over time, such as project plans, schedules, and to-do lists.

The authors illustrate the challenge: virtual teams lose the spontaneous hallway conversation and water-cooler interaction of colocated teams. Yet they cannot use synchronous communication for everything because of time zones and scheduling difficulties. The book emphasizes establishing clear email guidelines (including subject-line conventions, response timeframes, and acceptable use of "reply all") to maximize the effectiveness of asynchronous communication while preventing misunderstandings that arise from text-only exchanges.

## Why It Matters

This concept clarifies a fundamental design decision for distributed teams. By deliberately separating communication paths by urgency and persistence, teams avoid both the inefficiency of unnecessary real-time meetings and the chaos of critical decisions made in asynchronous channels where some members may miss the message. It also acknowledges that different types of information have different lifecycles—one-time urgent announcements differ fundamentally from living project documentation that must be referenced repeatedly across time zones.
