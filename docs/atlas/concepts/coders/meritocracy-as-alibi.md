# Meritocracy as Alibi

## Definition

When an industry believes it hires and promotes purely on demonstrated skill, it treats any resulting demographic skew not as a signal to investigate but as proof the skew is deserved. The belief in meritocracy becomes self-sealing: it supplies a ready explanation ("they must not be as good") for outcomes that are actually produced by culture, gatekeeping, and interpersonal hostility — while making that explanation feel like a factual, not ideological, conclusion.

## In the Book

Chapter 6 documents Silicon Valley's founding myth through PayPal: Keith Rabois calls the company's success "a perfect validation of merit," and David Sacks tells the New York Times, "If meritocracy exists anywhere on earth, it is in Silicon Valley." This belief is built on the "10X coder" mythology, traced to Hal Sackman's flawed, tiny 1966 study of programmer productivity — a study whose methodological weaknesses "never took hold" against its cultural appeal, because it was "poetically affirming" for an industry that already wanted to believe ability was innate and starkly unequal.

Chapter 7 shows the same belief operating as exclusion machinery. Cate Huston, a Google engineer, describes being told women's underrepresentation (15 percent of Google engineers) was "probably genetic," since "Google was a meritocracy; they only hired the best, right?" Thompson traces how women were pushed out of programming over decades (from 35 percent of the field in 1990 down to 26 percent by 2013) even as they moved into every other technical profession — the reversal correlating not with any change in women's ability but with the field's rising prestige and status, defended by hostile code reviews, personality-test hiring filters, and harassment Huston and colleagues absorbed as "normal."

## Why It Matters

This concept lets you separate a belief system's stated logic (we reward pure merit) from its actual function (it protects existing gatekeepers from having to account for who they exclude and why). It applies anywhere a field claims objective, merit-based selection while showing a stark demographic skew — the claim itself should raise, not lower, the burden of proof, since "meritocracy" is precisely the story a biased system tells about itself.
