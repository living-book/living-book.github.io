# Institute of Creative Technology

## Definition

Mervin Kelly's term for what Bell Labs was: not a laboratory but an organization
engineered to convert ideas into products on purpose, over and over. He broke
the process into three linked groups — research (a "reservoir" of new knowledge),
systems engineering (translating that knowledge against the needs of the existing
network), and development/manufacture (turning validated ideas into deployable
devices). "Inventing the future wasn't just a matter of inventing things for the
future; it also entailed inventing ways to invent those things."

## In the Book

Chapter Nine centers on Kelly's 1950 lecture to the Royal Society in London,
where he laid out this three-part structure and the conditions required to sustain
it: a "critical mass" of top talent housed close together, an internal training
program (informally "Kelly College") to raise employees past what graduate
schools could teach, patient multi-year funding from AT&T and Western Electric,
and — critically — a real market that would eventually use whatever was
invented, which "focuses the mind" even for researchers pursuing pure
understanding. The transistor is his worked example: it moved from Shockley's
solid-state research group, through systems engineers assessing where it fit in
the phone network, to Jack Morton's development and manufacturing operation.
Kelly never used the word "innovation" in the speech, but the book treats this
as the moment industrial research stopped being ad hoc and became a
managed, quasi-predictable system.

## Why It Matters

It reframes innovation as a design problem for the organization itself, not just
for the product: what pipeline moves an idea from raw discovery to something
someone can buy, what has to be true at each handoff, and what resources
(talent density, training, patient capital, a waiting market) the pipeline needs to
keep running. It gives you a checklist for diagnosing why an organization
generates ideas that never ship, or ships things nobody asked for.
