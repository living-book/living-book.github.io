# The Tyranny of Numbers

## Definition

By the late 1950s, circuits built from thousands of individual transistors,
resistors, and capacitors required thousands of tiny interconnecting wires —
a problem Bell Labs device-development chief Jack Morton named "the tyranny
of numbers." Morton's answer was to engineer exotic multi-function
"functional devices" to shrink the component count. He never seriously
pursued the simpler alternative — building an entire circuit out of one piece of
silicon — because his engineering judgment held that a chip with hundreds of
components would inevitably have some fail, making the whole device useless.
Jack Kilby (Texas Instruments) and Robert Noyce (Fairchild) built it anyway,
choosing to believe manufacturing reliability could be solved later. It became
the integrated circuit.

## In the Book

Chapter Fifteen makes the point explicit through Ian Ross, a Bell Labs
engineer who worked in Morton's department: "we knew we could make
multiple transistors within a piece of silicon, we knew we could make
resistors, we knew we could make capacitors" — every underlying process
(diffusion, photolithography) had been developed at the Labs. What Bell Labs
lacked wasn't capability but permission from its own conviction: "it was the
received wisdom under Jack Morton... that such devices could never be
reliable." Morton, described elsewhere in the book as having "an intense
distaste for anyone opposing him," had already committed to a harder,
different path (functional devices) to the same problem. Morry Tanenbaum,
a Bell Labs scientist who saw an early Noyce prototype and called it "really
neat," admits: "I can't say I had the imagination to see how far it would go."
The book treats this as a rare instance where the world's leading solid-state
lab was beaten to a major advance built entirely on its own science.

## Why It Matters

It's a sharper, more specific version of "incumbents miss disruption": here the
miss wasn't caused by lacking resources, talent, or even the relevant
knowledge — it was caused by an internal, well-earned engineering standard
(don't ship something you know will fail) that outsiders with less invested in
that standard were free to ignore. It's a prompt to ask, of any organization that
has all the pieces of an obvious next step: what belief, not what resource, is
stopping them from assembling it.
