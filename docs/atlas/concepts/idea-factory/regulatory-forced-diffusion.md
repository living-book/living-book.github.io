# Regulatory-Forced Diffusion

## Definition

AT&T was a government-sanctioned monopoly under continuous antitrust
scrutiny. Rather than hoard the transistor, Bell Labs executives decided almost
immediately that they were "compelled to share and license" it — the political
logic of appeasing regulators was overwhelming, and an open-door policy also
burnished AT&T's reputation as a national resource that justified its monopoly.
The 1956 consent decree that formally settled the government's antitrust suit
required AT&T to license all its existing patents to any American applicant,
royalty-free in most cases.

## In the Book

Chapter Seven describes the transistor's diffusion starting almost at its 1948
public unveiling — the Labs mailed sample transistors as gifts to academic and
industrial scientists, then in 1951-52 ran multiday licensing conventions at
Murray Hill for $25,000 a seat (free for hearing-aid applications, in deference to
Alexander Graham Bell's work with the deaf). Chapter Fifteen shows the
consequence: by the late 1950s, in what Ian Ross calls a "precompetitive era,"
Bell Labs freely exchanged information with the two companies that would go
on to invent the integrated circuit and found Silicon Valley — Texas
Instruments and Fairchild Semiconductor (the latter founded by Robert Noyce
and Gordon Moore after they left Shockley's own company). Jack Kilby of
Texas Instruments made a pilgrimage to Bell Labs in 1952 specifically to learn
how to build transistors. The regulatory pressure that forced AT&T's openness
ended up seeding the semiconductor industry that eventually broke the Labs'
own technological lead.

## Why It Matters

It shows regulation reshaping where value from an invention accrues without
anyone intending it: the same legal threat that constrained AT&T's ability to
exploit its own breakthrough is what let a whole ecosystem of outside firms
build on it faster than the inventor could alone. It's a case for thinking about
IP and diffusion strategically rather than defensively — sometimes an
organization's constraints on capturing value from an idea are exactly what
make the idea valuable at scale.
