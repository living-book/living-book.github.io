# The Three C's of Motivation

## Definition

Kohn's positive alternative to reward systems is not a better incentive but a set of conditions to build instead: Collaboration (the chance to work with, not against or apart from, other people), Content (work whose substance is inherently interesting or meaningful, not merely well-compensated), and Choice (real say over how a task gets done, not just whether to comply with it). He treats these as what authentic — as opposed to induced — motivation is actually made of, in the workplace, the classroom, and child-rearing alike.

## In the Book

Under Collaboration, Kohn quotes McGregor's observation that most "managerial teams" are really just individuals competing for the boss's favor, and argues the fix is organizational commitment to real teamwork, not compensation tweaks — quoting a business professor: "Telling people you are going to change the compensation system rallies them around compensation when what you want them to do is rally around making teams work." Under Content, he cites Herzberg's aphorism "if you want people motivated to do a good job, give them a good job to do," and the case of San Francisco garbage collectors who reported high satisfaction because their work was organized cooperatively (a worker-owned structure, varied routes, strong peer relationships) — evidence, he argues, that no category of work is inherently unmotivating by nature alone. Under Choice, he links restricted autonomy directly to burnout and to coronary heart disease research showing stress tracks with how much control a person has over their work situation, not with job prestige or income — and notes that autonomy deprivation is worst precisely for the workers already doing the least interesting, lowest-paid jobs. In the classroom (chapter 11) and with children generally (chapter 12), he returns to the same three C's as the basis for intrinsic engagement with learning and good behavior, in place of grades, gold stars, and behavior charts.

## Why It Matters

This converts "stop using rewards" from a purely negative prescription into a constructive one: instead of asking what incentive will make people do the thing, ask whether they're working with others rather than against them, whether the work itself has substance worth caring about, and whether they have genuine control over how they do it. It's a diagnostic for stalled motivation in any domain — a demotivated team, a disengaged student, a burned-out employee — that doesn't reach for a bonus or sticker chart but asks which of the three C's is actually missing.
