# Subjective Time as the Primary Reality

## Definition

Wolf argues that "subjective time" — the felt duration of an experience, which stretches during boredom or pain and vanishes during absorption — is not a psychological error to be corrected against clock time. It is the more basic phenomenon. Objective, linear clock time is itself a mental construction: a comparison of rhythms (a pendulum's swings against one's own heartbeat or breathing) that a mind performs and then treats as if it existed independently, "out there."

## In the Book

Chapter 2 opens with the Hindu myth of Narada, sent by Lord Vishnu to fetch a cup of water and understand the meaning of "maya" (illusion): Narada is captivated by a woman at a door, marries her, raises two children, loses his whole family to a flood, and clings to a tree in grief — only to hear Vishnu still waiting for the water, and realize only a few moments had actually passed. Wolf uses this to argue that lived, subjective duration and measured, "objective" duration are radically different things, and that the felt one is what actually happens to a person. He then walks through how clocks work: a clock is only ever "true" by comparison to another rhythm (Galileo timing a swinging chandelier against his own pulse), so objective time is built out of an underlying, prior subjective sense of rhythm, not the reverse. From this Wolf concludes that Western culture inverted the real order of dependency: it gives "more credence to objective, or mechanical, clock time than ... our inner, subjective time sense," even though clock time is the abstraction and the inner sense is what makes any measurement — of time or of space — possible at all.

## Why It Matters

This concept licenses treating a person's felt sense of duration, pacing, and flow as data about reality rather than noise to be corrected by the clock. Speculative claim, reported as the book's position, not a settled finding: Wolf goes on to build his entire theory of mind-based time travel on the premise that because subjective time is primary, changing how the mind attends to experience can change time itself, not merely one's perception of it. Even bracketing that leap, the underlying observation — that measurement always presupposes an observer's own rhythm as a reference — is a genuinely useful lens for any domain where a "neutral" metric (a KPI, a survey scale, a benchmark) is treated as prior to and independent of the perceiving system that calibrated it.
