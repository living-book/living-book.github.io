# The Sphere of Many Radii and the Counterfactual Past

## Definition

Wolf builds his most elaborate device on a real physics proposal: Yakir Aharonov and Lev Vaidman's 1990 thought experiment, in which a traveler sits inside a massive hollow sphere linked to a quantum computer held in a superposition of possible states. Real general relativity supplies the seed effect (mass dilates time), which Aharonov and Vaidman show can, in principle, be hugely amplified through quantum superposition and "post-selection." Wolf's own addition is to argue that what actually shifts inside the sphere is not the traveler's clock time but his "possibility-wave" — and that a backward shift returns the traveler not to what actually happened to him, but to the "counterfactual past": the past he would have had if he had been isolated from the world the entire time.

## In the Book

Chapter 7 walks through the mechanics: connecting the sphere to a quantum computer held in a special superposition ("state-S") causes the sphere to split across parallel radii, and inducing a rare "post-selection" transition ("state-T") coalesces those separate, tiny time-dilations into one large forward or backward shift. Wolf illustrates the resulting "counterfactual past" with a worked example: suppose on Monday someone made you angry, and by Wednesday you've calmed down and step into the machine set for a backward shift — you would not return to Monday's actual anger-provoking encounter, but to the calmer state you would have been in had you never met that person at all, "isolated from outside stimuli." He extends this to claim the device "could also be used to cure illnesses," if the counterfactual past was a healthier state than the real one. Chapter 8 then reframes the entire device as scaffolding for the book's real interest: "what actually gets shifted inside Aharonov and Vaidman's sphere of many radii ... is the possibility-wave of the time traveler," and once that idea is grasped, Wolf writes, "it is just a short journey to a mind yoga for time travel" — i.e., the same possibility-wave shift the sphere would force mechanically is what he claims mind yoga can accomplish directly, without hardware.

## Why It Matters

Speculative physics-mysticism, reported as the book's claim and not as physical fact: the Aharonov-Vaidman thought experiment is real published physics, but the extension to a human "possibility-wave" that carries autobiographical memory and can be redirected to an "isolated" counterfactual past is Wolf's own addition, without experimental grounding. What is exportable is the sharper underlying idea, independent of the mysticism: a backward move through time (or, less literally, through memory or through an organizational history) does not restore the actual prior state, it restores the state that a system would have reached under a different, counterfactual set of inputs — a genuinely useful distinction wherever people conflate "undo" with "revert to what actually happened."
