# Code Halo Instrumentation

## Definition

A "Code Halo" is the surrounding cloud of digital information created when a physical thing — a customer, a product, a piece of equipment, an entire organization — is continuously instrumented and connected. The book's operating rule, drawn from the authors' earlier work: "any product costing more than $5 that you can't eat" should, as a default, be instrumented. Once instrumented and always connected, the thing itself becomes a "code generator," and the resulting data often becomes more valuable than the physical object it comes from.

## In the Book

Chapter 8 states three rules of this competition: instrumentation is no longer optional but "core curriculum," code is frequently worth more than the physical thing it describes ("a running shoe of old is the data platform of today"), and instrumented things must never be turned off, because value compounds only with always-on connection. The chapter's central case is Discovery Limited, a South African insurer legally required to accept every applicant at the same premium — a business model that should be unworkable. Discovery's leaders instrumented customers' health behavior instead (gym visits, food diaries, wearables, driving habits) and built a wellness business on top of the insurance business, trading real benefits (discounts, an earned Apple Watch) for personal data; the company's stock nearly doubled between 2013 and 2015 and its new business grew 51% in the following year. Boeing's Digital Airline, Philips's connected lighting, and Toyota's smart cars are cited as parallel examples of turning key products into code generators.

## Why It Matters

This gives a concrete decision rule — instrument anything valuable and keep it always-on — for converting a static asset or relationship into a live data stream, which is the raw material every learning system downstream depends on. It also reframes a seemingly disadvantageous business constraint (Discovery's forced flat-premium regulation) as the trigger for a data-driven business model, a pattern of "constraint forces instrumentation forces new model" that recurs well outside insurance.
