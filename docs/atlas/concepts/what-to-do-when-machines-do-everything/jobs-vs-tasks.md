# Jobs vs. Tasks

## Definition

Doomsday automation studies typically ask a binary question — will this job be automated away, yes or no? — and that framing is too coarse. Any knowledge job is really a collection of discrete tasks, and automation nearly always hits at the task level: some tasks disappear, some are made faster, and others are barely touched or even enhanced. Whether a job survives depends on what fraction of its tasks get cannibalized, not on whether "the job" as a label gets automated.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 makes the case through Tamara, a fictional company accountant whose job is unpacked into roughly a dozen distinct tasks — documenting transactions, preparing tax filings, auditing statements, providing strategic counsel, and so on — only some of which are ripe for automation. The book leans on Forrester Research's task-based (versus job-based) analysis of robotic process automation: Forrester's headline finding that 92% of "workflows, processes, and metrics" in management/business/finance jobs will be changed by 2022 does not mean 92% of those jobs vanish; it means the tasks inside them get restructured, while only the roughly 12% of jobs with too many cannibalized tasks disappear outright. The book also cites the historical example of the 1992 cable TV technician, whose job absorbed internet installation, VoIP, wireless routers, and home security over time rather than being eliminated — illustrating that "job transformation" (25%+ of tasks automated, triggering redeployment) is the far more common pattern than job elimination.

## Why It Matters

This reframing turns an emotionally loaded, all-or-nothing question ("will AI take my job?") into an operational one that can actually be measured and planned around: which specific tasks in this role are routine and data-rich enough to automate, and what does the remaining bundle of tasks look like once they're gone? That task-level lens applies to any domain debating automation, restructuring, or outsourcing — the unit of analysis should be the task, not the job title.
