# Cadence and Slack

## Definition

Cadence is the predictable, regular beat that emerges once you visualize a stream of work rather than looking at isolated tasks — the same rhythm a metronome gives a musician or a stroke meter gives a rowing crew. Slack is the open space between units of work that lets that rhythm keep moving: "We need slack in our workflow, we need space to adjust. Without slack, we will overload." Neither is optional — a system with no cadence has no discernible flow to observe and improve, and a system with no slack has no room to absorb the variation that flow inevitably encounters.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 builds the case through two very different registers. It opens with Jim Benson's own career — psychology, urban planning, government technology, Agile and Lean consulting — which felt chaotic and disjointed until he stepped back far enough to see its cadence: "community," a through-line invisible up close ("my nose was too close to the canvas") but obvious once observed as a stream of events rather than a pile of isolated ones. The mechanical case is Oliver Evans' 18th-century automated grist mill: grain fed into the millstones at anything but a steady rate caused the furrows to clog and the whole works to grind to a halt, so "the miller limited his WIP and optimized his slack" by careful, continual judgment of flow relative to capacity. The book then applies the same logic to people: pulling too many sticky notes into TODAY sets an expectation of completing them all, which "will diminish your ability to pull, interrupt your flow, and impact your cadence" — exactly the overload the mill was designed to avoid.

## Why It Matters

Systems that are only measured on throughput treat the gaps between units of work as pure waste to be squeezed out — but those gaps are exactly what let the system absorb the unpredictability that real work always contains, whether that's a delayed shipment, a sick day, or a life emergency. This concept gives a name to the tradeoff: push cadence too tight and any perturbation cascades into overload; leave enough slack and the same rhythm can flex without breaking. It's the same insight that shows up as buffer stock in supply chains, white space in a schedule, or margin in a budget — a resource that looks like idleness right up until the moment it's the only thing standing between a disruption and a collapse.
