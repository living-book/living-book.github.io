# Existential Overhead

## Definition

Existential overhead is the anxiety-producing weight of work that exists only as a fuzzy mental concept — undone tasks, unresolved commitments, anything competing for attention without a concrete shape. Benson and Barry state it plainly in their PKFlow tips: "Existential overhead impedes effectiveness. Clarity dispels existential overhead." The overhead is not the work itself but the cost of carrying it around unresolved and unseen.

## In the Book

The book introduces the term in Chapter 1's discussion of Personal Kanban's psychological mechanics: "Work we have yet to complete, or any aspect of our life that distracts us, creates existential overhead. As existential overhead mounts, our effectiveness diminishes." The central illustration is Carl, who lies awake at 3 a.m. paralyzed by the amorphous fear of financing his daughter's college education — a goal with no shape, no next step, and therefore no way to act on it. The moment Carl writes the goal on his kanban board and pulls one small task (email his investment advisor) into DOING, the dread lifts; he "no longer tosses and turns in bed" because the concept has become a tangible, actionable object. The book returns to the term repeatedly, including in the grimmer case of tornado survivors in Grand Island, Nebraska, whose sudden loss of home and possessions is described as a shift "from a roof overhead to existential overhead" — showing the concept scales from everyday anxiety to genuine crisis.

## Why It Matters

This names a cost that most productivity systems ignore entirely: the toll of simply holding unresolved things in mind, independent of how much actual labor they require. It reframes "feeling overwhelmed" as a measurable, addressable condition — solved not by doing more work faster, but by converting the conceptual into the tangible so the mind can stop guarding it. Anywhere a system leaves commitments implicit or unlisted (a household, a team's undocumented obligations, a person's private worries), this concept predicts a hidden effectiveness tax that visualization alone can partially collect back.
