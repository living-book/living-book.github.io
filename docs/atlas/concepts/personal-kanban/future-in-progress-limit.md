# The Future-in-Progress Limit

## Definition

A Future-in-Progress (FIP) limit caps the number of large, long-horizon goals a person actively pursues at any one time — a WIP limit applied one level up, to aspirations rather than to the individual tasks those aspirations generate. The book introduces it for people who are "goal-collectors": someone can rigorously limit tasks-in-process and still be overwhelmed, because each active goal is itself a generator of tasks, and too many active generators will overrun any WIP limit downstream no matter how disciplined the task-level system is.

## In the Book

Appendix A tells this through Jessica, a single mother with two jobs who is simultaneously pursuing a financial-advisor certification, triathlon training, writing a book, starting a business, and considering another degree. Mapping her Personal Kanban revealed the actual problem wasn't task management — it was that she was "not simply goal-oriented, she was a goal-collector," and each of those goals was independently spinning off daily tasks. The fix was choosing two goals to run with (the certification, needed for her job, and the triathlon, needed for her health) and explicitly deferring the rest, rather than trying to make room for all of them inside a single WIP limit. The two chosen goals were then given their own dedicated swim lanes with goal-specific metrics — daily calories, hydration, and workout intensity for the triathlon; module-by-module study progress for the certification — visualized alongside, but distinct from, her everyday task flow.

## Why It Matters

A person, a team, or an organization can be perfectly disciplined about capping work-in-process at the task level and still collapse under load, because the real constraint was never violated at that level — it was violated one level up, in how many concurrent commitments were allowed to generate tasks in the first place. This concept supplies the missing constraint: limit the number of active large commitments before limiting the tasks beneath them, because a WIP limit on tasks can't compensate for an unlimited number of task-generating goals feeding it. It generalizes to any hierarchy of commitments — a company's simultaneous initiatives, a portfolio's active bets — where the visible bottleneck sits downstream of an invisible one at the goal level.
