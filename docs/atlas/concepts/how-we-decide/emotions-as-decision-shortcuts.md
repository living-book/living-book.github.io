# Emotions as Decision Shortcuts

## Definition

The brain faces a fundamental problem: there are always more alternatives to evaluate than there is time. Conscious deliberation—comparing pros and cons, estimating probabilities, weighing utilities—is too slow. Emotions solve this by assigning a visceral signal to each option: an attractive option produces a subtle burst of positive feeling, an unattractive option produces a twinge of fear or disgust. These somatic markers (literally, feelings in the body) are the brain's way of encoding the results of analysis that happened too fast for consciousness to track. When you feel drawn to one job offer over another, or one romantic prospect over another, the feeling is not irrational—it's a compressed summary of pattern-matching across thousands of implicit variables.

## In the Book

Lehrer anchors this concept in Antonio Damasio's patient Elliot, a man whose orbitofrontal cortex (the brain region behind the eyes where emotions are integrated into decision-making) was damaged by surgery. Before the surgery, Elliot was a successful manager, devoted father, and engaged church member. After the surgery, his IQ remained high—he still tested in the 97th percentile—but he became incapable of making even mundane decisions. Choosing where to eat lunch became an hour-long agony, as Elliot methodically visited each restaurant to assess its menu, seating, and current crowd. He knew he needed to decide but couldn't. Eventually, he lost his job, his family, his savings.

What Elliot lacked was emotion. Damasio tested this by hooking Elliot to a machine that measured sweat-gland activity (a sign of emotional arousal). When shown images that normally trigger strong reactions—a severed foot, a naked woman, a house on fire—Elliot remained unmoved. His palms never got sweaty. He had the "emotional life of a mannequin."

This should have made Elliot a better decision-maker, according to classical economic theory: no feelings to cloud judgment, pure reason ruling the day. Instead, Elliot became a cautionary tale. The orbitofrontal cortex doesn't produce emotion as noise in the decision-making system; it produces emotion as the *mechanism* of decision-making. When Elliot looked at a potential lunch destination, his intact intellect could enumerate the pros and cons, but without a feeling attached to each option, he had no way to choose. His conscious brain could analyze forever; his emotional brain would normally say "this place feels right."

The evolutionary logic is clear: if decision-making depended on conscious deliberation, humans would starve while calculating. Emotions shortcut this paralysis. They encode the wisdom of past experience into a feeling, allowing us to act decisively in real time.

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes emotions not as hindrances to rationality but as essential infrastructure for it. It explains why people with damage to emotional processing are decision-making disasters despite intact intellect, why the best negotiators and salespeople are skilled at reading (not ignoring) emotional signals, and why trying to suppress emotion in decision-making often produces *worse* choices, not better ones. It also provides a model for design and leadership: if emotions guide choices, then the goal is not to eliminate them but to shape them. A product feels good because it subtly communicates competence and care through a thousand details; a leader's credibility depends on emotional signals of trustworthiness, not just logical arguments; a negotiation succeeds when each party feels heard and respected, not just fairly compensated. The implication is profound: the deepest rationality requires emotion. Decisions without feeling are decisions without a decision-maker.
