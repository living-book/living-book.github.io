# Choking on Thought

## Definition

When a skilled performer becomes anxious and shifts from automatic, intuitive action to conscious self-monitoring, performance collapses. The performer begins scrutinizing movements that were previously automatized—where to position the tongue while singing, the exact angle of a golf swing, the mechanics of a throwing motion. This shift from implicit to explicit processing hijacks the prefrontal cortex, which is too slow for real-time expert action, and confidence erodes into a downward spiral of error and self-doubt.

## In the Book

Renee Fleming, a world-class opera singer, choked on Mozart's "Dove sono," a piece she had sung hundreds of times. As she approached the crescendo, self-doubt struck. She could not stop thinking about the high notes, about her breath, about her tongue position—technical details she hadn't consciously considered in years. "It caught me by surprise," she wrote. "I had an enormous amount of experience with this piece." Yet her diaphragm constricted. The anxiety became chronic, and Fleming nearly quit opera.

Jean Van de Velde's collapse at the 1999 British Open provides the starkest example. Holding a three-stroke lead on the eighteenth hole, Van de Velde should have coasted to victory. Instead, he began thinking about the mechanics of his swing—the position of his hips, the rotation of his wrists—details a professional golfer executes automatically. His swing disintegrated. He took seven errant shots and lost the tournament.

Sian Beilock's research shows the mechanism: novice golfers improve when they think about their putts, because conscious attention helps them avoid beginner mistakes. Experienced golfers get *worse* when forced to think about their swing. "When you are at a high level, your skills become somewhat automated. You don't need to pay attention to every step." The part of the brain that monitors behavior—the prefrontal cortex—intrudes and second-guesses skills honed through years of practice.

Choking also appears in test-taking. Claude Steele's experiments show that black students taking a test described as measuring "intelligence" choke due to stereotype threat; they become so worried about confirming a negative stereotype that they shift into careful, second-guessing mode and perform far below their ability. When the same test is described as a "preparatory drill," the stereotype threat vanishes and performance equalizes.

## Why It Matters

Choking reveals a fundamental truth: more deliberation is not always better. The rationalist ideal—that careful analysis leads to optimal decisions—fails in real-time, high-skill domains. It explains why experienced surgeons sometimes perform worse when they slow down to consciously verify every step, why athletes choke under pressure, and why overthinking a decision can produce worse outcomes than trusting accumulated intuition. This has implications for training (repetition to automaticity is the goal, not conceptual understanding), performance psychology (managing anxiety so the athlete doesn't shift into conscious mode), and organizational decision-making (some decisions are best made quickly and intuitively by experienced people, not by committees engaging in exhaustive deliberation). It also suggests that confidence is not overconfidence—the feeling of knowing what to do, without needing to think about it, is often the signal of expertise, not delusion.
