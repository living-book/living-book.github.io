# Clean Language Questions

## Definition

Clean Language questions are a fixed set of twelve question forms — such as
"(And) what kind of X (is that X)?" and "(And) is there anything else about X?" —
where X is always replaced by the speaker's own exact words, never a paraphrase.
Because the questions themselves are stripped of nearly all assumptions and
metaphors, they let a speaker elaborate on their own experience without the
questioner's worldview leaking into the answer.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 introduces the two most versatile questions — "what kind of X?" and
"is there anything else about X?" — through the story of a mother asking her
daughter Jenny about a drawing, and a chair asking a sales director "What kind
of breaking point is that?" only to discover he meant breaking through, not
breaking down. Chapter 2 contrasts Clean questions with open and closed
questions: closed questions force yes/no, open questions invite elaboration,
but Clean questions are "ultra-Open" because they add almost no content of
their own. The book stresses "don't paraphrase — parrot-phrase": using the
speaker's exact words, not a rewording, is what keeps a question clean.

## Why It Matters

Most questions smuggle in the questioner's assumptions, categories, and
metaphors, which quietly steers the answer toward what the questioner already
expected to hear. A question format engineered to carry almost no content of
its own — just a slot for the other party's own words — lets you find out what
someone actually means before you act on what you assumed they meant. That
distinction is exportable anywhere information has to cross from one mind (or
one system) into another without distortion: requirements gathering, research
interviews, diagnosis, negotiation.
