# Symbolic Space

## Definition

Because human cognition is embodied, spatial location is one of the two most
fundamental metaphors underlying language (alongside force): things are always
seen, heard, or felt "somewhere." In a Clean Language session, a client's
metaphoric symbols acquire literal locations — in front of them, behind them,
at particular distances — and the facilitator treats these "addresses" with
the same respect they'd give someone's physical belongings: never moved,
never reinterpreted, never occupied by the facilitator's own frame.

## In the Book

Chapter 12 opens sessions by asking the client, in this exact order, "And
where would you like to be?" then "And where would you like me to be?" —
letting the client's own placement of self and facilitator align with their
inner landscape before any content is explored. It cites David Grove's
observation that room furniture often turns out to be "in just the right
place" once a session gets underway, and tells of a client whose metaphor of
"a door to a new world" led her to physically stand, open the room's actual
door, and step through it mid-session. A striking case describes a
14-year-old who described provocation as "holding chocolate under a dog's
nose" and, through location questions, built a literal ten-step spatial scale
across the room from calm to about-to-kick, which she then used to monitor
her own state during the school day. The chapter also reframes eye contact:
a Clean facilitator deliberately looks at and gestures toward the client's
symbols in space rather than at the client's eyes, keeping rapport with the
client's information rather than with the client personally.

## Why It Matters

Spatial arrangement is not a neutral backdrop to how people represent
problems to themselves — it is part of the representation, and disturbing it
(by moving furniture, imposing your own gestures, or reinterpreting where
something "is") corrupts the very thing you're trying to understand. This
generalizes to any facilitation, design, or research context where a
practitioner must resist projecting their own frame onto someone else's
structure: the discipline of leaving the other party's arrangement of their
own material untouched is itself a source of insight.
