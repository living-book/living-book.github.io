# The Righting Reflex

## Definition

The righting reflex is the near-automatic impulse to "put things right" the
moment we see someone in trouble or distress, usually by offering advice drawn
from our own experience. The book argues this impulse, however well-meaning,
is frequently unhelpful because the advisor is solving a problem from inside
their own model of the world, not the other person's.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 opens with the observation that "How can I help here?" is one of
people's most constant preoccupations, and names this urge the righting
reflex. It runs a paired exercise: partners first give a minute of unsolicited
"If I were you…" advice about a minor difficulty, then are asked a Clean
Language question — "What would you like to have happen?" — about a second
difficulty, and compare which conversation actually moved them toward a
resolution. The book gives three concrete reasons advice fails: it can solve
the wrong problem (fixing "get to the gym" when the real issue is "eat less"),
it can highlight the wrong benefit (selling weight loss on looks to someone who
wants to do adrenaline sports), or it can be aimed at only one of several
internally conflicted "parts" of the advisee. A training example shows Wendy
almost giving generic confidence-building advice to nervous trainers, only to
find via Clean questioning that their actual wants ranged from "techniques for
difficult participants" to "I want my face to stay normal."

## Why It Matters

Naming the righting reflex turns an invisible, virtuous-feeling habit into
something you can notice and choose not to act on. It reframes "helping" as a
two-part risk: advice fits the advisor's model of the problem, and it can
overshoot into managing someone else's decision for them. This generalizes far
beyond conversation — anywhere a person or system is tempted to intervene
based on incomplete information about someone else's actual constraints and
goals, from management to product design to policy, the same failure mode
recurs: solving the problem you can see rather than the problem that is
actually there.
