# Positive Challenge

## Definition

Positive Challenge is a way of confronting inconsistency or under-performance that stays forward-looking: instead of telling someone how they screwed up, it calls them toward the best version of themselves. Stoltzfus contrasts "Telling Mode" ("That decision isn't going to get you where you want to go") with the reframed version ("Take a minute and envision the future that you really want. Is this decision going to get you there, or is there a better way?") — same underlying observation, opposite posture.

## In the Book

The book lays out a graduated toolkit rather than a single technique. It opens with **Positive Challenge** examples that rewrite blunt confrontation as future-oriented questions. It then offers **Asking for More** — challenging a goal as too small ("What if you set out to accomplish that goal in two years instead of five?"). Before escalating further, it recommends **giving the person the chance to challenge themselves** first, since that "doesn't use any relational capital." Only after that does it introduce **Observation** — juxtaposing two things the client has said that don't add up (e.g., a stated priority on family time set against a schedule that leaves no room for it) and asking a neutral question that forces the client to reconcile them — and **Ownership Questions**, which push responsibility for the past, the future, and personal growth back onto the client rather than the other party in a conflict. The book frames escalation explicitly: "start gently and ramp up," using techniques that spend less relational capital first (open questions, self-challenge) and reserving the more aggressive tools (naming the issue directly, ownership questions) for when gentler moves don't land.

## Why It Matters

Confrontation and growth-oriented challenge produce the same information but opposite relationships: one positions the challenger as an adversary judging past failure, the other as a partner calling out future potential. The transferable mechanism is a graduated escalation ladder for delivering hard feedback — start with the version that costs the least trust (letting the person self-challenge, or a neutral observation of their own stated facts), and only escalate to a direct, pointed confrontation if the softer version doesn't produce movement. That ordering applies anywhere feedback risks triggering defensiveness: management, peer review, negotiation, therapy.
