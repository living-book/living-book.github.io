# Destiny Experiences

## Definition

A Destiny Experience is "a specific event where you felt totally in the zone... NOT a role or an extended period in life where you felt fulfilled, but a specific event." The exercise asks a client to recall three such discrete moments — preferably from different seasons of life — describe each in granular detail, and then compare the three side by side to find what repeats. The premise: purpose isn't discovered by introspecting on the question directly, it's inferred from the common structure of moments where it was already showing up.

## In the Book

Stoltzfus lays out the exercise in three parts. Part I gathers three specific stories using narrow prompts ("Give me an example of a time in your life — a specific day or single experience — where you felt like you were doing what you were born to do?"). Part II mines each story for texture: who was served, what task was performed, how the person got into that situation, what impact resulted. Part III is the actual synthesis — comparing all three stories at once and asking "What stands out to you? What do they have in common?" — trusting that "the specifics of these stories can tell us about what we were born to do," especially details that recur across more than one story. The book's own worked example: a 22-year-old army private who talked his colonel into funding a homeless-relief project using army resources — years later, he still has "great favor with people in authority" and still approaches them for resources to serve the less fortunate. The pattern in the youthful anecdote turns out to predict the adult trajectory.

## Why It Matters

Asked directly, "what's your purpose?" or "what's the underlying pattern here?" tends to produce whatever answer sounds good, not the one that's actually true — self-report about identity is unreliable in a way that self-report about a specific remembered event is not. Collecting several concrete, independently-recalled instances and looking for what repeats across them is a general case-based-reasoning move: it works for finding a person's purpose, but the same structure — gather specific instances before asking the abstract question, then let the pattern emerge from comparison rather than introspection — applies to diagnosing a recurring failure mode, distilling a team's real strengths, or inferring an organization's actual (as opposed to stated) values.
