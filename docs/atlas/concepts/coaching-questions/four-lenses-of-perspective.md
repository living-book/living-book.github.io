# The Four Lenses of Perspective

## Definition

When someone says they've "lost perspective," Stoltzfus argues that phrase actually names one of four distinct problems, each needing a different question to fix: **Proportion** (losing the big picture — how important is this really, relative to everything else?), **Objectivity** (losing emotional distance — seeing things as they really are rather than through the current mood), **Viewpoint** (losing other angles — how does this look from the boss's chair, the team's, ten years from now?), and **Clarity** (losing the confidence to act on what you already know).

## In the Book

The book treats "perspective" as underspecified and breaks it into these four named failure modes before offering question sets tuned to each. For Proportion, it asks the client to weigh the issue "relative to your relationships, family, spiritual life, health, etc." For Viewpoint, it offers the Perspective of Time ("What will this decision look like in ten years?"), the Perspective of Others ("How would this look through your boss's eyes? Your team's? The media's?"), and even arguing the opposite case ("Let me hear you make the case against it"). For Objectivity, it suggests naming what's under the client's control versus not, to strip away distortion from feeling powerless. The book pairs this diagnostic taxonomy with a concrete technique for delivering it: sketching a vivid, sensory scene from the alternate vantage point — "drawing a picture of the client sharing a victory with his or her grandkids" — because, as the book notes, "it's not easy to leave your own viewpoint and take on another," and a half-built scene the client can step into and complete works better than an abstract instruction to "get perspective."

## Why It Matters

Treating "I've lost perspective" as one problem leads to one generic intervention; treating it as four distinct, diagnosable failures lets you pick the specific corrective — pull back the frame, strip out emotion, borrow someone else's vantage point, or simply surface confidence in a conclusion already reached. The same four-way split is diagnostic in any domain where judgment goes wrong under pressure: a bad forecast, a stuck negotiation, or a strategy review can each fail for a different one of these four reasons, and naming which one narrows what actually needs fixing.
