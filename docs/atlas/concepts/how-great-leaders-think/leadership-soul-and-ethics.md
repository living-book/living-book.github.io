# Leadership Soul and Ethics

## Definition

Great leadership goes beyond effectiveness to embrace ethics. Each of the four leadership frames embodies a distinct ethical obligation and a corresponding gift or contribution leaders can offer. More fundamentally, organizations need soul—a deep, resolute sense of character, identity, beliefs, and values—to provide ethical guardrails. Without soul, organizations drift toward "the morals of the marketplace," which often amounts to no morals at all. With soul, ethical action becomes systemic rather than dependent on individual virtue.

## In the Book

The book contrasts Johnson & Johnson and Medtronic. In 1982, J&J CEO James Burke faced a crisis when Tylenol bottles were poisoned. He pulled the product at enormous cost because the J&J Credo put patients ahead of profits—soul in action. But thirty years later, under CEO William Weldon, J&J abandoned this ethical core. The company paid billions in fines for hiding drug dangers, bribing doctors, and deceiving customers. The Credo remained on the wall, but as "lip service" rather than commitment. Weldon's "relentless focus on the bottom line" created a culture where employees tried "to get their bonuses, hit their numbers and keep their job."

By contrast, Medtronic's founder Earl Bakken created the "Mission and Medallion Ceremony." Every new employee, even night-shift workers, met the CEO to review the mission and received a bronze medallion showing a patient walking into a full life. This ritual anchored soul. When CEO Bill George discovered a talented executive maintaining a secret Swiss bank account for doctor payments, George fired him—publicly and painfully—to demonstrate that Medtronic values were non-negotiable. The cost was short-term; the payoff was long-term trust. During George's tenure, Medtronic's share price grew at 36% annually while Enron and WorldCom crashed.

Each frame contributes an ethical dimension and leadership gift:

- **Structural Frame**: Excellence and Authorship—Leaders create conditions where people experience their work as meaningful, where they're accountable for results and get feedback.
- **Human Resource Frame**: Caring and Love—Leaders show genuine compassion, listen to people's needs, and create servant-leadership that safeguards collective well-being.
- **Political Frame**: Justice and Power—Leaders ensure fairness in resource distribution and empower constituencies to have a voice in decisions that affect them.
- **Symbolic Frame**: Faith and Significance—Leaders help people discover meaning in their work, sustain faith through ritual and story, and connect daily effort to larger purpose.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates transactional leadership from transformational leadership. It reveals that organizations with soul sustain ethical action through culture rather than compliance, and that leaders are custodians of that soul. The concept also clarifies that ethics isn't separate from good leadership—it's foundational. Leaders who lose touch with any ethical dimension ultimately fail, because they're leading a soulless organization that cannot maintain loyalty, trust, or meaning. The Four Ethical Dimensions offer leaders a framework for building soul across all dimensions of organizational life, not just in piety or codes of conduct.
