# Reframing in Action

## Definition

When a leader faces a tough situation that triggers fight-or-flight instincts, they're trapped in a single frame. Reframing in action means stepping back (sometimes to "go to the balcony") to view the same situation through each of the four frames in turn, generating multiple interpretations and response options. Each frame suggests a different script, a different diagnosis of what's happening, and different actions. The skill is recognizing which frame(s) fit the situation and which response will be most effective.

## In the Book

Chapter 10 walks through a detailed scenario: Olivia Martin arrives for her first day as manager of a customer service unit. Her predecessor, Jack Davis, is sitting at her desk in a meeting with staff. He tells her to wait outside—a deliberate provocation to establish dominance and undermine her credibility in front of her new team.

**The Structural Frame Response**: Martin asserts formal authority, invoking the chain of command and threatening to report insubordination. She gets the desk, but risks long-term tension and being seen as autocratic. Better: She acknowledges the meeting, takes notes as an observer, then schedules a transition plan with clear priorities. She's firm but not harsh, establishing authority while avoiding escalation.

**The Human Resource Frame Response**: Martin risks acting like a waitress, apologizing profusely and offering coffee—so eager to be liked that she appears weak. Better: She greets the staff warmly, acknowledges Davis's legitimate concern about the department, and offers to sit in as an observer, then works with him on transition. She combines advocacy (confidence that she can do the job) with inquiry (asking for his help and insights).

**The Political Frame Response**: Martin could use intimidation and threats, invoking her power and Davis's vulnerability. This satisfies in the moment but creates long-term antagonism. Better: She's diplomatic and indirect, appealing to Davis's self-interest in his career and his relationship with the vice president, softly deflating his posturing while maintaining her political position.

**The Symbolic Frame Response**: Martin could hand out buttons with a slogan about customer service, appearing naive and theatrical. Better: She turns Davis's gruffness into an opportunity to name the department's core value: If a customer came through the door, would you tell her to wait outside for an hour? This frames the situation as a chance to articulate shared values and purpose.

Each frame generates viable responses. The skill is recognizing when to apply which, and how to combine them. Alan Mulally at Ford demonstrated this: he used structural clarity (matrix organization, weekly Business Plan Reviews), human resource support (retention plans, leading by wandering around), political finesse (managing constituencies), and symbolic storytelling (resurrecting Henry Ford's mission) in concert.

## Why It Matters

This concept transforms leadership from reactive instinct to deliberate choice. When leaders master reframing in action, they escape the trap of single-frame thinking. They stop blaming situations or people for "not getting it" and instead ask, "What frame is this situation asking for? What response will actually work here?" The concept also reveals that there's often no single right answer—multiple frames can work, and leaders gain freedom and creativity by expanding their repertoire.
