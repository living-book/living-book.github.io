# Leadership Worldviews and Stories

## Definition

A leader's worldview comprises four elements: the concepts and categories they use to notice and interpret reality, their beliefs about causality (what actions lead to what outcomes), their values (what matters and why), and their self-image (who they are and what they're capable of). Leaders also carry a personal story—a narrative accumulated over a lifetime that shapes how they interpret situations and what they try to accomplish. Organizations, too, have evolved narratives rooted in history. When a new leader arrives, the interplay between the leader's worldview and story and the organization's narrative produces an emerging shared story. If that shared story is compelling and grounded in reality, it provides guidance and meaning. If it's disconnected or false, it leads to disaster.

## In the Book

**Alan Mulally at Ford** succeeded because he brought a rich worldview across all four frames, developed over years at Boeing. His concepts included both structural (process, systems) and symbolic (meaning, culture). His beliefs came from aviation: success depends on clear communication and shared understanding. His values included integrity and respect for people. His self-image combined Midwestern humility with quiet confidence. When he arrived at Ford, he didn't impose a pre-formed story. Instead, he studied Ford's history, discovered the mission Henry Ford had stated in 1925—"Opening the highways to all mankind"—and constructed a narrative: "One Ford," a return to the principles that had made the company great, combined with modern discipline and global leverage. He tested this story with every constituency, refined it, and invited employees into the drama. The shared story provided clarity even as the company faced bankruptcy.

**Ron Johnson at JCPenney** failed because he never tested his story against JCPenney's circumstances. He arrived with a compelling narrative from his success at Target and Apple Stores: create an exciting destination where hip consumers could latte-sip and kids could be entertained. His worldview was rich in structural and symbolic concepts but sparse in understanding JCPenney's customer base and culture. He believed in rapid, confident change without testing. He valued innovation over tradition. His self-image was a visionary disrupting legacy retailers. But his story was conjured in Silicon Valley, not embedded in JCPenney's history. When it clashed with the organization's reality—customers loved the hunt, employees felt disrespected, the brand alienated both old and new customers—Johnson interpreted the feedback as resistance to be overcome, not as a signal that his story was wrong. "Johnson never seemed to grasp the essence of JCPenney's story."

**Lou Gerstner at IBM** also demonstrates the power of this concept. He arrived as a hard-nosed strategist (a sparse worldview in symbolic thinking). But as he studied IBM's history, he discovered Thomas J. Watson Sr., a founder whose story had cast "a long silhouette" on the enterprise. Gerstner resurrected Watson's legacy and values, reinterpreting IBM's culture as its competitive advantage. By the time Gerstner spoke to Wall Street analysts, "he couldn't tell them facts about the future. He was telling them what IBM was going to do. It was all stories. And it worked."

The book frames leadership as the encounter of three narratives: the leader's personal story, the organization's inherited story, and the emerging shared story that both leader and constituents must author together.

## Why It Matters

This concept reveals that leadership is fundamentally about meaning-making, not just command-and-control. Great leaders don't impose visions—they discover or co-create stories that make sense of where a group or organization is, where it needs to go, and how it will get there. The concept also clarifies that leaders' own worldviews (including their blind spots and gaps in understanding) shape which stories they can see and tell. Leaders with sparse symbolic or human resource concepts can't tell stories that resonate in those domains. The implication: developing as a leader requires expanding one's worldview, not just skills. It also means testing one's story against reality, being willing to revise it when it stops fitting, and inviting the organization into the revision process rather than clinging to a narrative that has become disconnected from the actual terrain.
