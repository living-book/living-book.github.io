# The Rational-Panic Paradox

## Definition

Taleb and Norman describe a case where individually "rational" behavior and collectively rational behavior point in opposite directions. Facing a low personal risk of infection early in an epidemic, an individual who calmly weighs only their own odds concludes it is "irrational" to panic. But because that same individual's restraint feeds a multiplicative contagion, the paper concludes the opposite: "one must 'panic' individually (i.e., produce what seems [to be] an exaggerated response) in order to avoid systemic problems, even where the immediate individual payoff does not appear to warrant it."

## In the Book

The paper sets this up as a direct sequence, not an abstraction. First it grants the individual case for calm: the risk of catching the virus is low, "lower than other ailments," so treating it as a top priority looks like an overreaction by ordinary personal-risk accounting. Then it flips the frame: "if she or he does not panic and act in an ultra-conservative manner, they will contribute to the spread of the virus and it will become a severe source of systemic harm." The paper frames the resulting overreaction not as an error of judgment but as the correct response once the individual's action is understood as one input among many into a threshold-driven, system-wide dynamic — the same behavior that looks excessive against a private risk ledger is exactly calibrated against the collective one.

## Why It Matters

This concept isolates a specific failure mode of individual rationality: optimizing against your own risk exposure while your actions are actually inputs to a shared, nonlinear system produces behavior that is locally sensible and globally destructive. It reframes "overreaction" as potentially the more rational choice once externalities are priced in, and gives a concrete diagnostic — is my risk multiplicative and shared, or additive and mine alone? — for when personal cost-benefit reasoning should be overridden by a collective standard instead of trusted on its own terms.
