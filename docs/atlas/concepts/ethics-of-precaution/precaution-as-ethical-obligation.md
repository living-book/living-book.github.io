# Precaution as an Ethical Obligation

## Definition

Taleb and Norman's central ethical claim is that precaution, under systemic risk, is not optional risk management left to individual taste — it is owed to others. As they frame it starkly: "You are harming others by not 'overreacting.'" Because the danger one person's under-caution creates is "small to the individual but common to all," while that same person's other private risks (a car accident, say) may loom larger in their own life than in society's, individually "rational" self-interest and collectively necessary precaution come apart — and the paper argues the ethical duty falls on the collective side.

## In the Book

The paper names its own logic against a familiar category: "This is similar to other tragedies of the common, except that there is life and death" at stake. It contrasts personal risk rankings with society's: an individual may reasonably rank their own car-accident risk above their epidemic risk, but "under such conditions it becomes selfish, even psychopathic, to act according to 'rational' behavior" if that private ranking is what drives their conduct during a systemic emergency. The paper's stated conclusion is explicit about where responsibility lands: "the prudent and ethical course of action for all individuals is to enact systemic precaution at the individual and local scale" — precaution isn't just permitted at the local level, it is the ethical requirement placed on every individual once systemic risk is in play.

## Why It Matters

This concept draws a firm distinction between prudence (avoiding risk to yourself) and ethics (avoiding risk you impose on others), and locates the switch between them precisely at the point where risk becomes systemic rather than personal. It gives a non-naive basis for precautionary obligations — they apply not to risk in general, but specifically to the subset of decisions where your own risk tolerance becomes an externality on a shared, multiplicative system — which is exportable to any domain where individual restraint is the only lever available to prevent collective ruin (public health, financial systemic risk, environmental commons).
