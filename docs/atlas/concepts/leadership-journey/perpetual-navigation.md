# Perpetual Navigation: Real-Time Strategy Over Annual Planning

## Definition

Perpetual navigation treats strategy as an ongoing discipline, not an annual event. The leader stays grounded in present reality while keeping gaze on the horizon, continuously making small course corrections as conditions change. It replaces the illusion of a "perfect" plan with the practice of a "good enough" plan executed with agility and adjusted in real time.

## In the Book

Burnison critiques the static planning model directly: "I've always been amused by the arcane annual off-sites—they are only 11 months too late. Strategic thinking is far less about planning in the calm than it is about decision making and course correcting in the midst of the storm."

He illustrates the principle with an emergency aircraft example: a pilot avoiding a mid-air collision through a 600-foot emergency dive. While the collision couldn't be anticipated, the pilot could execute immediately because training had pre-anticipated the contingency. Similarly, leaders must "anticipate the problem well in advance" while remaining ready to navigate the unexpected "in the moment."

The book offers a practical formula: "It is better to have a strategy that is 75 percent perfect, but 100 percent executable, than a strategy that is 100 percent perfect, but only 75 percent executable." This captures the core insight—perfect plans that don't adapt fail; imperfect plans that course-correct succeed.

Burnison describes Korn Ferry's response to the 2008 recession: having anticipated economic downturn in advance, they made decisive adjustments immediately—completing strategic acquisitions, launching new initiatives, and positioning for growth while others scrambled.

## Why It Matters

This model replaces the false sense of control (we have a plan, we will execute it) with realistic adaptability (we have a direction, we will navigate toward it). It enables faster response to market shifts, unexpected competition, or internal learning that reveals planning assumptions to be wrong. It also distributes strategy-making throughout the organization—if strategy is a continuous conversation, not a quarterly cascade, frontline teams can contribute real-time observations. The shift from "planning" to "navigating" acknowledges that the future is unknowable while making the present actionable.
