# The Shepherd Leader: Leading from Multiple Positions

## Definition

The shepherd leader model rejects the heroic single-position leadership stance (always in front, always visible). Instead, the shepherd varies position relative to the team: in front to set direction and pace, beside to collaborate and connect, and mostly behind to keep others aligned and enable their success. The leader's focus is always on others' achievement, not the leader's visibility.

## In the Book

Burnison frames leadership through a shepherd metaphor: "As a shepherd, the leader keeps others aligned, sometimes by walking in front, sometimes behind, and sometimes beside." He emphasizes that despite the varying positions, "The leader's focus is always on others."

The book extends this with a sports analogy: "The leader can't be the star player, scoring all the points. Rather, the leader is committed to helping others do their best... you facilitate from the sidelines, but you are never removed from the front line, ultimately accountable for the results of the team. That's why it's the players who win games, but the coaches who lose them."

The shepherd positioning also addresses a subtle aspect of leadership: when people view you as a function (not a person), being "behind" paradoxically gives them the clarity and space they need. You are not the spectacle; the work is.

## Why It Matters

This model inverts a common trap: the assumption that visibility and centrality are leadership. In reality, leader-centric positioning creates bottlenecks, disempowers the team, and makes people dependent on the leader's presence. The shepherd model keeps people focused on the goal (not the leader), distributes decision-making (not everything flows through you), and scales the organization beyond the leader's bandwidth. It also inoculates against the myth that leadership is about personal star power—it's about facilitating others' achievement.
