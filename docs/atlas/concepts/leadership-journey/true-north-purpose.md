# True North: Purpose as Fixed Navigational Point

## Definition

True North is the concept of purpose as a fixed, unmoving reference point that guides navigation through uncertainty. Just as sailors historically used Polaris to orient themselves when landmarks disappeared, leaders use organizational purpose (the "why") to make decisions when circumstances shift. It is the constant that allows strategy (the "how") and vision (the "what") to adapt without the organization losing direction.

## In the Book

Burnison frames the chapter through an explicit metaphor: "A bright point in the night sky—the star, Polaris, the astronomical 'True North'—has guided explorers, adventurers, and travelers from time immemorial... Before charting any course, travelers must orient first to their own True North to find their bearings."

He describes the relationship between purpose, vision, and strategy: "With a clear and compelling purpose, you set the strategy and gather the people who will join you on the journey." The "why" precedes the "what" and "how." He notes that leaders often "jump ahead to the 'how' and forget the 'why'—in other words, the organization's purpose."

The book emphasizes the stabilizing function of purpose: "At every juncture purpose is your fixed point—your True North—by which you navigated." Because long journeys encounter detours, reversals, and course corrections, a fixed purpose prevents the organization from drifting into opportunism or losing coherence. "The more people embrace the purpose, the more likely they will follow—not only at the beginning when adrenaline is pumping and excitement is running high, like at the start of a marathon, but later on when the going gets tough."

## Why It Matters

Purpose as True North creates three effects: First, it enables strategy to be adaptive without becoming random—courses correct around the constant. Second, it creates alignment without requiring constant top-down management—everyone can make local decisions if they navigate toward the same star. Third, it sustains motivation on long journeys where the destination feels abstract and slow—people return to purpose when doubt sets in. Without True North, organizations become weather-vanes, responding to every external pressure; with it, they navigate toward genuine north even through fog and storm.
