# Problem Reframing

## Definition

Problem reframing is the action learning mechanism by which a group moves from the problem as initially presented (often a symptom or surface description) to the true root problem that must be solved. Through the reflective inquiry process—asking clarifying questions before jumping to solutions—the group reaches consensus on the core issue. The reframed problem may differ substantially from the original presenting problem, revealing that the organization or individual misunderstood what actually needed to be solved.

## In the Book

Marquardt illustrates this with the "Seven Blind Men and the Elephant" parable (Chapter 4). Each blind man touches a different part of the elephant and names it differently—a leaf, a tree trunk, a rope, a wall, a snake, a bag, a spear. The point: seven people encountering the same thing perceive it entirely differently because they come to reality with different experiences, ages, genders, and backgrounds. In action learning, this means the first step in solving any problem is to be sure the group knows what the problem actually is.

The book states: "After a series of questions, the group, often with the guidance of the action learning coach, will reach a consensus as to the most critical and important problem that the group should work on and will establish the crux of the problem, which may differ from the original presenting problem" (Chapter 1). The stages of action learning explicitly name "Reframing the problem" as distinct from "Presentation of problem"—the group first asks questions to gather information, then reaches consensus on the reframed problem before determining goals and developing strategies.

This reframing prevents the organization from solving the wrong problem. Marquardt notes that once the reframed problem is clear, the group can determine "the goal, the achievement of which would solve the reframed problem for the long term."

## Why It Matters

Most problem-solving fails not because the solution is weak but because the group solved the wrong problem—they treated a symptom as if it were the disease. Reframing embeds a discipline: delay solutions until the problem is fully understood by asking questions from diverse perspectives. This shifts the cost from implementing many failed solutions to investing time in clarifying the real issue. In complex systems, where interventions have unintended consequences, reframing often reveals that the "obvious" problem has root causes far deeper than initially visible.
