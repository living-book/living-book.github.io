# The Action Learning Coach: Catalyzing Learning Over Solutions

## Definition

The action learning coach is a dedicated facilitator whose sole responsibility is to optimize the group's learning and capability development, not to contribute to problem-solving. The coach is fundamentally different from a chairperson, project manager, or subject-matter expert. The coach has explicit organizational authority to pause problem-solving work and intervene with reflective questions whenever she perceives an opportunity for group learning. This power is necessary because, without it, the urgency of the problem will always overwhelm the importance of development.

## In the Book

Marquardt opens Chapter 7 by describing the coach as "the catalyst who optimizes the power of action learning as well as the questioner who accelerates the learning. She is the synergizer who helps to bond the group members. The action learning coach is the servant leader who enhances the group's ability to learn and to take vigorous action."

The key distinction: "The key role of the coach is to optimize the group's ability to learn and thereby become more capable to quickly and innovatively solve the problem. The focus of the coach must always remain on the learning, not the problem."

Marquardt explains why a designated person with power is essential: "To maximize individual and group learning, one person must be designated to focus exclusively on that task. Problem solvers (i.e., the group members) are focused, as they should be, on the urgent problem. The urgency of the problem always overwhelms the importance of learning."

The coach operates only through questions: "The action learning coach only asks questions. Why is it so important for the coach to only ask questions? First, questions are always more powerful and valuable than statements in helping others to reflect and to learn." Questions avoid the group becoming dependent, defensive, or misdirected by the coach's judgments. Questions empower the group to make its own meaning.

The coach controls the environment: manages time allocation (ensuring final 10–15 minutes are reserved for learning capture), retains absolute control during interventions (the group stops and listens until the coach indicates resumption), and sets the climate of psychological safety—"a practice field" where vulnerability, risk, and learning are safe.

## Why It Matters

Most organizations fail to protect learning from urgency because they embed the learning responsibility with the problem-solvers—a structural guarantee it will be deprioritized. Making learning someone's full job, giving that person authority, and requiring question-based facilitation creates accountability for development. The coach role model applies wherever capability-building must coexist with delivery: the distinction between "doing the work" and "improving how we do the work" requires separate attention and power. It also models a leadership style—questioning, listening, serving group learning—that participants can adopt in their own roles.
