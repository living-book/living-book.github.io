# Six Components of Action Learning

## Definition

Marquardt's framework identifies six critical, interdependent components that must operate together for action learning to reach its full power. These are: (1) a significant, urgent problem; (2) a diverse group of 4–8 people; (3) a reflective inquiry process emphasizing questions; (4) real action the group can implement; (5) a commitment to learning alongside problem-solving; and (6) an action learning coach with authority to intervene on learning. All six must function simultaneously; omitting any one dramatically diminishes the process's capacity to generate both breakthrough solutions and transformative development.

## In the Book

Marquardt opens Chapter 1 by stating that "action learning has six components, each of which is described briefly here and presented in greater detail over the next six chapters." The problem must be important, urgent, and within the group's responsibility to solve (Chapter 2). The group should have diverse backgrounds and ideally 4–8 members to balance perspective generation with communication feasibility (Chapter 3). Questions and reflective listening form the core inquiry process, not statements and advocacy (Chapter 4). Action taken outside sessions—testing strategies, implementing, learning from results—is essential; "there is no real meaningful or practical learning until action is taken and reflected on" (Chapter 5). Learning commitment means the group prioritizes understanding underlying causes and developing capabilities as seriously as solving the immediate problem (Chapter 6). Finally, the coach focuses exclusively on facilitating learning and group reflection, using questions to help members examine their assumptions and develop new competencies (Chapter 7).

The book emphasizes that these six components create synergy: "Action learning power is at its peak when all six of these components are in operation, that is, when there is both learning and action."

## Why It Matters

The six components model shows that problem-solving and development are not competing goals—they amplify each other when all elements operate together. This framework can be imported into any domain facing complex challenges: the structure guarantees that solutions emerge from diverse perspectives, that participants develop rather than exhaust themselves, and that knowledge spreads across the organization. Organizations using this model report not just problems solved, but sustained competitive advantage through the leadership and learning capabilities built in group members.
