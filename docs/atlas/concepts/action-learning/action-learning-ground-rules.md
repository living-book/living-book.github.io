# Action Learning Ground Rules

## Definition

Action learning operates on two non-negotiable ground rules that shift group dynamics from statement-driven problem-solving to question-driven inquiry and learning. The first rule is: statements can be made only in response to questions. The second rule is: the action learning coach has the explicit power to intervene to improve group performance and learning. Together, these rules protect the "important" (learning) from being drowned by the "urgent" (problem-solving).

## In the Book

Marquardt establishes these rules in Chapter 1 and develops them throughout Chapters 4 and 7. On the first rule, he explains that while questions may not increase the number of statements (since each question generates multiple responses), requiring "questions first" transforms the natural impulse to advocate. This forces members to listen first, clarify before solving, and recognize that "there is almost a direct correlation between the number and quality of questions and the eventual final quality of the actions and learnings."

On the second rule, Marquardt notes that without designated power given to the person who focuses on learning, "the urgency of the problem will always cause the group to push back and/or ignore the person who is focused on the importance of the learning." The coach intervenes by pausing problem-solving, asking reflective questions, and resuming only when learning is captured. Crucially, the coach retains full control during interventions; once a group successfully ignores the coach, regaining authority becomes nearly impossible.

Marquardt stresses that these rules feel alien at first but become quickly embraced once groups experience their benefits. They "recapture their natural way of communicating and learning as young children before the impulse was inhibited by adults."

## Why It Matters

These ground rules solve a universal problem: groups naturally sacrifice depth for speed. Under pressure, teams default to telling instead of asking, advocacy instead of inquiry. These rules institutionalize reflection and prevent the tyranny of the urgent from eliminating the important. They also democratize authority—the best question may come from the lowest-ranked person, and the rule structure ensures all voices get heard. This model applies wherever groups must balance immediate delivery with sustainable capability-building.
