# Tacit-to-Explicit Knowledge Conversion

## Definition

Marquardt identifies a critical organizational failure: most valuable expertise remains locked inside individuals' minds (tacit knowledge) and never spreads. Action learning deliberately converts this tacit knowledge—expertise, memories, beliefs, and insights—into explicit knowledge available to teams and the organization. This conversion happens through the reflective inquiry process, the emphasis on both action and learning, and the coach's questioning approach, which forces individuals to externalize what they know but have never articulated.

## In the Book

Marquardt devotes significant space in Chapter 6 to tacit versus explicit knowledge, drawing on Nonaka (1994). "Many organizations fail to tap the rich knowledge base or intellectual capital present in the brains of their own employees—what Nonaka (1994) calls 'tacit knowledge' in contrast to 'explicit knowledge,' which already exists outside the internal knowledge of an individual."

Sources of tacit knowledge include "an individual's expertise, memories, beliefs, and insights. This kind of knowledge is usually difficult to communicate or explain and, as a result, is used only by the individual; thus, its potential benefits to the organization are minimal."

The action learning solution: "Action learning, however, through the reflective inquiry process, the focus on both action and learning, and the questioning approach of the coach converts this tacit knowledge to explicit knowledge so that it becomes available for the team and ultimately for the organization to utilize, resulting in tremendous new assets and capabilities for both."

The mechanism works because questions force externalization. When a coach asks, "What did you observe?" or "Why do you think that approach would work?", the individual is forced to say aloud what was implicit. Once said aloud in front of peers, the knowledge becomes shareable and recordable.

## Why It Matters

Most organizations hemorrhage institutional knowledge when experts leave or retire. The tacit-to-explicit conversion mechanism shows that knowledge capital is not created by hiring smart people—it's created by building structures that force experts to externalize their reasoning. This applies far beyond action learning: any organizational learning system must address how silent expertise becomes documented capability. The mechanism is dialogue, not documentation—people externalize through being questioned, not through writing manuals.
