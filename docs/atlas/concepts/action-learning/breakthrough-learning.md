# Breakthrough Learning: Transformative Development

## Definition

Marquardt distinguishes three levels of learning achieved through action learning, with breakthrough learning at the deepest level. It is learning that goes beyond intellectual understanding or skill application to alter fundamental beliefs, values, and assumptions. Breakthrough learning touches on what psychologist Robert Kegan calls transformation—a shift in how one makes sense of the world, not just what one knows within that worldview.

## In the Book

In Chapter 1 and Chapter 6, Marquardt cites Weinstein (1995) to define three levels: "(1) understanding something intellectually, (2) applying a newly acquired skill, (3) experiencing and thereby undergoing an inner development that touches on beliefs and attitudes and leads to personal and professional development." He notes that "action learning is particularly effective at this third level since it provides the opportunity for internal dissonance, while the problem and/or action may provide the external trigger."

Marquardt elaborates: "In action learning we become more aware of our blind spots and weaknesses as well as our strengths; and we receive the feedback and help that we have requested. Action learning generates tremendous personal, intellectual, psychological, and social growth."

He cites Butterfield, Gold, and Willis (1998): "action learning participants experience 'breakthrough learning' when they become aware of the need to reach beyond their conscious beliefs and to challenge their assumptions about their present worldviews. This readiness to change and grow is a prerequisite for development and continuous improvement."

The mechanism is challenge within support: the reflective inquiry process questions deeply held assumptions; the group provides psychological safety to explore unfamiliar mental models. Failures, crises, and moments of being "stuck" become openings for this level of learning if the coach helps the group reflect on what changed in their thinking.

## Why It Matters

Skills can be taught; transformation cannot be transmitted directly—it must be experienced. Action learning creates the conditions (real stakes, diverse perspectives, guided reflection, supportive group) where people naturally undergo deep learning. This explains why action learning reports often mention participant accounts like "I see the problem differently now" or "I realized I'd been assuming the customer didn't care," rather than just "I learned how to run a meeting." The model applies wherever sustained capability requires people to think differently, not just do differently.
