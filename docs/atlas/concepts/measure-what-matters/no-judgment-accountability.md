# No-Judgment Accountability

## Definition

The third OKR "superpower": objectives and key results are living instruments, not "set them and forget them" targets — they're revisited through periodic check-ins, self-graded on a numeric or color scale, and revised or replaced mid-cycle if a key result is off track. Critically, the grading and self-assessment are decoupled from punishment. Doerr's example shows a rep who hit only 70 percent of a "bring in ten new customers" key result but self-scored the effort a 0.9, because market conditions made even that partial result exceptional — the number itself matters less than the contextual discussion it prompts. "There are no judgments, only learnings."

## In the Book

Chapter 10 traces what happens when tracking is absent: a Fortune 500 company that tried moving 82,000 employees from annual goals to quarterly OKRs in Word documents generated 328,000 disconnected files a year — technically "public," but functionally invisible, producing "zombie OKRs" nobody checks. AOL's CTO Bill Pence describes pre-OKR goals that "never really had a home" connecting to daily work. The fix the book documents is dedicated tracking software plus a designated "OKR shepherd" — Google's Jonathan Rosenberg sent public emails naming product managers who hadn't filed their OKRs, using peer visibility rather than formal discipline to drive near-100-percent compliance. Chapter 11 (the Gates Foundation story) shows the same tracking discipline applied to public-health goals, where an endangered key result triggers action to revise the approach, not to assign blame for the miss.

## Why It Matters

A tracking system only produces better outcomes if the signal it generates — an off-track score, a missed milestone — leads to a change in behavior rather than a search for who to blame. Stripping judgment out of the feedback loop is what keeps the loop open: people report bad news early and honestly only when doing so doesn't cost them, which is a precondition for any control system, human or mechanical, to actually correct course before failure is irreversible.
