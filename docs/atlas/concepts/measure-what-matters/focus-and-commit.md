# Focus and Commit to Priorities

## Definition

The first OKR "superpower": leaders must make hard choices among competing priorities, and the OKR itself is the precision tool that communicates the choice. An organization typically limits itself to three to five objectives per cycle, each with no more than five key results — a hard cap meant to force trade-offs rather than let goal lists sprawl into a wish list that means nothing is actually prioritized.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 opens with Doerr's dictum that "leaders must be evangelists for the mission" and that OKRs make ambiguity about what matters most impossible — either you did the thing on the sheet or you didn't. Chapter 5 grounds this in the Remind story: Brett Kopf, a founder with attention deficit disorder, credits OKRs with giving him an external structure to filter the constant stream of ideas a founder generates, so the company could commit to the few that counted rather than chase all of them. Chapter 6 tells the Nuna story, where Jini Kim used OKRs to hold her health-data company to a single-minded commitment — building better health care systems using Medicaid data — instead of drifting into adjacent, easier opportunities. Across all three chapters the emphasis is not on ambition but on subtraction: focus requires as much saying no as saying yes.

## Why It Matters

Any system — a person, a team, an organism — has finite attention and finite capacity, and a list of goals that is too long is functionally a list with no goals at all, because nothing gets the resources to actually happen. Naming what will NOT be pursued alongside what will is the mechanism that converts a wish list into an executable plan, whether the domain is corporate strategy, personal habit formation, or research agenda-setting.
