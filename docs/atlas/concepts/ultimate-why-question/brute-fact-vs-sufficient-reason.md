# Brute Fact vs. Sufficient Reason

## Definition

Every attempt to answer "why is there something rather than nothing?" first has to take a side on a prior question: does existence itself require a reason at all? One camp treats the fact that something exists (and always has) as a brute fact — true, but not the kind of thing that has or needs an explanation. The other camp holds, with Leibniz, that "nothing exists for which a sufficient reason of its existence cannot be given," which makes the ultimate why question not just askable but mandatory.

## In the Book

Wippel's introduction opens the entire volume on this fork: "For some philosophers, that something now exists and therefore that something has always existed is simply a brute fact and needs no explanation. Hence this question should not even be raised. For many other philosophers, however, the question is legitimate, interesting, and worth pursuing." Every subsequent chapter is a variation on the sufficient-reason side of this split — the volume never seriously develops the brute-fact position beyond naming it, which is itself a notable asymmetry in how the collection is built.

Daniel Dahlstrom's chapter on Leibniz and Heidegger (Ch. 6) traces the sufficient-reason side to its historical source: Leibniz's early formulation "nothing is without reason" (c. 1671), sharpened by 1677 into "nothing exists for which a sufficient reason of its existence cannot be given," where "sufficient" means the full aggregate of everything requisite for a thing to exist. Heidegger later reads this not as a claim about beings but as a claim about what it means to be at all — arguing that the unrestricted, modern pursuit of reasons and grounds (Grund) has come to eclipse another, more primordial kind of ground (Boden), the soil essential to human flourishing.

## Why It Matters

This is the fork that determines whether a domain of inquiry even has a "why" question waiting to be asked. Treating a starting condition as a brute fact closes off explanation-seeking; treating it as owed a sufficient reason opens an entire research program (and, per Heidegger, a "technological-scientific construction of the world" that keeps pushing for more reasons). The same choice recurs anywhere a field has to decide whether a foundational given — an axiom, an initial condition, a base rate — is simply accepted or is itself a target for further explanation.
