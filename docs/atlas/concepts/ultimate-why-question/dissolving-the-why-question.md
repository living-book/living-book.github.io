# Dissolving the Why Question

## Definition

Rather than answer "why is there something rather than nothing?", this move rejects the question itself as ill-formed. For a why-question to mean anything, it needs a genuine alternative to the state of affairs it asks about. "Absolutely nothing" fails to supply one: any attempt to specify it either smuggles in some content (making it a something, not a nothing) or specifies nothing at all — in which case no alternative has actually been proposed, and the "question" collapses into an unclear assertion rather than a real question.

## In the Book

Brian Martine makes this the opening move of Chapter 10 ("Pragmatic Reflections on Final Causality"): "If the 'ultimate why question' is taken to mean asking why there is something rather than nothing, where the expression 'nothing' is supposed to mean 'nothing at all' or 'absolutely nothing,' I don't think the question is worthy even of being called useless. It is simply meaningless. Proposing 'absolutely nothing' as the alternative to 'something' is the same as saying that there is no alternative to 'something.'" Where no alternative is proposed, he argues, "what was formulated as a question turns out to be an assertion, the assertion of something or other" — mysterious in content but not actually interrogative in form.

Martine traces this logical puzzle back to Parmenides, who used essentially the same reasoning to reject the possibility of change (becoming would require something emerging from or vanishing into nothing, which is unintelligible) and concluded that "philosophy stops as soon as it starts." Martine doesn't fully embrace Parmenides's conclusion — he goes on to rehabilitate a *restricted* form of the why-question ("why this state of affairs rather than some other?") answerable through final causality grounded in practical human ends — but the dissolution of the *unrestricted* ultimate question stands as his chapter's central move, distinguishing his response sharply from every other contributor in the volume, who all treat the unrestricted question as meaningful even when they can't fully answer it.

## Why It Matters

Not every hard-looking question deserves an answer; some deserve a diagnosis of why they were malformed in the first place. This move — checking whether a question's presupposed alternative can actually be specified — generalizes far past metaphysics: it's the same test that exposes false dichotomies, loaded comparisons ("compared to what?"), and requests for explanation where no coherent counterfactual baseline exists. Recognizing when a question needs dissolving rather than answering saves effort that would otherwise go into producing an answer to something that was never really asking anything.
