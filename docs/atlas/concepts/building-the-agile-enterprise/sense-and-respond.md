# Sense and Respond

## Definition

Sense and Respond is both a cultural orientation and an operational capability. It is the commitment that all employees serve as "the eyes and ears of the enterprise" to sense threats and opportunities—in financial markets, competitor actions, technology advances, regulatory changes, supply-chain disruptions, and internal innovations. Critically, the enterprise must also be "prepared to respond to mitigate the threat or exploit the opportunity," implying both early detection and swift, authorized action.

## In the Book

Cummins lists "Sense and respond" as a key cultural objective for agile enterprises. The specific phrasing—that all employees "should be the eyes and ears of the enterprise"—signals that sensing is not the responsibility of a single strategy department but is distributed across the organization. People closest to customers, operations, and suppliers often detect signals first. The book later dedicates Chapter 9 to "Sensing and Responding to Events and Circumstances," proposing a "sense and respond directory" to record events, resolve alert duplication, and route issues to appropriate responders.

This is distinct from reactive firefighting. Sensing encompasses business intelligence and analytics that reveal emerging patterns, observations by individuals, and both internal innovations and external ecosystem events. The respond side includes escalation mechanisms based on scope and severity, formal resolution tracking, and integration with transformation management and product lifecycle management. The implication is that sense and respond is not ad hoc; it is an architected system within the enterprise.

## Why It Matters

Most enterprises are structured for stability and efficiency—they execute plans. Sense and respond reorients them for adaptation. By distributing sensing responsibility, enterprises exploit local knowledge rather than waiting for information to bubble up the hierarchy. By architecting response mechanisms, they avoid the trap of employees who sense problems but lack authority or channels to act on them. This makes the organization genuinely adaptive rather than reactive, capable of evolving strategy in real time rather than discovering misalignment only when quarterly results arrive. In rapidly changing environments, this capability is often the difference between survival and disruption.
