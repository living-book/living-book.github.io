# Team Purpose Craft

## Definition

Team purpose is composed of three distinct, mutually reinforcing elements: a product vision that describes the desired future created by the product; a team mission that clarifies the team's unique contribution toward realizing that future; and mission tests that specify measurable indicators of progress toward mission achievement. Together, these three elements provide both inspiration (why the work matters) and concrete success criteria (how to know if the team is winning).

## In the Book

Larsen and Nies ground purpose in motivation research, citing Daniel Pink's finding that autonomy, mastery, and purpose are primary human motivators, and Katzenbach and Smith's observation that common purpose is the top characteristic of effective teams.

The product vision describes how the product will change the user's world and what problem it solves for which people. For example: "Customer service representatives will experience their work as highly productive and no longer engage in wasteful, tedious work."

The team mission clarifies what the team will uniquely accomplish and establishes five critical pieces: the customers, the team's actions and outcomes, the deliverable, differentiating attributes, and value to the customer. It answers "what is the work?" where the vision answers "why does it matter?"

Mission tests measure internal progress (what the team will learn: "downward trends for defect rates") and external progress (customer outcomes: "customer satisfaction rates" or "market penetration"). The System Tracking Project case study shows how a mission test emerged from team conversation: support reps' concern about learning time became "the new system should be easy for support reps to learn and use."

The book emphasizes that purpose provides the container for the work. It reinforces group identity and clarifies boundaries for tactical choices. Purpose is the most stable element of the charter—least likely to change frequently—yet it evolves as the team learns more about customer and business needs.

## Why It Matters

Explicitly crafting purpose transforms abstract business mandates into a shared rallying point that people can commit to and use to guide daily decisions. When team members understand the vision (why), the mission (what), and success criteria (how they'll know), they can exercise judgment instead of waiting for instructions. Purpose also allows the team to push back when asked to do work misaligned with the mission, and it lets product managers reprioritize the backlog with team consent when reality shifts.
