# Five Rules of Learning

## Definition

The Five Rules of Learning are a framework developed by Willem Larsen and Diana Larsen for enhancing and recognizing learning wherever it takes place. Applied during liftoff events, these rules help maximize the conditions under which humans learn together in groups.

## In the Book

Larsen and Nies introduce the Five Rules as essential principles for designing effective liftoff experiences. Knowledge workers in particular must continuously learn and apply what they've learned in novel situations, making learning conditions critical to team success.

The five rules are:

**Keep it alive.** Aliveness generates energy for learning. Peak learning experiences—whether physical, mental, or emotional—feel deeply nourishing. Things that enhance aliveness include: a sense of urgency or intense need; relationships and intimate connections; silliness, play, humor, and anything cute; and feeling successful or achieving mastery of a new skill. A liftoff that incorporates these elements produces deeper engagement than a purely informational meeting.

**Do it for real.** Learning happens most powerfully when the stakes matter. Playing a game in which no one cares about winning produces surface engagement; having the same game determine a real decision creates intrinsic motivation and focused attention.

**Start obvious, stay obvious.** Learning is fastest when goals and progress are visible. Making the agenda visible on flip charts and keeping it posted throughout the event helps participants track both the plan and their progress against it. Vague, hidden objectives leave people confused about whether they're on track.

**Focus on flow.** Flow emerges when the challenge level is matched to skill level and the activity has clear feedback. Unbalanced challenges (too easy = boredom, too hard = anxiety) prevent flow and learning. Activities designed to move naturally from one to another, with clear transitions, sustain better engagement than disjointed segments.

**Put setting first.** The physical and psychological environment shapes learning. Natural light, high ceilings, adequate space, availability of food and water, comfortable seating, and the ability for people to move around all influence learning capacity. A cramped, fluorescent-lit conference room in a basement produces fundamentally different results than a bright room with views and space to work in small groups.

The SAPHIR case study illustrates these principles in practice: the facilitator created aliveness by having participants get to know one another through multiple pair-introduction rounds; he made things real by focusing on genuine business challenges; he kept goals obvious by displaying them on flip charts throughout; he managed flow through a deliberate sequence from getting-to-know-each-other to business case to interactive work; and he attended carefully to setting, choosing a restaurant for evening work that provided privacy and cultural atmosphere.

## Why It Matters

Understanding these five rules shifts the focus from "did we cover the content" to "did people learn?" They make explicit what experienced facilitators do intuitively, allowing anyone to design more powerful learning experiences. The rules apply not just to liftoff events but to any team setting where learning is the goal—retrospectives, problem-solving sessions, and product discovery all benefit from attention to these conditions.
