# Team Alignment Through Working Agreements

## Definition

Alignment is the second critical element of an agile charter. It helps the team create an alliance to achieve the mission through three mechanisms: establishing simple rules (which sustain coherence), forming the core team (which accelerates collaboration), and creating explicit working agreements (which ease daily work). Working agreements are negotiated commitments about how people will interact, make decisions, and handle conflicts—made visible and revisited regularly.

## In the Book

Larsen and Nies present alignment as the middle element in stability—more stable than context (which is constantly recreated), but less stable than purpose (which guides for years). Alignment holds as long as the team stays intact and working together.

The book emphasizes that simple rules give teams directional guidance without dictating every action. Examples of simple rules include focusing on the whole system and the relationships between parts, working collaboratively to enable discovery, practicing "Good Enough for Now" to resist perfectionism, beginning each endeavor in a context of possibility, and learning more as work unfolds.

Working agreements are explicitly created during liftoff chartering sessions and become visible artifacts posted in the team space. They cover topics like communication norms (which devices are acceptable during meetings), decision authority (who decides what, when), meeting protocols (start and end times, agenda-setting), code review expectations, and conflict resolution approaches. The Power of Creating a Vision case study shows a critical alignment element: after a team of agile experts had been "paralyzed" for six months, bringing business and technical people together to establish "rules of how team members would engage with each other" proved to be the turning point.

Alignment also includes clarifying roles and responsibilities. The book notes that it's not the outstanding performance of individual experts that counts, but the joint success of the entire team—which becomes possible only when role boundaries are clear and interdependencies are acknowledged.

## Why It Matters

Explicit working agreements replace invisible assumptions and political navigation with clarity that everyone can reference. When disagreements arise, the team can say "this violates our agreement" instead of engaging in personality conflict. Simple rules guide decisions without micromanagement, allowing teams to move fast on routine judgments while escalating genuinely novel situations. Clear role clarity allows team members to understand whose approval is needed, what decisions they can make alone, and how their work connects to others'.
