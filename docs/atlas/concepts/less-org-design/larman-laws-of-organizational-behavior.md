# Larman's Laws of Organizational Behavior

## Definition

Four empirical patterns that explain why change initiatives fail in organizations. Rather than describing how change *should* work, they describe how change *actually* fails: organizations are implicitly optimized to avoid changing power structures; new terminology gets redefined to preserve the status quo; change is labeled "impractical" to deflect from real weaknesses; and culture follows structure, not the reverse.

## In the Book

Larman presents the four laws as a direct challenge to optimistic change management. The first law states: "Organizations are implicitly optimized to avoid changing the status quo middle- and first-level manager and specialist positions & power structures." This is not a conspiracy—it's structural. The second and third laws follow as corollaries: "any change initiative will be reduced to overloading or redefining the new terminology to mean basically the same as status quo," and any initiative "will be derided as 'purist', 'theoretical', and 'needing pragmatic customization for local concerns'—which deflects from addressing weaknesses and manager/specialist status quo." The fourth law—"Culture follows structure"—is the mechanism: changing words without changing structure leaves culture untouched. Larman applies these explicitly to fake Lean, fake Scrum, fake Kanban, and fake DevOps, showing how each gets absorbed into existing power structures rather than replacing them.

## Why It Matters

These laws separate genuine transformation from performative change. They explain why adopting a new framework or terminology without restructuring fails—not due to implementation error, but by design. Understanding them makes it possible to diagnose whether a change effort is truly addressing organizational structures or merely layering new language over old incentives. This shifts energy from blaming people for "not getting it" to examining whether the structure actually rewards what the initiative claims to ask for.
