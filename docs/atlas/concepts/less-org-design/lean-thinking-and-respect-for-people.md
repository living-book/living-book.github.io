# Lean Thinking: Respect for People and Continuous Improvement

## Definition

Lean thinking is an approach to sustainable product development grounded in two interdependent commitments: respect for people (developing long-term great engineers, providing mentoring and safety, building teams that evolve their own practices) and continuous improvement (through kaizen, Go See, small relentless experiments, and asking "Why?" five times to root causes rather than accepting top-down solutions).

## In the Book

Larman draws on Toyota's philosophy directly: "Build people, then build products" and "The root of the Toyota Way is to be dissatisfied with the status quo; you have to ask constantly, 'Why are we doing this?'" He contrasts two models: one in which "lean consultants identify problems and plan the solutions" from above (the antithesis of lean); and the Toyota model where "each individual employee is given the opportunity to find problems in his own way of working, to solve them and to make improvements." Larman emphasizes "job safety, but not role safety"—organizations keep people employed during transition but don't guarantee specific titles or functions; roles change as the system improves. The mechanics include Go See (direct observation at the Gemba, not reports), kaizen (small, relentless improvement), 5 Whys (getting to root cause), team room visualization, set-based concurrent development, and retrospectives. All of these are continuous, not episodic—they are embedded in how work happens.

## Why It Matters

This principle reframes organizational improvement from a top-down, expert-driven process to a bottom-up, distributed learning system. It explains why command-and-control, even well-intentioned, underperforms—frontline people have the most detailed knowledge of problems and potential solutions. Respect for people is not softness; it is structural pragmatism: organizations that develop and trust their people to improve their own work outperform those that treat improvement as something management imposes.
