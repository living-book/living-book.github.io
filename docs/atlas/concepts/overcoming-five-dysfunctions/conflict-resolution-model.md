# Conflict Resolution Model: Mining Obstacles Beyond the Issue

## Definition

The Conflict Resolution Model is a diagnostic framework that maps layers of obstacles preventing a team from engaging with the actual issue during conflict or decision-making. The model identifies four classes of obstacles, arranged from the core (easiest to address: the issue itself and informational disagreements about it) outward to the periphery (hardest to address: individual personality or capability gaps). Teams use the model retroactively on past difficult conversations to identify which obstacles, beyond the substantive issue, prevented resolution — and then design future discussions to neutralize those obstacles first.

## In the Book

Lencioni presents the Conflict Resolution Model (pp. 124–127) as a visual diagram with concentric circles: at the center, "the issue" surrounded by "facts, opinions, perspectives"; moving outward are four obstacle layers. **Informational obstacles** are closest to the issue — disagreements about facts, opinions, or perspectives. They are the most comfortable to discuss because they are directly relevant to the topic. **Environmental obstacles** include the physical setting, time pressure, the team's mood, organizational politics, or pending layoffs — anything about the atmosphere that distracts from the issue itself. **Relationship obstacles** involve history between people, unresolved past events, style clashes, reputational baggage, or position-based power dynamics that color how the issue is perceived. **Individual obstacles** are the furthest from the core — insufficient experience, IQ, knowledge, self-esteem, emotional intelligence (EQ), or misaligned values among participants. Lencioni provides a Conflict Resolution Exercise (pp. 128–129) in which a team picks a recent difficult issue, analyzes it against the model, and identifies which obstacles, beyond information, actually blocked resolution.

## Why It Matters

Most teams diagnose conflict failures as informational — "we disagree on the facts" — when the real barriers are environmental (bad timing, bad mood), relational (unspoken history), or individual (someone lacks the EQ or authority to move forward). This model teaches teams to ask "What prevented us from even getting to the real disagreement?" and to address those obstacles proactively. Before a high-stakes decision, a team might acknowledge, "The CFO is in a bad mood and we're short on time, so let's table this and revisit when conditions improve," rather than push through and misinterpret emotional defensiveness as logical objection. Or they might recognize, "We have untreated history here — let's get a neutral mediator." The model demystifies conflict by showing that resolution is often blocked not by genuine disagreement but by barriers you can name and manage.
