# Conflict Profiling: Understanding Team Conflict Tendencies

## Definition

Conflict Profiling is a 30–60 minute exercise (often paired with Behavioral Profiling) in which team members examine their individual conflict tendencies through two lenses: their MBTI or personality profile, and their personal history (family background, upbringing, cultural context, past experiences with conflict). Team members then share their conflict influences aloud, and the team discusses the collective pattern — areas of similarity and difference in how members approach disagreement. The exercise creates explicit understanding of why some team members may avoid conflict (because they were taught it was rude or destructive at home), why others may engage too aggressively (because they learned conflict was a competition to win), and how the team's collective conflict style may be skewed toward one end of the spectrum.

## In the Book

Lencioni describes Conflict Profiling (p. 122) as a precursor to Conflict Norming. The three-step exercise is: (1) have team members review their behavioral profile (from the MBTI exercise), highlighting conflict-specific implications; (2) have team members each share those implications along with other conflict influences in their lives (family experiences, cultural background, life experiences); (3) discuss the similarities and differences of the team in terms of the collective outlook on conflict, as well as the potential implications. For example, a team might discover that several members come from backgrounds where conflict was considered shameful or disloyal, and that this has created a team norm of avoiding disagreement. Or it might discover that the team is dominated by confrontational types and is missing voices that would push for consensus-building. The exercise is grounded in the observation that conflict is not a preference but a conditioned response — people bring their family patterns and cultural conditioning into the workplace, and unless those patterns are made visible, they will unconsciously drive team dynamics.

## Why It Matters

Conflict Profiling prevents the trap of treating conflict style as a character flaw rather than a learned pattern. A team member who avoids conflict is not "passive" or "weak" — they are likely replicating a family script that taught them conflict is dangerous. A team member who dominates debate is not "aggressive" or "domineering" — they are replicating a script that taught them to fight for what they believe in. By naming these patterns, Conflict Profiling creates psychological safety for people to experiment with different conflict approaches. It also reveals blind spots: a team dominated by conflict-avoiders will struggle to surface disagreements; a team of conflict-seekers will exhaust itself in debate and struggle with closure. Most importantly, it shifts the narrative from "you need to change your personality" to "you need to understand your instincts and choose differently when the team needs you to." A person who instinctively avoids conflict can learn to "enter the danger" if they understand it is a learned habit, not a core identity.
