# Superstitious Thinking

## Definition

Superstitious thinking is the widespread misunderstanding that circumstances, achievements, or external conditions cause our emotional states and experience of life. It's a flawed piece of logic so deeply embedded in culture that it feels like obvious truth: "I'll be happy when I get the promotion" or "You make me anxious." This superstition is the hidden hamster wheel—an addictive trap that generates stress, anxiety, and endless striving because the premise itself is false.

## In the Book

Smart identifies superstitious thinking as the central trap ensnaring millions. He calls it "the hidden hamster wheel"—subtle, pervasive, and so embedded in culture it appears in everything from children's books to leadership programs. The book names it explicitly: "Superstitious thinking, arising from the outside-in misunderstanding, is the only thing that ever keeps us from clarity."

Smart shows how this misunderstanding operates through two attribution errors. First: mistakenly attributing fulfilling experiences to circumstances ("I'll be successful once I get the promotion," "My dog makes me happy"). Second: mistakenly attributing uneasy feelings to circumstances ("I feel anxious about the job interview," "You make me angry"). These are the same misunderstanding wearing different masks. The book illustrates the cost: superstitious thinking leads to stress, worry, addiction, poverty, poor productivity, dissatisfaction, conflict, anxiety, mental illness, depression, burnout, bullying, criminality, and violence. The trap is so pervasive that people exhaust themselves pursuing the "visible solution" (changing circumstances) for the "invisible problem" (misunderstanding how experience works).

## Why It Matters

Seeing superstitious thinking for what it is—a misunderstanding, not a truth—removes its power. This is the first escape hatch: realizing you've been caught in a trap. Once you see how the trap works, the endless cycle of external striving becomes optional. This concept enables the shift from chasing circumstances to understanding how your mind actually works, which is why Smart says "seeing through a superstition can give you an 'out of proportion' increase in the results you get."
