# The Four Strands

## Definition

Deutsch's organizing thesis: four theories developed almost independently — quantum physics (the multiverse), Karl Popper's epistemology (knowledge grows by conjecture and refutation), Alan Turing's theory of computation (universality), and Richard Dawkins's theory of evolution (replicators and the selfish gene) — are each already the accepted, working theory in their field, yet each has an unexplained gap that makes it feel "cold" or incomplete on its own. Deutsch's claim is that the four close each other's gaps: none of the four strands can be fully understood in isolation, but taken together they form a single, non-hierarchical explanatory structure — the "fabric of reality" — in which no strand is more fundamental than the others.

## In the Book

Chapter 13 documents a recurring historical pattern: Everett's multiverse, Popper's epistemology, Turing's universality, and Dawkins's selfish-gene Darwinism have each become the *pragmatically* prevailing theory in their field (used routinely, rarely seriously rivaled) while being rejected or ignored *as explanations* — physicists use quantum theory's equations while disavowing what Everett showed they mean; the possibility of artificial intelligence, which follows straightforwardly from the Turing principle, remains "bitterly contested." Deutsch traces this to an "explanatory gap" in each strand taken alone: the Turing principle explains that a machine could do everything a brain does, but not consciousness or free will; Dawkins's theory explains adaptation but is accused of not explaining why the universe should be comprehensible enough for genes (or minds) to encode true information about it at all. He then shows each gap closing when another strand is invoked — free will becomes coherent once "the future" is understood via the multiverse's branching structure rather than classical spacetime's single fixed future (Chapter 11); the reliability of adaptation depends on the physical feasibility of virtual-reality rendering, which is itself a Turing-principle consequence; and quantum complexity theory is offered as the eventual home for a physical statement of Popper's conjecture-and-refutation pattern.

## Why It Matters

The four strands is a model for a specific, disciplined kind of unification: not reducing everything to one "master" theory (which Deutsch treats as a hallmark of reductionism, criticized already in Chapter 1), but showing that several independently-validated theories are mutually necessary — each is incomplete without the others, and none is more basic. This is a template for connecting fields that each work locally but feel philosophically unsatisfying in isolation: look for the specific gap each leaves unexplained, and ask whether a neighboring, independently-established theory closes exactly that gap. It offers a positive alternative to both naive reductionism (one strand swallows the rest) and postmodern relativism about incommensurable "paradigms" (Deutsch's target, via Kuhn, in this same chapter).
