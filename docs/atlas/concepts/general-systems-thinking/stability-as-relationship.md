# Stability Is a Relationship

## Definition

To call a system "stable," Weinberg argues, is to make two claims at once: a claim about the range of behaviors the system is allowed to display, and a claim about the range of disturbances the environment is expected to produce. Change stays "within certain limits" — a definition that already presupposes limits on both sides. Strip away either half (what counts as an acceptable response, or what counts as a plausible disturbance), and "stable" becomes meaningless.

## In the Book

Weinberg dismantles the everyday conflation of "stable" with "unchanging" using the Empire State Building, which visibly sways in wind yet is uncontroversially stable — stability means bounded change, not absence of it. He then attacks the idea that stability lives "in" a system independent of its environment: a tree in a dense forest grove partly derives its wind-resistance from the surrounding trees, so loggers who cut down "extra" trees to leave a few standing routinely watch the survivors blow down in the next storm — they mistook a relationship for a property. His starkest case is the passenger pigeon, which an 1857 Ohio state legislative committee deemed needing "no protection" from "ordinary destruction" because it existed in the billions — right up until extinction, because its reproduction depended nonlinearly on flock size (the birds would not breed below a threshold density), so a disturbance that should have been safely absorbed instead collapsed the population entirely. Weinberg also debunks the assumption that stability equals goodness with a coal mine that has been burning, highly stably, for fifty years, and shows the same situation can simultaneously be judged "stable and good," "stable and bad," "unstable and good," or "unstable and bad" depending on whose point of view is asked.

## Why It Matters

This concept blocks a specific, recurring error: judging something as stable — a company, an ecosystem, a political arrangement, a habit — by looking only inside it, when stability is actually a bet about what the environment will and won't throw at it. It reframes "why did this suddenly collapse" away from "it was secretly always fragile" and toward "the environment produced a disturbance outside the range the system's stability was ever defined for," and it separates the question of whether something is stable from the separate, value-laden question of whether that stability is a good thing.
