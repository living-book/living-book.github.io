# The Power Curve

## Definition

If you rank thousands of large companies by average economic profit, the
result is not a bell curve but a power curve: long, nearly flat "middle
three quintiles," then tails that rise and fall at exponential rates. The
authors argue this curve — not last year's numbers, not your closest
competitor — is the correct reference point for judging whether a
strategy is actually working: "Success for your strategy becomes moving
up on the Power Curve."

## In the Book

Chapter 2 builds the curve from financial data on 2,393 of the world's
largest non-financial firms (2010–2014), ranked by average economic profit.
The middle three quintiles average just $47 million a year — companies
there are, in effect, "paying the rent." The top quintile averages $1.4
billion, about 30 times the middle, and captures nearly 90% of all economic
profit created; the top 40 companies (Apple, Microsoft, Exxon, Samsung,
Walmart, and others) alone earned more than half the profit of all 2,393
firms combined. The curve is also getting steeper over time — top-quintile
collective profit roughly tripled from 2000–4 to 2010–14 while the bottom
quintile's losses quintupled — because capital increasingly chases winners
regardless of industry or geography. The book stresses that roughly half
of a company's position is explained by its industry's own power curve
(the median pharma or software company would be a top-quintile chemicals
company), which is why "where to play" is inseparable from "how to win."

## Why It Matters

Comparing yourself only to last year or to your nearest rival makes
incremental improvement look like success, when in a power-law world it
usually isn't enough to change your fate. Plotting the full distribution
reframes the goal from "beat the plan" to "move to a different, much
richer region of the distribution" — a lens that applies to any domain
where outcomes are power-law distributed (income, city size, firm value,
citation counts) and where local comparisons systematically understate
how far ahead the real leaders are.
