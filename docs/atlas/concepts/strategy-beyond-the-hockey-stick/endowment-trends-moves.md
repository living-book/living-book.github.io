# Endowment, Trends, and Moves

## Definition

Analyzing 40 candidate variables across 2,393 large companies over 15
years, the authors found 10 that dominate: three describe a company's
starting endowment (size, debt level, past R&D investment), two describe
the industry and geographic trends it rides, and five describe the moves
it makes (see Five Big Moves). Each variable only helps once a company
clears a specific relative threshold against peers — "getting one point
better doesn't seem to do much; you need to put yourself in another
league" — and the resulting model predicted which third of the Power
Curve a company would occupy a decade later with 86% accuracy.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 reframes the "average" 8% odds of jumping from the middle to the
top of the Power Curve into company-specific odds, using a Bayesian
analogy: knowing only that someone is "a person" gives you the global
average income, but adding nationality, age, gender, and occupation
narrows the estimate sharply — the same logic applied to the 10 variables
turned an 8% base rate into individually modeled odds of 69%, 87%, or 76%
for three real companies (Dai Nippon Printing, UNFI, and Precision
Castparts), each of which then moved as predicted. The resulting scorecard
is called a "mobility dashboard" and is illustrated later with Harris
Corp, which ticked seven of ten boxes (favorable trend, low debt, high
R&D, and four of the five big moves) and carried an 80% modeled
probability of a middle-to-top jump, which it then achieved.

## Why It Matters

Turning "improve your strategy" into ten scored, threshold-based variables
lets a team see its actual, calibrated odds of success before committing
resources, rather than relying on confidence built from a well-rehearsed
internal narrative. The general move — decompose an outcome into a small
number of empirically validated predictors, each with a real threshold
rather than a vague direction — is exportable to any domain where people
mistake "more effort in the right direction" for "enough effort to change
the outcome."
