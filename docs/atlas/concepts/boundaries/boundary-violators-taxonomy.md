# Three Types of Boundary Violators

## Definition

Cloud and Townsend identify three primary patterns of boundary violation, each with different underlying injuries and healing paths.

**Compliants** cannot say no to bad things. They say yes to harmful requests, absorb others' emotions, accept blame that isn't theirs. They were typically taught that boundaries are wrong, that "good people" sacrifice endlessly, that saying no is selfish or unloving.

**Avoidants** cannot say yes to good things. They cannot ask for help, cannot accept support, cannot let others into their pain. They confuse boundaries with walls, making them rigid and impermeable when they should be gates—selective but permeable.

**Controllers** cannot accept others' no. They violate others' boundaries by ignoring limits, manipulating, coercing, and making excuses. Unlike compliants and avoidants (who often injure themselves), controllers actively harm others by refusing to respect their property lines.

## In the Book

Each type gets a distinct case study. Robert, the compliant, was told as a boy that "it's bad manners" to hit girls even when they were beating him. He learned that his boundaries didn't matter, that protecting himself was selfish. Rachel, the avoidant, could lead a Bible study but couldn't accept support when struggling. She equated asking for help with burdening others.

Steve, the controller, couldn't understand why his assistant wanted to quit. Steve thought no meant "convince me otherwise." He called his employee during dinnertime, demanded unpaid hours, and never heard the message: I have a boundary here.

The book notes that some people are both compliant and avoidant—they say yes to the bad (others' demands) and no to the good (their own needs). This "reversed boundaries" pattern creates chronic depletion with no refill.

Controllers, though they harm others, are also injured. They are undisciplined, unable to delay gratification, and dependent on controlling others to function. They need to experience consequences for their violations just as much as compliants need to learn to say no.

## Why It Matters

This taxonomy helps people recognize their own pattern and understand why standard advice often fails. Telling a compliant to "be more assertive" without healing the underlying injury (fear of abandonment, guilt, shame) won't work. Telling an avoidant to "open up more" misses the real problem: they've confused intimacy with violation. Telling a controller to "respect boundaries" without consequences won't shift behavior. Each requires targeted intervention at the root belief, not just behavioral coaching. Understanding your own type and that of people in your life clarifies what kind of help—and what kind of firmness—is actually healing.
