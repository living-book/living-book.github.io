# Responsible TO Others, But FOR Yourself

## Definition

The boundary between responsibility TO and responsibility FOR is one of the sharpest and most misunderstood distinctions in human relationships. You are responsible TO others: to help them when they face true crises, to treat them with compassion, to speak truth to them. You are responsible FOR yourself alone: for your feelings, your thoughts, your behavior, your spiritual and emotional growth, your daily choices. You cannot do another person's emotional work, and they cannot do yours.

## In the Book

Cloud and Townsend ground this in two biblical verses that appear contradictory at first. Galatians 6:2 says "Carry each other's burdens" (responsibility TO). Galatians 6:5 says "each one should carry his own load" (responsibility FOR). The book clarifies that being responsible TO means offering help when someone faces a boulder-sized crisis. But it does not mean taking responsibility FOR their emotional growth, their spiritual maturation, or their daily choices.

The authors illustrate this with the case of Stan, who believed that true love meant saying yes to every request, sacrificing his own well-being. But his "loving" led to depression, not joy—a sign that it was not love at all. Stan learned that his depression was a symptom: he was responsible FOR his own health and well-being, not FOR keeping others happy. Real love includes saying no, because saying yes out of fear or guilt is not love; it is compulsion.

Similarly, the book warns against what happens when people blur the boundary: they either become martyrs (taking responsibility FOR others' choices and growth, then resenting it) or become selfish (refusing responsibility TO others, rationalizing neglect as "self-care").

## Why It Matters

This distinction cuts through years of confusion in relationships. Many people cannot distinguish between compassion (responsibility TO) and codependency (false responsibility FOR). The parents who give in to every demand because they fear disappointing their child are confused about this boundary. The friend who cannot name their own needs because they're focused entirely on the other person's problems is confused about this boundary. Clarifying it allows people to love without self-erasure, and to be independent without indifference.
