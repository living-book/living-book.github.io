# The Downward Spiral

## Definition

Waitzkin identifies a specific failure mechanism: after a single error, a
performer who stays emotionally attached to "what was" (their prior
advantage, their prior composure) keeps evaluating new options against that
outdated reference point instead of the actual, changed situation. In chess
terms, a player who had an advantage, made an error that only equalized the
position, but still believes they're ahead will reject perfectly good
"merely equal" continuations as unacceptable and keep pressing for more than
the position allows — manufacturing a second, then a third error out of
refusal to accept the first.

## In the Book

Waitzkin develops the idea while coaching young chess players at Public
School 116 in New York, describing how beginners "fall to pieces after
making the first mistake" and how even strong players who cling to the
belief that they retain an advantage will reject drawing lines and press for
wins they no longer have — often losing as a result. He anchors the concept
in a devastating real-world parallel he witnessed: a pedestrian in midtown
Manhattan steps into oncoming traffic looking the wrong way, is nudged but
unhurt by a swerving bicyclist, and — instead of treating the near-miss as a
warning and stepping back onto the curb — turns to curse at the biker with
her back to traffic, and is then struck and gravely injured by a taxi. He
uses this to teach his students that the first error is rarely fatal; getting
"frozen in what was" while time moves on is what compounds it. The lesson
pays off three years later at the National Championships in Knoxville, when
his student Ian Ferguson, mid-blunder and about to lose his queen after his
opponent laughed at a hung bishop, recalls "the woman and the bike," takes a
few breaths, and salvages the win.

## Why It Matters

This names the mechanism by which a single, recoverable mistake becomes a
crisis: not the error itself, but the decision-maker's continued reference to
a reality that no longer exists. It gives a concrete tell — pressing for an
outcome the current situation no longer supports — and a concrete
intervention — a brief physical reset (breath, cold water, a sprint outside)
to force re-evaluation from the actual present state rather than the
remembered one. The pattern applies anywhere sequential decisions compound:
a trader holding a position past the point the original thesis broke, a
team doubling down on a failing plan, or a driver overcorrecting after a
skid.
