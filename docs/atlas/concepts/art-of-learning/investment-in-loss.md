# Investment in Loss

## Definition

A term Waitzkin credits to his Tai Chi teacher William C. C. Chen: "giving
yourself to the learning process" by letting yourself be pushed around,
outmatched, and visibly beaten while you learn a new mechanic — instead of
protecting your ego by falling back on old habits that still "work" well
enough to avoid looking bad. The strong athlete who resists being pushed
just to prove he's strong learns nothing; the one willing to lose,
repeatedly and visibly, is the one who actually absorbs the new skill.

## In the Book

Waitzkin introduces the concept through his early Push Hands training, where
advanced students "manhandled" him and he "went flying" from attacks he
couldn't yet neutralize. The centerpiece example is Evan, a 200-pound
karate/Aikido black belt who repeatedly slammed Waitzkin against a wall hard
enough to knock plaster loose, week after week, with no easing up as
students traditionally do once a partner is off balance. Waitzkin describes
consciously choosing to keep training with Evan rather than avoid him,
absorbing months of being "smashed" until his body stopped fearing the
impact, relaxed under fire, and began sensing Evan's attacks before they
landed — at which point their roles reversed and Evan, unwilling to invest in
loss himself, stopped training with him. Waitzkin contrasts this with other
students who, when Chen corrected them, explained and justified their
technique instead of releasing it — "locked up by the need to be correct."
He also cites Michael Jordan, who missed more last-second shots to lose games
than any player in NBA history, as evidence that willingness to fail
publicly, not perfection, is what greatness actually requires.

## Why It Matters

Investment in Loss names the specific trade a learner has to accept and
names why so many people quietly refuse it: real skill acquisition requires
a visibly worse period, and protecting your image during that period —
explaining away mistakes, avoiding tougher opponents, sticking to what
already works — forecloses the very growth you're nominally there for. It
reframes "losing" from a verdict on ability into a cost you pay on purpose,
which matters anywhere a competent adult has to look temporarily
incompetent in public to build a new capability — switching tactics
mid-career, adopting an unfamiliar tool, or taking coaching from someone
who will make you look bad before you look better.
