# Making Smaller Circles

## Definition

Waitzkin's method for converting a large, deliberately learned movement into
an economical, nearly invisible one: internalize a technique's essence (its
body mechanics, its feeling) in an expansive, easy-to-monitor form, then
incrementally shrink its external size in small enough steps that the body
barely notices the change — so potency is preserved while telegraphing
disappears. He pairs this with what he calls "depth over breadth": plunging
into the detailed mystery of one micro-element to understand what makes the
macro system work, rather than collecting many techniques shallowly.

## In the Book

Waitzkin frames the depth-over-breadth principle with an anecdote from
Robert Pirsig's *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance*: a student
blocked on writing 500 words about her town is freed by being told to write
instead about a single brick on the opera house across the street — she
returns with twenty pages. He contrasts martial arts schools that rate
students by how many flashy forms they've collected ("form collectors")
with his own approach of drilling a single technique — a classic straight
punch — first in slow motion with a large, exaggerated wind-up until the
felt sense of ground-to-fist connection is fully internalized, then
condensing the motion inch by inch without losing power, until the punch
becomes nearly invisible, the way a Mike Tyson or Muhammad Ali knockout
sometimes only becomes visible in slow-motion replay. He draws the same
principle from his own chess study of reduced-complexity endgames (king and
pawn versus king) and cites Grandmaster Michael Adams, who appears to
casually surrender central control yet wins because he has internalized the
principle so deeply that its outward form has vanished — "he has made the
circles so small, even Grandmasters cannot see them." Waitzkin credits this
approach — condensing a large, well-understood technique rather than
collecting more techniques — with winning his first Push Hands National
Championship after only two years of Tai Chi study.

## Why It Matters

This gives a concrete answer to "how does a slow, deliberate fundamental
turn into fast, instinctive mastery": not by learning more things, but by
shrinking the same thing while protecting what makes it work, monitored by
an internalized feeling rather than external cues. It also reframes a
common failure mode — chasing breadth, more techniques, more frameworks,
more tools — as a trap that produces showy but shallow competence, while the
performers who actually win have usually condensed a smaller repertoire to a
much higher degree of refinement. The same shrinking-without-losing-potency
move shows up anywhere a beginner's exaggerated, correct form needs to
become an expert's compressed, efficient one — a golf swing, a design
process, a negotiation tactic.
