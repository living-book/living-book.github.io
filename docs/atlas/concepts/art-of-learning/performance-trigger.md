# Building Your Trigger

## Definition

Rather than searching for a single external catalyst (the perfect song, the
perfect ritual) that will magically induce peak focus, Waitzkin's method
works backward: find an activity where you are already naturally, reliably
present and relaxed, then build a short sequence of steps that precedes that
activity every time. Repeated daily, the routine itself becomes
physiologically linked to that state of mind — at which point the routine
can be relocated in front of any high-pressure situation, and it will
reliably summon the same state, even though the situation itself is nothing
like the original low-stakes activity.

## In the Book

Waitzkin walks through his coaching of "Dennis," a Smith Barney producer who
struggled to focus in meetings and wanted to find his "hot button." Asked
when he last felt effortlessly serene, Dennis named playing catch with his
twelve-year-old son, Jack. Waitzkin built a five-step routine around that
existing peak state — a light fruit-and-soy shake, fifteen minutes of
breath-focused meditation, ten minutes of stretching, ten minutes of a Bob
Dylan song ("Sad-Eyed Lady of the Lowlands"), then playing catch — which
Dennis rehearsed daily for a month before the actual game of catch. Once
internalized, Waitzkin had him run the identical routine the morning of an
important meeting instead; Dennis reported entering the meeting in the same
serene state he felt with his son. Waitzkin notes the specific content of
the routine is arbitrary — cartwheels and a scream into the wind would have
worked as well — what matters is the conditioned link between routine and
state. He connects this to his own decade of pre-competition routines: an
hour of meditation to a soothing tape before chess tournaments, and a
thirty-minute visualization exercise before his first Push Hands World
Championship in Taiwan.

## Why It Matters

This solves a specific, common failure: performers who chase inspiration as
if it were an external event waiting to be discovered, rather than a state
they can manufacture on demand. By anchoring the routine to a state that
already exists naturally rather than inventing one from scratch, and by
rehearsing the link in a context with no stakes before deploying it in a
high-stakes one, the method turns "getting in the zone" from a matter of
luck into a mechanical, repeatable procedure — applicable to any performer
whose job requires reliably reaching full presence on a schedule they don't
control: an athlete, a surgeon, a musician, a salesperson before a pitch.
