# Entity vs. Incremental Theory of Intelligence

## Definition

Drawing on developmental psychologist Carol Dweck's research, Waitzkin distinguishes
two ways people explain their own success or failure. "Entity theorists" treat
intelligence or skill as a fixed, unalterable quantity — you either have it or
you don't — and describe results with language like "I am smart at this."
"Incremental theorists" (Waitzkin's "learning theorists") see ability as
something built step by step through effort, and describe results with
language like "I got it because I worked very hard." The same failure reads
as identity-threatening evidence to one group and as useful information to
the other.

## In the Book

Waitzkin grounds the distinction in Dweck's math-problem experiment: children
identified in advance as entity or learning theorists were given easy
problems (everyone succeeded), then problems too hard for anyone to solve,
then easy problems again. The learning theorists were excited by the
challenge and breezed back through the easy material afterward; the entity
theorists were dismayed by the hard problems and then floundered even on the
easy ones — their confidence had collapsed. He extends this to his own
scholastic chess world: rivals who memorized "opening traps" to win fast
became entity theorists whose identity was "I'm a winner," and he describes
meeting a boy in Arizona who hadn't lost in over a year and refused to play
anyone who might beat him — "a classic anorexic hermit crab," starving itself
to avoid having to grow into a bigger shell. By contrast, Waitzkin and his
coach Bruce built his early game from a bare board (king and pawn endings),
prizing the struggle and the incremental accumulation of principle over
quick wins, and he explicitly credits losing painful games — the 1993 U.S.
Junior Championship playoff loss, the drawn-offer-declined loss in the Under
18 World Championship final — as more valuable to his development than his
victories.

## Why It Matters

This reframes what looks like a talent gap as a difference in how failure is
interpreted: the same setback either confirms a fixed verdict about you or
supplies data for the next attempt. It explains why the most naturally
gifted performers in a field are often the most fragile under pressure — they
have the most identity riding on a "winner" self-image — and why a
process-praising environment ("you're becoming a great writer") produces more
durable performers than a results-praising one ("you're so smart"). Because
the theory a person holds is shaped by feedback from parents, coaches, and
managers and can be shifted with different framing within minutes, it applies
anywhere someone's sense of self gets fused to an outcome — school, sport,
sales, or any skill being built under scrutiny.
