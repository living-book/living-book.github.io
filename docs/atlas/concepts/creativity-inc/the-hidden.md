# The Hidden

## Definition

Catmull names "the Hidden" as the category of problems a leader or organization cannot
currently see—not through negligence, but as a structural feature of how perception and
group dynamics work. His core management belief: "If you don't try to uncover what is
unseen and understand its nature, you will be ill prepared to lead." The danger isn't the
existence of blind spots—everyone has them—it's the assumption that because a problem
isn't visible, it doesn't exist.

## In the Book

The chapter opens with the myth of Cassandra, cursed to speak truths no one believes—
Catmull reframes the curse as belonging not to Cassandra but to everyone unable to
perceive what she sees. He connects this directly to his opening question about why smart
Silicon Valley leaders at companies like Silicon Graphics made obviously bad decisions: not
hubris exactly, but an inability to register that some problems were invisible to them by
construction. He credits a formative moment with Steve Jobs in 1995: Jobs argued Pixar
should go public because a box-office failure was inevitable and unpredictable—a genuinely
"hidden" problem the company had to prepare for financially without knowing its shape in
advance. From this Catmull built a discipline of actively hunting for unseen problems rather
than assuming their absence, and the chapter goes on to lay out multiple "levels" of what's
hidden, starting with his own early experience as a first-time manager at New York Tech in
1974.

## Why It Matters

This reframes organizational failure as a perception problem before it's a competence
problem. Leaders don't usually fail because they ignore warnings they can see—they fail
because the structure of their position, their success, or their group dynamics makes certain
problems literally invisible to them. That shifts the response from "try harder to notice
things" to building deliberate mechanisms—retrospectives, outside perspectives, dissenting
voices with standing—that compensate for perception you cannot personally achieve, no
matter how self-aware you believe yourself to be.
