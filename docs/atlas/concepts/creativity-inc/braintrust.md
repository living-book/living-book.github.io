# The Braintrust

## Definition

The Braintrust is a recurring meeting where a group of people with deep expertise in a
craft—at Pixar, storytelling—watch a work in progress and give unfiltered, specific feedback
to the person responsible for it. Its power comes from two design choices: membership is
limited to people who have done the work themselves, and the group has no authority to
force any change. As Catmull puts it, "without the critical ingredient that is candor, there
can be no trust. And without trust, creative collaboration is not possible."

## In the Book

Catmull traces the Braintrust's origin to the five people who led Toy Story—John Lasseter,
Andrew Stanton, Pete Docter, Lee Unkrich, and Joe Ranft—who developed a habit of blunt,
non-personal argument about the work itself. It became an official practice during the 1999
crisis rescue of Toy Story 2, then had to evolve from a fixed five-person group into a larger,
fluid one as Pixar ran multiple productions at once. The chapter details the mechanics:
directors screen a rough cut ("reels"), the Braintrust diagnoses what isn't working but does
not prescribe solutions, and the director alone decides what to do with the notes. Catmull
contrasts this explicitly with the Hollywood studio-notes system, where executives who
aren't filmmakers hand directors a list of demands, breeding resentment rather than
improvement. He is blunt about why the process is necessary: "early on, all of our movies
suck," and the Braintrust is the mechanism for the iterative climb "from suck to not-suck."

## Why It Matters

Most feedback systems fail because they conflate two different things: identifying a problem
and having the authority to dictate its fix. By stripping the group of mandate power, the
Braintrust removes the incentive to posture or protect turf, and lets participants argue about
the work instead of about who wins. That separation—credible expert diagnosis, decoupled
from command authority—is a transferable design for any setting where you need honest
critique of something someone is emotionally attached to, without triggering defensiveness
that shuts candor down.
