# The Postmortem Ritual

## Definition

A postmortem, in Catmull's usage, is a dedicated meeting held shortly after a project's
completion to examine what did and didn't work. He frames its purpose sharply: "Companies,
like individuals, do not become exceptional by believing they are exceptional but by
understanding the ways in which they aren't exceptional." The value isn't in any single
insight—it's in institutionalizing the otherwise-unnatural act of deliberate self-assessment
after people are already eager to move on.

## In the Book

Pixar's first postmortem was held in Tiburon in 1998 after finishing A Bug's Life—Catmull
describes the almost domestic detail of using a rooster-shaped kitchen timer to keep speakers
to fifteen minutes, and recalls the session's spirit of openness as more valuable than any
single "Aha!" finding. But he's candid that quality was inconsistent afterward: "some were
profound, and others were a complete waste of time," because people naturally pull their
punches rather than "poke a sleeping bear." He diagnoses the underlying resistance as a
general human aversion to self-assessment, in which success gets read as "we must be doing
it right" and failure as "what we did was wrong"—both of which he calls shallow. Against
that resistance, he lays out five specific reasons to force the ritual anyway, starting with
consolidating fragmented lessons before they're forgotten, teaching people who weren't on the
project, and preventing unresolved resentments from festering.

## Why It Matters

Most organizations already believe, in principle, that they should learn from what they just
did. The postmortem's contribution is procedural, not philosophical: it converts a belief
everyone holds abstractly into a forced, recurring, time-boxed practice that survives the
universal desire to skip it. That distinction—between agreeing self-assessment matters and
actually building a mechanism that makes it happen on a schedule regardless of mood—is the
transferable lesson for any team that wants institutional memory rather than individual,
fading memory.
