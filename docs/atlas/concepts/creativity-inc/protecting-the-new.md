# Protecting the New

## Definition

Catmull calls early versions of a creative work "ugly babies": awkward, unformed, and
nothing like the polished thing they will become. Because they can't yet defend themselves
on their merits, they need deliberate protection—time, patience, and insulation from being
held to the standards of a finished product. This protection is threatened by what he calls
"the Beast": the growing infrastructure of a successful organization that needs a constant
supply of new material to stay fed, and which has no patience for anything not yet
production-ready.

## In the Book

Catmull grounds the Beast in what he observed firsthand at Disney Animation in the late
1980s and early 90s, where he heard executives repeat the phrase "you've got to feed the
Beast." As hits like The Little Mermaid and The Lion King drove studio expansion—new
buildings, new staff, new release-schedule commitments—the pressure for a continuous
pipeline of product overwhelmed the patience new ideas require, contributing to a sixteen-year
drought (1994–2010) with no Disney animated film opening at number one. Pixar's own first
collision with the Beast came with Finding Nemo in 1999: Andrew Stanton's pitch about an
overprotective clownfish was magnificent, but the film still had to survive being an "ugly
baby" inside a studio whose infrastructure was pushing for output. Catmull is careful to
distinguish this from reflexive conservatism—protecting the status quo, or a legal
department's excess caution—which uses the language of protection for the opposite purpose:
keeping things safely unchanged rather than letting something new survive its vulnerable
early stage.

## Why It Matters

Growth and quality pull against each other through a specific mechanism, not just vague
"bureaucracy": success builds infrastructure, infrastructure needs continuous throughput, and
throughput pressure collides with the fact that anything genuinely new looks bad before it
looks good. Recognizing this lets a leader distinguish a legitimate protective act (buying an
unfinished idea time to mature) from an illegitimate one (shielding an already-entrenched
process from disruption)—a distinction useful anywhere organizations must keep producing
originality after they've already succeeded once.
