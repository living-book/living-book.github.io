# Life Values Hierarchy: Ends and Means

## Definition

A life values hierarchy is the consciously articulated, ranked list of the emotional and existential states that matter most to you. It distinguishes between *ends values*—the emotional states you actually desire to experience (love, growth, contribution, freedom)—and *means values*—the circumstances or possessions you think will get you there (family, money, career status). Most people confuse the two and spend years pursuing means thinking they're pursuing ends, only to achieve the goal and feel empty. A hierarchy is the ranked order of these values, revealing what trade-offs you'll make under pressure.

## In the Book

Robbins uses the example of a woman who valued caring and contribution deeply. She became an attorney to "help people." As she succeeded and pursued partnership, her work transformed into protocol management in meetings—still "successful" by external measures, but disconnected from the emotional states she actually valued. She achieved the means (status, money) but lost access to the ends (meaningful human connection, direct impact).

He describes the process of identifying moving-toward values (the emotional states you'll do the most to achieve: love, success, freedom, security, adventure, etc.) and ranking them. Different hierarchies produce different lives. Someone who ranks freedom above intimacy will make different relationship decisions than someone who ranks intimacy above freedom. Someone who ranks growth above comfort will take risks; someone who ranks security above growth will minimize them.

Robbins emphasizes that most people never do this ranking. They drift through values inherited from parents, culture, and circumstance. When you know your hierarchy—and it's consciously yours, not inherited—you can make decisions aligned with your deepest needs. You can also see where you're chasing means that no longer serve your actual ends, and where you're setting yourself up for emptiness by achieving external goals without meeting emotional values.

## Why It Matters

This concept is the foundation for decision-making and long-term fulfillment. Without knowing your value hierarchy, decisions are arbitrary and you're vulnerable to cultural narratives about what should matter. With it, you have a compass. It also reveals why two people can achieve identical external outcomes and feel completely different: their value hierarchies are different. The same job might feel meaningful (contribution + growth hierarchy) or hollow (control + status hierarchy). Understanding this is the difference between chasing someone else's life and living your own.
