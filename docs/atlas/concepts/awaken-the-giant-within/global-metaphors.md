# Global Metaphors

## Definition

Global metaphors are the encompassing images through which people make sense of their entire lives. Unlike individual metaphors used for specific situations ("I'm at a crossroads"), global metaphors are the foundational lens through which all experience is filtered. They operate largely unconsciously, inherited from culture, family, and lived experience. A single global metaphor generates an entire constellation of beliefs, behaviors, and emotional responses.

## In the Book

Robbins catalogs global metaphors and their consequences. If life is a "test," the person expects stakes, pass-fail outcomes, and constant evaluation—high stress, binary thinking. If life is a "competition," there are winners and losers; relationships become rivalrous. If life is a "game," there's play, skill development, the possibility of losing without catastrophe. If life is a "mystery," there's wonder, discovery, and acceptance of the unknown. If life is "sacred," there's reverence and possibly constraint. If life is a "gift," there's surprise and gratitude.

He illustrates through Martin Sheen's transformation. Before a heart attack, Sheen saw life as something to fear. After nearly dying and experiencing calm, he realized he had actually been afraid of *life itself*, not death. He reframed his global metaphor: life is a mystery, not a threat. That single shift changed his perception, decisions, and sense of possibility.

Robbins also warns that metaphors carry hidden constraints. A physicist working with the "solar system" metaphor for atoms unconsciously assumed electrons orbited at fixed distances, limiting inquiry for years. When the metaphor was abandoned, understanding advanced. He emphasizes that having a single global metaphor is "a great way to limit your life." Expanding the number of metaphors you can flexibly adopt expands your capacity to respond to different situations.

## Why It Matters

This concept names the deepest filter through which we interpret experience. Most people never consciously choose their global metaphors; they inherit them. Understanding this means you can audit which metaphor is actually running your life, recognize its costs, and deliberately adopt alternatives that better serve your values. It's also more powerful than individual reframes because it affects decision-making across all domains simultaneously. Shifting from "life is a war" to "life is a dance" will instantly change how you approach relationships, work, risk, and challenges.
