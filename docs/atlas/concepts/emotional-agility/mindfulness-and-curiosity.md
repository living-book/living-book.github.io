# Mindfulness and Curiosity

## Definition

Mindfulness is not meditation or relaxation; it is paying attention on purpose and without judgment — noticing what's actually happening, both inside you and around you, as it happens, with openness and curiosity. In doing so, you create space between the thinker and the thought, allowing you to observe your inner experiences rather than be entangled in them. This is the ground from which stepping out becomes possible.

## In the Book

David contrasts mindfulness with its opposite, mindlessness — the state of unawareness and autopilot, where you're not really present and you rely too heavily on rigid rules or stale distinctions that haven't been thought through. You know you're being mindless when you forget someone's name as soon as you hear it, put your credit card in the trash, can't remember if you locked your door, or feel emotions "came out of nowhere."

By contrast, mindfulness allows you to notice your uncomfortable feelings and thoughts rather than be entangled in them. When you're mindful of your anger, you can observe it with sensitivity, focus, and emotional clarity, discovering where the anger is actually coming from. You might discover your "anger" is really sadness or fear. But the calm awareness that comes with mindfulness doesn't come easily to everyone. David cites Harvard research where participants sitting alone with their thoughts for ten minutes were so miserable that some chose to give themselves mild electric shocks rather than simply sit.

David emphasizes that mindfulness is more than knowing "I'm hearing something" or "I'm seeing something." It's doing all this with balance and equanimity, openness and curiosity, and without judgment. It allows you to create new, fluid categories and see the world through multiple perspectives. She offers concrete practices:

**Begin with the Breath**: For one minute, focus on your breath. Count to four as you inhale, four as you exhale. Your mind will wander; notice that and bring your focus back. It's not about winning; it's about engaging in the process.

**Mindfully Observe**: Pick an object and focus on it for one minute, really looking at it as if you've just arrived from Mars. Isolate its various aspects — color, texture, movement.

**Rework a Routine**: Pick something you do every day (making coffee, brushing your teeth) and the next time you do it, focus on each step — sight, sound, texture, smell — with full awareness.

**Really Listen**: Select quiet music and really tune in as if you'd grown up in a cave and this was the first music you'd ever heard. Don't judge; just identify different aspects of rhythm, melody, structure.

David also explores the marriage of mindfulness with curiosity. She tells the story of reading her daughter Harold and the Purple Crayon — about a curious four-year-old who draws things into existence. Harold doesn't know where he's going, but he keeps using his purple crayon to draw out potential experiences. Curiosity like Harold's is a decision. When you decide to curiously explore the world inside and outside yourself, you can make other decisions more flexibly. You can intentionally breathe space into your reactions and make choices based on what matters.

Importantly, David notes that Harold doesn't try to stop his emotions. When he's scared, he doesn't run away. He looks at his fear and moves forward with creative solutions, drawing water over his head to hide from the dragon, creating a window to slip through. The fictional four-year-old demonstrates emotional agility: feeling the fear and acting from values anyway.

David also addresses the concept of "social snacking" — small moments of connection that keep you linked to someone when you can't be physically present. If you value being a loving partner, a quick email while stuck at work, or a call to your kids at bedtime while traveling, maintains that value. Mindfulness helps you notice these micro-opportunities for connection rather than being on autopilot.

## Why It Matters

Mindfulness is the foundational practice that makes all other tools work. Without it — without actually noticing what's happening in your inner world — you can't step out, you can't walk your why, you can't make the tiny tweaks that stick. It is the antidote to autopilot. It also opens the possibility of curiosity: instead of defending against a difficult emotion or thought, you can be curious about it. That shift from defensiveness to curiosity is where flexibility begins.
