# Getting Hooked

## Definition

Getting hooked is when you accept a thought as literal truth, become fused with it, and then act automatically in response — often avoiding situations that trigger those thoughts, or endlessly replaying them, or trying to suppress them. The hook is the mechanism by which rigid thinking and feeling take over your behavior. It happens through three layered processes: first, internal chatter turns facts into judgments; second, thoughts blend with emotions and imagery so powerfully they feel indistinguishable from reality; third, you respond reflexively, like a fish snapping at bait.

## In the Book

David traces how a neutral observation ("I'm a slow writer") slips into self-critical evaluation ("I'm too slow"), comparison ("I'm slower than other writers"), anxiety ("I'm falling behind"), and finally, devastating judgment ("I'm done for"). The progression is nearly automatic, like the brain's completion of "Mary had a little ___" (the answer "lamb" pops in without conscious effort). She illustrates this with her own story of being hooked when a colleague took her concept for a book title: rather than confronting him directly, she called her husband in the middle of his emergency surgery, got angry at him when he couldn't talk, and then spent two days giving him the silent treatment because she'd decided he was "never there for me" — all from a single misfire.

The book introduces the concept of "sensory blending" — how the angular gyrus in the brain integrates sounds, visuals, feelings, and meanings so thoroughly that our thoughts come "fully accessorized" with emotion and imagery rather than as neutral data. This is an evolutionary advantage (a hunter-gatherer who thought "I'm in danger" needed to feel and act danger immediately, not deliberate), but in modern life it locks us into hooks. David shows how people get hooked by stories ("I'm a loser," "I always fold," "Nobody loves me") that may have originated in childhood and were never objectively tested, yet completely control their behavior.

## Why It Matters

This concept explains why trying to "think positive" or willpower your way out of a pattern fails: the hook isn't a choice you can simply reject through effort, because the thought is neurologically fused with emotion and image. Naming the mechanism — that you are hooked, that the thought is a thought, not a directive — creates the first opening for agility. It also explains why so many people spend years trying to "fix" the same emotions with no progress: they are fighting the hook rather than stepping out of it.
