# "Walking Your Why"

## Definition

Values are not goals to achieve; they are ongoing directions for how you want to show up in the world. To "walk your why" means to identify what truly matters to you — not what you think you should care about, or what others think you should value, but what actually moves you — and then to act in alignment with those values, especially in moments of difficulty. Values are like a psychological keel that keeps you steady. The walking part is crucial: without action, a value is just an aspiration.

## In the Book

David distinguishes values from goals. A goal has an endpoint: "Lose ten pounds." A value doesn't: "Be healthy" is an ongoing direction you walk toward by making choices aligned with it. She challenges the cultural definition of values as rules that constrain you, showing instead that values are liberating — they free you from comparison and give you a continuous source of meaning.

David illustrates this through stories of people who walked their values under extraordinary circumstances. Irena Sendler, a Polish rescuer in World War II, was told as a child by her doctor father: "If you see someone drowning, you must jump in to save them." During the Nazi occupation, this value led her to shelter Jewish neighbors, then create false papers, then smuggle children out of the Warsaw Ghetto — at enormous personal risk. She was arrested and sentenced to death, then escaped with a guard's help. Instead of hiding, she continued: she walked her why, not because it was easy or safe, but because not doing so would betray who she was.

Similarly, Sergeant Joseph Darby, a 24-year-old at Abu Ghraib, faced pressure from military conformity and group coercion to participate in or ignore detainee abuse. But his core values — beliefs about right and wrong instilled from childhood — made the choice simple: he turned over evidence. Values gave him the ground to stand on.

David addresses a common objection: if you walk your values, won't you feel worse in the moment? Yes. Courage is not the absence of fear; courage is fear walking. If you value friendship but are socially anxious, the "toward move" is to accept the invitation to a party you dread. You'll feel more discomfort than if you'd stayed home. But that discomfort is the price of admission to a meaningful life.

She also reframes the "work-life balance" paradox: the choice is not between work and home, but between being fully committed to both (bringing quality to each domain) versus being conflicted and torn. If you value being a loving parent, bring that love to your interactions with your kids. If you value being a productive worker, bring that productivity to your desk. The quality and presence matter more than quantity of time.

David introduces the concept of "choice points" — moments where you face a fork in the road and must choose whether to move toward your values or away from them. Over time, the more you choose moves toward your values, the more vital, effective, and meaningful your life becomes. Conversely, when hooked by difficult thoughts and feelings, people often make "away moves" — avoiding what matters to them.

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes change from willpower-driven to meaning-driven. It explains why New Year's resolutions fail (goals) and why identifying values actually helps people persist through obstacles (direction). It also shows that living your values doesn't eliminate difficulty — it makes difficulty meaningful. When you know what matters, you can make deliberate, satisfying moves toward those things instead of reflexive, unproductive moves away. That distinction is the difference between a life of regret and a life of intention.
