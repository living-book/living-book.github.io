# Emotional Agility

## Definition

Emotional agility is flexibility with your thoughts and feelings, so that you can respond to situations in ways that align with what matters to you, rather than being controlled by autopilot reactions. It is not about controlling your thoughts or forcing positive thinking. Instead, it is about loosening your grip on rigid patterns of thinking and behavior, and choosing to act with intention. As Viktor Frankl wrote, there is a space between stimulus and response; in that space lies our power to choose and our freedom.

## In the Book

David frames emotional agility as a counterpoint to emotional rigidity — being hooked by thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that don't serve you. She cites research showing that emotional rigidity is associated with depression and anxiety, while emotional agility is key to well-being and success. The book's central architecture is that people often spend years trying to "fix" or "get in touch with" their emotions with no result, when the real shift comes from learning to accept them, acknowledge them, and then move forward based on values rather than emotional weather.

David illustrates this through the metaphor of a lighthouse (the book's opening story): the captain of a battleship keeps demanding that another vessel change course, not realizing he is the one heading toward the light — a fixed feature of the landscape. Our emotions, like the lighthouse, are not problems to solve but signals to read. The book develops four core practices: showing up (being present with difficult emotions), stepping out (creating distance from unhelpful thoughts), walking your why (identifying and living by your values), and moving on through tiny tweaks and balance (the See-Saw Principle).

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes emotional struggle from a sign of weakness or disorder to an invitation to flexibility. It offers a radically different approach to change: not through willpower or positivity, but through acceptance plus intentional action aligned with what matters. The distinction matters because decades of self-help culture — "think positive," "fix yourself," "control your emotions" — often backfire. Emotional agility says: feel what you feel, notice it, then decide what to do about it. This permission to feel, combined with values-based action, creates space for people to move through difficulty rather than getting stuck in it.
