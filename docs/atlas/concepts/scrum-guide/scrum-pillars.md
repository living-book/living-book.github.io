# Scrum Pillars: Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation

## Definition

Scrum is built upon empiricism—the belief that knowledge comes from experience and observed outcomes. Three pillars enable this empirical approach: transparency (making work visible), inspection (observing progress frequently), and adaptation (adjusting based on what is learned). These pillars form the backbone that turns Scrum from a checklist into a learning system.

## In the Book

The 2020 Scrum Guide explicitly states that Scrum is "founded on empiricism and lean thinking." The three pillars are introduced immediately after this foundation: Transparency enables inspection, which enables adaptation. The guide gives a concrete progression: "Artifacts that have low transparency can lead to decisions that diminish value and increase risk" and "Inspection without adaptation is considered pointless."

The guide illustrates transparency through the three formal Scrum artifacts (Product Backlog with Product Goal, Sprint Backlog with Sprint Goal, Increment with Definition of Done). These artifacts are designed so "important decisions are based on the perceived state of its three formal artifacts." Inspection happens through Scrum's five formal events (Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective, and the Sprint container itself). Adaptation occurs when "people involved are empowered or self-managing" and "a Scrum Team is expected to adapt the moment it learns anything new through inspection."

## Why It Matters

Most frameworks prescribe *what to do*; Scrum's three pillars define a *learning mechanism*. Teams often practice Scrum activities (run ceremonies, move cards) without activating the empirical loop. Understanding that transparency is purposeless without inspection, and inspection is pointless without adaptation, reveals what makes Scrum work—and what breaks when any pillar is weak. In any complex domain (software, product, organizational change), this pattern applies: you cannot improve what you cannot see, you cannot respond to what you do not measure, and measurement without change becomes bureaucracy.
