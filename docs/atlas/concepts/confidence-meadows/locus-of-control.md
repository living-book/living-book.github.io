# Locus of Control

## Definition

People with high self-efficacy believe their own actions and decisions control the outcomes in their lives. Those with low self-efficacy see themselves as victims of external forces—luck, economic conditions, other people's choices. This is the locus of control: the extent to which you believe you can control events affecting you. It is not optimism or denial; it is a causal belief about who holds agency. Shifting locus from external to internal—taking responsibility for outcomes rather than blaming circumstance—is fundamental to building self-efficacy.

## In the Book

Meadows illustrates the contrast. When a high-self-efficacy person loses money on a business, they attribute it to their own decisions: bad hiring, poor strategy, insufficient capital. When a low-self-efficacy person loses money, they blame the economy (despite other businesses thriving in the same economy). Both face the same setback; their locus of control determines the inference they draw.

Meadows describes his own business failure this way: his software business failed because he shouldn't have started it and he sabotaged it—everything in his control. While counterintuitive, this belief is "reassuring" because if the failure was entirely external, he could learn nothing. Taking responsibility makes the failure intelligible and improvable. By systematically catching himself blaming others or circumstances and asking "what did my decisions cause?" he shifted his locus inward.

The mechanism is practical: external locus increases stress (you have no control, so anxiety is permanent). Internal locus decreases stress even when facing setbacks (you control the variables, so you can adjust). Changing locus is not instantaneous—it requires rewiring causal beliefs formed through experience. The practice is simple: each time you blame something external, ask what role your actions played.

## Why It Matters

This concept explains why people with similar talents and circumstances achieve vastly different outcomes. Two people face the same job rejection; one treats it as external ("the market is bad") and stays passive; the other treats it as internal ("my pitch was weak") and improves. Over time, internal locus compounds into agency and self-efficacy. It also reveals a paradox: taking full responsibility for failure is psychologically harder but practically more powerful than blaming circumstances, because it returns control to you. The concept bridges individual and systemic thinking—while luck and systems matter, dwelling on them undermines the belief-forming capacity that builds capability.
