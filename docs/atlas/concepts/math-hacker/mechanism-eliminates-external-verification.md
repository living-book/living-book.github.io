# Mechanism Eliminates External Verification

## Definition

A procedure learned as a memorized sequence of steps offers no way to tell,
from the inside, whether you've applied it correctly — a single misremembered
step produces a wrong answer that looks the same as a right one, so you must
ask someone else to check. A procedure learned as an understood mechanism
lets you verify it yourself, the way understanding *why* a hot stove burns
means you never need to be told twice not to touch it, and never need to ask
whether touching it "was right."

## In the Book

The book opens Chapter 1 by diagnosing why students struggle with school
multiplication methods (grid method, long multiplication, and others):
"the methods require memorisation of a number of steps, which, if any are
wrong, makes the answer incorrect... it is not possible to know yourself if it
is incorrect. You have to ask someone else." It cites the recurring student
question "Is this right, Miss?" as a symptom, and argues that memorizing steps
without understanding trains students "to act like a robot," dependent on a
"controller" to say whether they got it right. Against this, Carson gives the
hot-stove analogy — nobody has to memorize a rule against touching a hot
stove, because they understand the consequence — and uses his cat's one
accidental encounter with a hot stovetop as a real-world instance of the same
principle. The chapter's stated method is to make the reader derive "what is
multiplication?" themselves, on the claim that self-derived, mechanism-level
understanding is what makes a fact "impossible to forget."

## Why It Matters

This reframes "understanding vs. memorization" from a vague virtue into a
concrete, checkable property: does the learner have an internal way to test
their own output, or must they route every check through an external
authority? That distinction generalizes past arithmetic to any skill taught as
a procedure — a checklist, a recipe, a compliance rule — where teaching the
underlying mechanism (not just the steps) is what lets the learner self-correct,
adapt the procedure to novel cases, and stop needing permission to trust their
own answer.
