# The Program Charter: Vision and Strategy for Organizational Alignment

## Definition

A program charter is a shared vision and strategy document created collaboratively by the core team that defines why the organization is undertaking a program and what success looks like at the program level. Unlike a project charter that limits scope to a feature set, a program charter covers the entire product and creates common understanding across business, software, hardware, and deployment functions about what done means for the whole effort.

## In the Book

Rothman positions chartering as essential work before the feature teams begin, not as a bottleneck. For a smartphone, the charter might be "Provide users a way to communicate wirelessly with multiple modalities" — it doesn't prescribe the specific projects (call, voicemail, messaging, data) but establishes why those projects exist together. The core team (program manager, software and hardware leads, marketing, sales, deployment, finance representatives) spend days together to write the charter, ensuring they understand the program's purpose and can translate that vision into subordinate marketing, sales, training, and deployment plans.

The charter becomes a visible artifact posted where everyone can reference it when making daily trade-offs. When feature teams face choices about scope or technical direction, they refer back to the charter vision rather than asking permission from above. This coalescence of the core team around a single document is itself a key outcome — it forces the cross-functional stakeholders to align on strategy before work begins, surfacing disagreements that would otherwise remain hidden.

## Why It Matters

A program charter solves a distinctive program-level problem: how to align people across multiple organizational functions (not just engineering) when all have legitimate concerns about the product's direction. Without a shared charter, teams optimize locally — marketing optimizes for launch readiness, deployment for operational stability, software for architectural simplicity — creating invisible conflicts that emerge later as rework and delay. The charter forces those conflicts to the surface early, while they are still negotiable, and makes visible the trade-offs the organization is willing to accept. This is how larger efforts maintain agility despite their complexity.
