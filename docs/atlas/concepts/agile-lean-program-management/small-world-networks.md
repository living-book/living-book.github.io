# Small-World Networks: Decentralized Information Flow Across Program Teams

## Definition

A small-world network is a communication structure where teams and individuals are connected to each other not in a uniform hierarchy or rigid chain, but through multiple overlapping connections where some people are highly connected and others less so. Information and problems flow virally through these networks — one person tells someone, who tells someone else — rather than being routed through formal channels. In a nine-team program, every team connects to others, but not in the same way; some team members are bridges to many teams, others to just a few. This structure allows the network to scale almost without limit, requiring no formal management, and enables problems to be solved at the point closest to where they occur.

## In the Book

Rothman explains that the rumor mill — the way information spreads through an organization informally — is actually the most efficient communication channel. She advocates deliberately organizing the program to take advantage of this. Feature teams are the primary structure; small-world networks emerge as team members naturally connect across teams (developers talk to other developers, testers to testers, analysts to analysts). The program manager can help by making these connections visible: "We have a community of practice for architects; anyone interested, attend Fridays at 2pm." 

Crucially, Rothman distinguishes small-world networks from hierarchies: in a network, there is no "chief" who must approve every crossing of team boundaries. Instead, she trusts people to ask for help and collaborate. This requires clear expectations: people are asked to work across the organization to solve problems they encounter, using the networks and communities of practice available to them.

## Why It Matters

Small-world networks solve the program-level problem of communication bottlenecks. In a traditional hierarchy, every message from one team to another must travel up and down chains of command, creating delays and distortion. In a program with 9+ teams, if every inter-team question requires an approval or a meeting, the program stalls. Small-world networks allow problems to move directly to whoever can help — a developer asks another developer a question, they pair to solve it, the solution spreads through the developer network, and no manager ever needs to be involved. As programs scale, this informal distributed problem-solving enables speed that hierarchical coordination cannot match.
