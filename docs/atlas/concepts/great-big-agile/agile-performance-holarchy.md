# Agile Performance Holarchy

## Definition

The Agile Performance Holarchy (APH) is an organizational operating system designed for large-scale agile adoption. Unlike process models or maturity frameworks, it provides three interdependent layers—why (values), what (frameworks), and how (behaviors)—that work together to enable sustainable agility. It is composed of six performance circles (Leading, Providing, Envisioning, Crafting, Affirming, Teaming) and eighteen holons, each representing a self-reliant unit of organizational behavior that also serves a larger whole.

## In the Book

Dalton introduces the APH in response to the failure of large organizations to achieve true agility despite adopting agile ceremonies. His assessment of over 200 companies found that most adopted practices like daily standups and sprints while maintaining command-and-control leadership and low-trust cultures. The APH model is explicitly object-oriented rather than linear—it reflects how agile actually works: teams instantiate their own versions of ceremonies and techniques while remaining aligned to organizational values. Each performance circle encapsulates a discrete set of behaviors needed for successful adoption and transformation. The six circles work interdependently: Leading sets values and vision; Providing builds infrastructure; Envisioning clarifies customer needs; Crafting delivers quality; Affirming measures and observes; Teaming develops people. Holons within each circle are described as "self-reliant entities" that can stand alone but are integral to the whole, borrowing Koestler's concept of self-regulating hierarchies from *The Ghost in the Machine*.

## Why It Matters

The APH solves a critical gap in agile adoption: it provides guidance on how behaviors should align with stated values, not just what ceremonies to run. By making values traceability explicit—showing which frameworks enable which values and which behaviors demonstrate them—the model gives leaders a concrete way to recognize real agility versus ceremony-only adoption. The holarchy structure also reframes organizational design around interdependent behavioral units rather than linear processes, which matches how innovation and self-organization actually emerge in practice.
