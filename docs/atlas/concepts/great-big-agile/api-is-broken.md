# The API Is Broken: Rules of Men vs. Rules of Nature

## Definition

The core diagnosis of large-scale agile failure is an organizational type mismatch: leaders tell teams to "be agile" while themselves practicing command-and-control governance, creating a low-trust environment that cannot support self-organization. Dalton frames this as a clash between "the rules of men"—hierarchical authority where masses are organized to serve leaders' goals—and "the rules of nature"—self-organizing systems where roles and accountabilities are distributed and people coordinate without central permission. When the operating system remains command-and-control while the mechanics become agile, the architecture breaks.

## In the Book

Dalton observes that in every large organization he assesses, leaders want to push decision-making down and give autonomy to teams, yet their actual behaviors contradict this. Specifically, the book documents patterns including task management by project managers, low-trust performance reviews, command-and-control risk management, and processes designed to control rather than enable. These stem from decades of organizational tradition and leadership training in environments where distrust was adaptive. The problem is not with the ceremonies themselves but with the cultural context. Agile frameworks like Scrum and XP become what Dalton calls "ScrumBut" or "water-scrum-fall"—adopting the surface while the underlying operating model remains unchanged. The metaphor of "the API is broken" suggests that the interface between organizational values and actual governance is fundamentally misaligned.

## Why It Matters

This concept clarifies why copying agile practices fails at scale. It reframes the problem as not one of technique adoption but of organizational alignment. Leaders can now see that asking teams to self-organize within a low-trust command-and-control structure is a direct contradiction—the system itself prevents what the ceremonies are designed to enable. The distinction between "rules of men" and "rules of nature" provides a diagnostic lens to distinguish true agility (distributed trust and decision authority) from agile theater (ceremonies without culture change).
