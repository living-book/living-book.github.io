# Holons: Self-Reliant Organizational Units

## Definition

A holon (from Arthur Koestler's *The Ghost in the Machine*) is a self-reliant unit that possesses independence and can handle contingencies without asking higher authorities for permission, while also functioning as an integral part of a larger whole. In Dalton's Agile Performance Holarchy, eighteen holons are distributed across six performance circles. Each holon—such as Valuing, Engaging, Partnering, Planning, Solving, Delivering, Understanding, Confirming, Organizing, and Growing—can stand alone as a complete behavioral unit but is also dependent on and coordinates with other holons.

## In the Book

Dalton borrows Koestler's concept to frame agile organizations not as rigid hierarchies or flat structures but as holarchies: "hierarchies of self-regulating holons that function first as autonomous wholes in supraordination to their parts, secondly as dependent parts in a subordination to controls on higher levels, and thirdly in coordination with their local environment." This structure allows teams and functions to make autonomous decisions (pulling work, setting their own "agile brand," choosing ceremonies) without central approval, while remaining coordinated through shared values and transparent information radiators. Importantly, Dalton notes that freedom to self-organize comes through mastery. Teams progress through shu-ha-ri stages—learning forms, internalizing principles, then mastering them—and only at the ri stage can they safely innovate without explicit oversight.

## Why It Matters

The holon concept solves a fundamental dilemma in scaling agile: how to enable autonomous decision-making in teams without losing organizational coherence. Traditional hierarchies achieve coherence through control; flat structures achieve autonomy but lose alignment. Holarchy achieves both by making each unit—each team, each function—responsible for its own excellence while remaining tightly coupled to others through shared values, metrics, and ceremonies. This allows large organizations with hundreds of teams to coordinate without the bottleneck of centralized decision-making.
