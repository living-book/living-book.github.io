# Pain-Point Process Mapping

## Definition

A pain point is a specific area where a customer experiences frustration,
boredom, or inefficiency while pursuing a job — and pain points are best found
by mapping the customer's process step by step, for one narrowly defined
occasion, rather than for an "average" scenario. This same mapping exercise
also surfaces stakeholders beyond the buyer: the person who buys, the person
who prepares, and the person who consumes are often different people with
different, sometimes conflicting requirements.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 opens with an alarm clock owner who glued a bump onto one of fifteen
identical buttons because he couldn't find the single button that silenced the
alarm while fumbling half-asleep in the dark — a workaround that reveals a pain
point the manufacturer never saw. The book then walks through grocery research
where the authors identified at least three distinct stakeholders in the
"shelf-to-table" flow — the shopper, the meal preparer, and the diner — and
shows that studying only one (say, the in-store shopper) would have wrongly
concluded that price and shopping-pattern fit were the dominant jobs, missing
ease-of-preparation and spiciness entirely. The chapter insists process maps
be built per specific occasion (a Tuesday lunch, a Friday date-night dinner),
not for "shopping for meals" in general, because functional and emotional jobs
shift by occasion. It also covers the decision of whether to replace or
complement existing behavior: Kellogg's Breakfast Mates (cereal, milk, and a
spoon in one package) failed because it forced an unresolved choice — refrigerate
and eat chilled cereal, or pour warm milk — while the book's account of
girls'-education programs in post-Taliban Afghanistan shows the same lesson at
civilizational scale: only when NGOs stopped working around community elders
and started "swimming with the tide" of existing attitudes did enrollment
programs succeed.

## Why It Matters

Treating "the customer" as a single undifferentiated actor, and treating
their journey as one generic average scenario, both erase the exact place
where a solution needs to intervene. Mapping the real process for a real
occasion — and naming every stakeholder who touches it — turns a vague
intention to "reduce friction" into a specific, actionable target, and
surfaces whether a new solution must fight entrenched behavior or can simply
slot in alongside it.
