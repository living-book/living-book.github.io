# The Job to Be Done

## Definition

A job to be done is the underlying task a customer is trying to accomplish in
their life — independent of any product they currently use to accomplish it.
Jobs have both a functional dimension (the practical task) and an emotional
dimension (how the customer wants to feel while doing it and after). As the
authors put it, quoting Theodore Levitt: "People don't want to buy a
quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole." Studying what customers
currently buy tells you about today's solutions; studying their jobs tells you
where the market can actually go.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 opens with a direct contrast: in the late 1990s, Stephen's team at
Psion built one of the first smartphones by stuffing in every feature its
engineers could imagine — you could even send a fax from it — while
Research in Motion built the BlackBerry around a single job: keeping in touch
through email. The BlackBerry dominated the market for seven years despite
doing far less. The book uses this to argue that satisfying a handful of
high-priority jobs beats a "kitchen sink" feature list, and that jobs differ
from success criteria — Brookwood School's "community of exuberant learners"
branding missed that community wasn't the parents' actual job (feeling their
child was valued as an individual was). The chapter also develops the
functional/emotional split at length: Snapchat won millennial users not by
matching Facebook or Instagram feature-for-feature but by satisfying emotional
jobs (documenting real, unglamorous moments; belonging to a closed community),
and Beats Electronics out-marketed acoustically superior Sennheiser and Bose
headphones because a $300 price tag bought "a seat at the lunchroom's cool
table" — an emotional job, not a functional one.

## Why It Matters

Framing a market around the job rather than the product category widens the
field of both competitors and customers you can see. A bank that thinks its
job is "process mortgages" misses that the customer's real job is "move into a
new home" — which is why First Ipswich Bank could differentiate by rolling
renovation costs for pre-1750 houses into the mortgage itself. Once you name
the job, you can predict which product attributes are noise (Excel's jump from
56 to 4.3 billion colors per workbook) and which are load-bearing, and you can
spot adjacent, currently-unaddressed jobs worth building for.
