# Governance by Boundaries, Not Plans

## Definition

A control model in which leaders replace detailed plans, budgets, and predetermined actions with a small set of explicit principles, values, and boundaries — the strategic domain, ethical limits, reporting intervals, the line between what a team must do versus what it may choose to do — and then let teams decide freely inside them. Control shifts from specifying the action in advance to bounding the space of acceptable actions and trusting judgment within it.

## In the Book

Chapter 7's first principle is built almost entirely from Svenska Handelsbanken under CEO Jan Wallander, who told the authors "what holds the organization together is not a plan, but a commitment to a clear purpose and to a set of clearly articulated principles and values" — famously noting the bank's only "organization chart" was its internal phone directory. At Borealis, finance director Bjarte Bogsnes describes an "ethics test" replacing detailed rules: "Is it acceptable if the results of their actions appear on the front page of the newspaper? If yes, go ahead. If no, don't do it." Chapter 6 shows customer ownership itself becomes a boundary — at Handelsbanken and Ahlsell, branches own their customers outright, so large clients accustomed to escalating to head office instead build a relationship with the local decision-maker. The book explicitly frames the underlying logic as the same one separating a market economy from a centrally planned one: continuous local adaptation within known rules outperforms detailed central direction, because the rules can hold even as the plan they'd otherwise require becomes obsolete.

## Why It Matters

A plan tells people what to do and goes stale the moment reality diverges from its assumptions; a boundary tells people what not to do and stays valid regardless of what happens inside it. This distinction generalizes to any system trying to balance speed with control — API rate limits versus request-by-request approval, a legal code versus case-by-case permission, a style guide versus mandatory templates — wherever the volume or unpredictability of decisions makes pre-approving each one impossible.
