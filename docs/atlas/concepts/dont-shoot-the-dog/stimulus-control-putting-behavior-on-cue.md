# Stimulus Control: Putting Behavior on Cue

## Definition

Stimulus control is the process of teaching a behavior to occur reliably in response to a specific cue (a signal, word, gesture, or circumstance) and nowhere else. A behavior "under stimulus control" happens when the cue is present and tends to extinguish in its absence. This principle—that behavior naturally tends to disappear when the cue is withheld—provides an elegant way to eliminate unwanted behavior: bring it under cue control, then never give the cue.

## In the Book

Pryor explains: "When a behavior is brought under stimulus control—that is, when the organism learns to offer the behavior in response to some kind of cue and only then—the behavior tends to extinguish in the absence of the cue. You can use this natural law to get rid of all kinds of things you don't want, simply by bringing the behavior under the control of a cue... and then never giving the cue."

She discovered this by accident with a dolphin named Makua who would sink to the bottom of the tank to avoid blindfolds. Instead of forcing or bribing him to the surface, she "captured" his sinking behavior and reinforced it on cue—deliberately rewarding him when he sank. Once he learned that sinking earned fish on command, it became "his" choice rather than avoidance. She then stopped giving the cue. The behavior extinguished, and blindfold training proceeded smoothly.

Pryor applies the principle to children: when excited kids are too loud in a car en route to the circus, instead of punishment (Method 3) or games (Method 5), she puts noise-making "on cue": "Okay, everybody make as much noise as you possibly can, starting now!" After thirty seconds of authorized loudness, it becomes boring, and the novelty is gone. The behavior that was fun because it was forbidden becomes unrewarding once it's officially allowed and predictable. She uses the same principle with her daughter's toy poodle's barking: a towel hung on the door knob signals "barking won't work"; when removed, barking is answered. The puppy learned not to bother trying when the towel was present.

The broader principle extends beyond eliminating behavior. Stimulus control is how trainers achieve precision communication: a police dog learns to search high when it hears a click from its handler, identifying the exact height where the handler saw the target object. An agility dog hears "jump" or "tunnel" mid-run and instantly chooses the right obstacle. A person learning to control chronic lateness might deliberately practice being late to low-stakes events, bringing lateness under the cue "when I decide to be late," which then extinguishes lateness in other situations where the decision cue is absent.

## Why It Matters

Stimulus control reveals that behavior is not random or uncontrollable but highly lawful and context-dependent. This has practical implications: many unwanted behaviors (kids fighting in the backseat, dogs jumping on guests, teenagers staying out late) persist because they're not under stimulus control—they can occur anytime and anywhere. The framework suggests that precision training (clearly communicating what's wanted and when) can eliminate problems that punishment never touches. It also demonstrates that "getting behavior under control" literally means making it controllable—teaching the subject to express it on command makes it available for refinement and conditional use while extinguishing random occurrences. For anyone managing behavior—parents, teachers, managers, coaches, therapists—the principle shows that defining the *contexts* where a behavior is appropriate is often more powerful than punishing its occurrence.
