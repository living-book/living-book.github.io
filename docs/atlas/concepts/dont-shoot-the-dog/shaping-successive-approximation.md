# Shaping: Successive Approximation

## Definition

Shaping is the process of building complex or unlikely behaviors by breaking them into small steps and reinforcing each successive approximation toward the ultimate goal. Rather than punishing failures or waiting for the complete behavior to occur spontaneously, the trainer identifies small variations in the subject's natural behavior that move in the right direction, reinforces those variations, then gradually raises the criteria for reinforcement. The formal term is "successive approximation."

## In the Book

Pryor introduces shaping through the example of training a chicken to "dance" by first reinforcing leftward movement, then stronger leftward movement, then quarter-circle turns, then half-circles, building toward the final goal of full spinning dances at speed. She emphasizes that shaping is possible because "the behavior of living things is variable"—organisms naturally vary their actions, and skilled trainers exploit this natural variation.

The book presents Ten Laws of Shaping that govern how trainers should apply the principle: raise criteria in small enough increments that the subject always has a realistic chance of earning reinforcement; train one aspect of behavior at a time (not multiple criteria simultaneously); put current-level responses on variable schedules before raising criteria; temporarily relax old criteria when introducing new ones; stay ahead of your subject by planning the full program; don't change trainers mid-stream; try alternative procedures if one isn't working; don't interrupt sessions gratuitously; review the whole shaping process if behavior deteriorates ("go back to kindergarten"); and quit while you're ahead on a high note.

Pryor illustrates shaping with examples across domains: teaching children to putt by separating distance and direction into independent practice, training figure skaters by breaking movements into tiny components (starting with falling and getting up), and shaping dogs to perform complex obedience behaviors. She contrasts shaping—which emphasizes constant progress and rapid skill acquisition—with traditional drill-and-repetition approaches that are slower and more frustrating.

## Why It Matters

Shaping reveals that learning speed is not primarily determined by a subject's "natural ability" but by the quality of the training design. A trainer who understands shaping principles can teach almost any learner almost any behavior—given enough time and careful sequencing—without requiring force, fear, or punishment. This has profound implications beyond animal training: it applies to children learning academic and social skills, employees developing job competencies, athletes improving performance, and anyone trying to acquire new abilities in themselves. The concept transforms failure from a character defect into a signal that the current step is too large and should be broken down further. It also explains why some teachers, coaches, and trainers are dramatically more effective than others—not because they have better subjects, but because they understand how to design learning sequences that keep the learner continuously successful.
