# Distrust as a Projection of Missing Self-Trust

## Definition

Before someone can accurately judge whether others are trustworthy, they first have to be able to trust themselves — and when that self-trust is missing, the resulting anxiety gets misattributed outward as distrust of managers, colleagues, or partners. Self-trust in turn rests on two testable components: character (integrity — honesty and congruence — plus intent — motive and agenda) and competence (capabilities plus a track record of results). Credibility, in this framing, is simply character and competence made visible to others.

## In the Book

The chapter opens with Roger Merrill's story of his father, a company founder who refused advisers' suggestion to declare bankruptcy after a product failure and instead personally repaid every debt over years — a bank manager later told Merrill's father would be given "any amount within my power at any time" because of what he'd seen him do "when he didn't have to." The book's diagnostic example is a man who approached the authors after a presentation and, listing "I can't trust my manager... my company... my spouse," finally admitted, in a whisper, "The problem is that I don't trust myself. And that's what I'm projecting onto others." The authors generalize this into the "Start with Self" principle — the first step in every trust-building process the authors use with client organizations — and name its counterfeits: pretending, faking credibility ("Trust me!"), taking credit for success while blaming others for failure, and using ego and arrogance as a substitute for genuine self-trust.

## Why It Matters

This reframes a persistent, unexplained pattern of distrust — "I can't trust anyone here" — as a diagnostic question pointed inward before it's pointed outward: is the evidence actually about the other party, or is it free-floating anxiety from an unexamined gap in one's own integrity, intent, capability, or results? The character/competence split also gives a concrete audit: distrust rooted in a character concern (dishonesty, hidden agenda) calls for a different intervention than distrust rooted in a competence concern (unproven skill, no track record), and the same two-axis test applies to assessing one's own credibility before extending judgment about anyone else's.
