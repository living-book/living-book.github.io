# False Trust and Fake Trust

## Definition

Leaders who withhold real trust often believe they're extending it, because they've substituted one of two counterfeits for the genuine act. "False trust" hands someone responsibility for an outcome without the authority or resources actually needed to deliver it — trust in name, set up to fail in practice. "Fake trust" looks identical to real trust from the outside — the words, the delegation — but is paired with hovering, checking up, and micromanaging, so the recipient experiences surveillance, not confidence. Genuine trust requires transferring real discretion and then leaving it transferred.

## In the Book

Chapter 8 opens with Greg Link's own story: as a "hellion" teenager with a shaggy-haired, dubious background he hadn't disclosed on his job application, his grocery-store manager Ralph unexpectedly tossed him the store keys and told him to close up — full responsibility for supervising staff and handling the cash drawers. Link describes the unfamiliar surge of responsibility and loyalty that moment produced, calling it a turning point that shaped his entire career and his own instinct to extend trust to others. The chapter names the false-trust/fake-trust counterfeits explicitly against this backdrop, then backs genuine extension of trust with results: the Richmond, BC detachment of the Royal Canadian Mounted Police used trust-building "positive ticketing" to cut juvenile arrests and court referrals by 50% and recidivism to 5%; Best Buy's headquarters created a "Results Only Work Environment" (ROWE) — no fixed schedule, no meetings, judged purely on output — that produced a 35% productivity increase within six weeks and a 50% drop in turnover, which the authors attribute specifically to crystal-clear expectations combined with genuine (not false or fake) trust.

## Why It Matters

This gives a check against the common self-deception of believing you've delegated when you've actually either set someone up without the means to succeed, or retained control while performing the language of trust. It separates delegation failures into two distinct, differently-fixed problems: a false-trust failure is fixed by transferring actual authority and resources, not more encouragement; a fake-trust failure is fixed by genuinely withdrawing oversight, not by better-worded delegation. The distinction travels to any principal-agent relationship — funding a team, outsourcing a decision, empowering a subordinate — where the visible act of "trusting" can mask either sabotage-by-underresourcing or control-by-surveillance.
