# Declaring Intent Closes the Attribution Asymmetry

## Definition

There is a structural asymmetry in how people interpret behavior: we judge ourselves charitably by our intent ("I meant well"), but we judge others harshly by their observable behavior alone, because their intent is invisible to us. Declaring intent — stating both *what* you plan to do and *why* — closes that gap in advance, so the other party interprets your subsequent actions through the motive you supplied rather than through whatever motive they infer on their own. The book treats this as a paired action: declare your own intent, and by default assume positive intent in others until their behavior proves otherwise.

## In the Book

Chapter 6 opens with the authors contrasting chaotic Bangalore traffic, where drivers signal only by honking as they cut in, with orderly Swiss driving, where blinkers are used well in advance — both are "declaring intent," but the Swiss version is legible enough for others to plan around, and the Bangalore version isn't. The central case study is a company entering contract negotiations that had historically been tit-for-tat: instead, its leaders declared up front that they intended to negotiate differently, disclosed their real objectives and their best offer immediately, and stated what mattered to them and why. Their partner reciprocated with its own disclosure, and both sides reported their best-ever deal, reached faster, with higher resulting trust. A second example — Charlotte County Public Schools after Hurricane Charley — shows the superintendent publicly declaring the district's intent (pay employees immediately, no layoffs) before any formal union negotiation, which compressed a subsequent financial negotiation that would normally have taken months into two hours with "no caucusing, no threats." The book names the counterfeits of each half explicitly: hiding intent, operating with a hidden agenda, or overpromising on one side; giving lip service to trusting others while secretly checking up on them on the other.

## Why It Matters

Most trust breakdowns are not disagreements about facts but disagreements about inferred motive, and motive is the one thing the other party cannot observe directly — they only see behavior and must guess the "why." Making the why explicit removes the need for the other party to guess, which is where suspicion usually originates. This generalizes past interpersonal trust to any situation where an actor's behavior will be watched and interpreted without direct access to their reasoning — a policy change, a system's automated decision, an organization's public announcement — because in all of these, silence about intent gets filled in by the observer's own worst-case assumptions.
