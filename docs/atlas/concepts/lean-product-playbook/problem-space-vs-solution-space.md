# Problem Space versus Solution Space

## Definition

Solution space is any product, mockup, or prototype that embodies a specific
implementation choice; problem space is where customer needs — pains, desires,
jobs, user stories — live independent of any solution. The two are distinct
and sequential: real needs exist whether or not a product yet addresses them,
while a design or prototype has already committed to a "how."

## In the Book

Olsen's central illustration is the space pen story: NASA's contractor spent
$1 million developing a pen that writes in zero gravity, while the Russian
space agency simply used pencils. Framing the problem as "a pen that works in
zero gravity" fixates on a pen; reframing it as "a way to record notes in zero
gravity that is easy to use, inexpensive, and needs no power source" opens the
door to cheaper, equally valid solutions — while still, on reflection,
explaining why NASA's actual choice (nonflammable, no floating debris) was
sound. He pairs this with Intuit founder Scott Cook's observation that
TurboTax's biggest competitor wasn't rival tax software but pen-and-paper tax
filing — a competitor invisible if you define your market by existing
solutions rather than by the underlying need. The chapter also reconciles the
"should you listen to customers" debate (Henry Ford's "faster horse," Steve
Jobs's inside-out design at Apple) by noting that Apple's breakthroughs, like
Touch ID, still required a deep read of the problem space (convenience,
security) even without soliciting solution ideas from customers directly.

## Why It Matters

Naming a market, a competitor set, or a requirement in solution-space terms
silently narrows the space of possible answers before anyone starts looking —
disruption often arrives exactly from a competitor that was invisible under
that narrow, solution-anchored definition. Deliberately restating a problem
without reference to any particular fix is a cheap way to surface options
(and competitors) that a solution-first framing would never expose, in any
domain where "we need an X" is really shorthand for a need X happens to
satisfy today.
