# The Daily Oscillation (Peak, Trough, Rebound)

## Definition

Across a normal day, most people move through the same three-stage pattern regardless of what they're doing: a morning peak of vigilance and analytic sharpness, an afternoon trough of low mood and impaired attention, and a late-day rebound favoring looser, more associative thinking. Pink calls this "as predictable as the ocean tides." It is not caused by how tired you are or how much coffee you've had — it tracks an underlying circadian and mood rhythm independent of workload.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 opens the book with Captain Turner's fatal afternoon decisions aboard the Lusitania, then builds the case that mood-tracking studies of hundreds of millions of tweets, and separately of vigilance and analytic-task performance, show the same universal curve — a mid-morning high, an early-to-mid-afternoon low, and an evening recovery. Chapter 2 ("Afternoons and Coffee Spoons") applies this to consequential real-world timing: anesthesia errors, colonoscopy quality, and judicial parole decisions all cluster worse in the afternoon trough. Pink argues the effect is large enough to matter in life-and-death settings — hospital patient outcomes and courtroom leniency both measurably shift with time of day — and that the trough is a biological floor, not a discipline failure.

## Why It Matters

The oscillation gives you a template for sequencing any day's tasks by type rather than by priority alone: put vigilance-and-precision work (analysis, decisions, procedures) in the peak, expect impaired judgment in the trough and build in guardrails rather than more willpower, and save loosely associative or insight-driven work for the rebound. It also reframes "bad performance in the afternoon" as a predictable systemic risk — in medicine, courts, or any high-stakes process — rather than a matter of individual competence, which is what makes the pattern actionable at an institutional level, not just a personal one.
