# Restorative Breaks

## Definition

A restorative break is a deliberately placed pause — moving, socializing, or stepping outside — that resets attention before it fully depletes, rather than a break taken only after exhaustion sets in. Pink distinguishes these from mere "vigilance breaks" (short check-ins before high-stakes moments, like a captain reviewing the watch) and argues breaks work best when they involve some combination of movement, nature, full detachment from work, and social contact with others.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 traces a body of evidence Pink summarizes as "breaks are not a sign of sloth but a sign of strength": studies of Israeli parole judges whose favorable rulings collapse before a break and rebound sharply after one; University of Michigan hospital staff performing better after mandated vigilance pauses; and school systems that cut recess despite evidence that restorative pauses improve children's arithmetic performance afterward. He builds toward what he calls the "Platonic ideal" of a break — short, social, outdoors, and away from screens — contrasting it with the common but weaker habit of scrolling a phone at one's desk, which doesn't detach attention from the same cognitive mode at all.

## Why It Matters

This concept turns "taking a break" from a guilt-laden indulgence into a specific, designable intervention with predictable payoffs — it tells you not just that breaks help, but which features of a break (social contact, movement, being outdoors, full detachment) actually restore vigilance versus which merely feel like a break. It applies anywhere sustained attention is required — courtrooms, hospitals, classrooms, offices — and reframes recovery time as an engineered input to performance rather than time stolen from it.
