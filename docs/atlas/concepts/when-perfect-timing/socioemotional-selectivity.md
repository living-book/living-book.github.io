# Socioemotional Selectivity (How a Sensed Ending Reshapes Goals)

## Definition

Socioemotional selectivity, a theory developed by Stanford psychologist Laura Carstensen, holds that it isn't age itself that changes what people want — it's a shrinking sense of remaining time. When time feels expansive and open-ended, people pursue "knowledge-related goals" and build wide, loose social networks to gather future information and options. When an ending becomes salient — time feels scarce — people pivot to "emotional goals," actively pruning their networks down to the relationships that matter most.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 presents Tammy English and Carstensen's ten-year study of people aged 18 to 93, which found social networks shrinking sharply around age 60 — but shows this isn't due to isolation or loneliness. Using a concentric-circle mapping exercise, they found the outer, more peripheral circles of acquaintance shrink while the innermost circle of close relationships stays stable; older people are "actively eliminating" — pruning — peripheral connections that feel less emotionally meaningful. Crucially, Pink shows this isn't really about chronological age: Carstensen found the same pruning behavior in graduating college seniors versus incoming freshmen, in people about to switch jobs or cities, and in residents of Hong Kong surveyed four months before the 1997 handover to China. Conversely, when Carstensen told older experimental subjects to imagine a medical breakthrough had just added 20 years to their life, their pruning behavior vanished — matching younger people's. The mechanism is the horizon, not the birthday.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates "getting older" from "sensing an ending" as the actual cause of a well-documented behavior change — which means any bounded transition (a graduation, a move, a merger, a terminal diagnosis, a project's final phase) can trigger the same selective narrowing of goals and relationships that we normally attribute only to old age. It gives you a predictive tool: whenever you engineer or observe a shrinking time horizon, expect people to disengage from broad, exploratory goals and concentrate on what they judge emotionally essential — a pattern relevant to team dynamics near a project deadline, employee behavior before a known departure, or customer retention near a subscription's end.
