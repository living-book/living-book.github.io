# The Uh-Oh Effect (Midpoints as Slump or Spark)

## Definition

Midpoints — of a project, a game, a holiday, even a life — produce two opposite effects, and Pink names both. The "slump" is a relaxation of standards and effort in the middle of a sequence, evidenced by U-shaped patterns of behavior. The "spark," which Pink calls the "uh-oh effect," is a burst of urgency triggered when people consciously register that half their time is gone, functioning as a psychological alarm clock that reorganizes effort toward the goal.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 documents the slump first: Connie Gersick's research on Hanukkah-candle-lighting behavior found participation dropping from 76% on night one to under 50% on middle nights before climbing back above 50% by night eight — even among the most religious participants — and a related scissors-cutting experiment found people "literally cut corners" in the middle of a five-card sequence but not at the start or end. The book then flips to the spark: Gersick's earlier landmark study of project teams found groups made an explicit "uh-oh" remark (e.g., "Now we're really in trouble") almost exactly at the temporal midpoint of a deadline, and six of eight teams made their most significant progress in the immediate aftermath — a "concentrated midpoint burst." She found the same pattern at a longer scale in a venture-backed startup whose CEO deliberately scheduled major strategy reviews at the calendar year's midpoint. Pink also uses the NBA/NCAA halftime literature (including the 1982 Georgetown–North Carolina championship game) to show teams trailing by a moderate amount at halftime sometimes outperform teams that were tied or leading.

## Why It Matters

The uh-oh effect tells you that a midpoint's psychological impact is not automatic — it depends on whether you notice it and how you frame it. Deliberately marking the midpoint of any long effort (a scheduled midyear review, an explicit "we're halfway" checkpoint) converts a passive, corner-cutting slump into an active jolt of urgency, giving anyone managing a project, a habit, or a team a specific, low-cost intervention point rather than relying on the ends of the timeline alone.
