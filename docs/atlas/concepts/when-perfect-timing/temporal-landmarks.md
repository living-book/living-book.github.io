# Temporal Landmarks and the Fresh Start Effect

## Definition

A temporal landmark is a date that stands out from the ordinary flow of days — the first of the year, a birthday, the start of a semester, even a Monday — the way a gas station on a road serves as a landmark for navigating space. Pink, drawing on research by Katy Milkman and colleagues, calls the resulting boost the "fresh start effect": landmarks interrupt attention to daily minutiae, prompt people to step back and view their lives from a higher, more abstract vantage point, and psychologically detach their present self from a previous self's failures — making it easier to begin a diet, an exercise routine, or a new goal.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 shows fresh-start effects appearing at social landmarks shared by everyone (New Year's Day, Mondays, the start of a school semester) and personal landmarks unique to an individual (a birthday, an anniversary, moving to a new city). Milkman's research on Google searches for "diet" and gym check-ins finds reliable spikes right after these landmarks. Pink cites the "New Year, New You" framing explicitly and describes people deliberately manufacturing personal landmarks — like picking January 8th, a date with no inherent significance, as a self-declared fresh start for a new project — showing the landmark's power comes from the psychological meaning assigned to it, not from any property of the calendar date itself.

## Why It Matters

This concept gives you a lever for behavior change that doesn't depend on willpower: instead of waiting to feel motivated, you can manufacture a landmark — a symbolic line between a flawed past self and a resolved future self — and use it to trigger a fresh start on demand. It also explains why habit interventions and campaigns (a company's fiscal year kickoff, a "Meatless Monday" public health campaign) that attach themselves to an existing or invented landmark outperform interventions launched on an arbitrary, unmarked day.
