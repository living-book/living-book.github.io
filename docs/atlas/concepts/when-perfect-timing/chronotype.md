# Chronotype

## Definition

Chronotype is a person's individual circadian signature — the biological timing that decides whether their peak mental sharpness lands in the morning, midday, or evening. Pink divides people into three groups: "larks" (early risers, sharpest in the morning, roughly 15-20% of people), "owls" (late risers, sharpest in the evening, another 15-20%), and "third birds," the majority in between. Genetics explains at least half the variability, and age reliably shifts it — children skew lark, adolescents swing owl, and people become larks again after 60.

## In the Book

Chapter 1 establishes that everyone moves through the same daily arc — "a peak, a trough, and a rebound" — but chronotype determines the order and timing of that arc for each individual. Pink walks through the nineteen-question chronotype instrument developed by Swedish and British researchers (and a shortened three-question version readers can self-administer), plotting chronotypes as a bell curve. He uses Hall and Oates as an example of contrasting types working together, and cites research showing owls display more risk-taking and even some antisocial correlates, while larks and third birds show the classic peak-trough-rebound pattern and owls often show the reverse (rebound-trough-peak). He also traces jury and courtroom studies and a factory manager who reorganized shifts around chronotype fit, arguing that matching task type to your personal biological window — not fighting it with a fixed 9-to-5 script — improves both performance and ethics.

## Why It Matters

Chronotype reframes "I'm not a morning person" from an excuse into a diagnosable, partly heritable trait with real performance consequences — it tells you when to schedule analytic work (peak), when to expect your judgment to be worst (trough), and when creative, associative thinking actually improves (rebound). Anywhere a system assumes one uniform daily rhythm for all its people — schools, hospital shifts, courtroom schedules, standard office hours — chronotype exposes a hidden mismatch between imposed timing and biological timing, and a lever for closing it.
