# Multi-Sided Platforms

## Definition

A multi-sided platform brings together two or more distinct customer segments that need each other, where the platform's value proposition is largely the matchmaking itself — one segment cannot exist, for this business, without the other. The pattern's structure is distinct: multiple Value Propositions and Revenue Streams (one per side) hung off shared Key Resources (the platform) and Key Activities (platform management, service provisioning, and platform promotion), with pricing decisions turning on which side to subsidize to grow the whole.

## In the Book

The book traces Apple's evolution from a stand-alone hardware maker into a platform operator in three moves: the 2001 iPod as a closed device with no platform economics; the 2003 iTunes Music Store connecting rights-holders directly to buyers, which made Apple the world's largest online music retailer; and the 2008 App Store, which channels all third-party iPhone application sales through Apple in exchange for a 30 percent royalty. A parallel case is the Nintendo Wii: rather than chase raw processing power like Sony and Microsoft, Nintendo built a cheaper, motion-controlled console aimed at casual gamers, then monetized both sides of a "double-sided platform" — console sales to players and royalties from game developers — which the book credits, alongside low-cost differentiation and a new target market, for the Wii's commercial success.

## Why It Matters

Once you see a business as connecting two dependent groups rather than serving one customer, the strategic question changes: which side do you subsidize to make the platform attractive enough to tip, and where do you actually recoup the cost? The same matchmaking logic explains dynamics far outside commerce — any system where growth on one side is what makes participation valuable on the other (marketplaces, standards, communities) lives or dies on getting that subsidization asymmetry right.
