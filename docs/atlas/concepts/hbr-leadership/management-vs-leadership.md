# Management vs. Leadership: Two Complementary Functions

## Definition

Leadership and management are fundamentally different systems of action with distinct functions and characteristic activities. Management's job is coping with complexity—creating order, consistency, and predictability in large organizations through planning, budgeting, organizing, staffing, and controlling. Leadership's job is coping with change—mobilizing people toward a new direction through vision-setting, alignment, and inspiration. Neither is superior; both are necessary. Most U.S. corporations suffer from being over-managed and under-led.

## In the Book

Kotter distinguishes the two through parallel activities. Where management plans and budgets (setting targets, creating detailed steps, allocating resources), leadership sets direction (developing a vision, often distant, and strategies to achieve it). Where management organizes and staffs (designing structures and assigning people to jobs), leadership aligns people (communicating the vision and building coalitions who understand and are committed to it). Where management controls and problem-solves (monitoring results, identifying deviations, solving problems), leadership motivates and inspires (keeping people moving despite major obstacles by appealing to untapped human needs and emotions).

His military analogy illustrates why both matter: A peacetime army can survive with good management and minimal leadership at the top. A wartime army requires competent leadership at all levels—"no one yet has figured out how to manage people effectively into battle; they must be led." Similarly, as business environments grow more volatile—faster technological change, greater competition, deregulation, overcapacity, unstable markets—the demand for leadership grows. Doing yesterday's work 5% better is no longer a survival formula. Major change is necessary, and "more change always demands more leadership."

Yet strong leadership with weak management is sometimes worse than the reverse. The real challenge is combining both: using management and leadership to balance each other.

## Why It Matters

This distinction prevents organizations from confusing execution with direction, order with purpose. It shows why hiring charismatic visionaries without operational discipline fails, and why process-driven managers without change capacity stagnate. For any organization facing disruptive markets or needing cultural shifts, it clarifies that the problem is often not "we need better people" but "we need different functions from different people—and we're conflating them."
