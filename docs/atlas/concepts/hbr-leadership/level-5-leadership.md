# Level 5 Leadership

## Definition

Level 5 leaders blend extreme personal humility with intense professional will. They are not the celebrity CEOs or charismatic visionaries that organizations typically seek. Instead, they are quiet, often awkward, self-effacing leaders who set unwavering standards and persistently reshape their organizations for long-term greatness, not personal glory. This paradoxical combination is a necessary requirement for transforming a good company into a truly great one.

## In the Book

Collins' research studied 1,435 Fortune 500 companies and identified 11 that achieved and sustained greatness—generating stock returns at least three times the market's for 15 years after a major transition. All had a Level 5 leader at the helm.

Darwin Smith, Kimberly-Clark CEO from 1971 to 1991, exemplifies this type. He was shy, unpretentious, awkward, and shunned attention. When a board member doubted his appointment, Smith wasn't defensive; he simply accepted the challenge. Yet he possessed iron will and unwavering standards. He determined that Kimberly-Clark must divest profitable but non-leader-ranking paper businesses (despite Wall Street skepticism) and redefine the company's core business. He acted quietly, calmly, and determinedly—not through inspiring charisma but through inspired standards. Under his stewardship, Kimberly-Clark became the leading consumer paper products company in the world, beating Scott Paper and Procter & Gamble, and generating cumulative stock returns 4.1 times greater than the general market.

Level 5 leaders routinely credit others, external factors, and good luck for success, but when results disappoint, they blame themselves. They sit atop a hierarchy of four lower leadership capabilities (Highly Capable Individual, Contributing Team Member, Competent Manager, Effective Leader) and possess all of them, plus the distinctive Level 5 paradox.

## Why It Matters

This concept disrupts the assumption that great leadership requires larger-than-life personalities, celebrity presence, and constant visibility. It reveals that sustained greatness—not just turnarounds or short-term wins—may require a fundamentally different leadership profile: one where personal ambition is subordinated to institutional purpose, and fierce determination is communicated through standards and focus rather than charisma. For boards seeking to transform legacy businesses or sustain excellence, it suggests looking for quiet resolve and institutional commitment, not star power.
