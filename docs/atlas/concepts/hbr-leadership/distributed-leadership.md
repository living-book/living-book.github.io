# Distributed Leadership

## Definition

The myth of the "complete leader"—a flawless executive who has it all figured out—is damaging and unsustainable. No single person can simultaneously master the intellectual capacity to interpret complex issues, the imagination to paint a compelling vision, the operational know-how to translate strategy into plans, and the interpersonal skills to build commitment. Effective leadership is distributed across people and capabilities. Leadership requires four interdependent capabilities: sensemaking (understanding the business environment and its implications), relating (building trusting relationships), visioning (creating credible, compelling images of desired futures), and inventing (developing new ways to achieve that vision). Rare individuals excel equally in all four; most leaders are incomplete in some dimension, and that incompleteness is not weakness but realism—the foundation for building leadership capability throughout an organization.

## In the Book

Ancona and her coauthors note that organizations have become less hierarchical and more collaborative for decades. Today's problems require distributed responsibility and initiative. Yet many executives cling to the myth of the complete leader, exhausting themselves and damaging their organizations by trying to do everything themselves—provide all the answers, maintain all relationships, see all possibilities, and execute all plans.

The incomplete leader, by contrast, knows when to let go: when to let the team doing the local market work own the advertising plan, when to let engineers run with their ideas about customer needs. The incomplete leader also understands that leadership exists throughout the organization—wherever expertise, vision, new ideas, and commitment are found. Rather than hoarding leadership at the top, incomplete leaders surface leadership voices from below and coordinate them.

The framework identifies four capabilities. Sensemaking is interpreting the business environment: How will new technologies reshape the industry? How does globalization affect recruitment? A CEO who feels they're always right and gets blindsided by change likely lacks sensemaking. Relating is building trusting relationships—balancing advocacy (explaining your viewpoint) with inquiry (listening to understand others')—and cultivating networks of supportive confidants. Visioning is creating credible, compelling images of a desired future that people want to create together. Inventing is developing new ways of approaching tasks, overcoming seemingly insurmountable problems, and turning visions into reality.

Incomplete leaders understand their strengths and weaknesses in each domain and recruit others who excel where they don't. They create diagnostic tools to assess organizational capability gaps and match people to opportunities based on what the organization needs.

## Why It Matters

This concept transforms incompleteness from a liability into strategic clarity. It shows that the path to organizational leadership is not grooming one perfect CEO but weaving together people's different capabilities and being transparent about who is strong where. It also clarifies that leadership is not about control or omniscience but about cultivating coordination—bringing information to many places, not just the center. For any organization trying to scale, distribute decision-making, or leverage diverse expertise, it provides a framework for thinking about how leadership capabilities need to be orchestrated rather than concentrated.
