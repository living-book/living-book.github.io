# OARS and Reflective Listening

## Definition

OARS names MI's four basic communication microskills — open-ended questions, affirmations, reflective listening, and summaries — the toolset practitioners deploy strategically throughout a session, distinct from MI's higher-level strategies. Reflective listening, the most important and hardest of the four, is not restating what someone said; it is stating a guess at what they meant and delivering it as a statement, not a question, so the other person can confirm, correct, or extend it. Rosengren distinguishes simple reflections (stay very close to the client's own words) from complex reflections (add inference, going meaningfully beyond what was said).

## In the Book

Chapter 3 grounds reflective listening in Thomas Gordon's communication model: a speaker forms a thought (point A), says it (B), the listener hears it (C), and the listener interprets it (D) — and every one of those four transitions is a place meaning can be lost or distorted. The book walks through a therapy example with Arthur, an unwilling adolescent client, tracing exactly how his internal thought ("I'm not sure if I want to be in therapy... how can I trust he won't tell my parents") degrades by the time it reaches the practitioner as "I'm not sure I buy this therapy crap." Reflective listening is the practice of closing the loop back from D to A — treating your interpretation as a hypothesis to test, not a fact.

Chapter 3 also reproduces Thomas Gordon's list of twelve "roadblocks" — ordering, warning, advising, moralizing, judging, even praising and reassuring — behaviors that feel like listening but actually stop the other person's forward momentum; the chapter's point is not that these are always wrong, but that practitioners default to roadblocks and underuse reflection. A small but load-bearing detail: the same words delivered as a question ("You're not sure you want to be here?") versus a statement ("You're not sure you want to be here.") produce measurably different effects on the listener — the statement invites continued exploration, the question invites a yes/no answer and interrupts flow.

## Why It Matters

This gives a concrete, checkable definition of "really listening" that goes beyond attentiveness: it means voicing your interpretation as a falsifiable guess and letting the other person correct it, rather than either staying silent or acting on your inference unchecked. Because communication degrades at four distinct points (formed thought, spoken word, heard word, interpreted meaning), the fix has to be a closed loop, not better attention at any single point — a lens useful anywhere miscommunication compounds silently, from 1:1s to requirements-gathering to family conflict.
