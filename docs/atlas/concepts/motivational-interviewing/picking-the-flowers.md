# Picking the Flowers

## Definition

When a genuinely ambivalent person talks, they produce a mix of change talk, sustain talk, and neutral statements — because both sides of the ambivalence are real, all three kinds of language are equally "true" for them. Rosengren, citing Miller's own garden metaphor, describes the practitioner's job as walking through that garden with the client and choosing only the change-oriented flowers to pick and reflect back, giving limited attention to the sustain-talk flowers even though they were said with equal sincerity. This is a deliberately selective, not neutral, use of reflective listening.

## In the Book

Chapter 8 makes explicit that this selectivity is not incidental to reflective listening — it is a directive choice layered on top of it: "MI practitioners do not give equal weight to all elements; this is what clients are already doing and has allowed them to remain stuck." Working through the case of Carrie, a stalling supervisor, the coach hears her heavy workload (sustain talk) but doesn't dwell on it; instead the coach reflects the piece that carries forward momentum — "I can imagine it feels like there are not enough hours in the workday... and yet you know that the department requires this training as a priority" — a double-sided reflection that acknowledges the real difficulty while keeping the emphasis on the change-supporting half. The chapter pairs this with "providing bouquets": using collecting, linking, and transitional summaries to periodically gather the picked flowers into an organized whole, because being shown the pattern of one's own change-talk is itself often new information to an ambivalent person who "don't need our help to know that they've been unable to make a change" — they need help seeing what's already pointing forward.

## Why It Matters

This is a sharp corrective to the instinct that good listening means treating everything a person says as equally worth reflecting back — genuine even-handedness with an ambivalent person just re-grooves the ambivalence, since both sides get equal airtime and equal reinforcement. Deliberately, transparently amplifying one thread without denying or arguing against the other is a distinct move from either "hear them out neutrally" or "argue them out of the other side," and it's applicable anywhere someone needs to move from stuck to decided while still being heard on the parts that pull the other way — coaching, editing, negotiation, self-reflection.
