# Change Talk (DARN-CAT)

## Definition

Change talk is client speech that indicates movement toward a specific positive change: desire, ability, reasons, or need to change (preparatory language, DARN), and commitment or taking steps (mobilizing language, CAT). Rosengren defines it with four required elements: it must be a statement about change, tied to a specific target behavior, recognized as accurate by the client (even if the practitioner voiced it first as a reflection), and phrased in present tense — "it caused problems and maybe still does" counts; "it used to cause problems" does not.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 opens with a transcript of LaDonna, five-time treatment client, moving from "I don't know why I'm doing it" to acknowledging she stayed sober for three years once and wants to get back there — drawn out entirely through the practitioner's reflections, not advice. Rosengren grounds the mechanism in Darryl Bem's self-perception theory: when people are genuinely uncertain about their own attitudes (as most ambivalent clients are — DiClemente estimates two-thirds), they infer their beliefs from listening to themselves talk, the way they'd infer someone else's beliefs from watching that person's behavior. This is why having the client, not the practitioner, voice the argument for change is the mechanism, not a nicety: "we come to believe that for which we argue."

The chapter cites Amrhein's research distinguishing preparatory language (DARN) from mobilizing language (commitment, taking steps) and shows that it is the trajectory of change talk across a session — whether it rises toward the end — that predicts actual behavior change, not the client's starting level or overall amount of change talk.

## Why It Matters

This gives a concrete, trackable signal for whether an influence attempt is working, independent of how the conversation feels in the moment: listen for whether the other person's own language about the thing you want them to do is trending toward commitment or away from it. It also reframes persuasion generally — the goal isn't to make the best argument, it's to structure the conversation so the other party makes the argument, because self-generated reasons predict follow-through in a way that received reasons don't. This applies wherever someone is trying to move another person (a report, a customer, a teenager) rather than simply inform them.
