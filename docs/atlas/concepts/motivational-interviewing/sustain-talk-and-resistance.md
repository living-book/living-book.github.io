# Sustain Talk and Resistance

## Definition

Rosengren distinguishes two things older MI writing lumped together as "resistance." Sustain talk is ordinary ambivalence given voice — a client's desire, ability, reason, or need to keep things as they are (the mirror image of change talk). Resistance proper is different: an active, interpersonal pushing against the practitioner specifically — arguing, interrupting, discounting, going silent, or complying on the surface while ignoring the advice underneath. The book's key claim, citing Patterson & Forgatch and Moyers et al., is that resistance is not a fixed client trait; it rises and falls in direct response to practitioner behavior, especially arguing for change, warning of consequences, or telling the client how to change.

## In the Book

Chapter 6 opens with Sean, a 17-year-old facing possible criminal charges in a room with a detective, his mother, a principal, and a counselor. As the detective pushes harder — reciting charges and sentences — Sean visibly hardens, his jaw clamping tighter with each escalation; only when the practitioner steps into the hallway and leads with empathy ("You both must be scared to death") does Sean's posture relax. The chapter also gives the author's own case as a diabetic patient: he initially argued back against a well-meaning provider's lectures, then simply learned to agree and not follow through — "on the surface our interactions were friendly... I rarely implemented [her suggestions] in the manner she specified." That is resistance wearing the mask of compliance.

For responding to sustain talk and resistance, the book teaches three reflective-listening moves rather than argument: simple reflections (stay close to the client's words to buy time and keep momentum — "You are going to drink till you die"), amplified reflections (overstate the position slightly to test how firmly it's held — "You can't see any purpose in living"), and double-sided reflections (hold the status-quo statement next to something else the client has said — "For now you expect to die drinking, and on the other hand, you recognize you might change your mind"). Alongside these, the book recommends naming the client's actual choice explicitly ("making the obvious obvious") and shifting focus to a more productive area rather than pressing on a stuck one.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates two things that look identical from the outside — a person expressing legitimate attachment to their current situation, and a person actively fighting you — and shows that the second is frequently manufactured by the practitioner's own pressure, not an independent fact about the client. It gives a diagnostic move usable in any influence relationship (management, sales, parenting, negotiation): when you meet pushback, the first question isn't "how resistant is this person," it's "what did I just do that increased resistance," because the intervention that reliably works is de-escalating your own behavior, not overpowering theirs.
