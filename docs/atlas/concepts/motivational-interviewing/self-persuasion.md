# Self-Persuasion (Resisting the Righting Reflex)

## Definition

The "righting reflex" is the near-automatic urge of a helper to fix a problem the moment they see it — argue for the healthy choice, list the benefits, warn of the risks. Rosengren shows this urge is not just unhelpful with an ambivalent person, it is actively counterproductive: because the client already holds both sides of the argument, whatever the practitioner argues for, the client tends to voice the opposite ("yes, but..."), and — per Bem's self-perception theory — a person who is genuinely uncertain of their own attitude infers it from listening to their own speech. Argue someone into the "for" position and you have handed them the "against" position to defend, and hearing themselves defend it makes them believe it more.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 opens with the author's own near-miss: a training participant named Rick insists MI can't work with methamphetamine users because "they lie and steal... you have to confront them." Rosengren, irritated, feels the pull to argue him down with data and nearly does — catching himself as the very righting reflex the chapter is about to name. The book gives this urge a formal place in MI's RULE framework (Resist the righting reflex, Understand the client's motivation, Listen, Empower) and states plainly that practitioner behaviors which increase resistance are: convincing clients they have a problem, arguing for the benefits of change, telling them how to change, and warning of consequences of not changing — the four moves a well-intentioned helper reaches for first.

Chapter 5 supplies the mechanism behind why suppressing that reflex works: because most people considering a change are genuinely uncertain (DiClemente's two-thirds estimate), they are exactly the population for whom self-perception theory predicts attitude shift from self-generated speech, and exactly the population for whom an outside argument does nothing — "asking a practitioner who ardently believes in abstinence only to argue for harm reduction is unlikely to cause a change in those beliefs," because firm believers aren't uncertain. The lever only works on uncertainty, and arguing removes the client's need to voice their own reasons.

## Why It Matters

This names a specific, common failure mode of anyone trying to help: the more useful and correct your advice feels to give, the more likely it is to backfire on someone still ambivalent, because your argument becomes the thing they define themselves against. The corrective isn't withholding opinions — it's structuring the exchange so the other person voices the case for change themselves. This transfers directly to management, parenting, sales, and negotiation: pushing a conclusion on someone who hasn't voiced it themselves recruits their self-perception against you.
