# The Spirit of MI (Guide, Not Expert)

## Definition

MI spirit is the underlying stance that has to be present for MI's principles and techniques (OARS, change talk) to actually function as MI — Rosengren argues a practitioner can execute every microskill correctly and still not be doing MI if this stance is absent. It has three components: collaboration (working in partnership, treating the client as the expert on their own life and history), evocation (drawing motivation and solutions out of the client rather than installing them), and autonomy (the client, not the practitioner, owns the decision and the path, even when the practitioner has a strong opinion or a legal obligation to act).

## In the Book

Rosengren uses Miller and Rollnick's song metaphor: OARS and change talk are the lyrics, the principles are the song's structure (refrain, key changes), but spirit is the melody — the thing that "creates the music" and that people respond to even "when the lyrics fail us." He cites research (Moyers, Miller, & Hendrickson, 2005) that spirit is itself a measurable predictor of a practitioner's skill and, through that, of client outcomes — strong enough that Miller and Moyers place it as the very first stage in their eight stages of learning MI. Rollnick's 2008 formulation recasts the practitioner as a guide: someone who "does not choose the destination but offers information about the paths the client might choose." The book is explicit that this doesn't mean the practitioner has no opinions or never disagrees — collaboration is not agreement, and a practitioner can hold a concern (child neglect, continued drinking) while still recognizing the client alone can choose to change.

## Why It Matters

This separates technique from stance in a way that explains why the same words, delivered by two different people (or the same person on two different days), land completely differently — the skill can be present without the spirit, and clients seem to detect the difference below the level of explicit content. It generalizes to any advisory relationship where one party has more expertise and the other has to actually live with and enact the decision: the value of expertise doesn't require taking the client's autonomy along with it, and treating someone as a partner with their own expertise on their own life is a distinct, learnable stance from being technically correct.
