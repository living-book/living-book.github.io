# People Are Wired Very Differently

## Definition

Dalio's claim, built on years of consulting psychologists, psychiatrists, and neuroscientists, is that differences in how people think — conceptual versus literal, big-picture versus detail-oriented, linear versus lateral — are as real and as involuntary as differences in hearing range or color vision, not differences of effort or goodwill. Because these differences are largely invisible (you can't see that someone else's mind doesn't work like yours the way you can see they're colorblind), teams misattribute the resulting friction to bad faith, stupidity, or stubbornness instead of recognizing it as a structural mismatch that has to be diagnosed and designed around.

## In the Book

Dalio's founding example is an early Bridgewater research project run by Bob Prince, aimed at systemizing the firm's understanding of global bond markets. The team agreed conceptually on the goal but made no progress: the "conceptual" thinkers described what should be done in vague, high-level terms and expected the "literal" thinkers to work out the mechanics themselves; the literal thinkers, lacking that translation, stalled; each side concluded the other lacked imagination or had "their head in the clouds," and — critically — neither side could correctly identify which type it was, since people don't perceive their own blind spots. Dalio traces his interest in this to having his children psychologically tested by Dr. Sue Quinlan, whose results not only confirmed his own observations but predicted how the kids would develop later. He compares organizing a team of cognitively different people to conducting an orchestra: magnificent if the different instruments are deliberately arranged around each other's strengths, discordant if everyone is expected to play the same part.

## Why It Matters

This reframes a huge share of team dysfunction — the "you just don't get it" friction between people who are each individually competent — as a mapping problem rather than a character problem. If cognitive style is treated as fixed and legible (like a Baseball Card attribute) rather than as a moral failing to be argued out of someone, teams can deliberately pair complementary styles (a conceptual visionary with a literal executor) instead of accidentally staffing teams that are cognitively homogeneous, or blind, or locked in unresolvable "I explained it clearly" versus "no you didn't" standoffs.
