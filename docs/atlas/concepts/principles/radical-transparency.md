# Radical Transparency

## Definition

Radical transparency is the practice of making almost everything about how an organization or a person operates visible to the people affected by it — including uncertainty, disagreement, mistakes, and bad news — rather than managing what people see to protect them or to protect the leader's image. Dalio's version pairs it with "radical truth": saying what you actually believe rather than what is diplomatic. The two together are meant to close the gap between what people are told and what is actually happening.

## In the Book

Dalio grounds this in the story of Bridgewater's back-office reorganization. When the Management Committee began exploring spinning off the back-office team into a partnership with Bank of New York/Mellon, COO Eileen Murray held a town-hall meeting immediately — while the plan was still unformed and most questions were unanswerable — rather than waiting until a decision was final, which is the norm at most companies. Dalio argues the alternative (concealing the exploration until it's a done deal) would have produced worse rumor-driven anxiety, not less. The back-office team, though ultimately spun off, kept trusting Bridgewater and stayed part of "the extended family" afterward. Dalio also notes the cost side honestly: adapting to radical transparency is uncomfortable, takes new employees about eighteen months on average, and roughly a third of people who try it never adapt. He frames it as necessary infrastructure for an "idea meritocracy" — you cannot weigh people's opinions on merit if the relevant information (who said what, who was right, what actually happened) is hidden.

## Why It Matters

Most organizations default to controlling information flow because they assume people can't handle uncertainty or criticism well. Radical transparency is a bet that the trust destroyed by concealment (and the rumors that fill the vacuum) is larger than the short-term discomfort of hard truths delivered early. The concept generalizes past management: any system — a marriage, a scientific field, a government — that suppresses disconfirming or uncomfortable information trades short-run comfort for long-run brittleness, because problems that aren't visible can't be fixed by anyone except the person hiding them.
