# The Ego Barrier and the Blind-Spot Barrier

## Definition

Dalio names two structural obstacles to seeing yourself and reality clearly. The ego barrier is a subconscious defense mechanism rooted in primitive brain structures (he cites the amygdala) that makes criticism register as an existential threat rather than useful information — it wants to be right more than it wants to find out what's true. The blind-spot barrier is different: it's not defensiveness but a genuine perceptual limit, since a person whose mind naturally sees big pictures cannot perceive what a detail-oriented mind sees, any more than a colorblind person can see color — and, crucially, people usually can't tell they have this limitation, because the limitation is exactly what would let them see it.

## In the Book

Dalio frames this as a fight between "two yous": the logical, conscious you (seated in the prefrontal cortex) and the emotional, subconscious you (seated in older brain structures), comparing it to Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde — with the crucial twist that the higher-level you is not even aware the lower-level you exists or is influencing it. His illustration: when someone asks "why did I let myself eat all that cake?", the honest answer is that the lower-level you won the argument the higher-level you didn't know it was having. He argues that when someone is challenged and can't logically explain their own reaction, it's because the lower-level you is speaking through the upper-level you, producing explanations that don't hold together. He calls these two barriers, together, the "fatal flaws" (invoking Aristotle's definition of tragedy) that keep otherwise capable people from reaching their potential, and ties the fix directly to the book's other mechanisms: radical transparency exposes what the ego wants hidden, and believability-weighted input from others compensates for blind spots you can't see in yourself.

## Why It Matters

This gives a physiological account of why "just be objective" doesn't work as advice — objectivity about yourself is blocked by mechanisms operating below conscious access, not by insufficient willpower. It reframes disagreements where both parties feel certain they're right (which is most disagreements) as predictable outputs of these two barriers rather than as evidence that one side is unusually foolish or dishonest, and it explains why external checks — other people's honest input, explicit track-record data — are structurally necessary rather than optional politeness, since the whole point of a blind spot is that you cannot self-correct for it alone.
