# The 5-Step Process

## Definition

The 5-Step Process is Dalio's model of personal and organizational evolution: (1) have clear goals, (2) identify and don't tolerate the problems standing in the way, (3) accurately diagnose the problems to find their root causes, (4) design a plan to get around them, and (5) push the plan through to results. The steps loop — results from step 5 feed back into new, higher goals — but Dalio's specific claim is that they must be done one at a time and in order: while setting goals, don't think about how to achieve them; while diagnosing, don't think about the fix. Blurring steps, he argues, produces worse outcomes because it lets premature solution-thinking contaminate diagnosis.

## In the Book

The chapter opens by naming the five steps in a nutshell and immediately stresses sequencing discipline: "when setting goals, just set goals... When you are diagnosing problems, don't think about how you will solve them—just diagnose them." Dalio walks through each step with its own sub-principles — under goals, he distinguishes goals from desires (a goal might be fitness, a desire might be the bag of chips that undermines it) and warns against mistaking the trappings of success (a $1,200 pair of shoes) for success itself. Under "identify and don't tolerate problems," he reframes painful problems as "potential improvements that are screaming at you" and insists on precision in naming them, since different root causes need different fixes. Later chapters (Work Principles 11-14) apply the same loop at organizational scale, walking through a real case where Bridgewater's client-service outcomes slipped and using the process to trace the failure to its root cause and redesign around it.

## Why It Matters

The process gives a discipline for a failure mode that shows up everywhere: solving the wrong problem because diagnosis and design happened simultaneously and got mixed up, or chasing a goal that was actually a desire in disguise. Separating "what do I want," "what's actually stopping me," "why is it stopping me," "what do I do about it," and "did it work" into distinct, ordered passes is a general debugging discipline — applicable to a strategic plan, a personal habit, or an engineering incident — that resists the human tendency to jump straight from symptom to fix.
