# Four Degrees of Action

## Definition

All human behavior falls into one of four levels of action: doing nothing, retreating, taking normal action, or taking massive action. Most people operate in the first three levels and rationalize their inaction through justification and excuse-making. Only massive action—operating at 10x the level considered reasonable—separates the successful from the ordinary.

## In the Book

Cardone identifies these four degrees in Chapter 7 as the core framework of the 10X Rule. **Doing nothing** appears passive but requires tremendous energy to rationalize failure; a person must convince themselves and others why they didn't act. **Retreating** is backward motion—refusing to try again after imagined failure—and often stems from childhood warnings and fear of rejection. **Normal action** is the dangerous middle: the "acceptable" level that produces average lives and middle-class existence, yet leaves people defenseless when conditions shift. When recession hits or competition rises, those who took only normal action suddenly find themselves at risk.

**Massive action** is the fourth degree and the only path to extraordinary outcomes. Cardone illustrates this through his own career: for every sales call others made, he made 10. When buying real estate, he looked at 10 times more properties than he could purchase. This wasn't about working smarter—it was about operating at a fundamentally different scale of effort.

## Why It Matters

This framework redefines how we evaluate effort. It separates the myth that success requires talent, luck, or connections from the reality that it requires a different *degree* of action. It also exposes a trap: normal, "acceptable" action feels productive but produces only normal results. By naming and categorizing action levels, Cardone creates a tool for diagnosing why someone is stuck. If you're not succeeding, you're likely operating in degrees one through three. The framework makes the upgrade visible and achievable—not a vague moral exhortation, but a concrete shift in scale.
