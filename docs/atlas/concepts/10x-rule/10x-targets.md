# 10X Targets: Set Goals 10 Times Higher Than Realistic

## Definition

Instead of setting "realistic" goals aligned with what you think is achievable, set targets 10 times larger. Cardone argues that realistic goals have no motivational power; they don't excite you enough to sustain action through inevitable resistance. A small, attainable goal produces small actions, small results, and abandonment the moment conditions tighten. A 10x target creates fuel—a sufficiently ambitious vision that keeps your attention daily and energizes the massive action required.

## In the Book

Cardone explicitly rejects the goal-setting advice taught in schools and business seminars: "Never set realistic goals; you can get a realistic life without setting goals for it." In Chapter 9, he explains that most people fail to achieve their goals not because they're unlucky but because their goals are too small to sustain motivation. When he set out to build his first company, he didn't ask "can I realistically do this?"—he approached it with ambition scaled to his potential, not his circumstances. 

He uses his own written goals as examples: "I own 5,000 apartments that return over 12 percent positive cash fl ow," "My net worth is over $100 million," "I have written and published 12 or more best-selling books." These aren't incrementally larger versions of what he's already done; they're 10x multiplications. He writes them daily and words them as already achieved, creating a persistent gap between current state and target that pulls his actions upward.

## Why It Matters

This inverts standard goal-setting wisdom. Most motivation theory says: set challenging but achievable targets. Cardone says achievable targets are *too small*—they don't ignite the effort required to overcome real-world friction. Obscurity, competition, market shifts, and personal doubt are constant forces; a realistic goal has no margin for any of them. The 10x target is large enough that even if you hit only 50% of it, you've already exceeded realistic expectations. It's a scaling lever: the bigger and "juicier" the goal, the more energy it generates, and the more relentless action it sustains.
