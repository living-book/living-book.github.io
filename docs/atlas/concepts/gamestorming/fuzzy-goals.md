# Fuzzy Goals

## Definition

A fuzzy goal sits between two failure modes: a goal so precise it can only describe something already known (and therefore can't guide genuine discovery), and a goal so vague it can't focus effort at all ("peace on Earth"). It "motivates the general direction of the work, without blinding the team to opportunities along the journey" — a phrase the authors borrow from Cambridge researcher Alan Blackwell's concept of a "pole-star vision." The book operationalizes the right level of fuzziness with an acronym, ESP: a fuzzy goal must be Emotional (aligned with people's passion, giving the project momentum), Sensory (made tangible — sketched, modeled, made shareable — rather than left abstract), and Progressive (expected to change as the team learns; not a fixed target).

## In the Book

Chapter 1 sets up the contrast between industrial goals and knowledge-work goals: industrial processes want goals as specific and quantifiable as possible, because the path from A to B can be planned as a repeatable chain of cause and effect. Creative work is different — "new, by definition, means 'not seen before,'" so the goal cannot be precisely defined in advance; the authors compare it to Columbus setting out for a route to India and finding America instead. They borrow the military's concept of operations (CONOPS) as an analogous device — a living, constantly revised picture of the system and the goal, rather than a fixed plan — and use it to argue that in gamestorming, "games are not links in a chain, so much as battles in a campaign." The chapter closes essential #10 ("Try Something New") by tying fuzzy goals to the improvisational, discard-and-move-on nature of a gamestorming session: "keep your eye on the fuzzy goal — the mountaintop, the imagined thing over the horizon — and the next step... that moves you one step in approximately the right direction."

## Why It Matters

Fuzzy goals give teams doing genuinely novel work permission to set a direction without pretending to know a destination they can't yet see — which resolves the false choice between "no goal" (drift) and "precise goal" (premature commitment to an answer that forecloses better ones). The ESP test is a portable diagnostic for any ambiguous strategic goal, in or out of a workshop: if a stated goal has no emotional pull, can't be made tangible, or is treated as fixed rather than expected to evolve with what's learned, it will fail to do the job a goal is supposed to do in uncertain territory.
