# Artifacts as External Memory

## Definition

An artifact, in gamestorming's sense, is any tangible, portable object that holds information — a sticky note, an index card, a game piece — and thereby makes an idea explicit, movable, and persistent outside anyone's head. The book's illustration: playing chess blindfolded is technically possible (strong players can hold the whole board in their mind's eye) but far harder than playing with pieces in front of you, because "the more information you can store in material objects or the environment, the more your players' minds are free to engage with the situation at hand."

## In the Book

Chapter 2 ("10 Essentials") makes Artifacts essential #3, defining them by analogy to archaeology (anything made or shaped by a human hand) and generalizing to knowledge work: any piece of paper, sticky note, or card that carries information. Chapter 3 grounds the idea in the actual history of the sticky note — Dr. Spencer Silver's low-tack 3M adhesive, unmarketable until choir member Arthur Fry used it to keep bookmarks from falling out of his hymnal — and draws the mechanism out explicitly: sticky notes let you break a complex topic into small, movable "knowledge atoms or nodes" that can be distributed into physical space (on a desk, a wall, a steering wheel) and reconfigured at will, exactly like a deck of cards can be shuffled, grouped, or dealt into new combinations. The book pairs artifacts with node generation (essential #4): in the opening phase you want to generate as many artifacts as possible — one idea per sticky note, generated silently rather than called out — because each artifact becomes a modular unit that can later be sorted, clustered, and rearranged in Chapter 3's "Meaningful Space" methods (affinity mapping, grids, linking).

## Why It Matters

The mechanism generalizes past workshops: any domain drowning in complexity benefits from converting ideas into discrete, physical (or physically-manipulable) units, because the unit — not the person's memory — becomes the thing you reorganize, compare, and discard. It reframes "keeping notes" as cognitive offloading with a specific payoff: once an idea exists as an artifact, the group's attention is free to work on the relationships between artifacts (grouping, sequencing, prioritizing) instead of the much harder task of holding every idea in mind simultaneously while also trying to reason about it.
