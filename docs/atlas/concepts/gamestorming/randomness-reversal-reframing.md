# Randomness, Reversal, and Reframing

## Definition

Because "the human brain is a pattern-making machine" that finds patterns everywhere and then has trouble seeing past the first one it locks onto, gamestorming treats deliberately introduced randomness, reversal, and reframing as tools for breaking fixation and forcing new patterns to surface. Randomness disrupts an existing order (shuffling cards); reversal inverts a convention (a map with south at the top); reframing imposes a different lens on the same material. All three exploit the same mechanism: familiar orderings and conventions are themselves a kind of frozen pattern, and disturbing them creates room for a different one to emerge.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 makes this essential #7, opening with two historical illustrations: William S. Burroughs reportedly determined the page order of Naked Lunch by throwing the manuscript in the air and assembling pages in the order he picked them up, and the Qur'an was revealed to Mohammed in sections whose order was only fixed later. The book adds Leonardo da Vinci's practice of finding inspiration in stains on a wall — quoted directly: "Look at a wall spotted with stains...you may discover a resemblance to landscapes...battles with figures in action...strange faces and costumes...an endless variety of objects" — as an example of imposing randomness onto an unstructured surface to provoke pattern discovery. The book ties the mechanism back to biology (genetic shuffling and recombination as the source of variation that natural selection acts on) and to the book's core artifact toolkit: modular artifacts like index cards and sticky notes are valuable specifically because "they facilitate randomness; they can be easily shuffled, re-sorted, and rearranged to generate new patterns and ideas."

## Why It Matters

This names a specific failure mode of expert judgment — that once you've found a workable frame, your own pattern-recognition works against you by making alternative frames harder to see, not easier — and offers a concrete countermeasure: don't try to think your way out of a frame, physically or procedurally disrupt the arrangement that's holding the frame in place. The principle generalizes past workshops to any domain where an established convention (an org chart's up-down hierarchy, a map's north-up orientation, a dataset's default sort order) quietly constrains what people are able to notice, and deliberately inverting or randomizing that convention is often faster than arguing someone out of it.
