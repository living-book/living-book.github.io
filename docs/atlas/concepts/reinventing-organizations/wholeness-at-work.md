# Striving for Wholeness

## Definition

Most workplaces implicitly require people to show up as a narrow "professional self" — rational, resolved, masculine-coded — while hiding doubt, emotion, and vulnerability behind a figurative or literal uniform. Laloux calls the alternative "wholeness": organizations building practices that actively invite the rest of a person — their feelings, intuition, and even their physical presence — back into work, on the premise that every part of the self left at the door is also lost creativity and energy.

## In the Book

The book frames wholeness as one of three defining breakthroughs of "Teal" organizations, alongside self-management and evolutionary purpose. Its concrete examples are strikingly literal: Sounds True, a Colorado media company, lets employees bring their dogs to work (governed only by a loosely enforced "three poops and you're out" rule), and now has 20 dogs alongside 90 employees; Patagonia's Ventura headquarters houses an on-site Child Development Center, so it is common to see a mother nursing during a meeting. Laloux argues these aren't quirky perks but evidence that people have grown so accustomed to "aseptic" workplaces that ordinary human presence feels transgressive. The book also draws on Eric Berne's Parent-Child-Adult framework and Brian Robertson's use of the Karpman Drama Triangle (Persecutor-Rescuer-Victim shifting to Challenger-Coach-Creator) to explain how removing hierarchy changes the emotional register of relationships at work, pushing people toward peer-to-peer, adult-to-adult interaction instead of the parent-child dynamic a boss relationship tends to create.

## Why It Matters

Wholeness reframes emotional suppression at work not as professionalism but as a hidden cost: energy, creativity, and honest signal are lost every time someone edits themselves down to the acceptable slice of a person. The concept gives a name to what psychological-safety research approaches from a different angle — that systems designed around a fictional, purely rational actor pay a real, if invisible, tax for the mismatch, and that removing the coercive structures (bosses, rigid hierarchy, scarce promotions) that made the mask necessary in the first place is often more effective than adding wellness programs on top of them.
