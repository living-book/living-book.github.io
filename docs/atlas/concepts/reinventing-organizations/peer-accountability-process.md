# Peer-Based Conflict Resolution as Accountability

## Definition

In organizations without managers, some mechanism must replace the boss's role of settling disputes and holding people to their commitments. Several unrelated organizations converged on the same structure: a private one-on-one conversation first, escalating only if needed to a mutually trusted mediator, and only if that fails to a panel of relevant colleagues — with founder involvement as a last resort, never a first one. The process applies uniformly to technical disagreements, interpersonal friction, and underperformance alike.

## In the Book

Morning Star, the world's largest tomato processing company, documents its version — "Direct Communication and Gaining Agreement" — in a core internal document called the Colleague Principles, built on two founding values: never use force against another person, and honor your commitments. The process requires the initiating party to make a clear request (not a judgment or demand) and the other to respond with a yes, no, or counterproposal; only if that fails do they nominate a trusted colleague as mediator, and only if that fails does a panel convene, which can carry enough moral weight to resolve the matter even though it cannot force a decision. Laloux notes his own surprise at finding "virtually identical" processes at multiple unconnected organizations in his research, and concludes the mechanism is not really about occasional workplace spats — it is the load-bearing piece that lets peers hold each other accountable for commitments in the absence of a boss to escalate to, which is why organizations like Morning Star train every new recruit in it explicitly.

## Why It Matters

This addresses a structural gap that many "flat" or self-managed organizations never solve: without a hierarchy, who enforces commitments when someone doesn't deliver? A fixed, known, low-drama escalation ladder — talk directly, then a neutral peer, then a panel — substitutes for hierarchical enforcement without requiring anyone to hold formal power over anyone else, and its uniform applicability to both interpersonal conflict and performance issues means one mechanism replaces what a traditional organization splits across HR, management, and informal grumbling.
