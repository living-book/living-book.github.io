# Listening to Evolutionary Purpose

## Definition

Instead of setting strategy top-down to defend market share against competitors, an organization can be treated as a living entity with its own evolving sense of direction — a purpose that members "listen in" to and serve, rather than one imposed from above and defended through competitive maneuvering. Growth and market share become side effects of serving the purpose well, not goals in themselves.

## In the Book

Laloux contrasts Jack Welch-era GE — the "poster child" of Achievement-Orange leadership, whose retirement memoir is titled simply *Winning* — with organizations where competition, in his research, "is not mentioned anywhere." Buurtzorg's founder Jos de Blok actively publishes his organization's operating methods and personally advises ZorgAccent, a direct competitor, without charging for it, reasoning that if a rival adopts better patient care, the purpose is served either way; he calls competition "idiotic." Patagonia's "Don't buy this jacket" campaign and its Common Threads Partnership (reduce, repair, reuse, recycle) is offered as the same logic applied to consumer retail: the company accepted lower short-term sales because its purpose, not its growth target, set the course. The book also documents how this changes concrete practices — Buurtzorg has no written strategy document, no fixed budgets, and expanded into youth care and Sweden simply by "sitting in a few meetings to listen, sense if things are meant to happen."

## Why It Matters

This concept separates two very different failure modes in how organizations set direction: strategy as a fear-driven, zero-sum contest for survival against rivals, versus strategy as an ongoing act of noticing what the world needs and what the organization is positioned to offer. It gives language for why some of the most durable, fast-growing organizations behave as if they have no external enemies — because from inside a genuinely purpose-driven frame, an organization achieving the same purpose faster is an ally, not a threat, and the entire vocabulary of competitive strategy becomes optional rather than foundational.
