# Problem Absorption: Rules Extending to New Cases and Enabling Semantic Learning

## Definition

Problem absorption is the phenomenon by which organizations handle new, non-prototypical problems by extending existing formal rules to encompass them, rather than creating new rules. Contra earlier interpretations that viewed absorption as purely conservative (preventing learning), the book argues that absorption can generate "semantic learning"—learning that emerges from the evolving meanings of rules through their application to diverse cases. The process involves reflexive extension of a rule's categories to cases that are markedly different from prototypical members, creating cumulative shifts in understanding that can ultimately trigger higher-order learning and transformation.

## In the Book

Osadchiy, Bogenrieder, and Heugens's chapter "Organizational Learning through Problem Absorption: A Processual View" reframes absorption from a process perspective. They define it as "involving reflexive extension of a rule's labeled categories to cases that are markedly different from the prototypical members of those categories, which allows practitioners to maintain the pattern of practice and a sense of order in the face of ambiguity and situational variation."

The authors illustrate through two concrete cases: (1) a university department treasurer extending the "conference presentations" budget category to include membership fees not mentioned in the original rule, after careful reflection; and (2) a Rotterdam port authority extending the meaning of "liner service" from daily/weekly schedules to bi-weekly services when evaluating a shipping firm's berth request. In both cases, the organization absorbed the problem (made it workable within existing rules) rather than codifying a new rule. However, the repeated absorption of similar non-prototypical cases generates "semantic learning"—the meanings embedded in the rules shift subtly over time as practitioners encounter and interpret edge cases. This cumulative semantic learning can eventually destabilize the rule system itself, prompting higher-order learning, unlearning, and rule revision.

The process depends critically on reflexivity: "rule makers' recognition of these effects through reflection can lead to higher-level organizational learning, unlearning, and codification." Without reflexivity, absorption can trap organizations in routines disconnected from their original purposes.

## Why It Matters

Problem absorption explains how organizations maintain order while responding to novel situations without constant rule-making bureaucracy. It also reveals that "learning" in organizations is not always dramatic or explicit—it emerges through the subtle evolution of meaning as rules meet reality. The concept suggests that attending to the cases that stretch organizational rules, and reflecting on their cumulative effects, is how organizations develop adaptive capacity and avoid codification traps.
