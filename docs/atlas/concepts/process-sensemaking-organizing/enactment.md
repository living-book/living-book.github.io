# Enactment: Assembling Actions Into Sensible Sequences

## Definition

Enactment is Weick's fundamental conception of organizing: the process by which people assemble ongoing, interdependent actions into sensible sequences that generate sensible outcomes. Rather than viewing organizing as people implementing a predetermined structure, enactment treats it as the real-time construction of shared meaning through interaction. Organization emerges as an accomplished outcome of the process of sensemaking through which equivocality (ambiguity and multiple possible interpretations) is progressively removed.

## In the Book

The book's introduction frames enactment as the heart of process-oriented organization studies. Weick's (1979) theory of organizing emphasizes that "shifting attention from 'organization' as an already accomplished entity with certain pre-given properties to organizing, [Weick] underscores the process whereby ongoing, interdependent actions are assembled into sensible sequences that generate sensible outcomes." The chapters by Weick and others develop this idea through the lens of process metaphysics: the organization is not a thing that changes, but an emergent pattern arising from the ceaseless interaction of its members. As the text notes, "Organization is an emergent outcome of the process of sensemaking through which equivocality is progressively removed."

This concept appears most directly in Weick's chapter "The Poetics of Process," where he explores how attention-drawing and poetic language reveal the ineffable practices through which organizing actually happens. The process is messy, improvisational, and dependent on moment-to-moment responsiveness to what others do—not the execution of a master plan.

## Why It Matters

Enactment reframes how we understand organizational structure and change. Instead of asking "why did this organization fail to implement its strategy?" it asks "what sequences of actions, when assembled by these people in these moments, generated the outcomes we see?" This shift opens new possibilities: organization becomes not something externally designed and then inhabited, but something continuously brought into being through the interpretive activities of its members. The concept explains how organizations can be both patterned (we recognize IBM as IBM) and fluid (it is never exactly the same twice).
