# The Master and His Emissary

## Definition

The right and left hemispheres should stand in a relationship of trusted delegation, not symmetrical partnership: the right hemisphere (the Master) grounds meaning, value, and contact with the living whole, while the left hemisphere (the Emissary) is sent out to handle specific, delimited tasks on the Master's behalf. The arrangement only works if the Emissary remains a servant of a purpose it did not itself set. McGilchrist takes the book's title, and its organizing image, from a fable he attributes to Nietzsche: a wise ruler sends a trusted vizier to govern distant parts of his domain; the vizier grows to believe himself the true ruler, usurps the Master, and the domain collapses in tyranny and ruin.

## In the Book

McGilchrist introduces the fable in the Introduction as the book's central metaphor and returns to it as the argument's throughline: "though the cerebral hemispheres should co-operate, they have for some time been in a state of conflict... At present the domain — our civilisation — finds itself in the hands of the vizier, who, however gifted, is effectively an ambitious regional bureaucrat with his own interests at heart. Meanwhile the Master... is led away in chains." In the Conclusion he restates the proper relationship in structural terms: "The Master needs to trust, to believe in, his emissary, knowing all the while that that trust may be abused... If the relationship holds, they are invincible; but if it is abused, it is not just the Master that suffers, but both of them, since the emissary owes his existence to the Master." The left hemisphere is not the enemy — it is, rightly used, "a valued emissary" — but once convinced of its own self-sufficiency it stops serving the larger purpose it was created to advance.

## Why It Matters

This gives a template for diagnosing failure in any system built on delegation: a specialist function (fast, precise, good at its delimited task) is given authority by a broader, slower, context-holding authority precisely because the delegate cannot see the whole. The system fails not when the delegate is incompetent but when it succeeds enough to forget the limits of its mandate and starts treating its partial view as the whole truth. This applies wherever a narrow optimizing process — a metric, an algorithm, a bureaucracy, a specialist discipline — is meant to serve a broader purpose it cannot itself perceive, and gradually captures the authority that was only ever lent to it.
