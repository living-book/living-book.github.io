# Clarity Over Consensus

## Definition

Lencioni defines team commitment as "a function of two things: clarity and buy-in" — not agreement. Buy-in happens when "reasonable human beings do not need to get their way in order to support a decision, but only need to know that their opinions have been heard and considered." The book names two things that corrode commitment: the desire for consensus, and the need for certainty. Great teams "unite behind decisions... even when there is little assurance about whether the decision is correct," on the logic that a wrong decision made boldly and corrected quickly beats a delayed one made "safely."

## In the Book

Dysfunction 3, Lack of Commitment, is built as a direct consequence of Dysfunction 2: without airing real disagreement through conflict, "team members rarely, if ever, buy in and commit to decisions, though they may feign agreement during meetings." The book warns this dysfunction has the widest organizational blast radius — small unresolved gaps at the executive level become "major discrepancies" by the time contradictory marching orders reach employees several layers down, described as acting "like a vortex." Among the tools offered: "cascading messaging" (explicitly reviewing at the end of every meeting what was decided and what must be communicated downward, since team members routinely discover mid-exercise that they aren't actually aligned) and deliberately practicing decisiveness in low-risk situations to break a team's addiction to over-analysis.

## Why It Matters

Consensus-seeking looks like diligence but is often just conflict avoidance wearing a procedural mask — and it's expensive, because delay has a cost even when the eventual decision is right. Separating "everyone agrees" from "everyone was heard and will now execute" lets a group move at the speed uncertainty actually requires, and explains a common failure pattern in any hierarchy: ambiguity injected at the top doesn't stay small, it compounds as it cascades down through people who have no way to resolve a disagreement they never saw made.
