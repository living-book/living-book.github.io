# Interactive Planning

## Definition

Interactive planning is a comprehensive planning approach that begins with idealized redesign—imagining what the organization would design now if it could be anything—then works backward to close the gaps between current state and that ideal. The process includes five phases: formulating the mess (understanding current reality and future without change), idealized design (specifying desired future), ends planning (defining mission and specifications), means planning (determining how to close gaps), and implementation with learning and control systems.

## In the Book

Ackoff introduces interactive planning throughout Part II (chapters 4–8). In Chapter 3 (page 1534), he lists it as a feature of social-systemic organizations: "It uses interactive planning which involves idealized redesign of the organization and determination of the closest approximation to that design that can be realized." The approach differs from traditional planning because it works backward: "from destination to origin. Most planning proceeds in the opposite direction. Recall that the reason for working backward, counterintuitively, is that it is simpler, often very much simpler" (page 2961). Chapter 4 describes formulating the mess: creating flow charts of operations, documenting actual versus espoused culture, identifying conflicts, and projecting the future if nothing changes. Chapter 5 covers idealized design with its three constraints (technologically feasible, operationally viable, continuously improvable). Chapters 6 and 7 detail means planning (identifying gaps and selecting types of actions) and resource planning. Chapter 8 presents the Learning and Adaptation Support System—a decision-record mechanism that tracks expectations, monitors outcomes, detects deviations, diagnoses errors, and prescribes corrections, enabling organizational learning.

## Why It Matters

Interactive planning inverts the usual planning failure: instead of projecting incrementally from today (extrapolation that locks in current assumptions), it imagines ideals then works backward (creative redesign that questions assumptions). By embedding learning systems that compare expectations to actuals and continuously correct course, it creates organizations that adapt faster than their environment changes. This approach is particularly valuable in turbulent conditions where prediction is unreliable but rapid learning is essential.
