# Self-Sealing Traps

## Definition

A Trap is a recurring pattern of behavior — avoided conflict, meetings where people nod along and then complain in private, the same argument resurfacing verbatim months later — that people experience as imposed on them but actually produce themselves. Argyris's core claim: "people themselves are responsible for making the status quo so resistant to change. We are trapped by our own behavior." Traps are self-sealing because they are simultaneously "anti-learning and anti-corrective of the very errors they create," and because they "facilitate blaming others for creating and maintaining Traps" — which redirects attention away from the one lever that could actually open them.

## In the Book

Argyris opens with the everyday texture of Traps: people say they value openness, honesty, and trust, then say one thing in public and another in private, "and pretend that this is the rational thing to do." He then documents the mechanism of self-sealing — when caught creating or going along with a Trap, people first deny personal responsibility (blaming others or "the system"), then deny that they are denying by making the subject undiscussable, and then must make that undiscussability itself undiscussable for the strategy to hold. This produces a learned mind-set of victimhood: people conclude they are "helpless," trapped in an asylum not of their own making.

The book grounds this with cases spanning scale and stakes: successful, sophisticated organizations (Intel under Andrew Grove) get "caught up in behavior that traps it, strangling decision-making," and the same dynamic recurs in catastrophic failures — teachers helping students cheat to protect funding and covering up the help, Robert McNamara taking decades to see the Vietnam War problems he and others had created, and the Challenger and Columbia disasters occurring despite official reports meant to prevent exactly that recurrence. Policies, rules, and structures existed in every case; they helped but were "not enough," because the Trap lives in behavior, not in the absence of rules.

## Why It Matters

This reframes chronic organizational dysfunction as neither bad luck nor bad actors but a self-produced, self-perpetuating pattern — which means the fix is not more policy or a different leader but a change in the behavior that keeps sealing the trap shut. It gives a diagnostic test for any recurring "stuck" situation in any domain: is the group blaming an external cause while its own denial-and-cover-up behavior is what's actually preventing the problem from being named and corrected?
