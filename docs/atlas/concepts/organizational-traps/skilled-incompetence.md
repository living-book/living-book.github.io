# Skilled Incompetence

## Definition

Traps do not require ignorance, malice, or a hostile environment — they are produced by people who have become genuinely *skillful* at Model I defensive reasoning: skillful at self-justification, at denying personal responsibility, at "denial of denying," and at doing all of it without registering discomfort. Argyris's term for this is skilled incompetence and skilled unawareness: the same practiced competence that makes someone effective in normal situations is precisely what makes them reliably blind, under threat, to their own contribution to the problem.

## In the Book

The clearest demonstration is the Andy case (Chapter 5): thirty-four sitting CEOs, in a low-stakes classroom seminar with none of their usual time pressure, reputational risk, or career risk, were asked to help "Andy" (a COO who'd lost a promised CEO position for being top-down and politically blind) understand and correct his blindness. Andy openly admitted the diagnosis and asked for help. The CEOs still failed him — offering advice like "show a sample force of strength" and "measure their performance" that never engaged what Andy actually asked, then concluding *Andy* wasn't really open to learning, all while exhibiting the identical interpersonal blindness he had just admitted to. Argyris's point: none of the usual excuses (structural pressure, reward systems, career fear) were present, and the CEOs still reproduced the failure, because skilled incompetence lives in the reasoning pattern itself, not in the surrounding conditions.

Argyris generalizes this into a broader critique of leadership and culture-change literature in Chapters 4–6: researchers who catalog what "productive cultures" look like, or design frameworks for organizational change, routinely fail to notice that the actors implementing their advice — however well-intentioned — remain skillfully incompetent at exactly the interpersonal moves the advice requires.

## Why It Matters

This overturns the assumption that failure to solve a problem signals a skill gap that more training or expertise will close — sometimes the existing skill set *is* the obstacle, because it has been honed at defending against threat rather than learning from it. It reframes "why do capable people keep failing at this" as a question about what, specifically, they've become fluent at (self-protection, plausible-sounding advice, confident diagnosis of others) rather than a question of talent or effort, which matters anywhere expertise is assumed to transfer automatically to interpersonally threatening situations.
