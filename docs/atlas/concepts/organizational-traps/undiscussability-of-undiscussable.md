# The Undiscussability of the Undiscussable

## Definition

When people deny a problem or their responsibility for it, denial alone is fragile — it can be pointed out. Argyris identifies the reinforcing move that makes denial durable: people "deny that they are denying by making the subject undiscussable. In order for that strategy to work they must make the undiscussability undiscussable." The topic goes underground, and then the very existence of that underground layer goes underground too, so there is no longer any available move — inside the normal channels of conversation — for surfacing either one.

## In the Book

At Intel, Andrew Grove's leadership style itself became the undiscussable subject: subordinates could not raise how Grove led without risk, and — crucially — "the rules for dealing with Grove's leadership style were undiscussable and their undiscussability undiscussable." Argyris notes the particular trick in this case: Grove "acted openly and with transparency to rule out openness and transparency by making the rules undiscussable" — using the appearance of candor to close off the one topic that candor would have required addressing. The same double structure recurs at the State Department: the ambassadors privately "knew" they would resist Rusk's initiative and knew that everyone else knew it too, but treated saying so openly in the meeting as inappropriate — and one ambassador who privately promised Rusk candor over dinner still would not raise it in the room, preserving the undiscussability at the group level even while individually aware of it.

Argyris treats this two-layer structure — not just avoidance, but avoidance of naming the avoidance — as the load-bearing mechanism of Traps generally: in his summary of causes, Traps produce "skilled incompetence, skilled unawareness, and self-protective actions such as denial of denying, and making issues undiscussable, including the undiscussability of the undiscussable."

## Why It Matters

This names why some organizational problems feel not just hard to raise but structurally impossible to raise, even by people who privately agree the problem exists: the second-order rule (don't mention that this is off-limits) removes the meta-conversation that would normally let a group notice and repair its own blind spots. Recognizing the pattern — a topic that not only can't be discussed, but whose undiscussability can't be discussed either — is itself the first crack in it, because naming the second layer out loud is the one move the pattern doesn't have a defense against.
