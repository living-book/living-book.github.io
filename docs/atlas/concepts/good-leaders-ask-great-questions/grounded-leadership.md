# Grounded Leadership

## Definition

Grounded leadership is the intentional practice of staying centered and authentic despite the distorting effects of success and authority. It requires three structural elements: humility (honest assessment of strengths and weaknesses), authenticity (being real with others rather than performing a role), and calling (connection to a purpose larger than personal success or position).

## In the Book

In Chapter 2, Maxwell addresses the vulnerability of leaders to "an overblown sense of their own importance." He recounts a historical example: in Rome, generals honored with a triumph parade were preceded by marching legions and cheering crowds. But a slave held a laurel wreath above the general's head while whispering, "Hominem te memento"—"Remember you are only a man."

Maxwell defines humility as "being honest about your weaknesses." He cites Rick Warren: "Humility is not denying your strengths. Humility is being honest about your weaknesses. All of us are a bundle of both great strengths and great weaknesses and humility is being able to be honest about both."

On authenticity, Maxwell explains that successful leaders are often put on pedestals by people. To stay grounded, leaders must "get off that pedestal and stay with the people" through honesty and authenticity. He notes that inauthentic leaders create what he calls the "Success Gap"—a perceived distance between successful people and those less successful. Authentic leaders, by contrast, work to close that gap by being open about failures, using self-deprecating humor, and connecting with people before and after public moments.

On calling, Maxwell distinguishes a dream from a calling: "A dream is something you really want to do, but a calling is something you have to do." His calling—to add value to leaders who multiply value to others—has defined his forty-year leadership journey. He captures the power: "When I found my why, I found my way. When I found my why, I found my will. When I found my why, I found my wings."

These three elements work together: humility prevents the ego inflation that position encourages; authenticity prevents the alienation that pretense creates; calling prevents the drift toward self-interest that success enables.

## Why It Matters

Leadership authority creates psychological distance: people defer, project confidence onto leaders, and hesitate to speak truth. These pressures can erode the leader's judgment. Grounded leadership practices counteract this erosion by maintaining honest self-assessment (humility), preventing performance collapse under pressure (authenticity), and providing a reference point beyond personal benefit (calling). Organizations led by grounded leaders tend to have higher trust, more authentic feedback, and lower rates of ethical drift.
