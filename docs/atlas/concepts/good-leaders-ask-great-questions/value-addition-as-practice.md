# Value-Addition as Practice

## Definition

Value-addition as practice is the habit of measuring work against whether it improved the situation of customers, clients, team members, or stakeholders. Leaders ask "Did we add value?" and "Did we exceed expectations?" not as occasional retrospectives but as ongoing discipline, using the answers to inform what stays and what improves.

## In the Book

In Chapter 3, Maxwell explains: "My daily goal is to add value to other people. I want not only to make life better for my family, but also to encourage and reward the server at the restaurant where I eat lunch." He applies this to his organization: "I want members of my team to feel that the company and I do everything we can to help them succeed and improve every day. And I also want our clients and customers to feel we improved their situation and helped them to succeed, every time we come in contact with them."

Maxwell illustrates the question's power through a moment of failure. After speaking at an event, he was signing books in a long line. To him it was routine—he shakes hands, smiles for pictures, signs hundreds of books. However, a few days later, his assistant Linda forwarded an email from a man who had waited in line and felt treated abruptly and rudely by Maxwell. His response: "Wow! Not only had I not added value, I had actually devalued him. I felt terrible."

Rather than dismissing the complaint, Maxwell called the man to apologize. He learned from the experience, changed his behavior at book signings, and thanked Linda for her reminder to slow down and connect. The question surfaces blind spots that success can hide.

Maxwell also discusses "Did we exceed expectations?" in the context of continuous improvement. When leaders of his company created a "Rule of Five" (five disciplines for organizational excellence), they presented it to Maxwell. His initial reaction: "Unfortunately, what they had produced was just average. That wasn't good enough. I asked them to keep working on it until they had something all of us could be proud of."

## Why It Matters

Value-addition as a practice creates upward pressure on quality. Unlike static standards (meeting requirements, hitting targets), the question "Did we add value?" is continuous and relative to context. It prevents the complacency that comes from checking boxes and keeps organizations focused on impact rather than activity. This mechanism drives sustainable competitive advantage because it forces constant calibration to what stakeholders actually need rather than what the organization assumes they need.
