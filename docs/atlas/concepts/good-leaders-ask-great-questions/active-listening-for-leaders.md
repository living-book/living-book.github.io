# Active Listening for Leaders

## Definition

Active listening for leaders is the practice of creating structural conditions and habits that enable leaders to hear clearly and fully. It addresses the natural tendency toward "agenda anxiety"—the feeling that what a leader wants to say matters more than what others might say—and counteracts this bias through deliberate listening discipline.

## In the Book

Maxwell begins his section on listening by confessing: "I have to confess that I have not always been a good listener. My wife Margaret would tell you that when we first got married, I talked too much and listened too little." He brought this bias into his professional life too, hurting relationships and missing valuable input.

He identifies three questions that define listening discipline: "Do I have an open-ear policy?" (counteracting agenda anxiety), "Do I interrupt?" (a symptom of attitude problems), and "Do I want to hear what I need to hear?" (seeking truth over comfort). Maxwell explains that interrupting translates to: "What I want to say is more important than what you are saying."

On the third point, Maxwell empowered his executive assistants Barbara and later Linda to speak to him about anything they observed, strategically placing them at the center of communication. He told them: "Tell me what I need to hear on the front end of a situation, not what you want to tell me or what you think I want to hear." This creates psychological safety for truth-telling.

Maxwell notes that hearing positive news is easy; the listening discipline lies in "hearing something negative." He cites journalist Sydney J. Harris: "It is impossible to learn anything important about anyone until we get him or her to disagree with us."

## Why It Matters

Leaders naturally talk more than they listen because they're expected to know things. Listening discipline reverses this by making the leader's job partly to absorb information others possess. This is essential in complex environments where the leader cannot know everything and where late-stage corrections are expensive. Active listening also builds trust: people feel valued when genuinely heard and share more freely when they sense the leader seeks truth rather than confirmation.
