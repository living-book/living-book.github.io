# Self-Directed Questioning

## Definition

Self-directed questioning is the deliberate practice of leaders asking themselves tough questions about their motivations, investments, and impact. Rather than waiting for others to ask hard questions, effective leaders proactively interrogate their own leadership through daily reflection, taking responsibility for their growth and integrity.

## In the Book

Maxwell begins Chapter 2 by recounting a lunch with Coach John Wooden, who revealed, "There is one question I ask myself every day." This sparked Maxwell to develop his own daily self-questioning discipline. The chapter explores four key self-questions: "Am I investing in myself?" (tied to personal growth and self-image), "Am I genuinely interested in others?" (checking against selfish motives like power, position, money, or prestige), "Am I grounded as a leader?" (examining humility, authenticity, and connection to calling), and "Am I adding value to my team?" (assessing team development).

Maxwell emphasizes that asking himself "Am I genuinely interested in others?" keeps his "natural selfishness in check" and prevents him from abusing his leadership advantage. He notes that leaders are "always in danger of abusing their power," making internal questioning essential. The practice operates as a filter: leaders who ask themselves hard questions avoid the trap of believing everything revolves around them, especially when their organization is winning.

## Why It Matters

Self-directed questioning flips the power dynamic of accountability. Instead of being caught unaware by others' concerns, leaders who interrogate themselves stay ahead of their blind spots and maintain moral clarity. This discipline prevents the erosion of character that success often brings and creates psychological safety: people trust leaders who visibly struggle with their own growth rather than those who claim to have it all figured out. The practice is portable across any context where authority and integrity matter.
