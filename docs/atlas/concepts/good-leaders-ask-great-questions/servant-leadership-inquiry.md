# Servant Leadership Inquiry

## Definition

Servant leadership inquiry is the habit of asking team members "How can I serve you?" as a structural practice, not a rhetorical gesture. It reorients the leader's role: instead of being the person who has answers and directs work, the leader becomes the person who identifies and removes barriers, provides resources, and enables others' success.

## In the Book

In Chapter 3, Maxwell describes a dinner with his team where they identified the questions he asks most often. High on the list: "How can I serve you?" His executive assistant Linda Eggers, who has worked with Maxwell for over twenty-five years, said, "I can't remember a day that you didn't ask me if there was anything I needed to help me move forward."

Maxwell explains his reasoning: "I never want to be the bottleneck for my team." Rather than team members waiting for the leader to unblock them, the leader proactively asks what blocks exist and removes them. His writer Charlie Wetzel notes that when Maxwell asks this question, "He genuinely wants to do whatever he can to help me get my work done quickly and easily."

Maxwell also shares how this practice cascades: when team members internalize the question, they ask it of the clients they serve. One team member asked a client Dianna how he could serve her; she asked if he could introduce her to Joel Osteen; he made the connection happen. Dianna's response: "Once again I am in awe. You just keep getting better."

## Why It Matters

This practice flips the psychological dynamic of hierarchy. Most organizations create distance between levels; servant inquiry closes it by making the leader's job explicitly about enabling subordinates. It eliminates the friction of unclear expectations (team members don't have to guess what help is available) and shifts the leader's attention from their own agenda to the organization's output. The practice is especially powerful in knowledge work where the real blockages are often intangible: access, permission, attention, clarity.
