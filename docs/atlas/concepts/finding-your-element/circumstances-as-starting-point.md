# Circumstances as Starting Point, Not Destination

## Definition

Your current circumstances—your age, financial situation, family responsibilities, location, education, social position—matter. They are real constraints that shape your options and the difficulty of your quest. Yet they do not determine your destination. Starting from poverty is harder than starting with money. Starting from a location with no access to your passion is harder than starting in a hub where it thrives. But Robinson shows through example after example that people with severe constraints still find their Element, while people with every advantage sometimes don't.

## In the Book

Robinson illustrates this through the Labon family, who were living in San Francisco (the "favorite city in the world") with good jobs, good schools, excellent prospects—and weren't living the lives they were meant to be living. They could have easily rationalized: "Our circumstances are good, our commitments are great, we're dug in too deep." Instead, they recognized that their Element was somewhere else and made the radical decision to travel across North America, eventually settling in Park City, Utah. Robinson's point: "Starting points can be arbitrary. That they started in San Francisco was nothing more than circumstance. They could have launched their journey from New York, Mali, Liverpool or anywhere else."

He contrasts this with his own example: at twenty-two, unencumbered and free to make decisions just for himself, he had to choose between teaching in Sweden or studying for a PhD in London. Both were real options that emerged from his circumstances. Had his situation been different—with dependents, debt, health constraints—his options would have been narrower. But the principle remained: circumstances shape which paths are available, not whether a path exists.

Robinson introduces the SWOT analysis (strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats) as a framework for taking stock of where you are. The bottom two boxes—opportunities and threats—deal with your practical circumstances (external factors). The top two—strengths and weaknesses—deal with your personal qualities (internal factors). The exercise is not to despair about threats or constraints, but to see them clearly: What truly keeps you from pursuing your passions? What would it require to overcome these hurdles? What would the consequences actually be (not imagined, but real)?

Robinson notes that many people underestimate how much change is possible from their current situation. A partner might not leave if you change careers. Financial safety nets might be smaller than feared. Opportunities for moving, retraining, or redirecting might be more available than you assume. The worst obstacles are often imagined—the self-imposed barriers that live in attitude—rather than truly circumstantial.

Mariellen Ward exemplifies this. After traumas and losses that could have immobilized her, she eventually felt a voice directing her to India. She spent a year planning, saving, and preparing while fully embedded in responsibilities. When she finally went, despite fears about difficulty and danger, "the net not only appeared, but it turned into a magic carpet." Her circumstances didn't change her destination; they changed the timeline and the path, but not the possibility.

## Why It Matters

This concept liberates you from two equal errors: the passivity of "my circumstances make it impossible," and the denial of real constraints as if they don't exist. Robinson's framework says: Your circumstances are real, map them honestly with the SWOT analysis, but don't mistake starting difficulty for permanent impossibility. You can be deeply constrained by your current situation and still move toward your Element—it may take longer, require more creativity, demand more sacrifice, but the fundamental quest is not foreclosed by where you are now. This is what gives agency back to people in genuinely difficult circumstances while keeping realistic about the work required.
