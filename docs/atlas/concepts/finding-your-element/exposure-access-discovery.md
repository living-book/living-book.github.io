# Exposure and Access: You Cannot Discover What You Have Not Encountered

## Definition

Your Element exists within you, waiting to be discovered, but you cannot discover aptitudes or develop passions in domains you have never encountered. If you live inland and have never set foot on a boat, you cannot know whether you have a gift for sailing. Your aptitude remains latent, invisible, unrealized. This is a structural constraint, not a character flaw: the human brain cannot develop passion for or master skills in activities outside its experience.

## In the Book

Robinson makes this point explicitly when discussing Ellen MacArthur: "I said in chapter three that if you live far from the ocean and have never set foot on a boat, you'd never know if you had a gift for sailing. That's true, unless you're Ellen MacArthur." Ellen was born in a low-income community, far from the ocean, with no family tradition of sailing, no natural pathways into the sport. She couldn't have been further from having a life as a competitive sailor. Yet at age four, she visited a boat—a single exposure—and that encounter awakened an undeniable passion that eventually led her to develop world-class aptitude.

Neroli Makim grew up isolated on an Australian cattle station without electricity, television, or outside input. This extreme constraint forced her to be creative: "You had no external input. We just had to use our imaginations. I found I would draw a lot to relieve boredom. I used to write stories to go with the pictures." Her passion for creativity wasn't born from isolation; it was revealed by it. Only later, when she encountered the wider world, did she recognize that what she'd been doing all along—drawing, writing, creating—was her Element.

Robinson's implication is profound: many people never find their Element not because they lack aptitude or passion, but because they lack exposure to the domains where those capacities could awaken. A child from a family with no musical tradition may have perfect pitch but never know it. Someone brilliant at carpentry might spend their life in an office because they were never introduced to making things. Someone with a gift for teaching might never discover it if they come from families that value other professions.

The corollary is that access to diverse experiences is a form of privilege—and closing that privilege gap is a matter of expanding what young people (and adults reconsidering careers) are exposed to. The maker movement, which starts with the assumption that anyone can tinker and create, operates on this principle: expose people to making, and you'll discover makers and inventors who didn't know they had that aptitude.

## Why It Matters

This concept explains why some people find their Element early and others must search hard. It's not only about self-knowledge; it's about what the world has made available to know. Recognizing this has two effects: First, it explains gaps in self-knowledge without self-blame. You may not have found your Element not because you failed to look hard enough, but because you lacked access. Second, it prioritizes exposure and experimentation as strategies for discovery. Rather than introspection alone, you need to deliberately encounter new domains, new skills, new communities. Every new exposure is a potential catalyst for awakening an aptitude or passion you didn't know existed.
