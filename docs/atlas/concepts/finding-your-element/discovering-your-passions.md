# Discovering Your Passions

## Definition

Passion is what you love, what genuinely captivates you regardless of external reward. It's not what you're good at or what makes money, but what activates your energy, attention, and enthusiasm. Unlike momentary interests, true passion sustains engagement over time and across contexts. It's the difference between thinking "this is interesting" and "I can't imagine not doing this."

## In the Book

Robinson distinguishes passion from happiness. Happiness can be circumstantial; passion is deeper and more driven by intrinsic meaning. He emphasizes that many people lack passion because they've never been asked to look for it, or they've been trained to dismiss it as impractical. The accountant in his example didn't just understand numbers; she relished them, loved working with them. Confucius said, "Choose a job you love, and you will never have to work a day in your life"—this is not poetic exaggeration in Robinson's framework; it's a diagnostic statement.

Notably, Robinson addresses the gaps between aptitude and passion through real examples. Some people are good at things they don't love (the forced professional path). Others love things they're not naturally good at. Ellen MacArthur, the sailor, was drawn to the ocean from age four—she didn't have aptitude yet, but she had a haunting, undeniable passion that drove her to develop one. Neroli Makim grew up isolated on a cattle station, discovered through forced creativity that drawing and writing fascinated her, and later realized this childhood passion was her life's work, even as she tried other careers.

Discovering passion requires attention: What activities make you lose track of time? What do you do without external incentive? What would you pursue even if you couldn't be paid? Robinson treats passion as something to be discovered through experimentation and reflection, not something to be invented through willpower.

## Why It Matters

Passion is the sustaining force that carries you through the difficulty, tedium, and inevitable setbacks in any meaningful pursuit. Without it, you're reliant on discipline and external reward—both of which are fragile. With it, the work itself becomes the reward. This reframes career decision-making from "what job should I take?" to "what would I do if I had to choose for myself?" It also gives permission to take seriously what you love, even when it seems impractical initially.
