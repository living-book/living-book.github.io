# The Change Curve

## Definition

Leopold and Kaltenecker adapt Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's work to organizational change, showing that people move through five stages: (1) shock and loss of competence, (2) fear and resistance, (3) rational insight and emotional acceptance, (4) applying new behaviors through practice, and (5) learning and integration. Each stage is characterized by different emotions and competence levels, plotted on a curve over time. The key insight is that *everyone* passes through these stages—the depth and duration vary by person and change magnitude—and that rushing through them or pretending they don't exist guarantees failure.

## In the Book

The book grounds this in real organizational examples. A team facing agile transformation goes through shock when an external Scrum expert tells them their waterfall project management approach is "dysfunctional" and their experience is now worthless. The emotional wound (fear, loss of identity) is as real as the technical learning challenge. Shift supervisors at the factory retreat start at shock ("Why are we here?"), move through resistance ("This won't work, we know what we're doing"), then gradually accept the need for change only after their grievances are heard and respected in small-group discussions. The book emphasizes that the third stage—moving from "I see this rationally" to "I accept this emotionally"—is where most change initiatives fail: understanding why change is needed is not the same as being ready to change.

The curve also shows that different organizational levels enter stages at different times. Managers often see the need for change and move into action earlier than frontline workers, creating a dangerous gap where managers mistake early acceptance for organizational readiness. The book's breakthrough insight is that this gap—not resistance itself—is what derails change.

## Why It Matters

This gives change leaders a roadmap for *what's happening* beneath the surface of any organizational transformation. It explains why "just tell people" doesn't work and why early quick wins, far from closing the deal, can backfire if they're used as proof that people should just accept change faster. For leaders managing any complex change—whether implementing new tools, reorganizing teams, or shifting culture—the curve prevents the common mistake of spending resources on stage-1 (awareness) problems while stage-2 (emotional acceptance) is the real bottleneck. It redirects investment toward coaching, dialog, and time—the variables that actually move people through the curve.
