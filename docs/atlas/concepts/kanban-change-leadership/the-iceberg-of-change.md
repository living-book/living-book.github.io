# The Iceberg of Change

## Definition

Change initiatives have a visible tip—the new processes, technologies, or structures you can see and touch—and a much larger, hidden mass beneath the surface. The underwater portion consists of emotions, fears, beliefs, relationships, and cultural assumptions. Leopold and Kaltenecker argue that change fails when leaders focus only on the visible tip and ignore the emotional and psychological forces beneath. The visible changes matter, but the invisible depths determine whether people accept, resist, or sabotage the initiative.

## In the Book

The book uses the iceberg metaphor to explain why well-designed Kanban systems fail in practice. Part 1 teaches the visible mechanics: visualizing work, setting WIP limits, defining classes of service—all the tools you can see on a board. Part 2 then shows that technical changes alone are insufficient. The authors describe a factory resistance retreat where shift supervisors openly mocked a "new processes" workshop with folded arms and whispered dismissals, despite the initiative's official endorsement. The team's emotional resistance—wounded pride, fear of losing competence, feeling disrespected—dominated the room. The iceberg model explains this: the proposed changes were experienced as attacking what workers had spent years mastering. Until the emotional layer was addressed through facilitated dialog and shared problem-solving, the process improvements remained blocked.

The book extends this to organizational level. A long-established electronics firm ignored market changes until crisis forced change. When leadership brought in an external crisis manager who imposed new structures top-down, bypassing the existing knowledge and relationships of the organization, the most experienced employees left. The consultant focused on the visible iceberg tip (organizational design) while the underwater (human relationships, trust, cultural continuity) collapsed beneath.

## Why It Matters

This concept lets you see why technically correct change fails and politically-savvy change works. It separates the failure mode of "we have the right plan, people are just resisting" from the actual problem: hidden forces are more powerful than visible plans. For any change leader—in software, manufacturing, healthcare, or organizations—it redirects attention from *what* to change to *how* to change in a way that honors the human dimensions. Leaders working in complex, human-centered systems learn to scan both surfaces of the iceberg before launching an initiative, investing time in understanding emotions, relationships, and cultural assumptions that no process diagram reveals.
