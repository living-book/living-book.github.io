# Work-Alignment Matrix — Personal Meaning vs. Organizational Priorities

## Definition

A 2x2 matrix that plots all the work you do against two dimensions: (1) Does the organization care about this? (2) Do you care about this? This creates four quadrants, each suggesting a different strategy. The framework helps you see where your efforts are aligned with what matters to you *and* the organization (sweet spot), where you're wasting energy on things nobody cares about, where you're doing necessary work that doesn't engage you, and where you care about things the organization doesn't value.

## In the Book

Map 7 walks you through completing the matrix by first cataloging all your work: projects, administration, meetings, people management, skill development. Then you place each item into one of four boxes.

**Top right (You care / They care):** This is good work aligned with both personal meaning and organizational need. The book recommends spending time here and looking for seeds to turn some of it into great work.

**Bottom left (You don't care / They don't care):** This is pointless work. The prescription is direct: stop doing it. If you can't stop, do it at the minimal level needed. The book is blunt: "The very acme of pointlessness is doing this work excellently." Doing this work one iota better than adequately is to waste your life.

**Bottom right (You don't care / They care):** This is necessary work you don't enjoy. The book recommends either delegating it, or if you must do it yourself, embracing adequacy (good enough) or embracing laziness (finding the fastest, easiest way). The insight: "Lazy people are often extremely efficient, because they look for the fastest, easiest way of doing things."

**Top left (You care / They don't care):** This often contains great work, but the organization isn't interested—a misalignment. The book offers three strategies: (1) Do it undercover—"It's better to apologize than explain," get it partially done, then see if anyone's interested; (2) Relabel it—find a way to present it so the organization sees its value; (3) Do it elsewhere—accept you won't find time in your current role and do it outside work, transfer to a different role, or change organizations.

The example of Janet, a junior lawyer, shows her realizing that environmental law (which she cared about) was impossible in her firm, so she faced a hard choice about whether to stay. But she also found opportunities in the top-left box that could be reframed or pursued through other means.

## Why It Matters

This matrix prevents burnout by revealing where you're over-giving and where you're underutilized. It clarifies that alignment is not about "fitting the job description" but about two-way fit: are you the right person for this work *and* does this work feed you? It also normalizes the fact that perfect alignment is rare, and that managing misalignment requires deliberate strategy—not just harder work or better attitudes.
