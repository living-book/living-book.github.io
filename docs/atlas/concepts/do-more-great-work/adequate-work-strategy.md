# Adequate Work and Intelligent Laziness

## Definition

Adequate means "good enough." The book's core insight: many high-performers are programmed to operate at top level for everything they do, and this perfectionism bleeds away time and energy that could fuel great work. The strategy is to identify work that doesn't need your best effort and deliberately dial down to adequacy. Paired with this is embracing "intelligent laziness"—the observation that lazy people often achieve efficiency because they look for the fastest, easiest way of doing things.

This is not about lowering standards for important work, but about realistic triage: not all work deserves your peak effort.

## In the Book

Stanier introduces this in Map 7 as a strategy for the "You don't care / They care" quadrant—work that the organization values but you don't. The book explicitly states: "We are programmed to operate at top level for everything we do, so we overwork and overdeliver even on work that really doesn't require such dedication. We pay by losing the opportunity to do more Great Work."

The section on intelligent laziness is framed as a reframe: laziness isn't character flaw but a form of efficiency. The book asks: "If you were lazy rather than conscientious, how would you approach this work?" This flips the usual narrative. A lazy approach to a necessary but uninspiring report might be: use a template, fill in the required sections minimally, leverage existing data rather than creating new analysis, send it on schedule without agonizing over perfection.

The book's quote from David Dunham: "Efficiency is intelligent laziness." This captures the idea that trying to optimize non-critical work is the opposite of efficient; true efficiency means knowing what deserves your attention and what doesn't.

The paradox: "Adequate means good enough" is not the same as "sloppy." Work done at adequacy level should meet the actual requirement; it just doesn't get the extra polish that peak effort would provide.

## Why It Matters

Perfectionism is often dressed up as conscientiousness, but it's actually a hidden destroyer of great work. If you spend 80% of your energy on work that only needs 20%, you have no fuel left for work that genuinely needs you. The book reframes adequacy not as moral failure but as strategic choice—a way of respecting your limited energy and attention. It also implicitly challenges organizational cultures that expect excellence on everything, showing how that expectation prevents the kind of differentiated effort that actually drives great work.
