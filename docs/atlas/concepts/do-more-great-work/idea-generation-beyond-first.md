# Idea Generation Beyond First-Idea-Itis

## Definition

Most people suffer from "first-idea-itis"—the tendency to grab the first half-decent idea that comes along and run with it, regardless of whether it's the best idea available. This happens because of three limiting beliefs and habits: (1) the belief that creativity is something only "creative people" do; (2) a limited number of ways to generate ideas (usually just concentrating hard and hoping); (3) not giving yourself time and space for ideas to emerge (ideas require what the book calls "relaxed free time").

The antidote is to recognize that you have ideas all the time, but you need better methods to generate them faster, overcome self-censorship, and expand your range of options before choosing.

## In the Book

Map 9 directly addresses this. Stanier notes that "corporate brainstorming sessions can be dispiriting, soul-sucking experiences" and explains why: we approach idea generation as if it requires magical inspiration when it actually responds to structure. The book's provocative statement: "We're all better than we think we are at having ideas, our experience at brainstorming meetings notwithstanding."

The chapter acknowledges the imp in your head that says "I'm not much good at this" or "Unless the idea is perfect, I won't own up to it." The book frames creative work as a muscle that needs exercise and explicitly gives permission to generate ideas that are "improbable, impossible, illegal, or irresponsible"—because this isn't yet about deciding what to do, just about expanding choices. The example of Carlos shows him generating 10 new ideas about client development in 10 minutes, including some initially rejected as half-baked, but one (attending a niche conference) sparked a real insight.

Crucially, the book distinguishes between two phases of creativity: expansion (go big, go wide, quantity over quality) and narrowing (later you'll evaluate). Most people try to do both simultaneously, which kills the expansion phase. The phrase "six crayons to the full ninety-six-crayon extravaganza" captures the idea that limiting your palette early blocks possibilities.

The book also introduces "And what else?" as a coaching question that keeps ideas flowing beyond the first answer.

## Why It Matters

This concept removes the mystery from creativity and shows it as a learnable skill. By naming "first-idea-itis" and its causes, it explains why your solutions feel stale (you're choosing from a sample of one) and why big breakthroughs seem to happen to other people (they either give themselves more time, or they're less self-critical about half-baked ideas). It also reveals why rushing to action (a hallmark of busy organizations) actually produces worse solutions—you never expanded the possibility space. The practical payoff: systematic idea generation, with permission to be bad at it at first, reliably produces more and better options than waiting for inspiration.
