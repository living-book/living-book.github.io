# Doing the Right Thing the Right Way

## Definition

Pichler frames product and process correctness as two independent axes
crossed in a 2x2 matrix (Figure 1.1). Building the right product with the
right process yields enduring success. The right product built the wrong
way is a "quick but unsustainable win." The wrong product built the right
way is slow failure. The wrong product built the wrong way is fast
failure. The two axes map directly onto the book's split between the
product owner, "primarily responsible for the 'what' — creating the right
product," and the ScrumMaster, "primarily responsible for the 'how' — using
Scrum the right way."

## In the Book

The matrix appears in Chapter 1 immediately after the book explains why
product owner and ScrumMaster are distinct, complementary roles — never
combined in one person. The two roles are deliberately built to check
different failure axes: a product owner alone might optimize a process for
speed while building something nobody wants (the "quick but unsustainable
win" quadrant, cutting corners to hit a wrong target fast); a ScrumMaster
alone enforcing perfect Scrum mechanics doesn't guarantee the backlog
contains the right things. The book states directly that one individual
should never hold both roles, because doing so collapses the deliberate
separation between the two checks.

## Why It Matters

When an effort fails, "did we build the wrong thing" and "did we build it
badly" are different diagnoses requiring different fixes, and conflating
them wastes the correction. This applies well past software: any endeavor
benefits from separately checking that the target is correct and that the
method reaching it is sound, because a team can be excellent at execution
while chasing the wrong goal, or have the right goal and ruin it with a bad
process — and only distinguishing the two axes tells you which lever to
pull.
