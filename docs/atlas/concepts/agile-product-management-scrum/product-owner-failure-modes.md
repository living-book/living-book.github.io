# Product Owner Failure Modes

## Definition

Pichler catalogs a set of caricatured ways the product owner role fails in
practice, each traceable to a specific structural cause rather than to the
individual's competence: the role is under-empowered, over-loaded, split
across people, physically or attentionally distant from the team, or
delegated to a stand-in without real authority. Each failure mode has a
distinct symptom and a distinct fix — treating them interchangeably (e.g.
"just work harder") misses the underlying cause.

## In the Book

The book names the failure modes across two chapters. In Chapter 1: the
Underpowered Product Owner, who must escalate every major decision to a
boss (one example forced to consult "the head of the line of business" for
every call, eroding the team's confidence); the Overworked Product Owner,
who neglects backlog grooming and misses meetings because the role
competes with other jobs or too many teams; the Partial Product Owner,
where duties are split between an outward-facing "product manager" who owns
vision and an inward-facing "product owner" who is little more than a
backlog-item writer, reinforcing old handoffs; the Distant Product Owner,
separated from the team by rooms, buildings, or continents, causing
mistrust and slow progress; the Proxy Product Owner, a stand-in (in one
client case, a business analyst) who does the work without the authority to
decide; and the Product Owner Committee, a leaderless group prone to
"death by committee" — endless meetings, conflicting interests, no
progress. In Chapter 5, two more appear inside the sprint cycle: the
Bungee Product Owner, who appears at sprint planning, vanishes during the
sprint, and reemerges at review; and the Passive Product Owner, illustrated
by a product owner who watches a sprint review demo in silence, says "good
job," and leaves — turning a working session into a spectator show.

## Why It Matters

A single-point-of-accountability role fails in recognizably different ways
depending on which structural support is missing — authority, time,
proximity, or genuine ownership — and the fix differs by mode: an
underpowered owner needs sponsorship, an overworked one needs relief from
other duties, a distant one needs colocation. Naming the failure modes
turns a vague complaint ("the product owner isn't working") into a specific,
treatable diagnosis, a pattern useful for any single-accountable-owner
design, not just Scrum.
