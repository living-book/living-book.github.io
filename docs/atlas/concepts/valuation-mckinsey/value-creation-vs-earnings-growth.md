# Managing for Value vs. Managing for Earnings: The Critical Trade-off

## Definition

Many executives and boards treat earnings per share (EPS) as a proxy for value creation, but the two diverge significantly. EPS is an accounting metric that can be manipulated through accounting choices, changes in capital structure, or short-term decisions that destroy long-term cash flows. Value creation is the increase in intrinsic value, driven by ROIC exceeding cost of capital, sustained over time. A company can boost EPS while destroying value (by cutting R&D or maintenance), and it can sacrifice EPS to create value (by investing in growth or R&D when below-ROIC competitors are cut).

## In the Book

Chapter 1 opens with a corporate real-world example: from 1997 to 2003, a global consumer products company grew EPS at 11–16 percent annually. Impressed, investors pushed the stock price above peers. In reality, the company was shortchanging investment in product development and brand building to inflate short-term profits, even as revenue growth declined. In 2003, managers had to admit what they'd done. The stock went through a painful recovery, taking years to rebound. The chapter cites a McKinsey survey of 400 CFOs: fully 80 percent said they would reduce spending on value-creating activities (marketing, R&D) to meet short-term earnings targets, and 39 percent said they would give discounts to pull forward customer purchases to hit quarterly EPS targets.

The consequences are systemic. McKinsey found empirically that companies that invested heavily in R&D had stronger long-term total returns to shareholders (Exhibit 1.1), yet executives routinely cut R&D to smooth earnings. Chapter 4 shows that a company can dilute EPS through an acquisition that creates value, or boost EPS through financial engineering that destroys value, and neither is a reliable signal. "There is no empirical evidence linking increased EPS with the value created by a transaction. Deals that strengthen EPS and deals that dilute EPS are equally likely to create or destroy value."

The book notes that when a company's share price rises due to short-term EPS beats, managers face the expectations treadmill: they must run even faster next quarter to beat the now-elevated expectations. This pressure often leads to three destructive patterns: (1) short-term earnings manipulation, (2) risky acquisitions to justify high valuations, and (3) neglect of long-term investments. Chapter 1 cites the Mirant example: deregulation created sky-high expectations (valued at 30x EBITDA), so management invested aggressively across multiple countries to justify the valuation. The debt burden became unsustainable, and Mirant filed for bankruptcy in 2003.

## Why It Matters

This concept reveals a fundamental agency problem in modern corporate management. Executive compensation tied to EPS or short-term stock price misaligns incentives with long-term value creation. Boards often lack the sophistication to challenge EPS-focused strategies; executives themselves sometimes doubt the relevance of EPS but use it anyway "for the benefit of Wall Street analysts." The result is companies that optimize for the wrong metric, destroying shareholder value in the pursuit of illusory short-term gains. The antidote is discipline: focus on ROIC, growth, and cash flow; evaluate managers on these metrics and their performance relative to peers; and invest for the long term even when it hurts short-term earnings.
