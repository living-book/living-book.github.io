# The Expectations Treadmill: Beating Expectations Gets Harder as Performance Improves

## Definition

The expectations treadmill describes a dynamic in which investor expectations about a company's future performance become embedded in its current stock price. As a company beats those expectations and improves its operating performance, its stock price rises, which simultaneously raises the bar—the market now expects the company to maintain its newly improved level of performance. Managers face an ever-accelerating treadmill: they must run faster just to stand still. What made a company an exceptional investment yesterday becomes ordinary performance tomorrow.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 uses the parable of Theresa Turnaround and Prospectus Inc. (Chapter 4) to illustrate. Theresa is hired to run a company with below-average returns and low expectations. After two years of genuine operational improvement, Prospectus' stock price rises twice as fast as its peers', because the market wasn't expecting a turnaround. After four years, when Prospectus becomes an industry leader in ROIC and market share, its stock price has risen four times the industry average—remarkable outperformance. But then, despite sustained excellence, Theresa finds her company's shares performing only as well as peers. The company's high performance is now priced in; beating ordinary expectations has become impossible because expectations themselves have risen.

The book provides a real-world example: comparing Reckitt Benckiser and Henkel from 2008–2013 (Exhibit 4.1). Reckitt Benckiser outperformed Henkel on both growth (10% vs. 3% annually) and ROIC throughout the period, yet Henkel's shareholders earned 32 percent annual returns versus Reckitt's 19 percent. The difference was starting multiples: Henkel's enterprise value/NOPLAT multiple rose from 14x to 21x (ambition rewarded by the market), while Reckitt's flat multiple (18x to 20x) reflected already-high expectations. A good company is not always a good investment if future great performance is already priced in.

## Why It Matters

The expectations treadmill explains a critical disconnect between operating performance and shareholder returns. A manager can execute a flawless strategy, improve ROIC, accelerate growth, and still deliver ordinary stock returns because the market expected exactly that. Conversely, a manager overseeing a weak company can deliver extraordinary shareholder returns simply by meeting low expectations. This has profound implications for executive compensation, board evaluation of managers, and corporate strategy. It suggests that companies with high expectations embedded in their stock prices should focus on sustainable value creation rather than desperate moves to justify their valuations—and that the best investment opportunities often lie in companies with depressed expectations, not industry leaders.
