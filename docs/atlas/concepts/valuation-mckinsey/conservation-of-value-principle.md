# Conservation of Value: Only Cash Flow Creates Value

## Definition

The conservation of value principle states that value is created exclusively through increases in cash flows; anything that doesn't increase long-term cash flows—regardless of whether it improves reported earnings, changes ownership of claims on those cash flows, or reshuffles financial structures—does not create value. The principle applies even to actions that reduce a company's cost of capital (through risk reduction), which can increase value by lowering the discount rate applied to future cash flows.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 opens with the principle: "A corollary of the principle that discounted cash flow drives value is the conservation of value: anything that doesn't increase cash flows doesn't create value." McKinsey illustrates this through three concrete examples.

*Executive stock options accounting*: For decades, companies didn't expense stock options on income statements. When the FASB mandated expensing in 2004, executives predicted disaster for start-up companies relying on options for compensation. Yet stock prices didn't move. The market already reflected the dilution in its valuations, because the underlying cash impact was unchanged. One analyst told McKinsey: "I don't care whether they are recorded as an expense or simply disclosed in the footnotes. I know what to do with the information."

*Share repurchases and earnings per share*: A company can boost reported EPS by borrowing to repurchase shares (reducing share count while keeping total earnings flat). This mechanical EPS increase creates an illusion of value. But as leverage increases, equity becomes riskier, and the P/E ratio must compress to offset the higher EPS—conservation of value. Buybacks create genuine value only if they prevent management from destroying cash at low returns.

*Acquisitions and multiple expansion*: Many executives believe a low-P/E company acquiring a high-P/E company will see the target's earnings re-rated upward once mixed into the buyer's financials. This "multiple expansion" theory has no empirical support. Unless the combined cash flows actually increase through cost synergies or accelerated growth, the combined value remains the sum of the parts.

## Why It Matters

The conservation of value principle is the executive's filter for detecting financial illusions. It cuts through accounting tricks, complex structures, and Wall Street narratives to ask: *Does this actually increase cash?* Managers often pursue stock option expensing, financial engineering, or cosmetic restructurings because they believe markets don't understand economics. The market does. This principle explains why clever financial maneuvers almost always disappoint and why the best value creation comes from unglamorous, operational improvements: lower costs, higher margins, faster turnover of capital.
