# The Inner Game (Awareness Over Instruction)

## Definition

Drawing on Timothy Gallwey's Inner Game series (starting with The Inner Game of Tennis), Hunt distinguishes the "outer" game — the visible activity — from the more decisive "inner" game: the internal interference that instruction and self-judgment create. The core technique is cultivating nonjudgmental situational feedback — observe precisely what is happening, without trying to correct it or narrate a fix — summarized in the Inner Game maxim "Trying fails, awareness cures."

## In the Book

Hunt recounts Gallwey teaching a woman in her late fifties, who had never played a sport, to volley in twenty minutes — not through instruction but by having her say "Bounce" and "Hit" out loud as the ball moved, then hum the rhythm of a serve while watching, with no verbal breakdown of the mechanics at all. He contrasts this with his own experience in ski lessons, where an instructor's rapid-fire verbal stream ("keep your elbows in! bend your knees! tips together!") overwhelms L-mode's slow verbal-processing bandwidth until the brain simply "fries" and stops attending — what he calls brain freeze. The Inner Game of Music sidebar makes the mechanism explicit: a bass teacher who corrected posture verbally, bar by bar, produced a stiff pretzel of a student; switching to "just observe how this feels, don't judge it" produced immediate improvement.

Hunt extends the idea past sports into debugging: instead of jumping straight to fixing a bug, be fully aware of what the system is actually doing first ("don't just do something; stand there"), citing June Kim's technique of consciously imagining what the code should look like before opening your eyes to check it against the real thing. The chapter's second half (Pressure Kills Cognition) shows the flip side: when a bass student is told the judges aren't listening yet, freed of the pressure to perform, they suddenly play their best — because permission to fail removes the very interference the inner game is designed to quiet.

## Why It Matters

This concept locates the actual bottleneck in transmitting skill: not the quality or quantity of instruction, but whether the learner has bandwidth left over to attend to the raw feedback their own performance is generating. It applies well beyond sports and music to any coaching or code-review relationship — the fix is often not more explanation but creating room for the learner to notice a gap themselves.
