# Pressure Kills Cognition

## Definition

Perceived time pressure actively shuts down the brain's asynchronous, creative processing (R-mode) rather than sharpening focus, because R-mode has no sense of urgency or deadline — only L-mode handles the perception of time. When the mind registers a critical deadline, it narrows attention, stops considering options, and locks R-mode out of the shared cognitive bus — the opposite of the "pressure makes me sharper" intuition many people hold about their own performance.

## In the Book

Hunt's central evidence is the "Good Samaritan" seminary-student experiment: students told they had an urgent, career-critical meeting across campus stepped over an actor planted as a collapsed beggar in their path, while students given the same meeting but no time pressure stopped to help. He also cites Teresa Amabile's decade-long workplace creativity study, which found people are least creative under time pressure — and that the effect doesn't end when the deadline passes: creativity stays measurably depressed for roughly two more days afterward, a "time-pressure hangover" that's Hunt's explicit reason to end project iterations before a break rather than push through a crunch into one. He pairs this with a personal account of a Jerry Weinberg workshop simulation where escalating pressure caused participants in managerial roles to make progressively worse decisions, followed by a described sensation of the brain "coming back online" — like a limb waking up from having fallen asleep — once the pressure lifted.

The chapter's practical resolution is "grant permission to fail": a music teacher who told nervous students the judges weren't listening yet (a lie) produced their best performances, because removing the stakes removed the very pressure that was shutting down their R-mode.

## Why It Matters

This overturns a default assumption in high-pressure cultures — that urgency reliably produces better output — with a specific causal mechanism (R-mode lockout) and a specific cost structure (a multi-day recovery tax, not just an in-the-moment dip). It reframes protecting slack time, ending iterations before a crunch, and deliberately lowering the stakes of a rehearsal or a first attempt as direct interventions on cognition, not just morale — a concept exportable to any domain where deadline pressure is treated as a management tool rather than a creativity tax.
