# L-mode and R-mode Cognition

## Definition

Hunt models the brain as a "Dual-CPU" system. CPU #1, or L-mode ("linear mode"), processes step-by-step, verbally, and relatively slowly — it's also the source of the constant internal verbal "chatter" that runs when nothing else is happening. CPU #2, or R-mode ("rich mode"), works asynchronously and nonverbally, like a background search-and-pattern-match engine across long-term memory, and surfaces results — sometimes days later — without being consciously directed. Both share one memory bus, so when either mode dominates, it locks the other out.

## In the Book

The bus-contention idea explains a familiar phenomenon: trying to describe a vivid dream in words right after waking makes it evaporate, because the dream was generated in R-mode, and putting it into L-mode's verbal terms crowds R-mode off the shared bus. Hunt extends this to any nonverbal ability — recognizing a familiar face despite a new haircut, or the "aha" that arrives in the shower or the next day on a problem you'd consciously set aside. R-mode is described as diligent but not directly controllable — "the peripheral vision of your mind" — it stores nearly everything but isn't indexed the way verbal memory is, so retrieving it requires giving it room to run rather than demanding it on schedule.

The book's practical throughline (Chapter 4, "Get in Your Right Mind") is that most professional and educational habits are tuned entirely for L-mode — reading, writing, verbal instruction — while R-mode is starved of the sensory, visual, and exploratory input it needs to do its pattern-matching work, and rarely gets a channel back into L-mode once it does produce something.

## Why It Matters

This gives a mechanistic account of why "just think harder" or "just explain it more clearly" fails for intuition, insight, and creative problem-solving — those are R-mode products, and L-mode effort can actively crowd them out. It reframes activities that look unproductive (staring out a window, doodling, taking a walk) as a different kind of cognitive work, and it explains why verbal, step-by-step instruction is the wrong tool for transmitting a felt, holistic skill — a claim that connects directly to research on dual-process cognition and hemispheric specialization elsewhere in the catalog, though Hunt's framing is drawn from Betty Edwards' drawing pedagogy rather than decision-theoretic dual-process models.
