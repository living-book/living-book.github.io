# The Mental Marinade (Defocus to Focus)

## Definition

Certain problems yield only to a deliberately less-conscious approach: instead of grinding on a problem with focused attention, you load it up with relevant facts and then let it "marinate" — consciously disengaging and giving background cognition room to work. Hunt is explicit that this counts as real work, not idleness: "it's not the idea of not doing anything; it's the idea of not doing something."

## In the Book

Hunt draws on Tom Lutz's observation that creative process often requires "an enormous amount of sitting around doing nothing," and pairs it with Daniel Dennett's "multiple drafts" model of consciousness from Consciousness Explained: at any moment the brain holds many parallel, unfinished "drafts" of thoughts and interpretations (pictured as scattered clouds of fireflies), and consciousness is simply whichever draft currently has the most synchronized brain activity — not a single central theater directing everything. Under this model, unresolved problems are drafts still fermenting outside conscious awareness; giving them time lets them develop toward the threshold where they surface. He offers a practical test to tell marinating apart from procrastination: if you're still engaged with the problem but feel "stuck," diversions are marinade; if you no longer want to do the task at all, diversions are just procrastination (the "paper dolls" case: a friend who skipped studying to cut paper dolls the night before an exam — and still aced it, because for her it turned out to be a legitimate marinade, not an escape).

Hunt ties this to the "consultant's Rule of Three" — if you can't think of three ways a plan could fail or three different solutions, you haven't let the multiple drafts ripen long enough — and notes this kind of thinking time is systematically unrecognized as "work" in corporate settings where visible typing is mistaken for the only real labor.

## Why It Matters

This gives permission and a mechanism for a specific unconventional-looking behavior — stepping away from a problem rather than pushing through it — and a concrete way to distinguish it from avoidance. It is the practical, individual-scale complement to any claim that insight and creativity run on background, non-linear processing rather than sustained willful effort, and it directly conflicts with cultures (and the deadline pressure described alongside it in the book) that equate visible effort with progress.
