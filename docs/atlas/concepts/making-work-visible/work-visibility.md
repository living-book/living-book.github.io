# Work Visibility

## Definition

Work visibility is the practice of externalizing what people are working on, its state, and its priority into a shared visual form — a board, not a spreadsheet or a status meeting — so that anyone can see it at a glance without interrupting the person doing it. It is presented as the precondition to combating every other time thief: dependencies, unplanned work, and conflicting priorities are all hard to manage precisely because they are invisible until someone is forced to name them out loud.

## In the Book

Section 2.1 anchors the idea in Philippe Kruchten's "visibility grid," a diagram DeGrandis adapts, cross-tabbing visible/invisible work against positive/negative value — architecture (visible, positive) versus technical debt (invisible, negative) being the illustrative pair. She argues good visualization needs four properties: structure, usefulness, relevance, and honesty, and cites that roughly two-thirds of people are visual-spatial learners with a different brain organization than auditory-sequential learners — meaning a majority of any team is structurally disadvantaged by information that's only spoken or written as prose.

The concrete instrument is the simplest possible kanban board — To Do, Doing, Done — which she argues is powerful because it's self-explanatory: someone can walk into an office, read the board, and know what's being worked on without a meeting. She gives a practical rule for what deserves a card: skip anything under fifteen minutes unless (1) only one person knows how to do it, (2) it impacts another team, or (3) it's someone's primary, otherwise-untracked work — each exception mapping directly back to one of the five thieves (unknown dependencies or too much WIP) that stays invisible if the small task isn't captured.

## Why It Matters

This reframes "communication problems" as a visualization design problem: a board with the right structure eliminates status-update overhead entirely rather than optimizing it, because the information is ambiently available instead of requested. The visual-spatial-learner statistic also gives a concrete, non-rhetorical reason a picture beats a paragraph for most audiences, which generalizes well past software teams to any group trying to coordinate on shared, changing state.
