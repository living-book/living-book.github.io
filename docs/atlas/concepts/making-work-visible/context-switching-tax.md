# The Context-Switching Tax

## Definition

Every time a person is pulled off one task to attend to another, they pay a switching cost: rebuilding the mental state they had before the interruption. Unlike a CPU, which saves and restores process state automatically and near-instantly, a human brain has no such register — it can take up to twenty minutes to return to the same depth of thought after being interrupted. Too much work-in-progress guarantees this tax gets paid constantly.

## In the Book

DeGrandis opens Part 1 with "Too Much WIP" as the ringleader thief, and grounds the mechanism in the computer-science idea of context switching: a CPU alternates between processes at high speed, saving and restoring state so fast it looks like parallelism. Humans attempt the same trick and lose badly at it — there's no automatic save/restore, so "flow" (complete absorption in a task, citing focused, energized productivity) collapses the moment a distraction — email, a coworker, a meeting — arrives. She cites David Rock's *Your Brain at Work* on the twenty-minute recovery cost and uses the Sherlock Holmes "mind palace" as the illustration of what deep, uninterrupted thought requires and how easily it is destroyed.

Crucially, the book roots the cause of overload in psychology, not just scheduling: DeGrandis lists five reasons people take on more WIP than they have capacity for — wanting to be a team player, fearing humiliation for saying no, liking "new and shiny" work over finishing the unglamorous grind, underestimating how big a request really is, and wanting to be liked. She quotes social psychologist Vanessa Bohns: people say yes because they're "managing the impressions other people have of us." The result is chronic overcommitment that guarantees constant context switching.

## Why It Matters

This reframes "multitasking" from a productivity virtue into a measurable cost center: the tax is paid in irritated staff, degraded quality, and delayed delivery, not just in the obvious lost minutes. It also relocates the fix upstream of any scheduling tool — you can't out-process a habit of reflexively saying yes; the switching cost is incurred the moment the commitment is made, not when the work starts. Any domain where an agent (human or otherwise) juggles concurrent obligations under social pressure to accept more of them will pay this same tax.
