# Six Value Drivers of Agile Workforces: Demand Matching, Productivity, Quality, Talent, Cost, Innovation

## Definition

Agile workforce practices create economic value through six mechanisms: matching labor supply to demand fluctuations, increasing productivity per worker, improving quality of output, attracting and retaining talent, reducing costs, and enabling innovation. The AFF's research quantified these drivers, finding that existing agile practices generated value equivalent to 3–13% of total workforce costs, with potential for an additional 3–7%.

## In the Book

The AFF commissioned McKinsey & Company to study value creation in agile workforces across four pilot environments (Lloyds Banking Group office, Tesco superstore, Ford manufacturing plant, BT call center) and six sector workshops. Diagram 2.4 summarizes the research findings:

- **Better align workforce with demand** (26% of identified value): The largest driver. Multi-skilling allows redeployment to peak activities; part-time pools absorb seasonal surges without permanent headcount; shift variation matches customer hours. Tesco saved 13% of workforce costs by scheduling staff to match customer traffic patterns. Ford's manufacturing plant used agile shift arrangements to match production capacity to customer orders.

- **Increase productivity** (18%): Agile arrangements remove barriers to high-performance work. Eversheds law firm found that allowing informal agile working—where people choose their own pattern without contract changes—correlated with 28% of participants reporting increased productivity. BT insourced call center work from India by using agile staffing to lower costs on UK payroll while maintaining quality.

- **Increase quality of output** (18%): Better-trained, less-burned-out staff deliver better work. Tesco's demand-matching program correlated with higher customer satisfaction and shorter queuing times. Customer service staff trained in product knowledge (multi-skilling) improved service quality directly.

- **Attract and retain high-quality talent** (18%): This was historically the primary argument for flexible working. Retention matters: knowing you can negotiate working patterns reduces voluntary turnover, lowering recruitment costs. McKinsey's "Pathways" program—offering multiple career trajectories, rotations, and agile working options—became a recruitment and retention differentiator in professional services.

- **Minimise cost** (14%): Direct cost reduction from lower headcount needs during off-peak periods, reduced overtime, lower real estate costs (fewer desk-days in an office), and lower recruitment churn costs.

- **Drive innovation** (5%): Agile working enables collaboration across time zones and enables diverse working styles that can unlock creative solutions. The research found this driver less frequently cited but present in organizations with multi-site or highly flexible arrangements.

The AFF noted that the value came as a mix of cost reductions and revenue increases. Tesco realized value through better-matched service, Lloyds through both cost and service, and Ford through enabling a winning contract bid.

## Why It Matters

This framework prevents treating agile working as a monolith. Different agile practices drive different value. Job sharing drives talent retention; shift variation drives demand matching; multi-skilling drives both. Understanding which driver matters most in your context shapes which practices to invest in. A manufacturing plant with spiky, predictable demand needs role and time agility; a professional services firm with talent scarcity needs role and source flexibility to retain specialists.

It also shifts the conversation from cost to value creation. Rather than asking "how much will agile working cost?", organizations can ask "what value do we need to create?" and then choose practices that drive that value. This reorientation is the practical difference between treating agility as a benefit (cost-centered) and treating it as a capability (value-centered).
