# Dunbar's Number

## Definition

Anthropologist Robin Dunbar found that humans cannot maintain more than about 150 stable, trust-based relationships — the number of people, as he put it, "you would not feel embarrassed about joining uninvited for a drink if you happened to bump into them in a bar." Sinek treats this as a hard constraint on organizational design: below roughly 150 people, a leader can personally know and be known by everyone; above it, the Circle of Safety can only be sustained by delegating real leadership (not mere management) down through layers of people who each know their own slice of the organization.

## In the Book

Chapter 15 opens with Bill Gore, founder of W. L. Gore & Associates (maker of Gore-Tex), who noticed he no longer recognized people on his factory floor and, by instinct, capped every facility at about 150 employees — building new factories next door rather than expanding existing ones. Sinek shows this matches Dunbar's independently derived research: hunter-gatherer tribes, Hutterite and Amish communities, and a Marine company all cluster around the same 150-person ceiling, driven by two hard limits — the finite hours in a day and finite brain capacity for tracking relationships. He notes the internet didn't dissolve the limit; journalist Rick Lax's attempt to personally contact all 2,000 of his Facebook "friends" showed most were strangers in practice. The chapter's diagnostic is a Marine Corps detail: platoon members (about 40 people, well under 150) call their lieutenant "our lieutenant," while the more distant colonel is simply "the colonel" — the shift from personal, possessive language to formal, distant language signals a group has outgrown direct trust and needs structural scaffolding.

## Why It Matters

This turns "trust doesn't scale" into a specific, falsifiable threshold rather than a vague complaint, and gives a design rule: past ~150 people, don't try to manufacture personal knowledge of everyone — instead invest in empowering the next layer of leaders to extend real care to their own sub-groups. It applies wherever cohesion is supposed to survive growth: platform moderation, military structure, church congregations splitting into cell groups, or any company debating whether to "flatten" its hierarchy as headcount rises.
