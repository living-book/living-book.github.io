# The Four Chemicals (E.D.S.O.)

## Definition

Sinek groups four neurochemicals into a model of why people cooperate or compete: endorphins mask physical pain to keep us pushing through effort (the "runner's high"); dopamine delivers the hit of satisfaction that comes from progress toward or completion of a goal; serotonin is "the leadership chemical," the pride felt when others respect or rely on us; oxytocin is the feeling of trust and closeness generated by physical or social bonding. Endorphins and dopamine are "selfish" chemicals oriented toward individual survival; serotonin and oxytocin are "selfless" chemicals that only activate through relationships with others.

## In the Book

Chapter 6 builds each chemical from its evolutionary function before applying it to organizations. Endorphins explain why runners crave exercise and why Sinek cites Stephen Colbert's line "you can't laugh and be afraid at the same time" — laughter releases endorphins that mask stress. Dopamine explains the addictive pull of crossing items off a to-do list or watching a metric climb toward a goal, using the image of Paleolithic hunters getting incremental dopamine hits as a fruit tree they're walking toward appears to grow larger. Serotonin is illustrated through the pride of watching someone we mentored succeed, or wearing a medal earned by a team's win — it "reinforces the bond between leader and follower, both up and down the chain." Oxytocin is generated by acts like a colleague staying late to help with no expectation of credit; Sinek calls it "chemical love" and notes it also measurably boosts immune function. The chapter closes by noting the two pairs must stay balanced: an organization that only rewards dopamine (individual metrics, short-term wins) without serotonin and oxytocin (recognition, belonging) produces selfish, anxious cultures even when performance numbers look good.

## Why It Matters

This gives incentive design a biological floor: reward systems built purely around individual achievement metrics (dopamine) will reliably produce self-interested, less cooperative behavior, no matter how well-intentioned the leadership rhetoric is, unless they're counterbalanced by mechanisms that generate pride-in-others (serotonin) and trust-through-vulnerability (oxytocin). It's a generalizable lens for auditing any incentive structure — sales commissions, app engagement metrics, grading systems, gamified fitness apps — by asking which of the four chemicals it's actually optimizing for, and whether the selfish and selfless drives are in balance.
