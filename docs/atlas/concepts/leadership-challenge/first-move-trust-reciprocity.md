# Extending Trust First to Trigger Reciprocity

## Definition

Collaboration between people who could otherwise compete or free-ride depends on a "norm of reciprocity": if one party consistently gives and the other consistently takes, cooperation collapses. Kouzes and Posner argue the practical lever for a leader is to move first — extend trust, share credit, or share information before it has been earned back — because reciprocity norms mean the gesture reliably gets returned, and because a leader who waits to be trusted first will wait forever.

## In the Book

Chapter 8 (Foster Collaboration) grounds this in Robert Axelrod's Prisoner's Dilemma tournament, where the winning computer strategy among many submitted was the simplest: cooperate on the first move, then mirror whatever the other player did last time — a strategy that won "by eliciting cooperation from others, not by defeating them." The book applies this directly through Florian Bennhold's account of a new working relationship with consultant Wilson Rickerson, who "was willing to take the first step," sharing his professional contacts immediately after their first meeting — which Florian says made him feel "compelled to reciprocate," and which he credits with performing better than he expected. A second case, Cristian's agency network, shows the inverse mechanism at scale: once profit-sharing made agencies mutually dependent, they began sharing leads directly with each other without needing management to broker it.

## Why It Matters

This gives leaders a concrete first move in situations where trust feels risky to extend because it hasn't yet been demonstrated to be warranted: rather than waiting for proof of trustworthiness, initiate the cooperative act and let the reciprocity norm — documented in game theory as the dominant long-run strategy — do the work of eliciting it back. It generalizes to any interaction where two parties could either cooperate or defect without knowing the other's move in advance: negotiations, new partnerships, cross-team relationships, even international relations.
