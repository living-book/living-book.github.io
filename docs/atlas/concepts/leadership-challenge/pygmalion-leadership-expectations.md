# Leader Expectations as Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

## Definition

Kouzes and Posner name the "Expect the Best" essential of Encourage the Heart after the "Pygmalion effect": a leader's positive expectations aren't just an attitude but a broadcast — they change how the leader acts (more nurturing, more supportive, more forgiving of early stumbles), which changes how the other person performs. "When you expect people to fail, they probably will. If you expect them to succeed, they probably will."

## In the Book

Chapter 10 draws the analogy from the Greek myth of Pygmalion, the sculptor whose belief in his statue brought it to life, and cites research on self-fulfilling prophecies to argue exemplary leaders play this role for their constituents. The chapter grounds it in intern James Stout's account of his manager greeting him with a detailed work plan and an explicit statement of confidence in his skills — which he says made him feel "empowered to do my job... with independence and pride" — and in consultant Patti Kozlovsky's account of deliberately thanking team members for what they contributed rather than commenting on gaps, which she says shifted the group from sniping over what wasn't done to spontaneously sharing resources and covering for one another. The book frames this as a general finding from positive organizational psychology: leaders who project genuine confidence in ability produce measurably higher performance than those who don't, independent of the underlying skill level.

## Why It Matters

This turns "believing in people" from a platitude into a mechanism with a feedback loop: expectation shapes behavior toward the person, behavior shapes their opportunity and effort, and outcome then confirms (or disconfirms) the original expectation. It matters anywhere one party's judgment of another's competence is visible and repeated — teaching, coaching, hiring, parenting, even algorithmic scoring systems that get acted on — because the judgment is never a neutral read of fixed ability; it is itself an input that moves the ability it claims only to measure.
