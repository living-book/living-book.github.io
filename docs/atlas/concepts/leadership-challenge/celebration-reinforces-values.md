# Public Celebration as Value Reinforcement

## Definition

"An organization produces most what it honors most." Kouzes and Posner treat celebration not as a morale nicety but as a communication channel: how and what a leader publicly celebrates tells everyone, more persuasively than any memo, which values and behaviors the organization genuinely rewards. This requires the celebration to be both public (visible beyond the individual recipient) and personal (the leader shows up and participates, not just authorizes it).

## In the Book

Chapter 11's central case is DeVry University's PRIDE awards program, where the awards ceremony is followed by a full day of community-building — presentations, an "I TEACH" video exercise, and a service project building bicycles for local kids — that DeVry executive Bill Hughson describes as making clear "what activities, behaviors, and outcomes the organization values." A second case, Seagate's monthly "Standing Ovations" sales calls run by executive Kurt Richarz, shows the mechanism at lighter weight: peers nominate colleagues via a simple form, the recipient's photo and accomplishment are featured live on the call, and Richarz personally thanks the nominators — a ritual he calls "one of my favorite parts of the call." The chapter frames this as satisfying a documented human need for social connection (citing the growth of social networking generally as evidence people seek belonging), arguing that celebrations "bond people together and connect us to shared values and myths" in ways that are especially load-bearing during stressful periods.

## Why It Matters

This separates two things usually bundled together — rewarding an individual and communicating a value to everyone else — and shows the second only happens if the first is done publicly and with the leader visibly present. It applies to any group trying to shift what its members actually optimize for: a metric only becomes real to people once its winners are celebrated in front of everyone, not merely paid or promoted quietly.
