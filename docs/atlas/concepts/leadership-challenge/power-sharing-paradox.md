# The Power-Sharing Paradox

## Definition

"Leaders accept and act on the paradox of power: you become more powerful when you give your own power away." Kouzes and Posner argue that people who feel powerless — ignored, second-guessed, denied real input — disengage, hoard whatever scraps of influence they hold, and default to political self-protection ("covering your backside," "passing the buck"). People who feel a genuine sense of control over their own work, by contrast, hold themselves personally accountable for results without being asked to.

## In the Book

Chapter 9 (Strengthen Others) collects parallel lists from thousands of respondents describing what makes them feel powerless ("I was given responsibility but no authority to hold others accountable," "someone else took credit for my hard work") versus powerful ("I was able to make decisions about key aspects of the project," "my manager publicly expressed great confidence in my ability"). The chapter's central case is Casey Mork's experience being handed real latitude on a project — "not supervision" — which he says produced "the most efficient means of collaboration" precisely because success and failure were visibly his group's own. The chapter also imports Liz Wiseman's distinction between "Multipliers," who invest in others' success while keeping ownership with them, and "Diminishers," who jump in, solve problems themselves, and create dependency by reminding people how much smarter the leader is.

## Why It Matters

This names a genuine paradox rather than a platitude: the intuitive move to protect one's authority (retain final say, review everything, be the one who fixes problems) is the move that shrinks a leader's actual influence, because it produces people who wait to be told rather than people who act. It applies to any system where one node's authority depends on the initiative of many others — management, open-source maintainership, coaching, parenting — because control that isn't shared caps the total capability of the system at whatever the controller can personally attend to.
