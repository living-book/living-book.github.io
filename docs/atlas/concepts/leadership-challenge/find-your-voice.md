# Find Your Voice

## Definition

Before a leader can align actions with words, they need words that are actually theirs. "Find Your Voice" is the discipline of explicitly articulating one's own guiding principles — not borrowing a mission statement, a mentor's philosophy, or a speechwriter's language — so that later consistency between word and deed is possible at all. Kouzes and Posner treat this as logically prior to credibility: "To walk the talk, you have to have a talk to walk."

## In the Book

Chapter 2 opens Model the Way by asking readers directly, "What is your leadership philosophy?" and insisting that if you can't answer it, you aren't ready to lead credibly. The chapter contrasts two cases. Alex made his values understood by translating them into his own words and grounding them in concrete situations, which let his team retain and act on them. Casey Mork describes the opposite: a manager at an internal startup who "never had a true voice," instead acting as "a simple conduit for mixed messages" from three disagreeing directors above him — with no personal stance on how the team's distinct customers should be treated differently, undefined values got quietly exploited by team members, and the venture failed to cohere. Founder Josh Fradenburg is cited for the reverse causality: "it was really the values that formed the organization, rather than the organization forming the values."

## Why It Matters

This concept locates the failure point one step earlier than most trust or communication advice: a leader who has not done the internal work of naming their own principles has nothing stable to be consistent with, so any consistency they display is accidental and any inconsistency is inevitable. It applies wherever someone represents a group's judgment under pressure — a manager relaying policy they don't believe in, a founder repeating investor language, a spokesperson reciting talking points — because borrowed language cannot survive the improvisation that hard situations demand.
