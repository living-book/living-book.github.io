# Information 5S

## Definition

5S is a Japanese organizational system (Sort, Straighten, Shine, Standardize, Sustain) that Markovitz adapts for knowledge workers as a way to organize information—both physical and digital—by retrieval frequency rather than topic. The goal is to make valuable information instantly findable while keeping low-value information out of sight, reducing the cognitive and time waste of searching.

The three-tier system: working files (used one or more times per week), reference files (used less than once per month), and archive files (legally required but never accessed). This is grounded in the Pareto principle: 80% of your work lives in 20% of your files; the other 80% of files are rarely touched.

## In the Book

Markovitz opens Chapter 2 with Allison, an anesthesiologist whose office was buried in paper. She wasn't directly unproductive at clinical work, but her office chaos prevented her from publishing research—her priority. She spent hours moving paper left to right, searching for reprinted articles, looking for lost supplies. Her frantic activity was "pretty much nothing but waste." Studies cited: Wall Street Journal (2007) found Chevron employees spent 1.5–3 days per month just searching for information; Delphi Group and Butler Group found employees spend one-quarter of work time looking for information.

The book translates 5S: **Seiri** (sort—throw out obsolete stuff), **Seiton** (straighten—arrange by frequency), **Seiso** (shine—keep clean), **Seiketsu** (standardize—consistent system), **Shitsuke** (sustain—maintain it). Markovitz compares 5S to the chef's *mise-en-place*—the organized prep station that lets the chef work with eyes closed, everything at arm's reach.

For files: working files go in a desk drawer for daily access. Reference files go in filing cabinets farther away. Archive files go to off-site storage. The same structure applies to digital folders (numbered "1 Working," "2 Reference," "3 Archive" to force sort order). Email is simpler: delete liberally; save important mail to one "processed mail" folder and search when needed.

Systemic 5S saves more: an IT department reduced 350 monthly reports to 37 by asking managers which they actually used. A nursing team at Covenant Health reduced documentation time from 6.1 hours per 12-hour shift by 40%, freeing three hours to spend with patients—the real value.

## Why It Matters

5S removes a hidden form of waste: the cognitive load of deciding where things go, remembering where they are, and the mental friction of clutter. Markovitz notes that poor organization doesn't just waste time—it "reduces productivity by as much as 15%, as measured by the number of days each month lost to searching." More subtly, it creates psychological burden: Allison felt embarrassed by her office, which made it harder to focus on important work. By making high-value information instantly accessible, you free mental capacity for the actual problem-solving and creation you're paid for.
