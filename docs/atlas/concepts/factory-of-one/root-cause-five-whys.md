# Root Cause Analysis: Five Whys

## Definition

The Five Whys is a technique from Toyota's problem-solving toolkit, adapted for personal work. When you encounter a recurring problem, ask "why" repeatedly—typically 5 times—to move from the symptom to the underlying cause. Each answer becomes the prompt for the next "why." This prevents the common trap of treating symptoms (working late) instead of root causes (poor process design).

## In the Book

Markovitz emphasizes in Chapter 5 that most people jump to answers without understanding the problem. "Society rewards answers, not questions," he notes. This is dangerous because solutions built on guesses fix symptoms, not root causes. A band-aid on a symptom doesn't improve anything; it just postpones the day of reckoning.

The book contrasts two approaches:
1. **Symptom fixing**: You're always working late. Solution: try to stay more focused. Still working late? Try a new app. Still failing? You're not disciplined enough.
2. **Root cause fixing**: You're always working late. Why? Because there's too much work. Why? Because you haven't said no to low-value work. Why? Because you don't know what your customers actually need. And so on.

The Five Whys dig toward the system that produced the problem, not the person's willpower or intelligence. Markovitz doesn't give an extended personal example of Five Whys in the text, but the method is implicit in his case studies: Allison's publication block wasn't solved by "try harder"; it was solved by 5S—addressing the root cause (chaos preventing focus), not the symptom (lack of time).

The book frames it this way: "All manufacturing plants have a quality control group at the end of the production line." These plants catch defects and trace them back to root causes. "In traditional batch-and-queue plants, the production line never stops" to investigate—they just accept the defects. Continuous improvement requires stopping, investigating, and fixing the cause.

For personal work, this means treating recurring problems as signals to investigate. If you're always missing deadlines on a certain type of work, the root cause is not "I'm lazy"; it might be "I haven't created a standard process for this work type" or "I don't understand the customer's definition of done" or "I don't have the tools to do this efficiently."

## Why It Matters

Markovitz's broader point is that high-achieving adults are rewarded for fast answers, so they've trained themselves to jump to solutions. But in a complex system (your work), surface solutions often fail because they didn't address the real problem. The Five Whys method breaks this reflex by forcing you to pause and investigate.

Root cause thinking also shifts responsibility from "I need more willpower" to "what about my system or process is broken?" This is liberating and more effective. A system that forces you to repeat work is a broken system, not a personal failing. Fix the system, and the problem goes away.
