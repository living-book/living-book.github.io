# Customer Value Identification

## Definition

Rather than defining your work by your job title or formal responsibilities, identify the specific customers you serve and what value they actually need from you. This shifts focus from tasks and deliverables to the genuine outcomes that matter to each stakeholder. For knowledge workers, this often reveals that real value differs sharply from the formal job description.

## In the Book

Markovitz opens by distinguishing between your "job" (the fancy title and formal requirements) and your "work" (your actual value-creating activities). A lawyer claiming business development was his priority, but finding himself enslaved to email and unable to access files, needed first to identify his actual customers (prospects, colleagues, internal leadership) and what each valued.

The book introduces the "customer value map" as a tool: listing customers at the top of lines with their desired value below. Markovitz's example shows a marketing director serving five customer groups: direct reports (wanting career guidance, not performance reviews), product development (wanting briefs and prototypes, not meetings), the executive team (wanting pricing and forecasts, not attendance at every planning session), and others. Once mapped, the work became visible: many activities looked productive but didn't deliver what customers actually paid for—HR training sessions, pointless meetings, shuffling papers.

The root insight: "What your customer has asked you for—whether that customer is a paying client, a colleague, your boss, or even yourself—is the starting point for defining value." Markovitz notes that one VP of sales didn't need the author at finance meetings to get the revenue forecast; he just needed pricing and margin data.

## Why It Matters

This reframes productivity from "getting more done" to "getting the right things done." It exposes waste that productivity systems miss: you can become very efficient at delivering something no one values. Mapping customer value unlocks what Markovitz calls "the freedom of focus"—once you see what customers genuinely need, you can eliminate activities that *look* productive but don't serve that need, recover hours that were lost to misaligned work, and concentrate on the leverage points that matter.
