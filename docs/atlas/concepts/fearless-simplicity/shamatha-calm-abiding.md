# Shamatha: Calm Abiding

## Definition

Shamatha is the practice of settling the mind into relaxed presence with a support (breath, visual object) or without support (resting in the bare awareness of the present moment). Unlike meditation practices that suppress thought, shamatha allows thoughts to arise naturally while maintaining gentle, non-distracted attention. It quiets the constant mental elaboration and gives the mind a chance to rest, creating the conditions for both bodhichitta (genuine compassion) and the recognition of mind essence (rigpa). Shamatha is not the final goal but essential preparation.

## In the Book

Tsoknyi Rinpoche teaches shamatha as the Buddha's foundational recommendation: "First, train in shamatha." He explains that "without calmness of mind, it is very hard to have a sense of delight. Without this sense of delight, there is no genuine compassion." The book offers practical guidance: shamatha with support means resting attention gently on an object (breath, image, or simple presence) with eighty percent focus on the object and twenty percent open awareness of everything else. Shamatha without support means remaining in the present moment without holding any particular object, simply not following past thoughts or planning future ones. He uses the metaphor of the mind as a "Conceptual First Bank" earning constant interest in the form of thoughts and emotions; shamatha is a different investment—occupying the mind with nowness instead of allowing it to be occupied with attachment and anger. He emphasizes that shamatha is "a skillful method" to calm a wild mind but can be mistaken for the goal; it can create a meditative state that is "occupied" (with hope and fear about maintaining the state) rather than truly free.

## Why It Matters

Shamatha addresses a practical necessity: most modern minds are too scattered, anxious, and exhausted to recognize anything subtle. By calming the constant mental agitation, shamatha creates the stability necessary for deeper recognition to occur. It also teaches the fundamental insight that relaxation, ease, and delight are not frivolous but essential to authentic spiritual path. Paradoxically, slowing down and settling into peace often accomplishes more than striving. This challenges the Western conditioning that equates spiritual seriousness with effort and tension. For practitioners, shamatha becomes a daily refuge and a doorway into increasingly stable recognition of the mind's nature.
