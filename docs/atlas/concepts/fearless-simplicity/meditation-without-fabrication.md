# Meditation Without Fabrication

## Definition

The highest form of Dzogchen meditation is not meditation at all in the conventional sense: it is the state of "not dwelling in any way whatsoever, and yet totally present throughout everything." This is the result of the sequence looking → seeing → letting be. It requires releasing the habit of trying, the need to achieve, and the subtle grasping that arises even from subtle efforts. The practice is simple but paradoxical: how can one maintain what cannot be maintained without fabricating? The answer lies in recognizing that fabrication is itself a distraction from what naturally is.

## In the Book

Tsoknyi Rinpoche teaches through the example of his father, Tulku Urgyen Rinpoche, whose meditation was so natural and continuous that he could be interrupted by visitors, conduct conversations, and make decisions without any break in his awareness. His room, though tiny, felt spacious because of his genuine openness. In contrast, artificially maintained meditation creates a "frozen state"—the practitioner becomes rigid, compartmentalized, and disturbed by interruption. The book stresses the "threefold motionlessness" (of body, speech, and mind) not as technique but as the natural consequence of not fabricating anything artificial. Once stability is attained in meditation posture, the form no longer matters; the practitioner can sit in any posture or position while remaining undistracted. The key teaching is that the moment we try to create something, we move away from the natural state. True maintenance is "not contriving," which paradoxically is accomplished by recognizing that contrivance is itself absent.

## Why It Matters

This distinction between manufactured meditation and the natural state transforms practice from effort into recognition. It dissolves the exhausting dualism of meditator versus meditation, observer versus observed. Practitioners freed from the need to maintain special states can function fully in complex daily life while remaining undistracted. This makes enlightenment compatible with ordinariness: there is nothing exotic or separate about the state, which is why a truly realized practitioner is indistinguishable from an ordinary person, except in their unshakeable presence and freedom regardless of circumstance.
