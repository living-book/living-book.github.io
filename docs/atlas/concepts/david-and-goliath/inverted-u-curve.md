# The Inverted-U Curve of Resources

## Definition

An inverted-U curve has three distinct zones that each obey a different logic: a left side where adding a resource clearly helps, a flat middle where adding more makes barely any difference, and a right side where adding still more starts actively making outcomes worse. Gladwell's point is that people default to reasoning as if they are always on the left side of the curve — as if class size, money, or punishment simply scale linearly with benefit — when in fact many important relationships are U-shaped, and the same intervention that helps at low levels can reverse and harm at high ones.

## In the Book

Chapter Two builds the curve around class size: Caroline Hoxby's research found real gains from shrinking Israel's oversized Maimonides-rule classes (upward of thirty-six students) down to twenty, but no measurable benefit from shrinking Connecticut classes already in the high teens to low twenties — different points on the same curve. Gladwell extends the same shape to parenting and wealth, where a psychologist describes wealthy parents who have moved "well past the point where money made things better" into a zone where unlimited resources make raising well-adjusted children harder, not easier. The idea returns in Chapter Seven on California's Three Strikes law: longer sentences reduce crime on the curve's left side (the 1969 Montreal police strike, where the total absence of law enforcement produced immediate looting and violence, shows the value of some deterrence), but criminologist Todd Clear's Tallahassee research found that once more than 2 percent of a neighborhood's residents are incarcerated, additional imprisonment starts increasing crime — the collateral damage of shattered families and returning, unemployable ex-convicts outweighs the incapacitation benefit.

## Why It Matters

The inverted-U curve is a general corrective to "more must be better" reasoning in any domain where a resource, intervention, or pressure is being scaled up — funding, staffing, enforcement, screen time, optimization effort. It reframes the practical question from "should we add more?" to "where on the curve are we right now?" — because the same lever that produces gains on the left side can produce harm on the right, and knowing which side you're on is a separate, empirical question from knowing the lever works at all.
