# The Remote-Miss Effect

## Definition

Psychiatrist J. T. MacCurdy, studying the London Blitz, divided a bombed population into three groups: the killed (for whom morale is irrelevant), the "near misses" — close enough to be wounded or traumatized by the blast — and the "remote misses," who heard the sirens and the bombers and the explosions but were untouched, block over. A near miss reinforces fear into lasting shock; a remote miss, repeated, produces "a feeling of excitement with a flavour of invulnerability" — the same category of danger builds trauma in one group and confidence in the other, depending entirely on proximity, not on exposure to danger itself.

## In the Book

Chapter Five uses wartime London to explain a paradox: British and German planners expected the Blitz to break civilian morale, but Londoners grew steadily calmer under nightly bombing across two and a half months. Gladwell cites diary entries — "Ever since we came out of the dug-out I have felt sure nothing would ever hurt us" and a woman who described being bombed as producing "such pure and flawless happiness" — and a laborer bombed out of his house twice who refused evacuation ("What, and miss all this?"). MacCurdy's explanation was arithmetic: with forty thousand dead across a metropolitan area of eight million, remote misses vastly outnumbered near misses, so the net psychological effect of the Blitz on the surviving population tilted toward emboldenment rather than trauma. The chapter ties this to the same book's theory of desirable difficulty — courage, MacCurdy argued, is "not something people already have" but something built by successfully surviving a fear they thought would break them.

## Why It Matters

The remote-miss effect explains why identical categories of adversity — job loss, a health scare, institutional upheaval — leave some people shattered and others visibly more confident, and it locates the difference in something measurable (distance from direct harm) rather than an unmeasurable trait like innate resilience. It's a caution against assuming any exposure to a threat is uniformly damaging, and a reason to distinguish, when designing interventions for people under stress, between shielding someone entirely and letting them survive a threat that stays at arm's length.
