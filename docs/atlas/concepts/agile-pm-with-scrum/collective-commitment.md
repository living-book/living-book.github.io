# Collective Commitment

## Definition

In Scrum, the team makes a single commitment at the start of a sprint: that it will do its best to turn the selected Product Backlog into a completed increment of product functionality by the sprint's end. This is not a prediction—it doesn't mean the team guarantees delivery—but a shared promise that the team, as a unit, is accountable for the outcome. Individual team members don't commit to isolated tasks; the team as a whole commits to a sprint goal.

## In the Book

Schwaber argues that collective commitment is the engine that powers self-organization. In the Lapsec case study, he notes: "For Scrum to work, the team has to deeply and viscerally understand collective commitment and self-organization." Early in that classified project, the team members were acting as individuals from different organizations investigating a new process, not as a unified team. When Schwaber gave them a hypothetical product backlog and a sprint time-box, something shifted: "The team members had self-organized and become a single cohesive team. The team had gone from being a separate group of individuals at a class to a team committed to finding a solution."

This shift is crucial because commitment, not compliance, drives high performance. When the team collectively commits to a sprint goal, team members help each other, work through impediments together, and re-prioritize their own work to support the team's goal. If instead individuals are assigned tasks and told to complete them, they do the assigned task and nothing more.

Schwaber notes that the term "commitment" is sometimes misunderstood. The team is not committing to a guaranteed forecast—it's committing to making its best effort and to being transparent about what can and cannot be accomplished. If the team initially committed to ten items and completes eight of high quality, it has fulfilled its commitment to do its best.

## Why It Matters

Collective commitment creates psychological safety and shared accountability. It distributes ownership across the team, so problems are addressed by the whole, not escalated up the chain. It also creates intrinsic motivation: people work harder on goals they helped define and committed to collectively than on tasks assigned to them. In high-complexity work where individual task assignment is impossible, collective commitment is the only accountability mechanism that works.
