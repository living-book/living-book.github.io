# Habit Stacking

## Definition

Habit stacking is a specific form of implementation intention: instead of pairing a new behavior with a time and place ("I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION]"), you pair it with a current habit that already runs reliably ("After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]"). Clear credits the method to BJ Fogg's Tiny Habits program and frames it as exploiting "the connectedness of behavior" — one habit naturally cues the next.

## In the Book

Clear opens the chapter by noting people rarely need reminders to do things they already do automatically — brushing teeth, tying shoes — because each existing habit is itself a cue for the next action in the sequence. He gives a working set of examples using the formula: "After I pour my cup of coffee each morning, I will meditate for one minute"; "After I take off my work shoes, I will immediately change into my workout clothes"; "After I sit down to dinner, I will say one thing I'm grateful for." Later in the book he layers "temptation bundling" onto the same formula (habit stacking + temptation bundling), pairing a needed habit with a wanted one in the same anchored sequence.

## Why It Matters

Habit stacking solves the "when/where" problem that causes most vague intentions ("I'll exercise more") to fail — it converts a floating goal into a placed trigger by borrowing the reliability of a behavior that's already automatic. The underlying move — chaining a new, fragile process onto an existing, stable one rather than inventing new triggering infrastructure from scratch — generalizes to any system design problem where you need a dependable activation signal: onboarding a new step into an existing workflow, or triggering an automation off an event that's already guaranteed to fire.
