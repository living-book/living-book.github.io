# The Two-Minute Rule

## Definition

Clear's Two-Minute Rule (credited to David Allen's similar-sounding but distinct "if it takes less than two minutes, do it now" rule from Getting Things Done) says a new habit should be scaled down to something doable in two minutes or less: not "read more" but "read one page," not "run a marathon" but "put on your running shoes." "You have to standardize before you can optimize" — a habit must be established before it can be improved.

## In the Book

Chapter 13 maps sample goals on a difficulty scale from very easy to very hard — running a marathon down to putting on running shoes; writing a book down to writing one sentence; earning a PhD down to opening your notes — and argues the two-minute version is the "gateway habit" that should actually be practiced, because the point is not to accomplish the task but to master showing up. Clear reports a reader who used the rule to lose over one hundred pounds by starting with a gym visit capped at a token amount of effort. He also offers a stricter variant for when the rule feels like a trick: do the activity for exactly two minutes and then stop — run, meditate, or study Arabic for two minutes only, with no extension allowed, treating the ritual itself as the whole practice rather than a bridge to more.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates the two things people conflate when starting something new — acquiring the habit (frequency, consistency) and performing the habit well (intensity, duration) — and insists the first must be solved before the second is worth optimizing. It applies wherever adoption is the bottleneck rather than execution: minimum viable versions of a process, a product's onboarding flow, or an organizational change — the lesson is to engineer the smallest possible unit of the desired behavior first and let scale come later, rather than trying to launch the full-strength version and having it collapse under its own initial friction.
