# The Habit Loop (Cue, Craving, Response, Reward)

## Definition

Clear's "four-step model of habits" breaks every habit into cue (a signal that predicts reward), craving (the motivational force — wanting the change in state the cue implies), response (the actual thought or action), and reward (the satisfaction of the craving, which teaches the brain the cue is worth acting on). He splits these into a problem phase (cue + craving: noticing something is wrong) and a solution phase (response + reward: acting to fix it and confirming the fix worked).

## In the Book

Chapter 3 lays out the model as "the backbone of every habit," running continuously and unconsciously — Clear calls it a feedback loop "continually scanning your environment, predicting what will happen next." He is explicit about the mechanics of intervention that the rest of the book builds on: eliminate the cue and the habit never starts; reduce the craving and there's not enough motivation to act; make the response too hard and it can't be performed; make the reward unsatisfying and there's no reason to repeat it. This four-part structure is what later becomes the organizing spine of the book's "Four Laws of Behavior Change" (make it obvious / attractive / easy / satisfying) — each law targets one stage of the loop.

## Why It Matters

Naming the four stages turns "I have a bad habit" from a moral failing into a diagnosable mechanism: which stage is actually broken or exploitable? This is the same shape as a stimulus-response-reinforcement loop found across behavioral design, product/UX "hook" models, and operant conditioning, but the book's contribution is packaging it as an actionable four-lever intervention checklist rather than a description alone — useful anywhere a recurring behavior (individual or organizational) needs to be started, stopped, or redirected.
