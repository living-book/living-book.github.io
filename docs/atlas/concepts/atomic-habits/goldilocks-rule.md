# The Goldilocks Rule

## Definition

The Goldilocks Rule states that "humans experience peak motivation when working on tasks that are right on the edge of their current abilities." Too easy and the brain gets bored and disengages; too hard and it gets anxious and gives up. Clear links this to the Yerkes–Dodson law from psychology, which places optimal arousal at the midpoint between boredom and anxiety, and to flow-state research finding peak engagement around tasks roughly 4% beyond current ability.

## In the Book

Chapter 19 traces the eighteen-year rise of comedian Steve Martin, who began performing to nearly empty clubs as a teenager with a one- or two-minute act and, over fifteen years of steady work, expanded to a twenty-minute set and eventually stadium tours — expanding his material "only by a minute or two" each year, keeping a few guaranteed-laugh jokes alongside new, riskier material so there were "just enough victories to keep him motivated and just enough mistakes to keep him working hard." Clear uses a tennis analogy to make the mechanism concrete: playing a four-year-old is boring (too easy), playing Roger Federer or Serena Williams is demoralizing (too hard), but playing an equal keeps you fully invested because winning is uncertain but achievable with real effort.

## Why It Matters

This concept explains why habits that stay static eventually lose their pull — once a behavior is fully mastered it drops below the motivational threshold — and why habits that scale too aggressively also fail, from the opposite direction. It supplies a domain-neutral calibration principle for anything requiring sustained engagement over years rather than a single burst: game and curriculum design (adaptive difficulty), employee development (stretch assignments), or personal skill practice — the target is not maximum challenge but a moving target held consistently just past the current edge of competence.
