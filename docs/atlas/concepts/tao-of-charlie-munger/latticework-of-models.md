# The Latticework of Models

## Definition

Munger's case against specialization-as-worldview: "Most people are trained in one model — economics, for example — and try to solve all problems in one way. You know the saying: 'To the man with a hammer, the world looks like a nail.'" The alternative isn't more depth in one field — it's breadth: "Just pick up key ideas from all the disciplines, not just a few, and you're immensely wiser than they are."

## In the Book

Chapter 117, "Narrow-Mindedness," gives the mechanism its bite with a real policy contradiction: David Ricardo's free-trade theory, applied without regard for other forces, has helped ship American jobs overseas and fuel unemployment — while Keynesian theory tries to fix that same unemployment by dropping interest rates and printing money. Two single-model experts, each holding a hammer, working against each other inside the same government. Chapter 136, "Multidiscipline," makes the positive case through Munger himself: a man who can discuss Darwin, Stephen Jay Gould's reading of Darwin, Einstein's unified field theory, Walter Bagehot on central banking, Newton and Leibniz's calculus, the Oppenheimer-Teller hydrogen bomb dispute, and E. O. Wilson's sociobiology "all in the same breath" — versus "a man who can play only the viola," however brilliant on that one instrument, who can never write a symphony.

## Why It Matters

This concept explains a specific failure mode that credentials don't protect against: deep expertise in one lens actively produces blind spots, because a well-trained mind reaches for its best tool by default, not its correct one. Holding several models from unrelated fields simultaneously — even shallowly — gives you a way to check one frame's answer against another's, and to notice when a problem is being mis-shaped to fit the tool you happen to own.
