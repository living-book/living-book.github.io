# Listening to Structure

## Definition

Listening to structure is a sixth level of listening—distinct from but complementary to listening for content. It focuses on how a coachee is choosing to express themselves and tell their story: the pattern of their thought, the narrative of their experience, and the underlying logic that organizes their beliefs and behaviors.

## In the Book

Dunbar introduces this concept as an advanced capacity for Clean Coaches. While most coaching training emphasizes listening to understand content—what the coachee is saying—Clean Coaches develop the capacity to simultaneously listen to the **structure** of the message: how the coachee is organizing and expressing themselves.

The structure of language represents the pattern of thought and the narrative of the person's story. It is "the glue that holds their logic and beliefs in place." By listening for the narrative, coaches gain clues as to where the story may have come from and where it may be going. The same content told with a different structure can mean something entirely different.

Dunbar emphasizes that Clean Coaches listen for patterns in how coachees are organizing their thoughts and behaviors. These structures are not random. Repeating patterns of thought are often revealed through the coachee's metaphors (covered in Chapter 9 and in connection with systems thinking in Chapter 10). Understanding structure helps the coach ask questions that disturb the pattern itself, not just the content.

This capacity is linked to systems thinking and is explored more deeply in Chapter 10 on Modelling and Systems Thinking—seeing how individual elements connect and relate rather than treating each thought or experience as isolated.

## Why It Matters

Most coaching addresses content: "What is your goal?" "What is the obstacle?" But the structure—the narrative itself—is often what keeps a person stuck. A coachee might have explored many different contents (different jobs, relationships, strategies) but maintain the same underlying narrative or pattern. By listening to structure, coaches can help coachees become aware of the pattern itself, which often opens the possibility for genuine shift. Understanding structure also reveals how someone thinks and learns, informing how the coach asks questions and what kinds of insights might be most accessible to this particular person.
