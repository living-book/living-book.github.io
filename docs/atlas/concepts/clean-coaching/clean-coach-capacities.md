# Clean Coach Capacities

## Definition

Clean Coach capacities are the internal qualities and ways of being required to coach cleanly—not technical skills but deeper capabilities that emerge from how the coach shows up in the relationship. These capacities cannot be learned as behavioral competencies alone; they require attitudinal and philosophical shifts.

## In the Book

Dunbar argues in Chapter Six that while coaching organizations list behavioral competencies, mastery requires internal capacities that "transcend rules and modify existing knowledge." The essential capacities for a Clean Coach include:

**Capacity for Acceptance** involves letting go of the need to get everything right, tolerating ambiguity and vagueness, and accepting yourself as "good enough." A coach with acceptance can be okay with being almost invisible in the session. This enables the coach to avoid the need to prove themselves or have the coachee perform for them.

**Capacity for Silence** means breaking the habit of interrupting and instead creating space for the coachee to finish their thoughts and continue exploring. Dunbar references Grove's concept of "response inviting gaps"—pauses that invite the coachee to say more. Silence allows the deeper wave of insight to surface; in most conversations, people provide an initial wave of information followed by a dip, then a second, greater wave if silence is maintained. Coaches uncomfortable with silence often miss this deeper insight.

**Capacity for Listening** goes beyond normal hearing to multiple levels simultaneously: listening for content, for emotion, for what's *not* being said, and for the structure of how the coachee is organizing their thoughts. Dunbar describes this as "listening to help the client understand"—listening attentively enough to select the most useful question next.

**Capacity for Reflection** involves two aspects: reflecting back the coachee's exact words (not paraphrasing) to show you have honored their experience, and reflecting on yourself, examining your own thoughts and feelings that arise during coaching to recognize your biases.

**Capacity to "Park" Your Own Thoughts** is the practice of noticing when your own opinions, assumptions, or reactions arise, acknowledging them without judgment, but not letting them drive where you go next. Dunbar references the ancient Greek concept of *epoche*—"bracketing" your thoughts, putting them to the side. Your own thoughts can be useful as indicators of where you need to gain more clarity about the coachee's perspective.

**Capacity to See the Bigger Picture** enables connecting disparate elements the coachee has shared to see relationships and patterns. This is linked to systems thinking and requires intelligence in the literal sense—"reading between the lines."

These capacities form an interconnected whole that allows a coach to be truly present with a coachee without imposing their own perspective.

## Why It Matters

Dunbar distinguishes between competencies (what you do) and capacities (who you are and how you show up). A coach can learn the technical questions of Clean Language but lack the internal capacities to use them cleanly. Without these capacities, the coach remains trapped in the directive bias. Conversely, developing these capacities changes not just how a coach coaches but how they live—they become more accepting, more present, more able to listen and reflect in all their relationships.
