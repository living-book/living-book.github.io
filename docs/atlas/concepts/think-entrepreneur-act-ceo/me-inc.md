# Me Inc.: Career as Personal Business

## Definition

Approach your career as a personal enterprise where you are simultaneously the business, the owner, and the CEO. Rather than waiting for organizational infrastructure to support your success, you take full responsibility for your own development, positioning, and resilience. This mindset shifts from "my employer should ensure my success" to "I am accountable for navigating my career path."

## In the Book

Jones establishes "Me Inc." in Chapter 2 as the foundation for thinking like an entrepreneur. She explains that successful professionals—whether in large organizations or independent practice—operate with an entrepreneurial mindset by understanding their organization as "a collection of smaller operations, all of which have to produce products or services that somehow support a shared mission."

Her own career illustrates this mechanism: as a young law partner, she initially expected the firm to manage her success. Her first day at a new firm was disastrous precisely because "nobody else was in charge of making me successful. That was my job." She describes realizing that at a Fortune 500 company, she had to "sell the CEO and the board on my expensive programs" to justify her budget, operating like an internal entrepreneur. The key insight: every role in any organization can be reframed as a small business that must demonstrate value.

Jones emphasizes that thinking entrepreneurially involves knowing your mission (the organization's), focusing on your customer (whoever depends on your work), understanding business basics (operations, budgeting, marketing), and being willing to practice failure. The "Me Inc." framing is not about literally starting a business; it's about adopting the mindset that your career security comes from your own capabilities and initiative, not from organizational loyalty.

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes career powerlessness into agency. Many workers experience "workplace blues" from a sense of powerlessness—waiting for promotion, dependent on a single boss, assuming the organization is responsible for their trajectory. Me Inc. redirects that energy: you are responsible for spotting trends, building your network, developing skills, and positioning yourself for opportunity. In volatile labor markets where traditional loyalty-based careers have disappeared, this mindset becomes essential not as nice-to-have initiative but as structural necessity. It makes you antifragile to organizational change because your security is portable and self-owned rather than granted by a single employer.
