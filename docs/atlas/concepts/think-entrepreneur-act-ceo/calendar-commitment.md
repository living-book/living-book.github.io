# Calendar as Commitment Tool

## Definition

Your calendar is not a record of meetings you happen to attend; it is the concrete expression of your priorities and values. By scheduling time for your most important work—and treating those blocks as inviolable commitments—you align your actual time allocation to your stated priorities. The mechanism is simple: when you don't schedule high-priority work, it gets crowded out by urgent requests from others. When you do schedule it and honor it, you ensure it gets done.

## In the Book

Jones develops this concept through Chapter 20 ("Does Your Calendar Support Your Success?") and Chapter 21 ("Prioritize Your Priorities"). She illustrates through Gina, a manager who felt anxious and overwhelmed despite working long hours. Gina had a habit of saying "yes" to meetings and commitments throughout the day, then wondering why her important projects weren't advancing. 

Jones had Gina keep a time log for a week. Gina discovered she often spent her morning hours (when her mind was sharpest) answering email and chatting with colleagues, while deferring her most challenging work to late afternoon when she was exhausted. She'd go home "feeling like she heard not a single kind word" and stay late into the night, unable to think straight.

The turning point came when Gina realized that "every time she was late or a no-show, another person might be inconvenienced or disappointed" and that "one reason I felt so anxious is that I was going through life letting people down."

Gina then rebuilt her relationship with her calendar through these practices: (1) Review your calendar frequently—first thing every morning, and throughout the day; (2) Match your calendar to your body clock—schedule cognitively demanding work during your peak hours; (3) Block time for high priorities—make "standing appointments with yourself" for Tier One work; (4) Coordinate with your to-do list—batch similar tasks; (5) Push for shorter meetings—don't accept hour-long defaults; (6) Resist distraction—close email and silence phone during priority blocks; (7) Align time to priorities—regularly ask if your calendar distribution matches your stated priorities; (8) Say "no" tactfully—before saying yes, ask "what will I give up?"

By dedicating her high-quality morning hours to her top projects and treating those blocks as non-negotiable, Gina "felt more in control and much less stressed." The mechanism is not complicated, but it requires discipline: your calendar is a contract with yourself.

## Why It Matters

The calendar reveals an often-hidden truth: people do not fail to achieve priorities because of bad intentions or laziness; they fail because they never scheduled the work. In knowledge work, where interruption is constant and requests feel legitimate, the default is reactivity. Your email inbox becomes your agenda. Your calendar becomes a record of other people's requests. But when you invert this—making your calendar a statement of your priorities—something shifts. It forces specificity ("What exactly is Tier One work for me?") and honesty ("Am I actually willing to protect this time?"). For leaders, this reveals that time management is not a nice-to-have; it is the primary mechanism by which you either live out your values or betray them.
