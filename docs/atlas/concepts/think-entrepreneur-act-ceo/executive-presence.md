# Executive Presence: Building the "It" Factor

## Definition

Executive presence is the elusive quality that makes people perceive you as a leader even before you speak. It combines multiple observable and behavioral elements: organization, posture, confidence, clarity of speech, grooming, decisiveness, and energy management. Presence is not charisma or charm; it's a signal that you are in control of yourself and capable of handling the challenges others bring to you. You build presence through changes in how you look, behave, think, and feel about yourself.

## In the Book

Jones introduces executive presence in Chapter 16 through the example of Ed*, a brilliant corporate attorney who was repeatedly overlooked for management despite exceptional technical work. The COO explained: "He'll always be valued as a talented technical lawyer, but we're not going to move him up. Ed just doesn't have executive presence." When asked why, she could not articulate it, but Jones knew: "The attorney could write memos like a dream. When asked a question, however, he seemed hesitant. He'd mumble, then he'd shuffle down the hall. He just didn't have 'It.'"

Presence is not invented; it is built through a checklist of concrete elements: (1) Leadership vision—having a clear sense of the attributes you admire in leaders and practicing them; (2) Organization—being on time, having your papers in order, meeting deadlines; (3) Physical appearance—grooming and professional dress; (4) Communication—ability to give a presentation, engage with audiences, speak clearly without hesitation or self-deprecation; (5) Vitality—energy level, sleep, fitness, calmness; (6) Confidence—managing your internal voice; (7) Body language—posture, facial expressions, microexpressions that convey control and readiness.

Jones illustrates with Lydia*, a perfectionist economist whose worry about details translated into an anxious demeanor. Her boss said Lydia didn't appear sure about her plans. She worked on presence by (1) defining her intent before meetings ("I'm going to raise point X and come across as interested and positive"), (2) using affirmations ("My plan is on target and I know it will work"), (3) practicing body awareness (noticing that she hunched when anxious), and (4) adopting Amy Cuddy's "power posing"—holding a V-shape with her arms before presentations. Her boss remarked on her transformation within months.

## Why It Matters

Presence reveals a mechanism often invisible in competence-focused cultures: perception is not separate from capability; it shapes who gets trusted with bigger challenges. A technically brilliant person without presence will plateau in advancement because promotions require others to see you as ready. Presence is also learnable—not an innate trait—which means anyone can improve it through deliberate practice on observable behaviors and mindset. For career changers, people in underrepresented groups, or those with self-doubt, building presence explicitly addresses the gap between actual capability and how that capability is perceived, collapsing the "impostor syndrome" that often stems not from lack of skill but from signals others misread.
