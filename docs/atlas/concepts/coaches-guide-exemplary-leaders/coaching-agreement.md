# Coaching Agreement Framework

## Definition

A coaching agreement is a formal or informal contract between coach and leader that clarifies expectations, logistics, confidentiality, and conflict resolution before substantive coaching work begins. It may be a simple verbal conversation or a written document, but its core function is to prevent mismatched assumptions about what coaching is, how it will work, and what each party owes the other.

## In the Book

Kouzes, Posner, and Biech present the "Coaching Partnership Agreement" as Task 1 of the 6Cs process. The agreement addresses:

**Organizational Context**: "What we believe the organization expects of us..."

**Logistics**: How often to meet, where, who initiates contact, how long each meeting lasts, what happens when meetings are cancelled, first meeting date.

**Expectations**: What the leader expects from the partnership; what the coach expects from the partnership.

**Working Guidelines**: Standards for timeliness, rescheduling, communication between meetings, documenting actions and progress, bringing closure.

**Initial Focus**: 1-4 objectives identified for the coaching relationship.

**Conflict Protocol**: "How we will address issues that may arise, e.g., conflicts between us; one of us is not getting what we think we should; one of us thinks we are mismatched; lack of progress; gender, racial or cultural issues."

**Confidentiality**: "We agree that our discussions are confidential" with signature and date.

The book notes that some coaching pairs complete this in one meeting, others over several; some document it in writing, others rely on conversation. Either way, the key is that both parties reach explicit agreement on basic expectations. The book emphasizes that coaches should avoid allowing leaders to perceive them as experts prescribing solutions; the agreement clarifies a collaborative role.

## Why It Matters

A clear agreement prevents the most common coaching failures: unmet expectations about time commitment, lack of progress measurement, misunderstandings about confidentiality, and coach-leader mismatch revealed too late. For organizations, a standard agreement template ensures consistency across multiple coaches and provides a documented record. For leaders, it demystifies coaching and sets psychological safety—knowing that discussions are confidential and that conflict can be discussed directly builds trust for the vulnerable work of behavior change. The agreement transforms coaching from something that happens *to* a leader into something the leader co-creates.
