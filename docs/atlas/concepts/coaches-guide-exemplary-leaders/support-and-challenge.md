# Support and Challenge Balance in Coaching

## Definition

Support and challenge is a relational equilibrium coaches maintain to keep leaders moving toward goals. Support alone breeds comfort without growth; challenge alone breeds defensiveness or burnout. Effective coaches calibrate both simultaneously, adjusting the balance based on the leader's readiness and the difficulty of the goal.

## In the Book

Kouzes, Posner, and Biech define this as a core competency: "Support and Challenge the Leader to Stay Action-Oriented." They provide concrete examples of each:

**Support** takes the form of: brainstorming ideas for action; connecting the leader with other leaders; role-playing scenarios before action; celebrating key accomplishments.

**Challenge** takes the form of: soliciting ideas from the leader to create better processes; confronting missed deadlines; daring the leader to hold themselves to higher standards; urging the leader to take on difficult situations.

The book emphasizes that this balance is dynamic. Coaches must determine "what is compelling enough to move your leaders to do something," recognizing that leaders are being asked to stop long-standing behaviors and try uncomfortable new ones. During setbacks (the book's chapter on "Coaching When Things Go Awry"), support becomes more visible—making availability clear, breaking overwhelming tasks into smaller pieces, helping navigate organizational politics. Yet even then, coaches "hold your leader accountable even during setbacks, to make the point that these are a part of an organization's life cycle."

The book notes that coaches recognize "readiness for coaching" in part by how a leader responds to this dynamic—is the leader willing to try new behaviors and accept stretching challenges, or do they retreat?

## Why It Matters

This concept articulates the paradox of coaching: it requires simultaneous empathy and rigor. Leaders need to feel seen and supported to risk trying new behaviors, but they also need pressure to overcome resistance and inertia. The balance prevents coaching from becoming either cheerleading (support without growth) or cruelty (challenge without care). In organizational contexts, this framework helps coaches—especially internal coaches—avoid being captured as either therapists or enforcers. It's a third way: demanding, caring accountability.
