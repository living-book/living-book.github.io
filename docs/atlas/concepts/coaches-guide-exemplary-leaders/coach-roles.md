# The COACH Model: Five Coaching Roles

## Definition

The COACH model identifies five core roles that coaches assume throughout an engagement, each with distinct functions. Coaches are not one-role practitioners; they shift between roles based on the situation, the leader's needs, and the stage of coaching.

## In the Book

Kouzes, Posner, and Biech present five roles organized as COACH (mnemonic):

**Consultant**: Creates an environment conducive to personal and professional growth; presents all sides of decisions; focuses on individual needs aligned with organizational needs; identifies obstacles; interprets 360-degree feedback while avoiding personal bias; provides feedback on how the leader fits the organization.

**Orchestrater**: Expands the leader's professional network; recommends learning opportunities; introduces new experiences; schedules uninterrupted time to discuss progress and next steps; acts as a catalyst to help the leader achieve objectives.

**Advisor**: Provides guidance, support, and information; answers questions; provides insight into organizational needs, culture, and other leaders; uses experience to help navigate decisions.

**Challenger**: Asks thought-provoking questions that push the leader to stretch; confronts avoidance or lack of progress; dares the leader to hold themselves to higher standards; urges the leader to take on difficult situations; solicit ideas from the leader rather than imposing them.

**Helper**: Supports the leader through setbacks; shows availability during stressful periods; listens without judgment; helps break overwhelming tasks into manageable pieces; may help navigate organizational politics and "white water" of change.

The book emphasizes that this is a "catch-all list" showing the breadth of coaching roles. Coaches assume different roles at different moments; early engagement may require more Advisor role (directing), while mature relationships shift toward Challenger (pushing) and Orchestrater (expanding networks).

## Why It Matters

The COACH model gives coaches permission to be multi-functional rather than monolithic, and it gives leaders a language for what to expect. It also prevents role-confusion (when a leader mistakes a coach for a boss, therapist, or friend). Organizations can use the model to calibrate coaching assignments: external coaches may more naturally take Consultant and Orchestrater roles, while internal coaches may emphasize Advisor roles due to organizational knowledge. The framework also clarifies that coaching is not one thing—it's five overlapping functions that mature coaches balance.
