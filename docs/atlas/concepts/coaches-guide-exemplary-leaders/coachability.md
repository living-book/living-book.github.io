# Coachability: Readiness for Coaching

## Definition

Coachability is the capacity and willingness of a leader to benefit from coaching, assessed through observable characteristics across five dimensions: self-understanding, communication ability, self-starter orientation, openness to feedback, and self-assurance.

## In the Book

Kouzes, Posner, and Biech identify "coachable" leaders—those "who love to learn" and are "open to many ideas and new concepts"—and catalog specific traits across five domains:

**Self-Understanding**: Know themselves well, ask for help when needed, reflect on situations, describe preferred learning styles, capitalize on strengths and admit to weaknesses.

**Good Communicators**: Read others well, listen well, willing to ask questions.

**Self-Starters**: Motivated to try new ideas, act on recommendations, learn from observing others, set and achieve goals, identify innovative solutions.

**Appreciate Feedback**: Willing to discuss problems, value challenges and recommendations, solicit feedback and appreciate it whether correct or not.

**Self-Assured**: Willing to be vulnerable before mentors/coaches, desire candor and truth, embrace change, persistent, take risks and challenge themselves, discover their own resources.

The book also identifies readiness questions for the coach to ask at the initial meeting: "How does your leader reply to feedback? How do they respond to your ideas? How interested do they appear about changing behaviors?" Positive, open, excited responses signal readiness; defensive, excuse-filled, or reluctant responses are red flags.

Importantly, the book notes that readiness is not a binary first-meeting decision: "Don't make the decision about whether your leader is ready for coaching based on your first meeting only." First-time jitters may create initial resistance, so coaches should revisit readiness assessment across multiple meetings.

## Why It Matters

Coachability assessment prevents coaching assignment to leaders who will resist or disengage, which wastes resources and damages coaching credibility. It also identifies where preparation is needed: leaders who are not yet ready can be brought forward through preliminary work. The structured traits provide coaches with non-subjective selection criteria and give organizations a way to segment leaders into coaching pipelines by readiness level, targeting intensive coaching to those most likely to succeed.
