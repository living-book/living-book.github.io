# Active Listening in Coaching

## Definition

Active listening in coaching is a disciplined practice of listening for both what a leader says (content) and what they mean beneath it (intent), while managing one's own biases and distractions. It involves specific techniques—paraphrasing, reflection, silence, open-ended response—that signal understanding and help the leader think more deeply.

## In the Book

Kouzes, Posner, and Biech identify listening as "one of the most critical coaching skills—probably one of the top two or three." The book distinguishes between passive (one-way) and active (two-way) listening. Passive listening is appropriate when a leader wants to "air a gripe, vent frustration, or express an opinion" without immediate response—the coach serves as a sounding board. Active listening involves verbal feedback tools.

The book provides a competency self-assessment for coaches covering eight dimensions:

- Listen for main points (focus on key ideas, make mental outline)
- Listen for the intent (identify meaning beyond words, observe non-verbal messages)
- Listen for the entire message (feeling and fact, emphasis, emotion-filled words)
- Resist distractions (eliminate phone calls, focus on facial expressions)
- Use excess thinking time appropriately (don't let mind wander while waiting)
- Withhold judgment (limit emotional reactions, avoid premature conclusions)
- Prevent personal biases from interfering (manage hot buttons and prejudices)
- Summarize and paraphrase often (rephrase ideas, check understanding)

The book also teaches "what to listen for": a dual-channel analysis where coaches listen for positive intents (willingness to move, eagerness, flow of ideas, hope, acceptance of weaknesses) and negative intents (fears, reluctance, defensive reactions, self-doubt). Coaches use a checklist to identify these undertones, which then guide whether support or challenge is needed.

Specific techniques include: paraphrasing ("So, the way you see this is..."), reflection ("I hear that you're frustrated about..."), encouraging ("Can you tell me more about that?"), and capitulation (strategic silence).

## Why It Matters

Active listening is the foundation for trust and psychological safety in coaching. Leaders who feel truly heard are more willing to be vulnerable about struggles and to hear difficult feedback. The practice of listening for intent rather than just content prevents coaches from missing the real issue—a leader might describe a technical problem when the actual block is interpersonal or political. For coaches, it's a counterintuitive discipline: they must resist the urge to advise or prescribe, and instead use listening as a tool to help the leader discover. This mirrors the shift from expert consultation to partnership coaching. At scale, it teaches leaders to listen the same way to their own teams, creating a culture of genuine engagement.
