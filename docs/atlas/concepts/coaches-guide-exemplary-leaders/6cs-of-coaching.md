# 6Cs of Coaching: A Process Framework

## Definition

The 6Cs of Coaching is a repeatable, six-stage process model that structures the coaching journey from initial agreement through outcome celebration. The stages—Clarify, Create, Collect, Construct, Collaborate, and Complete—form an upward spiral where leaders gain increasing skill and coaches progressively raise expectations and introduce more complex challenges.

## In the Book

Kouzes, Posner, and Biech present the 6Cs as "a failsafe coaching model" that provides coaches with a roadmap from start to finish. The framework consists of:

1. **Clarify the Agreement**: Build initial relationship, recognize leader readiness, establish coaching agreement, and confirm coach-leader match
2. **Create a Partnership**: Model good partnership skills, balance responsibility and accountability between coach and leader
3. **Collect and Analyze Data**: Gather LPI results and supplementary data, provide feedback to the leader
4. **Construct a Development Plan**: Generate developmental options, create action plans with SMARRT objectives
5. **Collaborate and Challenge**: Maintain forward motion through ongoing support and challenge, monitor action plan
6. **Complete and Celebrate**: Evaluate outcomes and recognize progress

The book frames these as tasks that "naturally cross over and blend into each other." While presented sequentially for clarity, they operate as an upward spiral—as the leader progresses, the coach increases complexity and raises expectations. This contrasts with linear models; the relationship itself becomes the foundation that strengthens throughout the cycle.

## Why It Matters

The 6Cs provides a replicable structure that prevents coaching from becoming ad hoc or relationship-dependent. By institutionalizing the coaching process into defined stages with clear entry/exit criteria, organizations can scale coaching impact, train multiple coaches consistently, and measure progress against a shared framework. This is particularly valuable for internal coaching programs where consistency across multiple coaches matters, and for leaders new to coaching who need scaffolding rather than intuitive practice.
