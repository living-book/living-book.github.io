# Organizational-Wide Commitment Required for Agile

## Definition

A common misconception is that agility is purely a development methodology—something the engineering team adopts and implements. In reality, successfully becoming more agile requires commitment and transformation across the entire organization: business stakeholders, product owners, quality assurance, project management, leadership, and organizational culture. Business users must learn a new way of working and understanding progress; they must see the "dirty laundry" of iterative development (unfinished work, mistakes, rework) rather than having problems hidden until final delivery. Without this broad-based commitment, agile initiatives fail because development teams cannot improve what they don't control.

## In the Book

Cobb identifies this as a "quite common misconception on the business side"—that "if we can just get our development organization to be more agile and speed up the way they do things, it will solve all our problems." The truth is that becoming more agile "impacts much more than just the development side of the organization—it requires a broad-based commitment from the business side of the organization to work in a close, collaborative partnership with the development side of the organization, and it may also require some major shifts in organizational culture and thinking to make that work."

He quotes a Sapient white paper on common pitfalls: "The business stakeholders involved must learn the process as it is very different for them...if this group is not brought into the fold, there will be major disconnects...Once educated, they will be able to see the benefits of being able to direct the development as it progresses. The business also needs to clearly understand the expectation that it will also be frustrating for them to see the 'dirty laundry' being aired each iteration: unfinished work, team mistakes, and other issues that are often hidden in a methodology with long breaks between business reviews."

He further states: "Migration toward an agile approach without substantial sponsorship and long-term commitment from the business is probably doomed to failure. In many cases, the business side of the organization should take a major leadership role in driving the change that is needed."

## Why It Matters

This concept prevents organizations from treating agile adoption as a technical or development-only initiative. It explains why many agile transformations fail: not because the technical practices are wrong, but because business stakeholders are not aligned, trained, or empowered to work as true partners. It also clarifies that agile is not about imposing a new methodology on development teams; it is about fundamental organizational change that requires commitment from leaders. The concept applies to any organizational transformation effort: change that requires only one function to change, while others remain unchanged, will fail because the changed function has no authority over the constraints that bind it.
