# Influence Outcomes: Commitment, Compliance, Resistance

## Definition

Yukl identifies three discrete outcomes from leadership influence attempts. Commitment occurs when the target person makes great effort to implement the request or decision effectively, including initiative and persistence in overcoming obstacles—essential for complex, difficult tasks. Compliance means the target person will carry out the request but apathetically, making minimal effort without genuine conviction that the decision is sound—sufficient for simple, routine work. Resistance involves active opposition: refusal, attempts to persuade the leader to withdraw the request, escalation to higher authority, delay, or sabotage. Success is not binary; it depends on which outcome is contextually appropriate.

## In the Book

Yukl emphasizes that a single influence attempt affects not only the immediate outcome but the interpersonal relationship and how the leader is perceived (ethical, trustworthy, competent). Resistance can paradoxically be valuable if it catches a serious flaw in the leader's plan before implementation. The chapter clarifies that commitment is not always necessary—for routine orders, compliance may be all the leader needs, making quick, directive communication efficient. However, for strategic initiatives, innovation implementation, or tasks requiring creativity and problem-solving, compliance without commitment is insufficient; the target person's apathy becomes a bottleneck. Different influence tactics (rational persuasion, consultation, coalition building, appeals to shared values) are empirically associated with different outcomes. For example, consultation and joint decision-making increase likelihood of commitment, while coercive or unilateral tactics more often produce mere compliance or resistance.

## Why It Matters

This framework prevents confusing tactical victory with strategic success. A manager might use legitimate authority to force compliance on a complex change initiative, appearing to have succeeded until implementation falters due to passive resistance or lack of initiative. It introduces precision: "getting buy-in" is not a single achievement but a specific outcome (commitment) distinct from bare compliance or apparent resistance. The framework also reveals why influence tactics matter: a sophisticated leader chooses tactics matched to both the task requirement (routine vs. complex) and the desired outcome (compliance sufficient, or commitment necessary). This explains why some leaders succeed across diverse contexts while others fail when context shifts—their default tactics may have worked for compliance-level tasks but fail for commitment-level challenges.
