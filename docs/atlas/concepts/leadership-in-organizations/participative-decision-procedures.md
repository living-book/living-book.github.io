# Participative Decision Procedures: Consultation and Delegation

## Definition

Yukl describes decision procedures as methods for determining who influences the decision. On a continuum from no influence by others to high influence, four canonical types are: autocratic (leader decides alone), consultation (leader solicits input then decides), joint decision (leader and others decide together with equal influence), and delegation (leader gives authority and responsibility to an individual or group). The choice of procedure affects not only decision quality and acceptance but also subordinate skill development and satisfaction. Each procedure is contextually appropriate; the goal is matching procedure to situation, not defaulting to either participative or directive approaches.

## In the Book

Yukl emphasizes that participative leadership is not inherently superior but offers four potential benefits: higher decision quality (others contribute relevant information), higher decision acceptance (participants identify with solutions), more satisfaction with the process (procedural justice), and skill development (delegation builds capability). These benefits are not automatic—they depend on "situational variables" including task complexity, degree of conflict, time pressure, and subordinate knowledge and competence. Consultation is appropriate when the leader has legitimate authority but subordinates have valuable information. Joint decision works when consensus is feasible and the issue affects multiple people. Delegation is essential for developing subordinates, but creates risks if the subordinate lacks capability. The book clarifies that formal procedures (a scheduled meeting for consultation) are different from actual influence—a manager may appear to consult while ignoring suggestions, or may ask subordinates to decide while making clear what the "right" choice is, causing subordinates to suppress initiative.

## Why It Matters

This framework prevents the false choice between "participative (good)" and "directive (bad)" leadership. A CEO who consults peers before major decisions might be wise; a manager who delegates a routine, time-critical task to an inexperienced subordinate is negligent. The framework also explains why implementation fails despite apparent "buy-in"—if participation was consultation on predecided solutions rather than genuine joint decision, the accepted solution may be inefficient or impossible to implement. For subordinates, the procedure affects psychological outcomes: delegation creates responsibility and skill growth, consultation creates voice and respect, joint decision creates ownership, but all require genuine influence (not theater) to be motivating. The framework also reveals that matching procedure to situation is a learned skill; leaders must diagnose task complexity, subordinate capability, time pressure, and value consensus before choosing the procedure.
