# Task, Relations, and Change Behavior Taxonomy

## Definition

Yukl's three-category framework classifies the primary dimensions of leadership behavior by their organizational purpose. Task-oriented behavior focuses on accomplishing work efficiently and reliably. Relations-oriented behavior builds mutual trust, cooperation, and team commitment. Change-oriented behavior facilitates organizational adaptation by scanning the environment, articulating vision, and implementing innovation. All three are necessary for effective leadership; their relative emphasis varies by situational demands.

## In the Book

The task-relations distinction originates in Ohio State University research (1950s), with evidence that task and relations behaviors were "broadly defined meta-categories" that subordinates consistently used to perceive supervisor behavior. Yukl extends this to include change-oriented behavior as a distinct third meta-category, established through research in Sweden and the U.S. (Ekvall & Arvonen, 1991; Yukl, 1999). The book provides concrete examples: task-oriented behaviors include clarifying roles, setting performance goals, monitoring progress; relations-oriented behaviors include supporting subordinates, recognizing contributions, coaching; change-oriented behaviors include monitoring external threats, articulating compelling visions, encouraging innovation. Yukl emphasizes that these categories are not mutually exclusive—consultation with team members about an action plan can improve task efficiency, human relations commitment, and innovation quality simultaneously.

## Why It Matters

This taxonomy decouples leadership effectiveness from personality or style, instead identifying specific behaviors and their organizational targets. It prevents false dichotomies (task vs. people) by showing that effective leaders need all three dimensions. The framework makes clear that "high-high" leadership (concerned with both people and production) is not about using every task and relations behavior indiscriminately, but about choosing the specific behaviors relevant to each situation. This reframes development: leaders can learn discrete behaviors rather than trying to shift personality. It also explains why leaders who succeed in one context may fail in another—change environments demand more change-oriented behavior, while stable operations may need less.
