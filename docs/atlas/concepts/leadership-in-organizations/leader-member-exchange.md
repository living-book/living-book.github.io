# Leader-Member Exchange: Dyadic Relationships

## Definition

Leader-member exchange (LMX) theory describes how relationships between a leader and individual subordinates evolve over time and take distinct forms. Some relationships involve only casual, formal exchange of work for pay. Others develop into higher-quality alliances characterized by mutual trust, influence, loyalty, and shared objectives. The quality of these dyadic relationships directly affects individual subordinate motivation, commitment, and performance. Leader behavior is not uniform across subordinates; it varies based on the history of exchange and the quality of each relationship.

## In the Book

Yukl emphasizes that real leaders seldom have only a single subordinate, yet understanding dyadic dynamics is essential because most leaders' influence operates through individual relationships with direct reports. LMX theory reveals that leaders often develop different relationships with different subordinates. High-quality exchanges involve more consultation, support, mentoring, and access to information; low-quality exchanges are more formal and transactional. The quality of relationships affects influence effectiveness—requests from a trusted leader with whom a subordinate has high-exchange quality are more likely to generate commitment, while requests from a distant superior produce only compliance. LMX also explains attributional biases: leaders form impressions of subordinates' competence and motivation based on early interactions, and these impressions then shape future behavior, creating self-fulfilling prophecies (high-performer subordinates receive more coaching and opportunity, confirming their capability; low-performer perceptions lead to less investment, confirming low performance).

## Why It Matters

This concept breaks the monolithic "leadership style" assumption—the same leader may be highly supportive with one subordinate and directive with another, and both may be appropriate for the different relationship histories and competence levels. It explains performance variance: two equally capable people under the same manager may perform very differently based on relationship quality. For leaders, it highlights the danger of early misclassification—if a leader incorrectly attributes a subordinate's early mistakes to low ability rather than task novelty, the leader provides less development, the subordinate improves slowly, and both interpret this as confirmation of initial judgment. For organizations, LMX quality predicts turnover, engagement, and performance more reliably than average leadership style. The framework also reveals that "fairness" is not uniform treatment but appropriate treatment based on individual exchange history and contribution.
