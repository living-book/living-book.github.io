# Position and Personal Power

## Definition

Yukl distinguishes power sources into two broad categories. Position power flows from formal organizational roles and includes legitimate authority, control over resources and rewards, punishment authority, information access, and ecological control of the physical work environment. Personal power derives from individual attributes and relationships: task expertise (expert power) and admiration/loyalty (referent power). Real leadership influence depends on the interaction of both types—formal authority without credibility fails, and expertise without positional standing lacks enforcement mechanisms.

## In the Book

Yukl systematically catalogs power types drawn from French & Raven (1959) and extends them. Legitimate power rests on organizational members' implicit agreement to comply with authority in exchange for membership benefits, weakened if a leader deviates from accepted selection procedures. Reward power depends on target perception of the leader's willingness to deliver promised resources, not just formal control—a shabby-dressed millionaire may be disbelieved while a con artist gains credit. Coercive power over subordinates has historically declined, though it remains significant in military and political contexts. Expert power requires both actual expertise and perceived credibility; expertise vanishes once others learn the technique or the problem is solved. Referent power through personal identification is strongest but has limits—excessive requests exploit the relationship and undermine it. Information power stems from access to vital data and controlling its distribution; leaders often use it to interpret events and maintain dependence. Ecological power involves redesigning work, workflow, and the physical environment to alter behavior indirectly. Position power (legitimate, reward, coercive, information, ecological) and personal power (expert, referent) are "distinct categories depending on whether they are derived primarily from opportunities inherent in a person's position in the organization, or from attributes of the agent and the agent-target relationship."

## Why It Matters

This framework reveals that managers cannot rely on positional authority alone—legitimacy erodes without demonstrated competence or trustworthiness. It explains why high-ranking officials sometimes fail to influence peers or superiors (position power doesn't cross hierarchical lines as effectively), and why technical experts can wield influence despite low formal rank. The distinction also clarifies why leaders may gain or lose power: expertise becomes obsolete if others learn it, but position power persists regardless of performance. For design: understanding which power source is appropriate for each type of influence attempt increases effectiveness. A request to a subordinate backed by reward power may generate compliance but resentment; the same request backed by expert justification and referent trust generates commitment.
