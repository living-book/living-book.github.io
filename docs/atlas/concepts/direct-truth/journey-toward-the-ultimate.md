# The Journey Toward the Ultimate

## Definition

A person who has achieved everything they set out to achieve and still feels aimless has not run out of goals — they have never been on "a Journey toward the Ultimate," meaning a single pursuit total enough that "upon reaching it, one becomes satisfied for the rest of his life... he becomes life itself." What distinguishes such a journey isn't its object (money, meditation, a craft, God) but the totality of devotion to it: "devote means to allocate in its entirety." A person who "dabbles" — partial effort spread across many things, or effort undertaken as an obligation — "suffers endlessly and fails," while one who devotes the whole of himself "is due for an ultimate boon."

## In the Book

The chapter "I Have Already Achieved Everything. What Do I Do for the Remainder of My Life?" is built on a real case: a person with unlimited money and philanthropic causes to occupy himself still reports "an aimlessness." The Answerer diagnoses this as a mismatch between activity and devotion — "we are first and foremost a piece of life. And this life seeks to live... The way in which man has attempted to make it bloom has not worked" — and tests the diagnosis by asking whether an additional billion dollars, or meditation pursued "more of a practice," would satisfy him; both get "No." In the following chapter, "How Is Life to Be Lived?," the same structure appears from a different angle: trying to "live" a life is compared to jumping onto a moving train — bruising, endless, method-chasing — while devoting one's life to something replaces that chase entirely: "if he devotes the whole of his life to something, he is due for an ultimate boon." The object of devotion is explicitly said to be near-arbitrary — St. Francis and Meera devoted themselves to God and "lived in complete freedom," but Gupta is careful to strip out the trappings: "I did not say pray. I did not say worship... But to devote the whole of oneself to god or to any other endeavor takes one away from oneself."

## Why It Matters

This separates two things usually treated as one: how meaningful an activity's content is, and how total the commitment to it is. It suggests that chronic aimlessness after achievement is not solved by finding a "better" or more virtuous goal, but by finding the one thing a person can devote themselves to without reservation — and that the felt difference between a life that exhausts a person and one that satisfies them is the completeness of the devotion, not the prestige of the object.
