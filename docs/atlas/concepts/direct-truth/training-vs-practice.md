# Training vs. Practice

## Definition

Gupta draws a hard line between "practice" and what he calls "Siddha Training." Practice is repetition: it "leads to meager and incremental improvement... it becomes work. Over time, it saps one's inspiration. It maintains more than it transforms." Training starts differently — "it begins with a vision. A precise determination of who one seeks to become" — and proceeds by discovering the actual, individual-specific truth of what gets a person from where they are to that vision, rather than applying a generic method or grinding out hours.

## In the Book

The distinction surfaces in the chapter "How Do I Become a Legend?," triggered by the Questioner's assumption that "practice" is the next step after wanting something badly enough. The Answerer rejects the word outright and substitutes "Siddha Training," explaining that practice "becomes work" and erodes inspiration rather than building it, while training "must transform" — and crucially, "I cannot tell you the method. For there is no method. Each human being is his own unique universe." The book then attacks the "10,000 hours" framework directly: "The reason that it supposedly takes '10,000 hours' and decades to become what one wishes to become is because one uses a bicycle rather than a locomotive. If you do not know the Truth, you must take the long and arduous road." It names "hard work," "pain vs gain," and "striving" as myths standing in for that missing truth, and observes that "even the best coaches and institutions of society know only of becoming a 'practitioner.' They have no concept of becoming a Master."

## Why It Matters

This reframes the choice in any skill-development context as not "how much should I practice" but "do I actually know the individual-specific truth of what moves me toward this vision, or am I substituting volume of repetition for that missing knowledge." It's a direct challenge to generic, hours-based models of mastery: the claim is that repetition without the right individualized insight caps out at "practitioner," and that the gap between practitioner and master is closed by a different kind of knowledge, not more of the same activity.
