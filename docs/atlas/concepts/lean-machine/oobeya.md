# Oobeya (The Wall)

## Definition

Oobeya (Japanese for "big room") is a shared physical space where the status of every project, resource assignment, and bottleneck in a portfolio is laid out visually — traditionally with Post-it Notes and hand-drawn charts — so anyone can walk up and see how the system is doing without asking anyone else. Its power comes partly from what it isn't: unlike a speech, a status report, or a CAD model, a wall of notes is not exclusionary by authority, medium, or technical skill — anyone can read it, and anyone can move a note.

## In the Book

Chapter 15 tells the story of "the wall" almost as an accident: Oosterwal's team, guided by Takashi (who first piloted the oobeya process in an engine plant, since factory floors already used visual management), started experimenting in a basement hallway of the Harley-Davidson technical center, initially just to solve one resource-allocation puzzle — figuring out who should be working on which red- and yellow-flagged project. As the team realized how useful seeing everything laid out was, the wall grew and its purpose shifted from a narrow fix to a general tool for understanding the whole development system. It stayed hidden for months until stylist Willie G. Davidson — who, the book notes, understood the emotional power of visual imagery better than anyone at the company — observed the team working quietly for weeks and then insisted the wall be moved out of the basement to a prominent, central location: "This work is too important to be hidden... it needs to be a focal point."

## Why It Matters

Oobeya answers a specific coordination problem the book raises earlier: manufacturing has tangible, visible flow (you can see the line stop), but product development is intangible — you cannot see that the process has "stopped flowing." A big-room, low-tech visual space converts an invisible information-work system into something anyone can inspect, cutting the need for status meetings and giving every contributor, not just managers, a shared, current picture of where the real problems are. The pattern transfers to any coordination problem where progress is otherwise invisible — a comms plan, a research pipeline, a cross-team launch.
