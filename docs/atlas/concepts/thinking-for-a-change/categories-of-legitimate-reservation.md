# Categories of Legitimate Reservation

## Definition

The Categories of Legitimate Reservation (CLR) are a named set of ways any claim of cause-and-effect can legitimately be challenged, built from three fundamental questions: does the entity actually exist (Entity Existence), does the cause really produce the effect (Causality Existence), and does the statement mean what the speaker and listener both think it means (Clarity). Scheinkopf frames the discipline as replacing our default habit — collecting evidence to prove ourselves right — with actively seeking evidence that would force us to be wrong. "Any claim of cause–effect should be able to pass three tests: 1. Verification that what we say exists does exist. 2. Validation of the relationships between causes and their effects. 3. Agreement that what we say reflects what we mean."

## In the Book

Chapter 4 opens with the author's own cautionary story: she and her husband decided to relocate across the country, chasing a good school district and a promising local client base, and spent their search "justifying that decision" rather than rigorously checking it — dismissing friends' and relatives' doubts instead of treating them as free hints toward hidden assumptions. Three years and real financial and family costs later, they moved back. The chapter turns this into a rule: the greater the risk (to any stakeholder, not just financially) and the more extreme the level of agreement in a group — either everyone agreeing immediately (groupthink risk) or everyone arguing (unresolved assumption risk) — the more rigorously the categories should be applied, and ideally by a diverse group of stakeholders who don't already think alike, since they're the ones positioned to spot the arrow's hidden assumptions. The categories underpin every sufficient-cause tool in the book — current reality tree, future reality tree, and transition tree all depend on rigorous use of CLR to check each connection as it's drawn.

## Why It Matters

A checklist of legitimate objections turns "I disagree" into something actionable: which category does the disagreement fall under, and what evidence would settle it? That converts arguments about conclusions into productive arguments about the specific missing piece — whether the thing claimed to exist actually exists, whether the causal link is real, or whether the two parties are even talking about the same thing — which is portable to any domain where people argue past each other over unstated assumptions rather than examining them.
