# Current Reality Tree

## Definition

The Current Reality Tree (CRT) is a diagnostic tool for finding a "core driver" — one underlying cause that produces many of a system's separate-seeming problems — by connecting a short list of pertinent entities (usually undesirable effects) with sufficient-cause logic until the causal structure among them becomes visible. Unlike root-cause methods that drill straight down from one symptom, the CRT works laterally first: it documents the cause-effect web across five to ten named symptoms, and only in the final steps does it apply a "so what test" to identify the common cause underneath them.

## In the Book

Chapter 8 opens with a diagnostic checklist of symptoms — an endless problem list, difficulty prioritizing among screaming projects, "the same old problems with the same old solutions," an inability to name the system's constraint or even its purpose — as the signal that a CRT is warranted. The chapter's running example is Display Manufacturing, a metal display-rack maker that assembled a cross-functional team (manufacturing, accounting, engineering, sales, marketing, plus former employees of a customer and a store) to find what was really limiting their customers' perceived value. Their initial pertinent-entity list included things like "installed costs are too high from the customers' perspective" and "display installation is difficult for the customer" — the team explicitly guarded against smuggling in a pet solution disguised as a problem (e.g., "we haven't implemented the XYZ quality program" is not an entity that exists, it's an absent solution). Building the tree meant connecting entities pairwise — either a "V Connection" (one common cause for two listed entities) or a "Linear Connection" (one entity causing another) — while applying the Categories of Legitimate Reservation to each link, without adding new entities except where a connection genuinely required one.

## Why It Matters

Long problem lists paralyze organizations because every item looks equally urgent and independently caused, which sends effort in ten directions at once. The CRT's discipline — state the symptoms as real existing entities (not missing solutions), connect them with tested cause-effect logic rather than intuition, and let the core driver emerge from the pattern of connections — gives you a principled way to find the one lever that, if moved, collapses several problems simultaneously, in any domain where a scattered list of complaints actually traces back to a shared cause.
