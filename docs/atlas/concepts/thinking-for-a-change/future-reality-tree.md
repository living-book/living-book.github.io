# Future Reality Tree

## Definition

The Future Reality Tree (FRT) uses sufficient-cause diagramming to predict, before you act, the full chain of effects an idea ("injection") will produce — both the objectives you want and the undesirable side effects you're worried about — so you can verify the idea actually reaches its goals and then design branches that neutralize the bad consequences rather than discovering them after implementation. Its four building blocks are injections (entities that don't yet exist), entities that already exist today, entities predicted to exist in the future as a result of combining the two, and reinforcing loops that sustain the improvement once achieved.

## In the Book

Chapter 7 grounds the tool in the ordinary, constant activity of predicting the future — setting an alarm clock, hiring a candidate because you predict cultural fit, a manager who won't enforce a new plant behavior because he fears retaliation — arguing we already do FRT thinking informally and inaccurately, so making it explicit just makes it better. The three-step process is: define the basis (identify the injection, list its objectives/pros, list its potential undesirable consequences/cons); use sufficient cause thinking to connect the injection to its objectives while actively seeking and then blocking the undesirable consequences with additional injections; and finally enhance the solution by predicting further downstream effects and adding reinforcing loops. Chapter 11's Full Analysis shows the FRT's proper entry point in a larger sequence: its starting injection comes from an Evaporating Cloud that resolved a core conflict, and its objectives are literally defined as the opposites of the undesirable effects selected from a prior Current Reality Tree — the FRT is complete only when every one of those opposites is achieved, every foreseeable negative branch has been blocked, and at least one reinforcing loop is in place.

## Why It Matters

Ideas fail less often because the core logic was wrong and more often because nobody traced what else the idea would cause — the side effect that quietly undoes the benefit. Forcing yourself to list the cons alongside the pros and then draw the causal path from the idea to each of them, before committing resources, converts "I have a good feeling about this" into a testable, inspectable prediction — useful for any decision, technical or organizational, where the failure mode is an unanticipated consequence rather than a wrong immediate goal.
