# Kanban Principles: Change Management and Service Delivery Philosophy

## Definition

Kanban rests on two sets of principles: Change Management Principles (how to approach improvement) and Service Delivery Principles (how to serve customers). Change Management Principles include "Start with what you do now," "Agree to pursue improvement through evolutionary change," and "Encourage acts of leadership at all levels." Service Delivery Principles include "Understand and focus on customer needs and expectations," "Manage the work; let people self-organize around it," and "Regularly review the network of services and its policies in order to improve outcomes." Together, these principles define both *how* change happens and *what* work is for.

## In the Book

The Official Kanban Guide introduces both explicitly: "Kanban encourages you to take a service-oriented approach to understanding your organization and how work flows through it. This service-oriented organizational paradigm is based on the idea that your organization is an organic entity consisting of a network of services, each of them living and breathing, and evolving."

Under Change Management: "Kanban is not a big bang transformation going from a current state to some future state...Instead, Kanban uses an evolutionary change approach, building on the way of working already in place, seeking to improve it using many forms of feedback and collaboration. The Kanban Method engenders evolutionary change through insights gained by the people working with the Kanban board and taking acts of leadership to continuously improve their way of working."

Under Service Delivery: The guide emphasizes that Kanban is "used with a service focus in mind...an effective organizational development tool." Customer focus is foundational: "We improve service delivery...improvements should be guided by a set of principles...based on the idea that your organization is an organic entity consisting of a network of services...If we are to improve service delivery, improvements should be guided by a set of principles."

## Why It Matters

Most change frameworks assume a destination-driven model: define the future state, plan the transition, execute. Kanban's Change Management Principles reject this. Starting where you are and evolving incrementally works in systems too complex to forecast (most human organizations) and lowers the political cost of change because it does not require wholesale disruption. The Service Delivery Principles reframe work from "tasks to complete" to "services to customers," which reorients priorities—doing less work that matters beats doing more work that doesn't. In any organization, clarity about *how* change happens and *why* work matters determines whether improvement is sustained or merely performs for an audit.
