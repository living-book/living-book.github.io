# Deliberate Practice for Leadership

## Definition

Deliberate Practice for Leadership is applying Ericsson's deliberate-practice framework to leadership development: designing practice that is specifically aimed at improving performance, can be repeated many times, provides continuous feedback on results, and is highly mentally demanding. It builds on the observation that performance variation among people (28:1 ratio in debug hours, 25:1 in coding hours) is primarily due to practice, not innate talent.

## In the Book

The document cites research showing dramatic variation in programming performance across individuals, then draws on Ericsson's research on deliberate practice as the key driver of mastery in any domain. Deliberate practice has five characteristics: designed specifically to improve performance, repeatable, feedback available on results, highly demanding mentally, and not much fun. The document shows this applies to leadership: leaders develop through chunking practice (breaking skills into components), absorbing the whole (integrating pieces), slowing down and speeding up (varying pace), and learning to feel the pattern (intuition).

The book shows multiple practice models: the music model (performance), the chess model (study the masters), the sports model (conditioning and critical skills). It emphasizes that practice must be intentional, not casual repetition. Benefits of deliberate practice include building confidence, enhancing creativity, offering exploration, auditing ideas, fostering continuous improvement, and promoting learning.

## Why It Matters

Most organizations treat leadership as either innate (hire the right person) or transmissible through classroom training. Deliberate Practice reveals a third path: leadership capability grows through systematic, repeated practice with feedback. This shifts accountability from hiring alone to development, and makes leadership improvement a team responsibility rather than an individual burden. It also explains why some leaders improve while others plateau—those practicing deliberately improve, while those coasting stay the same.
