# The Four-Stage Perception Pipeline

## Definition

Adapting family therapist Virginia Satir's Interaction Model, Weinberg breaks every act of observing and reporting into four stages: intake (selecting which sensory data to notice at all), meaning (assigning an interpretation to that data), significance (deciding how much the meaning matters), and response (deciding what to do about it). Each stage is a selective, personal filter, not a mechanical pass-through — and each stage feeds back to reshape the ones before it, so "just observing" is never as neutral as it feels.

## In the Book

Weinberg uses a diner's "The soup's okay" to show that identical intake data can carry radically different meanings — genuine satisfaction, grudging tolerance, effusive praise, or "stop asking and leave me alone" — meaning is supplied by the receiver, not embedded in the words. He then shows the stages compounding in testing communication: a manager engaged in selective listening hears a tester's "there's very little chance we ship by September 1st" and retains only "ship by September 1st," so Weinberg recommends giving dates only in writing, only as ranges, and ideally as "what's-the-chance" probability graphs to force the later date past the intake filter. He also notes that the same message gets processed differently depending on its source — a consultant can often get a manager to hear something an employee has been saying for months, because the manager's intake filter treats the consultant's words as more significant.

## Why It Matters

This gives a diagnostic vocabulary for exactly where a piece of feedback died on its way to changing anything: was it never taken in, taken in but misread, correctly read but dismissed as unimportant, or understood and judged important but never acted on? That's a general-purpose debugging tool for any stalled communication — a status report nobody acts on, a warning nobody heeds, a customer complaint that goes nowhere.
