# The Seven Dimensions of Agile Performance

## Definition

Agility is not a single trait but a multidimensional capacity to respond swiftly and effectively to changing market conditions. Baker extends Rosabeth Moss Kanter's three-part model of speed (innovation, processing, recovery) by adding four additional dimensions (continuous improvement, customer responsiveness, problem-solving, changing direction). These seven dimensions form an interconnected framework where strength in one dimension typically reinforces strength in others—a truly customer-responsive organization is usually in continuous improvement mode as well.

## In the Book

The book introduces this framework in Chapter 3, building on the premise that "performance is essentially about speed." Baker defines each dimension: Innovation Speed (being first to market with wanted goods and services); Processing Speed (dispensing everything through the organization faster than competitors); Recovery Speed (responding quickly to customer mistakes); Continuous Improvement (improving something already in existence); Customer Responsiveness (reacting to customer needs); Problem-Solving (solving dilemmas that arise); and Changing Direction (pivoting strategy in response to market volatility). 

The model illustrates interdependency—a company practicing recovery speed (fixing customer errors promptly) likely demonstrates both problem-solving agility and continuous improvement. Baker emphasizes that these dimensions cannot flourish in rigid, hierarchical, process-driven environments; they require decentralization, cross-functional collaboration, and a tolerance for ambiguity. The framework stands in explicit contrast to scientific management, which emphasizes standardization and predictability rather than adaptive speed.

## Why It Matters

This framework redefines performance evaluation from static metrics (profit, goals achieved) to dynamic responsiveness. It allows organizations to diagnose agility gaps—determining whether slowness stems from innovation constraints, bureaucratic processing, or inability to change direction. For practitioners, the seven dimensions provide concrete levers for organizational design: each dimension maps directly to specific management practices (cross-functional teams improve problem-solving and processing speed; decentralized authority enables recovery speed). The framework also surfaces the tension between Taylorist efficiency (optimizing a narrow process) and agile effectiveness (adapting the whole system rapidly).
