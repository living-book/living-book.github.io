# The Autonomy-Mastery-Purpose Framework for Intrinsic Motivation

## Definition

Baker synthesizes research showing that widely held belief—job satisfaction (achieved through extrinsic rewards like bonuses) drives performance—is weakly supported or contradicted by evidence. Instead, high performance emerges from three intrinsic drivers: Autonomy (self-direction and decision-making authority over your work), Mastery (the opportunity and support to continually improve at things that matter to you), and Purpose (connection to a cause or mission larger than yourself). These three factors align with knowledge work but are undermined by Taylorist practices that separate planning from execution, standardize procedures, limit decision-making authority, and frame work as instrumental (a means to a paycheck) rather than meaningful.

## In the Book

Baker opens Chapter 8 with the Stanford study of children drawing with markers: those rewarded for drawing spent less time drawing when given a choice, and their drawings were judged lower quality. Extrinsic reward paradoxically reduced intrinsic motivation. He then challenges the "satisfaction → performance" myth: research is inconclusive or shows the reverse (performance leads to satisfaction) or shows no relationship at all. "The answer is literally under our nose. It's work itself that has the greatest potential to improve (or reduce) personal productivity." He draws on Dan Pink's framework and names the three drivers: Autonomy ("our desire to be self-directed"), Mastery ("our urge to get better and better at what we do"), and Purpose ("our thirst to be part of something larger than ourselves"). Baker illustrates autonomy with Jerry, a vacuum-cleaner salesman who chooses to solve customer problems over chasing new sales; his broader autonomy over how to spend his time actually yields better business results than narrow goal-chasing. He illustrates purpose with a floor-sweeper example: when a production supervisor explains that the tidiness matters because a major potential customer is visiting, the work acquires meaning—it becomes part of winning business. "Imagine for a moment the difference it could make if all business leaders—for every task, big and small—explained the why; what a difference it would bring." Baker contrasts this with Taylor's view: work consists mainly of simple, uninteresting tasks; the only way to motivate is monetary incentive and monitoring. That model worked for assembly lines but robs knowledge workers of the autonomy, growth, and meaning their complex work can provide.

## Why It Matters

This framework explains why many incentive programs backfire or produce unintended consequences (goal-chasing leading to data manipulation, as Michelle's opening anecdote shows). It also explains why "perks" (foosball tables, free snacks) don't fix fundamental motivation problems—they're treats offered alongside a control model that denies autonomy, limits mastery, and frames work as means to income rather than meaningful contribution. For practitioners, it suggests concrete design changes: expanding decision-making authority at the frontline (autonomy); creating clear skill-development paths and allocating time for learning (mastery); regularly explaining how individual and team work connects to customer benefit or organizational mission (purpose). It also predicts that Taylorist organizations will struggle to attract and retain knowledge workers who have options—intrinsic motivation is especially important to people with choice. The framework applies across industries and roles: even seemingly routine work acquires meaning when the "why" is explained and people have authority over how they approach it.
