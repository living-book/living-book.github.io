# Flexible Deployment of Skills

## Definition

Flexible deployment is the practice of accumulating and actively using a range of skills and competencies outside the strict boundaries of a formal job specification. Baker distinguishes it from both pure specialization (narrow mastery of one domain) and "jack-of-all-trades" generalism (shallow capability everywhere). An employee with flexible deployment has a broader skills-set and can understand and work across operational boundaries, appreciating how their work connects to the larger system. This systemic deployment of competencies across the enterprise—rather than concentrating each person's effort within functional silos—enables the adaptability, maneuverability, and responsiveness that define agility.

## In the Book

Baker uses optometry as his opening example. His father advised specialization as a path to job security, but optical laser surgery disrupted the field. Successful optometrists didn't double down on their narrow specialty; they flexibly deployed their expertise into ocular disease diagnosis, management, and other health-care services, maintaining their market position. In Chapter 4, he contrasts this with the job specification model, where learning outside the defined job scope is not encouraged and sometimes actively discouraged. A strictly specialized workforce cannot rapidly redeploy when customer needs shift, problems arise unexpectedly, or innovation demands cross-functional thinking. By contrast, in a flexibly deployed organization, an employee at the "coal face" understands enough about adjacent functions to make sensible decisions without always checking with their manager. The model supports the three characteristics Baker identifies as essential to agile workforces: high skill, high flexibility, and continuous improvement. He cites cross-functional project teams as a structured way to build flexible deployment: pulling specialists together to tackle problems forces them outside their narrow domains, building familiarity with how other parts of the system work.

## Why It Matters

Flexible deployment reframes skill development from a career-risk (overspecialization can become obsolete) to a competitive advantage (knowing more breadth enables faster, better decisions). It explains why organizations that survive disruption are often those that already had cross-functional exposure and systems thinking, not just depth within specialties. For performance management, it shifts the question from "Does this employee execute their narrowly defined job well?" to "Can this employee understand and navigate the broader system, and adapt when conditions change?" It directly enables all seven dimensions of agility: innovation speeds up when problems solvers see connections others don't; recovery speed improves when frontline staff have the judgment and breadth to solve issues without escalation; continuous improvement flourishes when employees understand why a process works and how it connects to outcomes. The concept also suggests a practical lever: organizations that deliberately cross-train, rotate people through functions, and measure non-job learning (not just job-specific certification) will naturally develop greater agility than those that tighten job specifications.
