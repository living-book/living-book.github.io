# Energy, Not Time: The Currency of Performance

## Definition

Energy, not time, is the true currency of high performance. While the number of hours in a day is fixed and finite, the quantity and quality of energy we bring to those hours is not. Every thought, emotion, and behavior has an energy consequence. Performance, health, and happiness are grounded not in time management but in the skillful management of energy—its quantity, quality, focus, and force.

## In the Book

Loehr and Schwartz introduce this concept by contrasting the futility of time management with the power of energy management. They show how you can attend a perfectly scheduled meeting without wasting a second yet still feel your energy collapse halfway through—or race through a twelve-hour day and be negative and irritable by midday. The core insight is that "energy, not time, is the fundamental currency of high performance."

The authors ground this in their work with professional athletes, where they discovered that the performance demands of executives in their everyday work environments actually dwarf those of professional athletes. The difference: athletes spend 90% of their time training to perform 10% of the time, with entire lives designed around expanding and renewing energy. Most of us are expected to perform at our best for eight to twelve hours daily with little systematic training in energy management.

They argue that while we have far more control over our energy than we ordinarily realize, most people blame external circumstances instead of taking responsibility for the energy they bring. This shift in accountability—from "how much time do I have" to "what quality of energy do I bring"—is transformative.

## Why It Matters

This distinction reframes the entire performance problem. If performance were truly about time, better time management tools would solve it. But since tools haven't, the insight that energy is the hidden variable changes where you look for leverage. It means that someone who works shorter days with higher energy may outperform someone grinding long hours in depleted states. It redirects attention from schedule optimization to capacity building—physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual—and makes recovery and renewal not waste but essential performance infrastructure.
