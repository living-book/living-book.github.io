# The Progress Loop

## Definition

The progress loop is the positive feedback cycle in which progress boosts inner work life (the progress principle) and, in turn, positive inner work life makes further progress more likely (the inner work life effect). Amabile and Kramer call it, borrowing mathematician Norbert Wiener's term, "cumulative causation": once moving, the loop is self-sustaining like a pendulum, continuing in either direction — virtuous or vicious — until an outside force interrupts it.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 lays out the loop explicitly (figure 5-1) as the synthesis of the book's two prior causal claims: chapter 4's progress principle (progress → better inner work life) and chapter 3's inner work life effect (better inner work life → more progress). The authors use the physical analogy of a pendulum in near-vacuum, and of a clock that keeps time only as long as someone winds it: the loop doesn't run on its own indefinitely, it requires a manager to continually remove obstacles and supply catalysts and nourishers to keep it turning, because in the real world friction (setbacks, inhibitors, toxins) is always present and will eventually stop the pendulum if unaddressed. The chapter frames this as "a secret weapon of high-performance companies" precisely because it is a win-win: the same daily forward movement that keeps employees' inner work life high is also what drives organizational output, so a manager focused on protecting daily progress serves both goals simultaneously rather than trading one off against the other.

## Why It Matters

The progress loop generalizes the book's specific finding into a systems-level claim: motivation and output are not independent variables you manage separately, they are two nodes in a single reinforcing loop, and the leverage point for a manager (or anyone managing their own work) is to intervene on whichever side is more directly controllable — usually removing obstacles to visible progress — rather than trying to manufacture motivation or performance directly. It is a domain-general instance of how virtuous and vicious cycles form around any effort that produces feedback about itself.
