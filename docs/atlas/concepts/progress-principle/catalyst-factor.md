# The Catalyst Factor

## Definition

Catalysts are actions and conditions that directly facilitate timely, creative, high-quality work — the opposite of inhibitors, which block progress or cause setbacks. Unlike progress itself, catalysts affect inner work life immediately, the moment a person becomes aware of them, even before they translate into actual accomplishment. The book identifies seven major catalysts: setting clear goals, allowing autonomy, providing resources, giving enough time (but not too much), offering help with the work, learning from problems and successes, and letting ideas flow freely.

## In the Book

Chapter 6 contrasts Sophie, a product marketer at "Karpenter Corporation" whose handheld mixer project was killed and restarted three times by R&D under conflicting goals, with Tim, a research engineer at "O'Reilly Coated Materials," whose team left its very first meeting energized because the group leader had laid out clear direction. The book frames catalysts through the chemistry metaphor (a substance that accelerates a reaction without itself being consumed) and shows via figure 6-1 that catalysts operate on two channels at once: a direct, instant boost to inner work life just from knowing help or resources are coming, and an indirect boost later, through the progress loop, once that support actually produces progress. Each of the seven catalysts is grounded in diary evidence — for instance, "learning from problems" is tied to psychological safety, the shared expectation that admitting mistakes will be met with support rather than punishment, without which people won't take the risks that creative work requires; "giving enough time" is shown to be curvilinear, since both extreme time pressure and boredom-inducing slack degraded inner work life.

## Why It Matters

The catalyst factor decomposes "support the team" into seven specific, actionable levers that a manager (or a self-organizing team) actually controls, rather than leaving support as a vague aspiration. Because catalysts hit inner work life before any output exists, the concept also shows that perceived support is itself a resource — announcing help is not merely preparatory to a later benefit but delivers value on its own, which generalizes to any context where anticipation of support shapes present effort and mood.
