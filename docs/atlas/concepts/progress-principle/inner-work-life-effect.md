# The Inner Work Life Effect

## Definition

The inner work life effect is the finding that inner work life is not just a byproduct of good performance but a cause of it: as a person's perceptions, emotions, and motivation rise and fall, their subsequent performance on four dimensions — creativity, productivity, work commitment, and collegiality — rises and falls with it. Positive inner work life predicts better performance over the long haul; it is not, as the "no pressure, no diamonds" school holds, that stress and unhappiness are what drive people to excel.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 opens with Helen, a software engineer at "DreamSuite Hotels," whose manager let her leave early for her daughter's school play; the resulting gratitude and boost in inner work life preceded, and by the authors' analysis caused, an unusually productive day in which a customer spontaneously praised her work. The chapter directly confronts the competing "stress breeds performance" view (citing Thomas Carlyle's "no pressure, no diamonds" and researchers like George and Zhou on negative mood and creativity) against the "happy workers perform better" literature (Riketta's meta-analysis, Staw's studies linking positive emotion to better evaluations and raises). Amabile and Kramer argue their diary data settles the debate more decisively than prior one-shot studies because it tracks the same people over many days, showing within-person swings in performance tied to within-person swings in inner work life — ruling out the objection that some people simply perform better when miserable, since it is the same individuals, not different personality types, whose output moves with their inner state.

## Why It Matters

This concept licenses a causal (not merely correlational) argument that emotional and motivational state is a performance lever, which justifies spending management attention on daily experience rather than treating it as a soft, secondary concern after strategy and incentives are set. It generalizes to any domain where sustained creative or effortful output is required — the claim is that the state-of-mind-to-output arrow points from the person's inner state outward, and that arrow can be traced day by day rather than assumed.
