# The Nourishment Factor

## Definition

Where catalysts are directed at the project, nourishers are directed at the person: interpersonal events that satisfy the basic human craving for connection. The book identifies four major nourishers — respect, encouragement, emotional support, and affiliation — and their mirror-image toxins: disrespect, discouragement, emotional neglect, and antagonism. Nourishers work partly by infusing work with greater meaning (people work harder for colleagues and managers they feel connected to), and thereby indirectly fuel the progress loop as well as directly lifting mood in the moment.

## In the Book

Chapter 7 opens with a small gesture: Helen's project manager Ruth reminds her, in response to an ordinary day-off request, that she has a "free day" owed for hard work — a note Ruth herself didn't think twice about, but which Helen recorded as making her want to "work harder to make the project manager and team a success." The authors call this "extraordinarily good management" precisely because of its unremarkable size, underscoring that nourishment doesn't require grand gestures. Each of the four nourishers gets a concrete mechanism: respect includes acknowledging effort, taking ideas seriously, and honest (not sugar-coated) treatment; encouragement works by transmitting a manager's own enthusiasm and by signaling belief in someone's capability, which raises self-efficacy; emotional support means validating feelings — both work-related frustration and personal grief — with empathy going further than mere acknowledgment; and affiliation is bonds of mutual trust built through shared face time and fun, which the book notes becomes more, not less, important as more people telecommute or work as contract team members. The chapter also documents that nourishment isn't just top-down manager-to-employee behavior — it includes setting the organizational climate (team composition, role clarity) that lets peers nourish each other.

## Why It Matters

The nourishment factor separates two kinds of support that get conflated in everyday management talk — helping the work move forward versus helping the person feel cared for — and shows both are independently necessary, since a project can be well-resourced while the people on it feel disrespected, or vice versa. Its cross-domain lesson is that the smallest, cheapest interpersonal acts (a thank-you note, remembered promise, or moment of empathy) can outweigh larger but impersonal interventions, because they are read as signals about the relationship, not just information about the task.
