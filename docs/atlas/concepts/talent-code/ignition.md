# Ignition (Primal Cues)

## Definition

Ignition is the process by which a primal cue — a vivid, identity-relevant signal that a particular kind of success is achievable — generates the sustained motivational fuel that deep practice requires. It is unconscious and often instantaneous: a single moment can convince someone "I could be that," and that belief alone can power years of otherwise unaccountable effort. Because the cue works through identification rather than instruction, it spreads: one breakthrough performer can ignite an entire population who see themselves in that person.

## In the Book

Coyle traces the ignition point behind several talent hotbeds to a single, dateable event. South Korean women's golf exploded after Se Ri Pak won the 1998 LPGA Championship — from zero LPGA players before her to forty-five within a decade, "one puff" that, given time, "brings many flowers." Russian women's tennis ignited the same summer when seventeen-year-old Anna Kournikova reached the Wimbledon semifinal. Coyle pairs these with Roger Bannister's four-minute mile in 1954: within three years of Bannister proving the barrier breakable, sixteen more runners had matched it, despite no change in training, track surfaces, or genetics — "the change didn't come from inside the athletes: they were responding to something outside them." A separate chapter (the Curaçao Experiment) examines how the same ignition mechanism can be deliberately engineered rather than left to a lucky breakthrough — and Chapter 7 profiles Mike Feinberg and Dave Levin's founding of KIPP charter schools, where ignition came not from a single star but from an intensely reinforced identity ("Work Hard, Be Nice") built into the culture of the school itself.

## Why It Matters

Ignition explains a pattern that pure resource or genetic explanations miss: talent clusters appear suddenly, in places with no obvious prior advantage, immediately after one visible breakthrough, and then persist for a generation even after the original spark fades from memory. It reframes motivation as an engineerable input rather than an inborn trait — visible role models, especially ones a person can identify with on some concrete dimension (nationality, background, appearance), can be deliberately supplied to trigger effort that no amount of instruction or incentive design would otherwise produce.
