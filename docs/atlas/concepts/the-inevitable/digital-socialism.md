# Digital Socialism

## Definition

Digital socialism is Kelly's deliberately provocative name for the mass collaborative structures the internet makes possible: "socialism without the state," operating in culture and economics rather than government. Unlike industrial-era political socialism, which imposed collective ownership top-down through central planning, digital socialism is decentralized, voluntary, and self-organizing — it "heighten[s] individual autonomy and thwart[s] centralization," running on peer production, borderless networks, and shared code rather than command economies.

## In the Book

The "Sharing" chapter takes as its provocation Bill Gates's 2005 dismissal of free-software advocates as "a new modern-day sort of communists," then argues Gates was pointing at something real while getting the politics wrong: the participants are more often libertarian than collectivist. Kelly's examples span the spectrum of scale — Wikipedia and the roughly 150 wiki engines tracked by inventor Ward Cunningham; the more than one billion Creative Commons licenses in use by 2015, which make sharing and remixing the legal default rather than the exception; and collaborative filtering platforms like Digg, Reddit, and Pinterest, where hundreds of millions of users rank, curate, and surface content collectively. He frames the substitution explicitly: "Instead of gathering on collective farms, we gather in collective worlds. Instead of state factories, we have desktop factories connected to virtual co-ops... Instead of national production, we have peer production."

## Why It Matters

Digital socialism separates the mechanism of large-scale collective production (pooled contribution, shared ownership of the output, decentralized coordination) from the historical political apparatus usually associated with that mechanism (a coercive state, central planning). That separation lets you evaluate any collaborative system — open-source software, crowdsourced data, prediction markets, mutual-aid networks — on whether its coordination structure actually produces the self-correcting, adaptive behavior markets are prized for, rather than dismissing or endorsing it by ideological label alone.
