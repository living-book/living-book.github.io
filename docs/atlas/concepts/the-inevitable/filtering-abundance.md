# Filtering Abundance

## Definition

Filtering abundance names the situation created once production and distribution costs collapse: the constraint on what people can enjoy stops being availability and becomes attention. Kelly's numbers make the scale concrete — 8 million new songs, 2 million new books, and 30 billion blog posts appear every year, an amount that "would consume more than a year's worth of our attention to merely preview" what's created in a single day. Because "life is short, and there are too many books to read," some filtering mechanism becomes mandatory, not optional.

## In the Book

The "Filtering" chapter catalogs the traditional filters society has long used to cope with this — gatekeepers, intermediaries (publishers' reject piles), curators, brands, government, cultural environment, and peer influence — and argues that digital abundance breaks or overwhelms most of them, forcing a shift toward computational and collaborative filters: recommendation algorithms, collaborative-filtering platforms, and social curation. Kelly notes the irony that the Library of Everything now exceeds what past elites could access — "you could... read more Greek texts in the original Greek than the most prestigious Greek nobleman of classical times" — yet this glut, rather than being pure gain, creates a new scarcity that must itself be managed, since a hard disk holding all recorded music in history is now cheap enough to carry in a pocket.

## Why It Matters

Filtering abundance identifies a structural inversion that recurs whenever a cost that used to bind (production, storage, distribution) collapses toward zero: the bottleneck doesn't disappear, it relocates to whatever remains scarce — usually attention, trust, or judgment — and whoever controls that new bottleneck (the filter, not the content) captures disproportionate power and value. It is a lens for diagnosing any market flooded with options: search results, job applicants, funding proposals, research papers — the interesting design problem is no longer "how do we make more," it's "who or what decides which ones we see."
