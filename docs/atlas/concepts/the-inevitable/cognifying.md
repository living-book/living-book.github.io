# Cognifying

## Definition

Cognifying is Kelly's term for injecting a small amount of artificial intelligence into something that previously had none, boosting its effectiveness the way electrification boosted any device it touched a century earlier: "You'll simply plug into the grid and get AI as if it was electricity." The intelligence being added need not be general or humanlike — even a "very tiny amount of useful intelligence embedded into an existing process" is enough to be disruptive, because it is cheap and ubiquitous rather than because it is deep.

## In the Book

The "Cognifying" chapter argues this AI will not arrive as a bounded, stand-alone supercomputer mind but as a property of the network itself — "planetary in dimensions, but thin, embedded, and loosely connected" — learning faster because it is plugged into billions of humans, devices, and exabytes of data rather than working alone. Kelly's central case study is IBM's Watson: once a room-sized machine that beat humans at Jeopardy!, now spread across cloud servers, sold on demand to customers who never own a single instance, and applied to problems like medical diagnosis for CVS pharmacy customers. The chapter's framing device is entrepreneurial: just as the early industrial economy consisted of taking one existing device — a plow, a fan, a stitching machine — and running electricity through it, the next economic wave, Kelly argues, will consist of taking one existing process and running cheap AI through it.

## Why It Matters

Cognifying separates two different questions that easily get conflated: "how smart is the intelligence" versus "how cheaply and pervasively is it distributed." Kelly's claim is that the second variable, not the first, is what reorganizes an economy — a mediocre capability that is metered, on-demand, and embedded everywhere beats a superior capability that is scarce and centralized. This is a template for evaluating any new general-purpose input (a material, a capability, a resource): the transformative moment is not when the input is invented, but when it becomes a utility.
