# Coveillance

## Definition

Coveillance is Kelly's term for mutual, transparent watching in which the watched can also watch the watchers, as opposed to a one-way panopticon where "'they' know about us but we know nothing about them." Kelly argues ubiquitous tracking itself is not preventable — "outlawing the expansion of easy tracking will probably be as ineffectual as outlawing easy copying" — so the only real design choice is whether surveillance stays asymmetrical (hell) or becomes symmetrical and accountable (tractable).

## In the Book

The "Tracking" chapter grounds coveillance in a small-town memory: neighbors who watched each other's comings and goings, but where the watching was reciprocal — "you knew who was watching you... you could hold them accountable for its accuracy and use" — and produced mutual benefit, such as calling the police on a stranger or collecting a neighbor's mail. Kelly extends this into technological examples: Bitcoin's public ledger, where transaction validity is "verified by a coveillance of other users rather than the surveillance of central bank," and public-key encryption, where trust comes from code anyone can inspect rather than a secret held by one party. He credits science fiction author David Brin's "Transparent Society" as the fuller articulation of the idea, and proposes it as a paired right and duty: a right to access data about yourself, matched by a duty to be watched by the watched.

## Why It Matters

Coveillance reframes the privacy debate away from "surveillance yes or no" — which Kelly treats as already settled by the trajectory of cheap sensors — toward the actually decidable question of symmetry: who can see whom, and who can hold whom accountable for what they see. This generalizes to any system where monitoring is unavoidable (organizational metrics, algorithmic moderation, financial audits): the design lever that remains is not whether to watch, but whether the watching runs in one direction or is reciprocal and inspectable by those being watched.
