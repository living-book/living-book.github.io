# The What-the-Hell Effect: Guilt-Driven Relapse

## Definition

The what-the-hell effect is a cycle where a small slip in willpower—eating one slice of pizza when you're dieting, drinking one extra beer, skipping one exercise session—triggers guilt and shame. That negative emotion then activates the brain's reward system, which seeks relief through the very behavior you're trying to change. The outcome: one slip becomes a full relapse. A lapse that could have been contained turns into "Well, I already blew it, what the hell" and you abandon restraint entirely until tomorrow (or never).

## In the Book

Dieting researchers Polivy and Herman coined the term in studies showing that dieters who felt guilty about eating one piece of cake were more likely to consume the entire cake than to stop after one slice. Chapter 6 extends this to addiction research: drinkers who felt worse about how much they drank the previous night actually drank more that evening. The guilt was driving them back to the bottle, not away from it.

The mechanism is emotional regulation gone wrong. When you feel bad (guilt, shame, anxiety, stress), your brain tries to make you feel better by pointing you toward rewards. Stress and negative emotions shift the brain into a reward-seeking state, making whatever temptation you're vulnerable to suddenly feel like the only solution. If you blame yourself for a lapse, you create the exact emotional state that makes relapse most likely.

## Why It Matters

Understanding the what-the-hell effect reveals why shame-based self-control strategies backfire. Criticizing yourself after a willpower failure doesn't strengthen motivation—it weakens it by creating the emotional state most likely to trigger further indulgence. The concept also explains the vulnerability to "one more won't hurt" thinking: that small slip triggers guilt, which then needs to be managed somehow, and the brain knows exactly what has historically provided relief. Breaking the cycle requires changing how you interpret lapses—from moral failure (which triggers shame) to simple deviation from plan (which you can adjust without spiraling).
