# The Three Powers of Self-Control

## Definition

Willpower is not a single capacity but three distinct powers working together. "I will" power enables you to initiate and persist at challenging tasks. "I won't" power helps you resist temptations and impulses. "I want" power maintains your connection to your deepest values and long-term goals, keeping you motivated when temptations arise. All three emerge from the prefrontal cortex and are essential for managing your behavior, emotions, and attention.

## In the Book

McGonigal introduces the three powers in Chapter 1 as the framework for understanding self-control. Most people think of willpower only as "I won't" power—the ability to say no to a doughnut or a cigarette. But she demonstrates that "I will" power is equally important: the ability to start and persist at boring, difficult, or stressful tasks you know you should do. "I want" power is the third and foundational power—the capacity to hold your true priorities in mind even when dopamine is screaming at you to pursue immediate rewards.

She uses the metaphor of the brain's prefrontal cortex, which has three specialized regions. The left side handles "I will" (initiating action), the right side handles "I won't" (impulse suppression), and the middle region (lower prefrontal cortex) handles "I want" (tracking your values and goals). The famous case of Phineas Gage—the railroad worker who survived a tamping iron through his skull—illustrates what happens when you lose prefrontal cortex function: all three powers vanish, and the balance between impulses and self-control collapses.

## Why It Matters

Understanding the three powers reframes self-control from a single all-or-nothing ability into a toolkit with distinct, trainable components. You might be strong in one power and weak in another. Someone might excel at "I won't" (resisting temptation) but struggle with "I will" (starting difficult projects). Once you see these as separate capacities, you can develop targeted strategies for each. More fundamentally, recognizing that motivation requires you to actively hold your true wants in mind—not just suppress temptations—explains why guilt and shame often backfire: they push you away from your goals rather than pull you toward them.
