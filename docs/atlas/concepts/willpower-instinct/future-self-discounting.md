# Future-Self Discounting: Treating Your Future Self as a Stranger

## Definition

Future-self discounting is the systematic tendency to devalue rewards you'll receive in the future compared to immediate rewards, and to overestimate how much self-control your future self will have. Neuroscience shows that your brain uses different regions to think about your present self versus your future self—the same regions it uses to think about *other people*. Your brain literally treats your future self as a stranger. This leads to decisions where you burden your future self with consequences you wouldn't accept for yourself today, and assume your future self will magically have more willpower, time, and motivation than your present self.

## In the Book

Chapter 7 details Pronin's experiments where students were asked to drink a revolting ketchup-and-soy-sauce mixture. When they chose how much they would drink now, they selected about two tablespoons. When choosing for their future selves, they volunteered more than half a cup. The same bias appeared in time commitments: they signed up their future selves for 85 minutes of tutoring but themselves for only 27 minutes. Brain-imaging shows this isn't just psychology—the neural networks for thinking about your future self activate identically to thinking about another person.

McGonigal also presents the marshmallow test and research on discount rates: people who heavily discount the future (those who devalue a larger delayed reward in favor of immediate gratification) are more likely to smoke, abuse drugs, have unprotected sex, accumulate debt, and achieve less academically. Future-self discounting affects not just single decisions but life trajectories.

## Why It Matters

Future-self discounting explains why you procrastinate (your future self will have more time), why you skip the gym today (your future self will be more motivated), and why present indulgence feels justified (you'll diet tomorrow). It reveals that many willpower failures aren't due to lack of control in the moment but to poor decisions made earlier, when you were failing to see your future self as *you*. Precommitment strategies—removing options, setting defaults, creating barriers between present impulse and action—work because they bypass the future-self bias by making decisions *now*, before your brain treats future you as a stranger. Techniques that make your future self feel more real—visualizing your future self, writing to your future self, seeing age-progressed photos—reduce discounting and improve long-term decision-making.
