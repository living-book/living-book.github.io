# Moral Licensing: When Good Behavior Licenses Bad Behavior

## Definition

Moral licensing is the psychological trap where succeeding at self-control—whether through actual achievement or merely contemplating future action—creates a sense of moral permission to abandon your goals. After making progress on a goal, your brain treats the unmet competing desire as "liberated" and suddenly more tempting. A diet day's restriction licenses an evening binge. Exercising licenses eating extra calories. Filing your taxes licenses procrastination tomorrow. The mechanism is automatic and invisible: your brain mistakes having acted consistently with a goal for having actually *satisfied* that goal, and then shifts its attention to the deferred desire that suddenly feels more legitimate.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 details Fishbach and Dhar's research showing that reminding dieters of their weight-loss progress made them 85% more likely to choose chocolate over an apple as a thank-you reward. The more people are reminded of progress, the more likely they indulge in goal-sabotaging behavior. McGonigal identifies three variants: licensing based on past good behavior, licensing based on progress toward a goal, and licensing based on the mere availability of future good options (if you see healthy choices on a menu, you're more likely to order junk food, thinking you can choose healthy "next time").

The root cause is goal liberation: when your brain senses progress on one goal, it temporarily satisfies that goal in your mental checklist, freeing up mental resources and allowing competing desires to resurface stronger. Progress creates an illusion that the goal is "handled," when really it was just one step in an ongoing process.

## Why It Matters

Moral licensing explains why progress often precedes relapse, why good days lead to bad nights, and why seeing a healthy option on a menu makes unhealthy eating more likely. It shows that the biggest willpower threat isn't lack of self-control but the self-sabotaging thought patterns that follow success. The solution is counterintuitive: reframe progress not as permission to stop but as evidence of commitment. When you make progress, remind yourself *why* you're pursuing the goal, not how much credit you've earned. Seeing your actions as signs of who you are (not deposits in a moral bank account) keeps the higher self engaged rather than signaling that the work is done.
