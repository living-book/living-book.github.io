# Knowledge Is Validated Communally, Not Individually

## Definition

Individual experience of knowing something is personal, but the knowledge itself is not — it is produced and certified through a community's interaction over time. Even a fact as basic as the earth orbiting the sun is knowledge you inherited from "centuries of understanding and practice developed by long-standing communities," not something you individually verified.

## In the Book

The book uses the scientific community as its central case: what counts as scientific knowledge is "the prerogative of scientific communities, which interact to define what facts matter and what theories are valid." There may be disagreements and mavericks, but a body of knowledge is developed through communal involvement, including its controversies — members produce scientific knowledge partly by participating in the community even when pushing against its mainstream. The book pairs this with a claim about complexity: "today's complex problem solving requires multiple perspectives... the days of Leonardo da Vinci are over," so appreciating knowledge's collective character matters more as fields change too fast for any individual to master alone. It explicitly rejects the reading that this diminishes individuals: "the best communities welcome strong personalities and encourage disagreements and debates. Controversy is part of what makes a community vital, effective, and productive."

## Why It Matters

This separates two questions that get conflated: "does this person understand X" and "is X actually known/valid." The second question can only be answered by reference to a community's ongoing process, which means legitimacy and quality control in any knowledge-driven field is a property of the group's interaction — including its capacity to host disagreement — not a property any single expert can supply alone, however capable.
