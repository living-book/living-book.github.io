# Designed Ignorance

## Definition

Designed ignorance is the phenomenon where becoming skillful at an action produces automatic, effortless behavior that is taken for granted—and in doing so, people forget the deliberate processes they used to develop the skill. Skillful actions work because they are automatic and tacit; but this automaticity creates a blind spot about how the skill was acquired and how it actually functions.

## In the Book

Argyris explains this as a key mechanism of skilled incompetence and skilled unawareness. When human beings become skilled, they stop thinking about what they are doing; the actions become automatic, appear effortless, and are usually taken for granted. A consequence of generating skills is designed ignorance—people no longer attend to or recall the learning journey (Chapter 1). For example, executives skilled at defensive reasoning do not see themselves as defensive; they see themselves as practical, realistic, or protective. Their Model I reasoning has become so automatic that they are unaware both of using it and of its counterproductive effects. This is not accident or negligence; it is "designed" because the very processes that create skill—practice, automaticity, tacit knowledge—necessarily produce a form of ignorance about how the skill functions.

## Why It Matters

Designed ignorance explains why skilled people are often the hardest to teach or change. They have internalized their reasoning patterns so deeply that they cannot see them as choices or as having alternatives. This distinction separates ignorance born from lack of training (correctable by education) from ignorance born from skill mastery (requires unlearning before relearning). Organizations attempting change often fail because they do not account for this: they train people in new skills without first creating conditions for people to become aware of and question their existing automatic competencies.
