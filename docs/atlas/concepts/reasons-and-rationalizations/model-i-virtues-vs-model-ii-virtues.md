# Model I Virtues vs. Model II Virtues

## Definition

Virtues like caring, respect, honesty, strength, and integrity can be enacted from either Model I (defensive) or Model II (productive) reasoning, with each producing opposite effects. Model I virtues aim to protect the self and avoid upsetting others but produce manipulation and distance. Model II virtues aim to enhance others' capacity for self-reflection and genuine choice while inviting inquiry into one's own reasoning.

## In the Book

Argyris presents a detailed comparison table (Box 1.1, Chapter 1) showing how the same virtue translates into fundamentally different actions under Model I versus Model II. For example:

**Caring and Support**: Model I caring means "give approval and praise to make others feel good about themselves; reduce hurt by telling them how much you care and agreeing that others acted improperly." Model II caring means "increase the others' capacity to confront their own ideas, create a window into their own mind, face their unsurfaced assumptions, biases, and fears."

**Honesty**: Model I honesty means "tell no lies; tell all you think and feel" but carefully frame it to minimize threat. Model II honesty means "encourage yourself and others to say what they know yet fear to say; minimize distortion and cover-up."

**Strength**: Model I strength is "advocate your position in order to win; hold your position in the face of advocacy; feeling vulnerable is weakness." Model II strength is "advocate your position and combine it with inquiry and self-reflection; feeling vulnerable while encouraging inquiry is a sign of strength."

The paradox is that organizations espouse Model II virtues (openness, honesty, integrity) yet practice Model I virtues, then expect Model II consequences. Managers tell employees they care (Model I framing) but produce feelings of manipulation because the actual actions—framed to protect the manager—prevent genuine dialogue.

## Why It Matters

This distinction exposes why espoused organizational values so often diverge from lived experience. Well-intentioned leaders who claim to value honesty and openness while using Model I theories-in-use are not hypocrites but are trapped in a coherent system where the virtues they espouse cannot be enacted through the reasoning they use. Changing organizational culture requires changing which version of virtue is enacted—not just articulating better values.
