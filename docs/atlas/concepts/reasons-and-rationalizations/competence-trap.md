# Competence Trap

## Definition

The competence trap is a self-reinforcing cycle where an organization's historic success with a particular capability causes it to over-invest in and emotionally attach to that capability, making it resistant to abandoning it even when market conditions change fundamentally and the capability no longer provides competitive advantage.

## In the Book

Argyris illustrates this concept through the Intel case (Chapter 3). Intel dominated the early DRAM (dynamic random access memory) business by leveraging distinctive technical competence in design and manufacturing. This success created emotional attachment to DRAMs among executives and reinforced organizational routines built around DRAM strategy. When the DRAM business became commoditized—shifting from premium-priced innovation to commodity pricing—Intel's traditional competitive advantage (being first to market with premium products) became obsolete. Yet Intel's top management persisted in believing they could regain the lead by applying their traditional process-technology strengths. Meanwhile, middle managers were taking actions that gradually shifted resources toward microprocessors, but these actions remained undiscussable because challenging the dominant belief in DRAMs was seen as career suicide. The competence trap is reinforced by self-interest (business unit heads did not want to put themselves out of business) and emotional attachment (the company's identity was "memory company").

## Why It Matters

The competence trap reveals a paradox: the very organizational features that create success—focused investment, emotional commitment, reinforced routines—become liabilities when conditions change. It explains why successful organizations often fail to adapt not due to lack of capability or intelligence, but due to the defensive reasoning that protects past patterns. Breaking the trap requires not just recognizing new opportunities but first becoming aware that the organization is trapped, which requires double-loop learning.
