# Implementable Validity

## Definition

Implementable validity is the test of whether knowledge can actually be put into practice by real actors in organizational contexts to produce the intended consequences. It goes beyond internal validity (the model is logically sound) and external validity (findings generalize across contexts) to ask: Can people actually use this knowledge to change behavior and outcomes?

## In the Book

Argyris argues that scholarly research on organizations typically achieves internal and external validity but largely ignores implementable validity. Scholars describe organizational problems—like defensive routines, inhibited learning, and counterproductive reasoning—without conducting research on how to overcome these problems in ways that practitioners can actually implement. The book documents how managers and scholars espouse theories (like "be more collaborative") but their theory-in-use (Model I reasoning) makes implementation impossible. For example, executives launch programs to increase openness and learning, yet the Model I theories-in-use that dominate design and execution undermine the stated intent. Implementable validity requires not just valid models but also: actors who intend to implement, actors with skills to do so, and organizational contexts that reward such implementation (Chapters 1, 5).

## Why It Matters

Implementable validity elevates knowledge quality from "correct but useless" to "correct and actionable." It forces a hard question: If scholarship identifies a problem, does it bear responsibility to research solutions? Without implementable validity as a research standard, organizations accumulate sophisticated descriptions of dysfunction but lack prescriptive knowledge of how to escape it—leaving practitioners stuck in the very patterns scholars document.
