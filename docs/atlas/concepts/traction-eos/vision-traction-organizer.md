# The Vision/Traction Organizer (V/TO): Strategic Clarity Tool

## Definition

The V/TO is a one-page document that distills an organization's strategic vision into eight clear sections: (1) Core Values (3–7 guiding principles), (2) Core Focus (Purpose/Passion + Niche—what you do best), (3) 10-Year Target (audacious long-term goal), (4) 3-Year Picture (medium-term vision), (5) 1-Year Plan (annual goals), (6) Marketing Strategy (Target Market, Three Uniques, Proven Process, Guarantee), (7) Scorecard (leading indicators), and (8) Issues List. The V/TO serves as a filter: every decision, hire, customer, and initiative is evaluated against it. If something doesn't fit, it gets rejected.

## In the Book

Wickman opens Chapter 3 with a laser-beam analogy from Al Ries's *Focus*: the sun provides billions of kilowatts of energy, yet standing in it causes only sunburn. A laser—a few watts focused in one direction—cuts through diamonds. The V/TO is how you become a laser beam. Wickman emphasizes that most leadership teams are not on the same page; if you ask each person where the company is going, you get wildly different answers. The V/TO forces alignment.

Core Values must come from the heart, involve everyone, and not be about money. Examples: "Integrity," "Customer Delight," "Winning." The Core Focus combines Purpose/Passion/Cause (why you exist) with Niche (what you do best); Wickman cites Orville Redenbacher's principle: "Do one thing and do it better than anyone." The 10-Year Target draws on Jim Collins's concept of BHAGs (Big, Hairy, Audacious Goals). Examples: "20,000 multifamily units owned and/or managed" (McKinley), "$10 million in revenue with 15% net income" (ZenaComp).

The V/TO is reviewed and updated quarterly; it serves as the foundation for Rock-setting and issue prioritization. Wickman notes that once you have a clear V/TO, the marketing strategy (Target Market, Three Uniques, Proven Process, Guarantee) almost writes itself.

## Why It Matters

Strategic clarity is the foundation for all other EOS tools. Without a V/TO, teams make decisions based on assumptions, personalities, and politics rather than shared direction. With a V/TO, every person knows the target and can align their work to it. It also acts as a filter: opportunities and crises that don't fit the vision can be rejected quickly. The V/TO is not static; it's reviewed quarterly and updated annually, but the discipline of having it written and shared is what creates alignment and prevents drift toward mediocrity.
