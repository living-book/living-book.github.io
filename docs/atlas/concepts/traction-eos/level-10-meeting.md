# The Level 10 Meeting: Weekly Cadence for Alignment and Execution

## Definition

The Level 10 Meeting is a structured 90-minute weekly meeting for the leadership team with a fixed agenda: Segue (5 min), Scorecard review (5 min), Rock review (5 min), Customer/Employee Headlines (5 min), To-Do List (5 min), IDS (60 min), Conclude (5 min). "Level 10" refers to Wickman's observation that most leadership meetings rate 4–5 out of 10; this agenda is designed to raise that to a 10. The meeting happens the same day, same time, every week; uses the same printed agenda; starts and ends on time. One person runs the meeting (moves people through the agenda), and one person manages the agenda (updates lists, ensures copies are printed). The agenda fits on one sheet of paper.

## In the Book

Wickman grounds the Level 10 meeting in Patrick Lencioni's *Death by Meeting*, which argues that great meetings must be "passionate, intense, exhausting, and never boring." The agenda was developed through 20+ years of trial and error with real clients. Wickman emphasizes that the key to a level-10 meeting is avoiding the trap of discussing issues during Scorecard and Rock review; those items are merely flagged, then brought to the IDS (issues-solving) portion, where 60 minutes are devoted to conflict and resolution.

Each section has a purpose. The Segue transitions the team from working *in* the business to working *on* it and reminds them of shared humanity. The Scorecard and Rock reviews identify problem areas without diving into discussion (which wastes time). Headlines share customer and employee news. The To-Do List holds the team accountable to commitments made the previous week (Wickham found this increased completion from 3 out of 10 to 9 out of 10). IDS is where the real work happens: the team tackles issues in priority order, with no politics, using the Issues Solving Track.

The book provides examples of teams that resisted weekly meetings ("Aren't things going well?") but afterward reported "Wow, I didn't think we'd have anything to talk about this quarter!" The weekly pulse creates a "spike" of productivity: people prepare for the meeting, driving completion of action items.

## Why It Matters

Most teams meet poorly or sporadically, losing alignment and momentum. The Level 10 meeting creates a heartbeat—a consistent cadence where the organization checks its pulse, stays focused on priorities, and solves obstacles. The fixed agenda and time limits prevent drift and decision fatigue. By front-loading reporting (Scorecard, Rocks, Headlines) and reserving the bulk of time for issue-solving, the meeting focuses on what matters: conflict and resolution. The weekly rhythm also combats the 90-day drift that Wickman observed; while Rocks reset quarterly, the weekly meeting keeps teams synchronized within that quarter.
