# Rocks: 90-Day Priorities as Organizational Focus

## Definition

Rocks are 3–7 specific, measurable, attainable priorities set for each 90-day quarter. They are the "must-dos" for the organization and each individual, designed to move the company toward its vision in bite-sized chunks. A Rock is not vague or open-ended ("improve customer service") but concrete ("hire a new customer service manager by March 31"). The discipline of limiting to 3–7 Rocks forces teams to focus on what is truly important, resisting the tendency to treat everything as equally critical.

## In the Book

Wickam grounds Rocks in an analogy from Stephen Covey's *First Things First*: imagine a glass cylinder representing your day, with rocks (your main priorities), gravel (daily responsibilities), sand (interruptions), and water (everything else). If you pour sand and water first, the rocks won't fit. Only by putting rocks in first—then gravel, sand, and water—does everything fit. Most organizations fail at this; they react all day to sand and water.

Wickham also draws on a cotton-picking metaphor: when workers faced acres of cotton, they would throw a stick as far as they could and "pick to the stick," then repeat. This 90-day horizon stems from human nature; Wickham observed across 900+ quarterly planning sessions that "roughly every 90 days, human beings stumble, get off track, and lose focus." The solution is to reset Rocks quarterly. Each quarter, the leadership team spends a full day setting 3–7 company Rocks, then each team member sets their own (1–3 for non-leaders) aligned to the company Rocks. A Rock is owned by one person; when ownership is shared, no one is accountable. Examples from the book: "Close $1 million in new business by March 31" or "Document delivery process and train all staff."

## Why It Matters

Rocks transform abstract vision into concrete, time-boxed actions. They combat decision fatigue and the "everything is important" trap that kills focus. The quarterly cadence aligns with human psychology: enough time to make meaningful progress, not so long that momentum fades. By limiting Rocks to 3–7 and making each specific and measurable, teams shift from reactive (responding to whatever landed this week) to proactive (executing toward defined priorities). This is the mechanism that converts strategy into traction—the gap where most organizations fail.
