# Six Key Components of the Entrepreneurial Operating System

## Definition

The Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS) identifies six foundational components that every successful organization must master: Vision (clarity of direction), People (right team in right seats), Data (measurable metrics), Issues (problem-solving discipline), Process (documented systems), and Traction (execution and accountability). These components work together as an integrated system; strength in all six transforms a struggling organization into a well-oiled machine.

## In the Book

Wickman opens *Traction* by arguing that most entrepreneurs worry about a hundred different issues, but "once I learned there were six components to my business and I focused on only those, those 100 different things I'd been worrying about went away." The book devotes one chapter to each component, teaching concrete tools for each. The premise is grounded in Wickman's 20+ years of hands-on experience and over 10,000 hours working with leadership teams across 120+ organizations. Successful organizations—regardless of industry or size—naturally strengthen these six areas; the book's contribution is making that pattern explicit and teachable.

The six components are not independent. Vision gives direction; People and Process execute it; Data monitors progress; Issues solves obstacles; Traction creates accountability. Most organizations operate below 50% on this measure; achieving over 80% turns a company into what Wickman calls a "well-oiled machine." The Organizational Checkup at the end of Chapter 1 provides a 20-item self-assessment tied directly to these components.

## Why It Matters

This framework provides a diagnostic tool for any leader: instead of chasing the latest management fad or feeling lost in a sea of problems, you have six clear buckets to work on. It shifts the conversation from "what's wrong with my business?" to "which of these six components are weak?" This makes improvement systematic rather than reactive, allowing leaders to allocate effort where it will have the most leverage. The model also applies across industries and organization sizes, making it portable across different business contexts.
