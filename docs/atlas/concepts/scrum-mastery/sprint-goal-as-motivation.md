# Sprint Goal as Purpose and Creativity Driver

## Definition

Sprint goals are the forgotten practice of Scrum. Teams routinely commit to individual product backlog items but skip the step of crafting a goal that ties them together. A good ScrumMaster ensures the team delivers items. A great one harnesses the power of a sprint goal to deliver more value and build energy, engagement, creativity, and effectiveness into the sprint.

The mechanism: a sprint goal frames work with shared purpose. It allows the team to look beyond top-priority items and find synergies—related features that, bundled together, deliver more total value than the highest-priority items tackled in isolation. A motivating, inspiring, and compelling sprint goal is a form of purpose, one of the three elements of motivation (alongside autonomy and mastery).

## In the Book

Watts tells the story of Team Speedwagon and their ScrumMaster Spencer. The team had been reliably delivering high-priority items, and the product owner Greg was happy with the business value (measured in "Greg Dollars"). Spencer challenged the team in a retrospective: "How can we increase the value delivered without compromising your sustainable pace?"

The team split into three groups. One proposed re-valuing technical debt items higher. Another suggested simplifying feature designs to strip complexity. The third group, led by Sian, looked further down the product backlog and found three related features—chat, forum, Facebook link—along with items at priorities 7, 8, 11, 12, and 19 that fit the same theme. By bundling these, the team saw they could deliver a thousand "Greg Dollars" in a single sprint, 25% more than if they'd taken the highest-priority items.

When Connor observed, "It almost looks like the whole sprint could be summed up with the goal, 'Getting to know you,'" the team found its purpose. The goal had come bottom-up—from identifying synergies first, then crafting a goal to match. Other teams might start top-down: the product owner states a desired future state and the team chooses items to achieve it.

Both approaches work. Watts warns against forced-fit goals, where teams take the top-priority items and then manufacture a goal to fit them post-hoc. Those are usually uninspiring because the items are unrelated.

Watts also describes teams using metaphors—The Mars Bar Sprint (a little fun, a little technical debt, a little new stuff), the Paris Hilton Sprint (style over substance), the Nelson Mandela Sprint (sacrifice for the greater good), the Lady Gaga Sprint (handling eccentric requests). These metaphors serve multiple functions: they make the sprint memorable, emotionally engaging, and they communicate the sprint's character and focus.

## Why It Matters

Sprint goals are a lever for three interconnected outcomes: they unlock creative synergies in the backlog (teams can find higher-value bundlings than simple priority order), they activate intrinsic motivation (purpose is a driver of engagement for cognitively demanding work), and they build team cohesion (a shared goal unifies effort and creates narrative). The concept applies wherever work is modular but collective; any team facing a portfolio of independent tasks can benefit from periodically framing the sprint around a unifying theme rather than purely cumulative delivery.
