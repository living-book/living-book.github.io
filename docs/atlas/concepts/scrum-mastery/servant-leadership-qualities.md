# Eight Qualities of Servant-Leadership in Scrum Masters

## Definition

The book is structured around eight qualities that distinguish a great ScrumMaster—one who practices servant-leadership—from a good one who simply executes the mechanics of Scrum. These qualities are: Resourceful, Enabling, Tactful, Respected, Alternative, Inspiring, Nurturing, Empathic, and Disruptive.

These are not independent traits but an interconnected fabric. Resourcefulness means adopting an "art of the possible" attitude—assuming a solution is possible and making movement toward it, no matter how small. Enabling means helping others be effective. Tactful means picking confrontations carefully and moving change forward with sensitivity to the fact that current processes, however dysfunctional, were rational once. Respected means having integrity both within the team and in the wider organization. Alternative means promoting a counter-culture to command-and-control. Inspiring means generating enthusiasm and energy. Nurturing means developing both individuals and teams. Empathic means sensitivity to those around you. Disruptive means shifting the status quo and helping create a new way of working.

## In the Book

Watts opens with Robert Greenleaf's definition of servant-leadership: "The servant as leader." Servant-leaders serve first and then come to lead. Their best test is: "Do those served grow as persons? Do they become healthier, wiser, freer, more autonomous, more likely themselves to become servants?"

The book then develops each quality through stories. Resourcefulness appears in how a ScrumMaster who can't get a product owner's attention quantifies the cost of unavailability and uses that business case to justify the PO's time. Enabling appears in how a ScrumMaster asks the team, then tries to make themselves redundant. Tactful appears in how a ScrumMaster confronts a team's over-reliance on them without blame. Respected appears in how a ScrumMaster builds trust and credibility through integrity. Alternative appears in how a ScrumMaster challenges the status quo while remaining part of the organization. Inspiring appears in how a ScrumMaster creates an environment where teams want to improve and feel energized. Nurturing appears in how a ScrumMaster views the team as both a work-in-progress and a work of wonder with unlimited potential. Empathic appears in how a ScrumMaster acknowledges that people have needs and desires outside their roles. Disruptive appears in how a ScrumMaster shifts the culture from management by plan to management by inspect-and-adapt.

Watts notes that these qualities are not rules but the character a ScrumMaster operates from. They're not a checklist; they're a coherent worldview. A ScrumMaster who is tactful but not resourceful will be ineffectual. One who is resourceful but not empathic will burn people out.

## Why It Matters

The eight qualities reframe leadership as service rather than authority. A ScrumMaster has no authority to command a team; influence comes from being resourceful, respectful, and genuinely focused on the team's growth. This model applies beyond Scrum: any leader who needs to drive change without command authority—a change champion, a technical lead, a peer mentor—can adopt these qualities to be effective. The framework also surfaces a key insight: servant-leadership isn't passive. It includes being disruptive, confrontational, and determined. It's an active choice to serve growth, not to accommodate dysfunction.
