# Goalodicy

## Definition

Management scholar Christopher Kayes coined "goalodicy" — modeled on "theodicy," the effort to preserve belief in a benevolent god despite evidence of evil — to describe what happens when a goal becomes fused with a person's identity. Once reaching the goal is no longer just an external target but part of who you believe yourself to be, mounting evidence that the goal is unwise gets reinterpreted as a reason to commit more resources rather than a signal to stop.

## In the Book

Burkeman builds this around Kayes's analysis of the 1996 Mount Everest disaster, in which climbers from three expeditions ignored their pre-agreed "turnaround times" and kept pushing for the summit hours late, resulting in the mountain's highest death toll on record. Kayes's hypothesis: the climbers' identities as accomplished guides or high-achieving amateurs had become bound up with reaching the top, making the goal impossible to sacrifice even as the danger became obvious. He supports the mechanism with a 1963 study of an American Everest expedition, in which a subgroup that chose the risky, untried West Ridge route showed a self-reinforcing pattern in their diaries: as uncertainty about the route grew, so did their emotional commitment to it, because abandoning the plan now threatened their sense of self, not just their itinerary. Burkeman links this to modern corporate goal-setting culture — SMART goals, audacious targets — where a goal announced with fanfare accumulates negative evidence that gets reframed as a reason to double down rather than reconsider.

## Why It Matters

Goalodicy names a specific failure mode distinct from ordinary sunk-cost bias: it's not just that past investment biases you toward continuing, but that the goal has become load-bearing for your identity, so contradicting evidence threatens the self and gets processed as an attack to resist rather than information to use. It's a warning sign to watch for in any high-stakes pursuit — personal, organizational, or physical — where "we've come too far to stop" starts sounding like courage instead of what it actually is.
