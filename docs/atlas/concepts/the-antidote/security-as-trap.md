# Security as a Trap

## Definition

Security is both a feeling and a fact, and the two routinely diverge: measures designed to make people feel safer often make them less safe, and the pursuit of psychological security in money, relationships, or safety measures tends to undermine the very things — flourishing relationships, financial peace of mind, a felt sense of stability — it's meant to protect. As Bruce Schneier puts it, "security is both a feeling and a reality, and they're not the same."

## In the Book

Burkeman builds the concept around security expert Bruce Schneier's idea of "security theatre" — measures that increase the feeling of security without increasing actual security, and can even reduce it by consuming resources that could fund real countermeasures (like intelligence-gathering) and by lulling staff and travelers into false vigilance. His example: after 2007, Britain announced blast-resistant barriers for Liverpool's main rail station but not its nearby suburban stations, prompting the headline "UK Spends Billions To Force Rail Terrorists To Drive a Little Further." Burkeman generalizes from air travel to everyday life: cognitive biases (availability bias, fear of situations we don't control) make us fear car travel less than flying despite cars being far more dangerous, and this same distortion runs through financial security-seeking (money past a threshold doesn't buy more happiness), relationships (striving too hard for security in a relationship stifles it), and gated communities (which correlate with lower community happiness). The book quotes Brené Brown and the psychotherapists Hal and Sidra Stone on vulnerability: you can't selectively numb only the negative emotions — armoring against grief and fear also blocks the positive experiences that depend on the same openness.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates the feeling of security from its object, which is the trap most security spending, personal and institutional, falls into: optimizing for the feeling (visible, reassuring, politically popular) at the expense of the reality (often invisible, unglamorous, and where the actual risk reduction lives). It's a lens for auditing any security, safety, or risk-reduction effort — ask whether it targets the fact or only the feeling — and for recognizing that some vulnerability is the entry price for things (intimacy, honest feedback, flexibility) that guaranteed protection forecloses.
