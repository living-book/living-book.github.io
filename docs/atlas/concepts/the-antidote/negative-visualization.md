# Negative Visualization (Premeditatio Malorum)

## Definition

The Stoic "premeditation of evils" is the deliberate practice of imagining, in specific detail, how badly a feared situation could actually go — losing your job, your money, a relationship, even a child. Unlike reassurance, which requires constant renewal and can quietly reinforce the belief that the feared outcome would be catastrophic, negative visualization forces a collision between your vague, unbounded anxiety and a concrete, survivable picture of the actual worst case.

## In the Book

Burkeman draws this from Epictetus and Seneca: Epictetus advises that each time you kiss your child goodnight you should consider she might die tomorrow, arguing this deepens love while blunting future shock. Seneca pushes further, prescribing an exercise of deliberately living the loss in miniature — "set aside a certain number of days, during which you shall be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare... saying to yourself the while: 'is this the condition that I feared?'" Burkeman tests this himself and tracks down a modern practicing Stoic, Keith Seddon in Watford, who — caring for a wife with severe rheumatoid arthritis while himself suffering chronic fatigue syndrome — describes hardship as a "dark gift" that taught him the Stoic insight about the limits of control. The book is explicit that this is a distinct move from mere reassurance: reassurance says "it probably won't happen"; premeditatio malorum says "imagine concretely that it does, and see that you could still cope."

## Why It Matters

This is a technique for defusing anxiety about a specific, imagined future loss by rehearsing it in detail before it happens — different from a pre-mortem, which imagines a project has already failed in order to surface present causes for correction. Negative visualization isn't diagnostic; it's an emotional inoculation, useful anywhere unbounded dread (about health, money, relationships, reputation) is worse than the concrete reality it's standing in for.
