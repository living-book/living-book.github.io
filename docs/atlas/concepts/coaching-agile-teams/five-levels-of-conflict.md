# The Five Levels of Conflict

## Definition

Conflict is not one thing to be "solved" but a spectrum with an escalation path, adapted from conflict researcher Speed Leas: Level 1 (Problem to Solve — open, factual, collaborative language), Level 2 (Disagreement — self-protection creeps in, language turns general and guarded), Level 3 (Contest — the aim shifts to winning, language distorts into overgeneralization and blame), Level 4 (Crusade — factions form, removing the other side becomes the goal, language turns ideological), and Level 5 (World War — the only aim is to destroy, and almost no constructive language remains). Each level requires a different coach response; the same intervention that resolves Level 1 can be useless or harmful at Level 3.

## In the Book

Chapter 9 ("Coach as Conflict Navigator") argues that agile makes conflict unavoidable and different from prior project work, because team members now stay together for the whole project rather than rotating through phases — the conflict can't simply be waited out. Adkins presents the five levels with a diagnostic built entirely on the language people use: at Level 1, team members "check in with one another if they think a miscommunication has just happened"; by Level 3, statements curdle into overgeneralizations like "He always forgets to check in his code" and presumption about the other side's motives; by Level 4, "the overall attitude is righteous and punitive" and identifying with a faction eclipses identifying with the team. The book is explicit that Level 1 conflict — constructive disagreement — is not a problem to eliminate but "the level... that characterizes high-performing teams," reframing the coach's job as keeping conflict at Level 1, not suppressing it entirely.

## Why It Matters

A single word, "conflict," collapses wildly different situations that need wildly different responses — coaching a team through healthy disagreement is nearly the opposite move from separating two people locked in a destructive standoff. Reading the actual language people use (specific vs. general, fact vs. interpretation, issue-focused vs. person-focused) turns a vague feeling of "there's tension" into a diagnosable severity level, which is the precondition for choosing the right response instead of a reflexive one — a lens that applies to any group whose members must keep working together after the disagreement.
