# Powerful Questions

## Definition

A powerful question carries no hidden correct answer and no judgment — it is "truly open," sending the listener "into the realm of discovery" rather than toward a specific conclusion the asker already holds. This distinguishes it from an ordinary question that sounds open but is actually steering ("What are other options?" implies you think there are better ones); a powerful question instead asks something like "What is here that you want to explore?" The signature response is silence: people pause to genuinely reflect rather than answer on reflex, and the coach's job is to hold that silence rather than fill it.

## In the Book

Chapter 6 ("Coach as Facilitator") pairs powerful questions with "powerful observations" and "powerful challenges" as the three tools for improving the quality — not the content — of a team's conversation. The book supplies a direct comparison table (Table 6.1, adapted from Whitworth et al.) mapping common but weak prompts to stronger alternatives across concrete team scenarios: instead of "What's your opinion?" when a team member is holding back, ask "What do you make of it?"; instead of "What do we need to do to get started?" when a team has decided but stalled, ask "If your life depended on taking action, what would you do?"; instead of "Why does this keep coming up?" when someone rehashes an old story, ask "What is the essence of that?" Adkins notes the question is often followed by "dead silence" the first time a team hears it, and that this silence should be read as reflection, not failure — the coach should look around without demanding a response and let people speak when ready.

## Why It Matters

Most habitual questions in a group setting are disguised statements or requests for justification, and they train people to answer defensively or performatively. A question engineered to have no correct answer changes what a group does with silence — instead of rushing to fill it with the expected response, people actually think — which is a technique transferable to any facilitation context: interviewing, therapy, retrospectives, or simply steering a stuck conversation without supplying the answer yourself.
