# Coach Failure Modes

## Definition

Coaches habitually fall into a small set of recognizable, over-helpful traps, each caricatured with a name: the Spy (observes just enough to harvest retrospective material and vanishes), the Seagull (swoops into stand-up, "poops" unsolicited advice, flies off), the Opinionator (states opinions and gets attached to them, losing coaching objectivity), the Admin (does meeting logistics for the team, undermining their ownership), the Hub (becomes the center of all team communication and coordination), the Butterfly (drifts between teams dropping pearls of wisdom), the Expert (gets so absorbed in technical detail that "the forest gets lost in the trees"), and the Nag (constantly reminds the team of things they committed to). None of these does damage in isolation; done habitually, they make the coach central to the team's operation — "the center of anything is the wrong place for a coach to be" — and quietly erode the team's capacity to self-organize.

## In the Book

Chapter 11 presents all eight modes as tongue-in-cheek personas, explicitly asking readers to "approach this chapter with a light heart" because they are "gross exaggerations" meant to reveal a truth people can laugh at rather than deny. Adkins traces their origin to two root causes: an unchecked ego running "'I'-focused" thinking ("Why can't they see what I see? What will people think of me?") until it curdles into fear-driven overcontrol, and "continuous partial attention" — a term Adkins borrows to describe an evolutionarily novel state of divided attention the human nervous system isn't built to sustain. The Hub, Admin, Opinionator, Expert, and Nag are named as the ego-driven cluster specifically. Mike Cohn's foreword personally confirms the pattern's grounding, admitting he fell into the Expert and Hub modes early in his career and still catches himself in the Opinionator.

## Why It Matters

Giving a failure a specific, slightly embarrassing name (rather than a vague "I was too involved") makes it recognizable in the moment, which is the entire mechanism of behavior change the book proposes — you can't stop what you can't first spot. The deeper move — tracing all eight surface behaviors back to two shared root causes, ego and divided attention — means fixing the underlying condition addresses every symptom at once, a pattern useful anywhere a helper's good intentions become the bottleneck they were meant to remove (management, teaching, parenting, technical leadership).
