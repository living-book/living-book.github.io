# Problem Formulation Shapes the Solution Space

## Definition

Ackoff argues that a decision maker never confronts a problem directly, only their own conception of it, and "if his conception is wrong, the solution to the problem as conceived may not solve the problem as it exists": "it has often been observed that we more frequently fail to face the right problem than fail to solve the problem we face." Two specific formulation errors recur throughout the book: cutting a problem down to a scope narrow enough to look tractable, and reflexively hunting for the cause of a deficiency to suppress rather than considering what would actually satisfy the underlying want.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 grounds this with two paired fables. In "A Fishy Story," a company freezing fish at sea keeps losing flavor; each fix (freezing on board, keeping fish alive in tanks) fails until a fish expert points out the fish need to move, which is solved by adding a predator to their tank — the real fix required enlarging the system under study (predator included) rather than narrowing it further, since "simplifying a problem can make it more difficult to solve." In "An Ups-and-Downs Story," consultants analyze an office building's slow-elevator complaints purely as a throughput problem and find every fix too expensive to justify — until a junior staffer reframes the deficiency as boredom during the wait, not wait-time itself, and mirrors installed in the elevator lobbies eliminate the complaints at a fraction of the cost. Ackoff draws the general lesson that removing a deficiency's cause and satisfying the underlying want are not the same move, and that formulation errors are invisible from inside the frame that produced them — which is also why interdisciplinary teams (as opposed to problems merely divided up among disciplines) tend to out-solve individuals: each background notices controllable variables the others' training filters out.

## Why It Matters

Before spending effort optimizing within a frame, it's worth asking whether the frame itself is the obstacle: a narrower system boundary or an unexamined "the problem is X" often forecloses the cheap solution, while widening the boundary (the fish tank) or re-locating the deficiency (the elevator wait) can dissolve a problem that looked expensive to solve. This is a check applicable anywhere problems get inherited from a prior formulation — a spec, a department's mandate, a "known" root cause — before any analysis of alternatives begins.
