# Circular Organization

## Definition

Ackoff resolves the apparent conflict between hierarchy (needed to coordinate divided labor efficiently) and democracy (which implies control flows up from those governed) by generalizing the familiar corporate-board relationship to every level of management. Each manager sits on the board that oversees their own superior, alongside that superior's other direct reports and the superior's own boss — so "the conventional constraint requiring one-directional flow of authority, 'down,' is violated." A board's job is not to manage but to evaluate the manager who reports to it and set the policies within which that manager operates; management authority still flows downward for execution, but evaluation and policy-setting authority now also flows upward and sideways.

## In the Book

Chapter 9 builds the structure level by level: an intermediate manager (labeled 1.1) sits as a member of the board of their own superior (1) — ensuring communication and avoiding divided authority — while also being a member of the board that oversees each of their own direct reports, and each of those reports sits on 1.1's own board in turn. Ackoff traces how this gives any manager with subordinates two levels below them "an extended vertical view of the organization, both up and down," while the board of peers reporting to a shared superior provides horizontal coordination that a conventional tree does not. The lowest-level manager's board includes every worker under them (requiring small enough units for a genuine "committee of the whole"), and the chief executive's board is completed by elected representatives from each level of the organization — deliberately not union representatives, since Ackoff is explicit the top board is not the venue for labor-management negotiation.

## Why It Matters

Circular organization shows that hierarchy and participation are not actually opposites — the conflict comes from bundling execution authority and evaluation authority into the same one-directional flow, and separating them lets each run in its natural direction without dismantling the division of labor a complex task requires. It is a reusable move wherever a group wants accountability to run toward the people being governed without giving up the coordination benefits of a chain of command.
