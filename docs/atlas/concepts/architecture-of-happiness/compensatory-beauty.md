# Compensatory Beauty

## Definition

Taste is not arbitrary, but it is not universal either: what a person or a culture finds beautiful reveals what they are inwardly missing. "The buildings we call beautiful contain in a concentrated form those qualities in which we are deficient." A whitewashed, rigidly ordered loft will appeal to someone oppressed by intimations of chaos; a rough, unadorned building of black brick and rusted steel will appeal to someone fleeing feelings of excessive privilege.

## In the Book

De Botton's central case study is Le Corbusier's 1925 workers' housing at Pessac, built in his signature spare, geometric Modernist style. Within years, the working-class tenants — whose daily lives were already austere, orderly, and lacking in decoration — had painted the houses in bright colors, added pitched roofs, shutters, and floral motifs, converting the flat-roofed white cubes into something closer to traditional cottages. To Le Corbusier this looked like vandalism of his vision; de Botton reads it instead as evidence that the residents, whose lives were already saturated with the plainness and discipline Modernism celebrated, craved ornament and warmth precisely because they didn't have enough of it. He generalizes the case with Stendhal's line, "There are as many styles of beauty as there are visions of happiness," and extends it to the whole idea of "home" in Chapter IV: we furnish rooms with the moods — gentleness, gravity, calm — that our "predominant commitments force us to sacrifice" elsewhere in our lives, using architecture to rebalance what he calls our "misshapen natures."

## Why It Matters

This dissolves the false choice between "there is one true beauty" and "all taste is equally arbitrary and undiscussable." Once you ask what a person would have to lack to find a given style beautiful, disagreements in taste become legible without becoming meaningless — you can understand, and even respect, a preference you don't share. The same diagnostic move works outside architecture: a person's or organization's stated aesthetic or stylistic preferences are often a readable inventory of what they feel they're missing, not a neutral fact about what's objectively good.
