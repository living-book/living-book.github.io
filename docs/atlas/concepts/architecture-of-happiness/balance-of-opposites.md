# Balance of Opposites

## Definition

We instinctively read our own inner tensions — reason against emotion, discipline against play — into a building's formal oppositions, so that a building which successfully combines qualities we assume are incompatible ("democratic and refined," "practical and elegant") strikes us not just as visually pleasing but as evidence of "a distinctively human kind of goodness, or maturity." De Botton names this the virtue of balance: "the balance we approve of in architecture... alludes to a state that, on a psychological level, we can describe as mental health or happiness."

## In the Book

The clearest case is the interior of Skogaholm Manor in Sweden (c. 1790): its furniture is carved in refined, aristocratic Rococo curves, yet it sits on rough, unvarnished wooden floorboards "of the sort one might see in a hayloft," and its Neoclassical wall motifs are executed in muted greys and browns instead of the expected reds and golds — luxury and simplicity, normally treated as opposites (luxury reads as pompous, simplicity as squalid), coexisting without canceling each other. De Botton pairs this with Louis Kahn's Yale Center for British Art, which reconciles historical reference with modern construction, and Herzog & de Meuron's Stone House in Liguria, where irregular, mortarless local stones are held inside a rational exposed-concrete frame — "weaving a pattern of beauty from two aesthetic strands... which we would never previously have imagined belonging together." In each case, the building "proposes a new human ideal" in which the two poles are synthesized rather than chosen between.

## Why It Matters

This gives a name to why certain designs, arguments, or personalities strike us as unusually mature: not because they pick a side of a familiar trade-off, but because they hold both sides at once without collapsing into incoherence. It's a useful test for any artifact that claims to resolve a supposed dilemma — does it actually integrate the two poles, or does it just average them into something bland.
