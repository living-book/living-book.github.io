# The Contingency of Bad Architecture

## Definition

We treat built ugliness with a passivity we would never extend to other human failures, because a finished building's "mass and solidity" and "the lack of clues as to their origins" lend it "the unchallengeable conviction of an ugly cliff-face or hill." But a bad development is never an act of God or an unavoidable economic necessity — it traces back to "a pedestrian combination of low ambition, ignorance, greed and accident" on the part of a few ordinary people. "We should be as unintimidated by architectural mediocrity as we are by unjust laws or nonsensical arguments."

## In the Book

De Botton opens Chapter VI with an ordinary field about to be paved over for a bland new housing estate, then argues the outcome was never predetermined. His central historical case is Christopher Wren's 1666 plan to rebuild London after the Great Fire with the boulevards, squares, and symmetrical avenues of a great European capital — a plan Charles II praised but which died because the City Council, dominated by merchants "anxious over their tax revenues," judged it too complex, condemning London to "three centuries and more of inferiority." He pairs this with the counterfactual proof that beauty doesn't require special terrain, special genius, or special money: Bath and Edinburgh's New Town were built on "generic swaths of earth" by John Wood the Elder and James Craig, men who "did not possess a unique genius" but simply followed known principles with confidence and persistence; and Le Corbusier's retort that Louis XIV and Haussmann built with nothing but "picks and shovels," so modern cranes and concrete "leave us with nothing to blame but our own incompetence."

## Why It Matters

This converts resignation ("that's just how things turned out") into a live question of accountability ("who decided this, and could it have gone differently"). It applies well beyond buildings: any large, path-dependent, seemingly-inevitable outcome — an org chart, a legacy codebase, a broken process — usually has an origin story of a few ordinary, non-malicious decisions made under time or budget pressure, which means it was also always changeable by a few ordinary decisions made differently.
