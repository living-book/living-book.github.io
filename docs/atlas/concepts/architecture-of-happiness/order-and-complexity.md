# Order and Complexity

## Definition

Order alone is not enough to make architecture beautiful, and neither is variety alone. A building or streetscape that is perfectly uniform — "every window an identical square of reflective glass" — reads as tedious, while one with no organizing logic reads as chaotic. Beauty appears at the intersection: "insofar as we appreciate order, it is when we perceive it as being accompanied by complexity... a variety of elements has been brought to order." As the poet Novalis put it, "In a work of art, chaos must shimmer through the veil of order."

## In the Book

De Botton contrasts two neighboring Venetian façades on St Mark's Square: the Procuratie Vecchie, whose ground-floor pattern is "unimaginatively imitated on a smaller scale" on every floor above, versus the Doge's Palace, where no storey duplicates another's height or motif, yet everything belongs to a recognizable "family" — he calls the difference "like the difference between a monotone drum beat and a Bach fugue." He makes the same point about masonry: a wall in which "every brick seems alive, unruly and individual... yet all these disparate characters will settle side by side... conforming to the selfsame master scheme" is more satisfying than uniform brick. On the order side of the ledger, he defends the deliberately repetitive terraced streets of Bloomsbury and central Paris, product of old municipal building codes, arguing that "architecture should have the confidence and the kindness to be a little boring" — restraint, not perpetual originality, built the coherent Parisian street he opens the chapter with (the rue de Castiglione), which "reminds us that we can occasionally master our baser impulses."

## Why It Matters

This gives a portable diagnostic for why a design — architectural, visual, organizational, or informational — feels either sterile or overwhelming: check whether it has genuine variety, and separately whether that variety is legible as belonging to one scheme. Systems and interfaces fail in exactly these two symmetric ways — too uniform to hold attention, or too varied to parse — and the fix is never "more of one dial," but the two turned together.
