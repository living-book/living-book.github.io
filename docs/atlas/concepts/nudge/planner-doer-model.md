# The Planner-Doer Model of Self-Control

## Definition

Drawing on Thaler and Hersh Shefrin's earlier dual-self theory, the book models each person as containing two agents in ongoing conflict: a Planner who cares about long-run welfare, and a Doer who is present-biased, impulsive, and responds to immediate emotion and temptation. Willpower is not a fixed resource so much as the outcome of a running negotiation between these two, and the Planner's most effective tool is not exhortation but externally engineered constraints that the Doer cannot easily undo — precommitment devices.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 illustrates the model with the alarm clock: an optimistic Planner sets it for 6:15 a.m., but the sleepy Doer turns it off and sleeps until 9:00. The alarm clock "Clocky," which physically runs and hides after a set snooze budget, forces the Doer to get out of bed to silence it — a device engineered around the Doer rather than trusting the Planner's resolve. The chapter's central real-world example is Thaler helping a colleague, "David," finish his PhD thesis: David wrote Thaler a series of postdated $100 checks, one per month, to be cashed (and spent on a party David wasn't invited to) if he failed to slide a new thesis chapter under Thaler's door by the deadline — a bet that worked even though David's real financial incentive (a retirement contribution worth far more than $100/month) had already failed to motivate him. A parallel case, economists John Romalis and Dean Karlan's $10,000 weight-loss bet with themselves as enforcers, produced the same result. Both cases are framed as evidence that abstract, delayed incentives lose to salient, immediate, self-imposed penalties — and the chapter points forward to Chapter 16's StickK.com, a website built explicitly to let Planners bind their Doers.

## Why It Matters

This model explains why telling people the "rational" long-term case for a behavior change routinely fails, and it reframes self-control failure as a design problem rather than a character flaw: the fix is not more willpower but a mechanism — a bet, a physical obstacle, a locked default — that changes what the impulsive, present-focused self is actually able to do in the moment. It is directly exportable to any domain involving delayed gratification: money, health, work, addiction, or organizational discipline.
