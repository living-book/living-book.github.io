# Libertarian Paternalism

## Definition

Libertarian paternalism holds that choice architects — private firms and governments alike — can legitimately try to influence people's choices in directions that make their lives better, provided that people remain free to choose otherwise, cheaply and without penalty. The "libertarian" half insists choices are never blocked or heavily burdened; the "paternalistic" half accepts that architects are not merely tracking preferences but self-consciously nudging toward outcomes people would endorse on reflection. A nudge, in the book's definition, is "any aspect of the choice architecture that alters people's behavior in a predictable way without forbidding any options or significantly changing their economic incentives" — cheap and easy to avoid, by design.

## In the Book

The authors distinguish this from both hard paternalism (bans, mandates) and the "Just Maximize Choices" position that treats any institutional influence as illegitimate. They argue the latter rests on a false assumption — that people almost always choose in their own best interest — which the book rejects using evidence on obesity, smoking, and retirement undersaving. They also name two misconceptions the position depends on: that neutrality is achievable (it isn't — some default or arrangement must exist, as with Carolyn's cafeteria), and that paternalism necessarily involves coercion (it doesn't — putting fruit before dessert forces nothing). The book frames the doctrine as consciously bipartisan, citing bipartisan Congressional support in 2006 for the automatic-enrollment savings ideas developed in Chapter 6.

## Why It Matters

This gives a principled middle ground between "let people fail on their own" and "force the correct choice on everyone" — a design stance usable anywhere an institution shapes decisions for people who are boundedly rational: interface defaults, health policy, organizational onboarding, even parenting. It reframes the debate from "should we influence behavior" (unavoidable) to "does the influence preserve a cheap exit," which is a testable, exportable criterion.
