# Default Effects

## Definition

For any decision point in a choice architecture, there must be some rule for what happens if the chooser does nothing — a default. Because of inertia, status quo bias, and what the authors call the "yeah, whatever" heuristic, a large fraction of people end up with the default regardless of whether it serves them. Defaults are therefore not a neutral fallback but one of the most powerful levers a choice architect has. The alternative — "required choice" or "mandated choice," where no default exists and the chooser must actively pick — is sometimes preferable but works best for simple, low-stakes, yes/no decisions rather than complex ones.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 works through the University of Chicago's benefits enrollment as a live example: whether an employee who takes no action should default to last year's contribution ("status quo") or to zero depends on the specific choice — status quo makes sense for health insurance (nobody wants to accidentally cancel it) but zero makes more sense for a flexible spending account whose right amount changes yearly. The chapter also recounts a real dispute over the No Child Left Behind Act, where the Defense Department insisted on an opt-out default (student contact info sent to military recruiters unless parents object) against school districts that had implemented opt-in — the same underlying rule, but the default direction determined the outcome for thousands of families. Chapter 6 documents automatic-enrollment retirement plans (e.g., Safelite Group in 2003) where 93 percent of employees took no action and were automatically enrolled, with only 6 percent later opting out.

## Why It Matters

Whoever sets the default effectively makes the decision for the majority of people who never revisit it — this turns "just add an opt-out" into one of the highest-leverage, lowest-cost interventions available to any system designer, and it explains why seemingly cosmetic policy or interface choices (opt-in vs. opt-out) produce dramatically different real-world outcomes even when every option remains technically available.
