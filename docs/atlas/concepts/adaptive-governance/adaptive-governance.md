# Adaptive Governance

## Definition

Adaptive Governance is the collaborative, flexible, and learning-based management of programs and portfolios at scale. It replaces traditional plan-driven governance—which emphasizes control through detailed plans, process standardization, and upward reporting—with a model that clarifies principles and boundaries, then trusts teams to make decisions within them.

## In the Book

The document positions Adaptive Governance as the evolution needed to overcome barriers to sustained Scrum adoption at scale. While agile methods like Scrum have enabled project-level evolution from plan-driven to adaptive management, organizations must now evolve from plan-driven governance to adaptive governance at the portfolio level.

Traditional PMOs are perceived as "process police" that block Scrum's success—they lack authority, focus on upwards reporting, enforce rigid process standardization, and suffer from staff-versus-line organizational structures that create overhead. Adaptive Governance flips this: Agile PMOs consider Scrum teams to be their customers and support them through project prioritization and selection, portfolio tracking using agile techniques, stable resource management, and sustained adoption support. The organization oscillates between centralized information discovery (where one role gathers and reports back) and network-based integration (where richly connected nodes hear everyone's opinion and make collaborative decisions).

## Why It Matters

Organizations scaling Scrum beyond single teams face a control dilemma: traditional governance models constrain the very flexibility that makes Scrum work, while no governance at all produces chaos and misaligned priority battles. Adaptive Governance offers a third path—clarifying the business constraints and values, setting portfolio limits (WIP, cycle time), and trusting teams to optimize within those boundaries. This preserves local autonomy while maintaining portfolio coherence.
