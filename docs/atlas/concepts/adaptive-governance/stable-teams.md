# Stable Teams

## Definition

Stable Teams is a resource management model in which multiple dedicated teams are each assigned to a single project at a time, receiving all resources needed to complete it, rather than fragmenting people across multiple concurrent projects. Stable Teams stress maximum project throughput over maximum resource utilization.

## In the Book

The document contrasts this with traditional Resource Management, which runs many concurrent projects with similar priorities and splits resources between them to "maximize utilization." This creates high context-switching and task-switching costs. Research cited by Clark and Wheelwright shows that workers switching between tasks incur hidden productivity loss—productivity drops sharply as task-switching frequency rises.

Stable Teams instead dedicates core resources to each project, ensuring each team has all resources needed to complete work. Teams may be focused on platforms or lines of business, with a platform owner prioritizing which project each team works on next. Result: teams support multiple business lines sequentially, focused effort delivers individual projects quickly, accountability is clear, and delivery becomes stable and predictable. The ROI is delivered incrementally with each project release, not deferred until the end.

## Why It Matters

Organizations measuring themselves by "resource utilization" often fragment people so thoroughly that task-switching overhead consumes more time than actual work. Stable Teams reframes the measure: throughput per project matters more than raw person-hours allocated. The hidden benefit is predictability—stable teams with stable composition establish patterns and rhythm, reducing ramp-up costs on each new project and enabling reliable delivery forecasts.
