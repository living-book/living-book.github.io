# The Deflationary Account of Meaning

## Definition

Baggini's stated method (announced in the Introduction and cashed out in
Chapter 10) is to treat "the meaning of life" not as one grand mysterious
question but as a placeholder for many smaller, unmysterious ones. Once you
separate the senses of "meaningful" — meaning as externally bestowed purpose,
meaning as a goal beyond this life, meaning as a life being worth living for
its own sake — most claims that "life is meaningless" turn out to be true
only in the first two senses and false, or simply not addressed, in the
third. Declaring life meaningless overall is then an equivocation: sliding
from "meaningless in sense A" to "meaningless, full stop."

## In the Book

Chapter 10 confronts Charles Schulz's cartoon line — "no purpose, no
direction, no aim, no meaning, and yet I'm happy" — and Camus's demand that
we confront life's "absurdity." Baggini argues both men are really only
denying two specific things established earlier in the book: that lives were
created with a purpose in mind (Chapter 1's verdict on naturalism) and that
purpose can be indefinitely postponed to a goal beyond this life (Chapter 2's
verdict on the why/because regress). Neither denial licenses "therefore life
is meaningless" once you admit the third sense — a life worth living for its
own sake — which the book spends its middle chapters defending. Baggini even
diagnoses why some (Camus's temperament) insist on gloom while others (Schulz)
shrug: not a disagreement about facts but a difference in emotional response
to the same facts, which he calls "existential snobbery" when it insists
distress is the only honest reaction.

## Why It Matters

This is a general debugging tool for arguments that trade on a word doing
several jobs at once: when a claim like "X is meaningless / worthless /
pointless" feels devastating, check whether it's actually true in every sense
of the word being used, or only in the narrowest one, with the rest smuggled
in by the word's rhetorical weight. It applies well beyond "meaning of
life" — to any debate (about work, art, institutions) where a single loaded
term is quietly carrying multiple, separable claims.
