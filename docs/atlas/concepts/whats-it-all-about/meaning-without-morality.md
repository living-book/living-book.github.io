# Meaning Without Morality

## Definition

If a meaningful life is defined as one that is worth living for the person
living it, a troubling case follows: someone could find genuine meaning and
purpose in joining the Gestapo. Baggini's response is not to deny the case
but to separate the two questions cleanly. Value-to-the-person-living-it is
a necessary condition for meaning, but it need not also be treated as
sufficient — morality can be added as a second, independent necessary
condition, so that a life counts as meaningful only if it is both valuable
to its owner and morally acceptable. Which route you take (deny the link
between meaning and morality, or add morality as a further condition)
matters less than seeing clearly that they are two different axes being
evaluated, not one.

## In the Book

Chapter 11 takes on John Cottingham's objection (from On the Meaning of
Life) that any account grounding meaning purely in what an "agent
systematically committed to certain projects" finds worthwhile ends up
calling a Gestapo officer's life meaningful "irrespective of its moral
status." Baggini grants the logical force of the worry but shows it doesn't
force despair: either bite the bullet and say meaning and morality are
separable — the Gestapo officer's life can be meaningful and immoral at
once, which is odd-sounding but not contradictory — or fold morality in as
a second necessary condition, making meaningfulness require both
value-to-the-liver and moral acceptability. He notes this isn't an ad hoc
patch, because his account already treats every human life as having an
equal claim to the good things in life, which is itself a moral commitment
sitting underneath the whole framework.

## Why It Matters

This names a mistake that recurs whenever people evaluate a choice, career,
or project along a single scale when it is really being scored on two
independent axes — is it good for me, and is it good, period. Recognizing
the two axes prevents the two easy failure modes: dismissing a
subjectively meaningful life as worthless because it fails a moral test it
was never a candidate for, or excusing a morally bad life because it feels
meaningful to the person living it.
