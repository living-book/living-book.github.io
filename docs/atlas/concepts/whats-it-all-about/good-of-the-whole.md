# The Good of the Whole

## Definition

Serving "the good of the species" is a genuinely different kind of value
claim than serving individual human beings, even though a species is
nothing over and above its members. Using Derek Parfit's ontology of
nations, Baggini shows that an entity can require nothing more than its
constituents living together in certain ways (so it is not a spooky
separate substance) while still being ontologically distinct from them (so
what's true of the whole need not be true of any part). A nation can be
under threat of extinction while none of its citizens are; a species can be
thriving while most of its members are miserable.

## In the Book

Chapter 5 opens with the moon landing: Neil Armstrong's "giant leap for
mankind" plainly advanced the species without making any individual human
being on Earth better off — which is precisely what separates "good of the
species" views from altruism, where the individual is the basic unit of
value. Baggini uses Margaret Thatcher's claim that "there's no such thing as
society" as a foil, showing the same logical move that lets her admit
families exist should let her admit society exists. He then borrows Parfit's
three-way distinction for nations (existence requires only citizens and
territory; a nation just is its citizens and territory; a nation is distinct
from its citizens and territory) and shows options one and three can both be
true at once — and applies the identical structure to species: humankind
could be numerous, dominant, and "thriving" as a species while the great
mass of individual humans are unhappy, because thriving-as-a-species and
thriving-as-an-individual are different predicates entirely.

## Why It Matters

This supplies a precise, non-mystical way to talk about collective
entities — organizations, ecosystems, nations, movements — that are made
of nothing but their members yet can succeed or fail on terms that don't
track any individual member's success or failure. It is the conceptual tool
for spotting when "the organization is thriving" and "the people in it are
thriving" have quietly become two separate, divergeable claims — and for
noticing when someone is smuggling one in as evidence for the other.
