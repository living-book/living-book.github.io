# The Why/Because Regress

## Definition

Ask "why" of any action and you get a "because"; ask "why" of that reason and
you get another, and so on. Baggini argues, following Aristotle, that this
"why/because series" must terminate somewhere in an answer for which a further
"why" is unnecessary, misguided, or nonsensical — something valuable for its
own sake, not for the sake of anything further. If it never terminates, nothing
in the chain is ever actually justified; if it only terminates in a goal
lying in the future, mortality guarantees the chain gets cut off before it
completes.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 builds the idea through a child's infinite "why" game (why do people
go to work? why do they need money? ...) and Aristotle's requirement that
teleological explanation "terminate with something which is an end in itself."
Baggini then shows the regress is not purely temporal — a restaurant's staff
justify their roles to each other in the present, not by appeal to past or
future — and uses a real dialogue (driving to Doncaster to scatter a relative's
ashes) to show why/because chains weave through past, present, and future
before bottoming out. The chapter's sharpest case against locating meaning in
a future goal is the "Can I die now?" problem: Sergio Leone gunslingers and
Dallas Cowboys coach Tom Landry ("there's always next year... if 'winning
isn't everything, it's the only thing,' then 'the only thing' is nothing")
illustrate that achieving a goal that was the whole point of living leaves
emptiness, because a life organized entirely around a future terminus has
nothing to justify itself once that terminus arrives — or, if immortal, is
"a donkey wearing a hat with a carrot suspended two feet in front of its eyes,"
never getting there at all.

## Why It Matters

This gives a general diagnostic for any system of justification — personal,
organizational, scientific — that seems to run forever without settling
anything: check whether it has, or even could have, a terminus that is valued
in itself rather than instrumentally. It also explains a recurring
real-world failure mode (the promotion, the exit, the launch that leaves
people empty once achieved) as a structural problem with locating all value
in a future state rather than in something sustainable across it.
