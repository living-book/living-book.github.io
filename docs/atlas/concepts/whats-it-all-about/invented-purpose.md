# Invented, Not Inherited, Purpose

## Definition

We assume that a purpose only counts as real if it was installed "from on high" —
by a creator, by fate, by cosmic design — and that a purpose we assign to
ourselves after the fact is somehow counterfeit. Baggini argues this assumption
is false: there is no general principle that a purpose is more real because it
was specified in advance by a designer rather than discovered or invented later
by the thing that has it. Dennett names the assumption directly: "the
trickle-down theory of importance — everything important has to get its
importance from something else that is even more important."

## In the Book

Chapter 1 opens with Sartre's paper-knife: a paper-knife has a determinate
essence because someone made it to fulfil a function, whereas a flint has no
built-in essence even though humans find uses for it — sharpening it, cutting
with it. If naturalism is true and there is no creator, humans are like the
flint, not the paper-knife: we have no essence handed down in advance, only
purposes we find or invent for ourselves. Baggini then uses the history of the
Post-it note to break the intuition that this makes invented purpose inferior:
the adhesive was discovered by a 3M scientist in 1968 with no idea what it was
for, and only six years later did another employee, annoyed at losing his
hymnal place, realize it made a useful bookmark. What matters, Baggini argues,
is not what the inventor had in mind but what the thing is actually good for —
and the same logic applies to a human life. He goes further: a predetermined
purpose can be worse, imagining a Frankenstein-like creature built solely to
clean a house — a life with less dignity than one that gets to author its own
ends.

## Why It Matters

This dissolves a specific, recurring anxiety — that meaning must be "given"
by something bigger than us or it isn't meaning at all — by showing the
inference doesn't hold: origin-story and value are separate questions, and a
purpose's timing of arrival (built-in vs. discovered) says nothing about its
worth. The same move applies wherever people treat a late-arriving or
self-assigned purpose, rule, or identity as second-class next to one that was
there "from the start" — in institutions, careers, or relationships that
found their point after the fact rather than being founded on it.
