# Attention Engineering

## Definition

Behavioral addiction to apps and devices is frequently not an unintended side
effect but a purposefully engineered design feature, built by teams trained in
techniques for changing how people think and act. Newport draws on two
specific mechanisms identified by psychologist Adam Alter: intermittent
positive reinforcement (rewards arrive unpredictably, which produces stronger
compulsive checking than a reward that always or never arrives) and the human
drive for social approval (likes, comments, and notifications exploit our
built-in craving for validation from others).

## In the Book

Chapter 1 ("A Lopsided Arms Race") opens by noting that Facebook and the
iPhone both launched with modest, narrow ambitions — Steve Jobs demoted
apps and internet access to the last few minutes of his 2007 keynote, calling
the "killer app" phone calls — and that neither product's addictive
trajectory was planned by its creators. The chapter then grounds the
mechanism in two figures: Tristan Harris, a Google "product philosopher" and
Stanford-trained veteran of BJ Fogg's Persuasive Technology Lab, who quit to
warn that tech companies engage in a "race to the bottom of the brain stem"
and compares a smartphone to a slot machine; and Adam Alter, whose research
(detailed in his book *Irresistible*) traces the addictive design back to
intermittent reinforcement and social-approval-seeking, and who notes the
DSM-5 first recognized behavioral (non-substance) addiction as a diagnosable
condition in 2013. Bill Maher's line — "Philip Morris just wanted your lungs.
The App Store wants your soul" — frames the tobacco-industry comparison the
chapter builds toward.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates two very different explanations for a compulsive
behavior: personal weakness, or a system built by someone else to produce
exactly that behavior. Once a hook is understood as engineered — swipe-to-
refresh mimicking a slot machine's variable reward, notification badges
engineered to demand a response — the appropriate response shifts from
willpower to structural countermeasures (removing the trigger, changing the
default, or opting out of the system entirely). The same lens applies to any
designed environment — a game's loot box, a retail store's layout, a
workplace's metrics — wherever a designer's incentives and a user's
well-being are not aligned.
