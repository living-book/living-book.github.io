# The Attention Resistance

## Definition

A mind-set and set of tactics for using attention-economy services (social
media, breaking-news sites) without being captured by them: approach the
relationship with "zero-sum antagonism" rather than casual trust, because the
company's business model and your autonomy are directly opposed. Newport
describes discovering "a loosely organized attention resistance movement" of
people who combine high-tech tools with disciplined operating procedures to
"conduct surgical strikes" on these services — extracting a specific value,
then leaving before the service's engineered hooks can capture more time than
intended. The signature tactic is deliberate friction: for example, removing
social media apps from your phone while still allowing (more effortful)
access through a browser, so casual, unintentional use no longer has a
frictionless path.

## In the Book

The chapter traces the attention economy's business logic back to 1830,
when publisher Benjamin Day's penny-press New York Sun made readers the
product and advertisers the customer — a model Newport (via Tim Wu's *The
Attention Merchants*) follows through radio, television, and finally
smartphones, which let Google and Facebook "storm" reservoirs of attention
older media couldn't reach. He treats a 2017 Facebook blog post by
researchers Ginsberg and Burke — which conceded that benefit "comes down to
how you use the technology" — as an unintentional admission of weakness:
if users audited Facebook use by actual value received, the company
estimated at 350 minutes of use per week would lose the vast majority of
that time, a potential order-of-magnitude hit to ad revenue. From this
Newport derives the chapter's core practice: deleting social media apps from
your phone, since Facebook's own data show mobile access (88% of its ad
revenue by 2017) is what converts casual interest into compulsive, engineered
"stickiness" via mobile-only features like swipe-to-refresh.

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes "using a service in moderation" as an unstable
individual project fighting a well-resourced, structurally opposed
institution — and proposes that the fix isn't more willpower but engineered
counter-friction that matches the asymmetry of the fight. It generalizes to
any relationship between an individual and a system whose designers profit
from behavior the individual doesn't actually want: gambling platforms, app
notification design, or algorithmic feeds in general, where "just be
disciplined" reliably loses to "make the harmful path structurally
inconvenient."
