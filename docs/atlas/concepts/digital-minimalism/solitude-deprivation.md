# Solitude Deprivation

## Definition

Newport defines solitude, following judge Raymond Kethledge and army veteran
Michael Erwin's book *Lead Yourself First*, as "a subjective state in which
your mind is free from input from other minds" — a condition you can meet in
a crowded coffee shop and lose while alone in a quiet room if a screen, book,
or podcast fills the gap. Solitude deprivation is then "a state in which you
spend close to zero time alone with your own thoughts and free from input
from other minds." Before ubiquitous smartphones, ordinary friction — waiting
in line, a walk, yard work — forced regular unplanned solitude on everyone;
that friction has now been engineered away.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 opens with Abraham Lincoln's habit of commuting nightly to a
cottage at the Soldiers' Home outside wartime Washington, where he was rarely
literally alone but was reliably free from the White House's crush of office-
seekers — solitude enabled him to draft the Emancipation Proclamation there
and, historians argue, work through the Gettysburg Address in the preceding
weeks. Martin Luther King Jr.'s "kitchen table" moment of resolve during the
Montgomery bus boycott is offered as a second case. Newport then argues that
young people born after 1995, the first cohort to enter adolescence with
constant smartphone access, function as a "cognitive canary in the coal
mine": Common Sense Media found this group consuming media roughly nine
hours a day, and Newport traces subsequent research linking this constant
input to spikes in anxiety and depression — evidence, he argues, that chronic
solitude deprivation has measurable costs, not just an abstract loss.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates "alone" (a physical fact about who else is in the
room) from "solitude" (a cognitive fact about whether your mind is processing
its own thoughts or someone else's inputs) — a distinction that explains why
a silent room with a phone in reach provides no solitude at all, while a
noisy commute without one can. It reframes constant availability and
connectivity — usually sold as an unambiguous good — as a cost with a
specific mechanism: the loss of the unhurried self-reflection that supports
clarifying hard problems, regulating emotion, and building the moral courage
Newport ties to both Lincoln and King.
