# Life Is Not Short, It Is Wasted

## Definition

The universal complaint that life is too short is, for Seneca, a category error: "It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a lot of it." A given amount of time, like a fortune, is squandered by a bad owner and preserved and grown by a good one. Life is long enough for the highest achievements — the shortage is self-inflicted, produced by how the time is spent, not by how much of it there is.

## In the Book

Seneca opens by cataloguing how people burn their allotment: insatiable greed, useless toil, wine, idleness, political ambition "always at the mercy of the judgement of others," the chase for profit, military anxiety, servile attendance on the great, aimless drifting between "ever-changing designs." He proposes a concrete diagnostic — an audit — imagining himself confronting a man near his hundredth year and demanding he reckon how much time was actually taken by moneylenders, mistresses, patrons, lawsuits, and disease, versus time spent on himself. The predictable finding: "you have fewer years than you reckon." From this he draws his sharpest distinction: a man with white hair and wrinkles "has not lived long, just existed long," the way a ship battered in a storm from the harbor mouth has not had a long voyage, just a long tossing about. He devotes the essay's examples — Augustus perpetually deferring "leisure" until a retirement he never reaches, Cicero calling himself a "semi-prisoner," Livius Drusus complaining he never had a holiday since childhood — to the same pattern: powerful, busy men who die still waiting to begin living.

## Why It Matters

This reframes scarcity as an allocation problem rather than a supply problem, which is exportable well beyond mortality: any resource that feels chronically short — money, attention, organizational capacity — should first be audited for waste and misdirection before its total supply is blamed. It also supplies a sharp behavioral test (existing versus living, activity versus achievement) for distinguishing motion from progress in any domain where busyness is mistaken for productivity.
