# No Place Is Exile

## Definition

Writing to console his exiled mother, Seneca strips the sting from banishment by attacking the premise that any location is uniquely "home." "There can be no place of exile within the world since nothing within the world is alien to men" — because the same natural laws, the same sky, and the same access to reason and virtue travel with a person everywhere. What actually matters — "universal nature and our individual virtue" — cannot be confiscated by a change of address; only "the most worthless of our possessions" are ever within another person's power to take.

## In the Book

Seneca builds the case empirically before he builds it philosophically. He walks through Rome itself — a city whose population is overwhelmingly immigrant — then through the barest, most inhospitable "places of exile," like the rocky islands Sciathus and Seriphus, noting that even these attract people "who linger because they want to." He surveys the ancient world's constant churn of migration — Greek cities inside barbarian territory, Tyrians in Africa, Phoenicians in Spain, Rome itself founded by a war refugee — concluding "nothing has stayed where it was born." He grounds the restlessness in cosmology: the human mind, "formed of the same elements as divine beings," is made of the same moving stuff as the planets, which never stop circling; wanting a fixed, permanent home is asking the mind to contradict its own nature. The chapter's target is his mother's grief specifically, but the argument is stated as a general claim about place and identity.

## Why It Matters

This separates identity and worth from location, giving a transferable resilience argument for any forced displacement — geographic, organizational, or social: what can be lost by relocation is bounded, because the load-bearing assets (capability, character, relationships to ideas) are portable by construction. It is also a useful check on any claim that a specific place, role, or context is existentially necessary — the argument asks what, concretely, would actually be lost, rather than accepting the loss as self-evidently total.
