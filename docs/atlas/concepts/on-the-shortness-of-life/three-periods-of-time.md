# The Three Periods of Time

## Definition

Seneca divides life into three periods — past, present, and future — with a counterintuitive ranking: "the present is short, the future is doubtful, the past is certain." The past is the only period Fortune can no longer touch; it is "an untroubled, everlasting possession" available for recollection at will. The present is a single instant, gone as soon as noticed. The future is not yet owned at all. Most people live as if the opposite were true, prizing an uncertain future and neglecting the one stretch of time that is actually safe to hold.

## In the Book

Seneca argues that the "preoccupied" (occupati) effectively lose the past because looking back requires either a clear conscience or leisure to reflect, and busy people have neither: reviewing a guilty past is unbearable, and a mind "harnessed in a yoke" cannot turn to look behind it. Their lives consequently "vanish into an abyss" — time poured into a bottomless container, with nothing there to catch and hold it. By contrast, "the mind which is tranquil and free from care ... can roam through all the stages of its life," calling any day of the past back for inspection. This capacity culminates in Seneca's picture of the philosopher, who "has grasped [past time] in his recollection. Time is present: he uses it. Time is to come: he anticipates it" — a fusion of all three periods that produces, in effect, a long life regardless of the calendar.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates the *feeling* of time scarcity from actual time available, by showing that one whole period — the past — is fully possessable and simply goes unclaimed by people too occupied to review it. It is a transferable diagnostic for any system (a person, a team, an organization) that treats memory and reflection as expendable overhead rather than as owned, low-risk assets — and it reframes "more time" as partly a retrieval problem, not only an acquisition problem.
