# Fleeing Oneself

## Definition

Diagnosing his friend Serenus's restless dissatisfaction, Seneca identifies a trap: people who are unhappy with themselves assume the fix is external — a new place, a new pastime, a new spectacle — and so they cycle through all three without relief. Quoting Lucretius, "Thus each man ever flees himself," Seneca adds: "But to what end, if he does not escape himself? He pursues and dogs himself as his own most tedious companion." The felt cause (the boring place, the dull routine) is not the real cause; the real cause travels with the person.

## In the Book

The essay opens with Serenus's own confession: not sick, not well, wavering between admiring frugality and being dazzled by luxury, between committing to public life and retreating from it — "an extremely peevish and quarrelsome" middle state he cannot name. Seneca answers with a portrait of men who chase relief through motion: "Let's now go to Campania" gives way to boredom, then to "let's explore the woodlands of Bruttium," then to the city for its noise and spectacle — "they make one journey after another and change spectacle for spectacle," restless the way Achilles turns from side to side in bed, never comfortable, using motion itself "as a cure." Seneca states the diagnosis directly: "our difficulty is not the fault of the places but of ourselves. We are weak in enduring anything." Some, he notes, are driven to their deaths by this pattern precisely because repeated novelty runs out and they are left facing themselves again.

## Why It Matters

This distinguishes a symptom from its cause in any situation where dissatisfaction is chronic and mobile — the same restlessness reappears in the new job, new city, or new relationship because the intervention targeted the setting rather than the disposition. It is a general caution against solving an internal problem with an external change: before relocating the discontent, this concept asks whether the discontent will simply relocate with you.
