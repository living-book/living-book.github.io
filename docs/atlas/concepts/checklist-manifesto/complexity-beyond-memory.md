# Complexity Beyond Memory

## Definition

Once a task accumulates enough interacting steps and variables, expertise alone stops being sufficient protection against failure — not because the expert is careless, but because human working memory and attention have hard limits that no amount of skill removes. The failure mode is not "didn't know how" but "knew how, and still missed a step under the actual load of doing it."

## In the Book

The book's founding case is the 1935 U.S. Army Air Corps competition at Wright Field, where Boeing's Model 299 — a technically superior four-engine bomber nicknamed the "Flying Fortress" — crashed on a test flight, killing two crew including Major Ployer Hill, the air corps' own chief of flight testing. The investigation found no mechanical fault: Hill had simply forgotten to release a new locking mechanism on the elevator and rudder controls while simultaneously managing four engines, retractable landing gear, wing flaps, and constant-speed propellers. A newspaper called it "too much airplane for one man to fly." Rather than requiring longer training — hard to imagine improving on the air corps' own chief tester — a group of pilots created a simple index-card checklist covering takeoff, flight, landing, and taxiing. With it, the same aircraft type flew 1.8 million miles without accident and the army ordered nearly thirteen thousand of them as the B-17. The book pairs this with Peter Pronovost's central-line checklist at Johns Hopkins: doctors performing a five-step hand-washing and sterile-technique routine they'd "known and taught for years" were nonetheless observed skipping at least one step in over a third of patients — again, not ignorance, but complexity outrunning memory under real conditions.

## Why It Matters

This concept explains why adding more expertise, seniority, or training to a system can stop producing returns — the bottleneck isn't skill, it's the reliability of unaided memory under load. It licenses looking for external, low-tech memory aids (not more schooling) as the correct fix once a task has crossed this complexity threshold, and it applies anywhere skilled people fail on routine steps precisely because those steps are, individually, too obvious to bother writing down.
