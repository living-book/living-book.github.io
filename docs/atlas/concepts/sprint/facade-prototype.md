# The Facade Prototype

## Definition

A facade prototype is built like an Old West movie set: realistic enough
from the one angle a customer will view it from, with nothing structural
behind it. The book's arithmetic: if a real product takes 100 days and
90% realism is "enough" to test, a naive team assumes it needs roughly
90 days to reach that realism — but a pure facade, with no working
plumbing, wiring, or backend, reaches that same 90% appearance of realism
in about one day. The team adopts a temporary "prototype mindset": trade
long-term quality for short-term simulation, on the belief that anything
can be prototyped, that the prototype is disposable, that you build only
enough to answer the sprint's questions, and that it must look real
enough to provoke genuine reactions rather than generic feedback.

## In the Book

Chapter 12 develops this through Daniel Burka's "Goldilocks quality" —
too low and customers won't believe it's real; too high and the team
misses the Thursday deadline chasing unnecessary polish; the target sits
in between and differs for every prototype (an iPad fitness app for
FitStar, built in Keynote with real acting and iPhone video; a clinic
lobby for One Medical, built by modifying a real physical space). The
book draws a sharp line between two customer states: while the illusion
holds, customers give authentic "reactions" — the gold of Friday's test;
the moment a prototype looks obviously fake, customers switch into
"feedback mode," offering suggestions instead, which the book calls
worthless by comparison. It also names the psychological reason
facades beat real builds even when time is plentiful: the longer you
work on something (a prototype or the real product), the more attached
you become and the less willing to accept a negative result — "após um
dia, você estará receptivo a feedbacks. Após três meses, estará
comprometido" (after a day, you're receptive to feedback; after three
months, you're committed).

## Why It Matters

The facade prototype names a specific fidelity target — just enough
surface realism to trigger genuine, unprompted reactions, no more — and
ties it to a psychological clock: commitment to an idea rises with hours
invested in building it, independent of whether the idea is actually
good. That reframes "how much should we build before testing" from a
technical question (how much can we build in the time available) into a
strategic one (how little can we build while our judgment is still
unclouded by sunk effort) — a tradeoff relevant anywhere the person
evaluating an idea is also the person who spent months making it real.
