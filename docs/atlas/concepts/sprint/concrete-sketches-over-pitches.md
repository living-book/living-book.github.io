# Concrete Sketches Beat Abstract Pitches

## Definition

Abstract ideas get systematically mis-evaluated: a good idea explained
poorly gets underrated, and a vague idea from someone persuasive or
senior gets overrated, because listeners fill in the missing detail
however suits them. Sprint's answer is to force every idea into a
detailed, self-contained sketch — words, boxes, and stick figures on
paper — before anyone evaluates it, so the idea has to work on its own
merit rather than on how well its creator can sell it. The sketching
itself happens solo and largely in silence, a mode the book names
"trabalho individual em conjunto" (working alone, together): everyone in
the same room, each person generating and refining their own concrete
solution in parallel, without the groupthink that group brainstorming
produces.

## In the Book

Chapter 9 tells the story of Byard Duncan, Blue Bottle Coffee's
communications manager, who worried aloud, "what if the person doesn't
know how to draw?" before sketching anyway. His sketch, called "O leitor
de mentes" (The Mind Reader), organized the online store as if a barista
were walking a customer through a conversation — greeting them, asking
how they brew coffee at home, then recommending beans and a brewing
guide. Built from nothing but boxes and text on ordinary paper, it went on
to win Friday's customer test outright and became the foundation of Blue
Bottle's actual new website — drawn not by a designer or illustrator but
by "the man who didn't know how to draw." The book operationalizes the
solo phase as the "Esboço em quatro etapas" (Four-Step Sketch): twenty
minutes taking notes on the room's whiteboards, twenty minutes of rough
"ideas mode" doodling that stays private, an eight-minute "Crazy 8s" round
of eight rapid variations to force alternatives, and finally thirty-plus
minutes producing one detailed, fully worded solution concept — modeled
on productivity writer David Allen's advice to break an intimidating task
into its first concrete next action rather than treat it as one
monolithic block.

## Why It Matters

The core claim generalizes past sketching: any decision process that
evaluates spoken or half-formed ideas is implicitly measuring
persuasiveness and status alongside (or instead of) merit. Forcing an
idea into a fixed, concrete, self-explanatory artifact before judgment —
and building it individually rather than through group discussion —
strips out both distortions at once: it can't lean on a charismatic
pitch, and it isn't shaped by the group's premature consensus. The
underlying move, make it concrete before you evaluate it, applies to
any setting where verbal argument reliably outpaces the actual quality
of the thing being argued for.
