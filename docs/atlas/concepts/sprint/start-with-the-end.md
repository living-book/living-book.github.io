# Start With the End

## Definition

"Comece pelo fim" (Start With the End) is a Monday-morning exercise with
two opposite moves. First, optimistically: the team agrees on a long-term
goal — what they want true about the business six months, a year, or five
years out — and writes it at the top of the whiteboard as a fixed beacon
for the week. Second, pessimistically: the team imagines traveling a year
forward and finding the project failed, asks what caused the failure, and
turns each answer — each hidden, unexamined assumption — into a specific
"sprint question" the week's work must try to answer.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 opens with Gene Kranz and Apollo 13's Mission Control drawing a
simple chalk diagram of the damaged spacecraft's path around the moon and
back to Earth — a single visible objective that kept the team focused on
solving problems in the right order (correct the trajectory, fix the air
filter, then land safely) instead of the first problem that grabbed
attention. The book then walks through two real long-term goals: Blue
Bottle Coffee's deliberately ambitious "distribute quality coffee to new
customers online" (rejected as too easy: "sell more coffee online"), and
robotics startup Savioke's narrowing of a vague goal ("more robots in more
hotels"?) down to "a better experience for guests." For sprint questions,
the book shows Savioke converting an assumption — "the Relay robot will
create a better guest experience" — into three testable questions ("Can
we make a delivery without a hitch?" "Will guests find the robot confusing
to use?" "Will anyone book a stay just because of the robot?") — and shows
Blue Bottle reformulating "customers need to trust our expertise" into
"Will customers trust our expertise?"

## Why It Matters

Every ambitious goal has dangerous assumptions hiding underneath it, and
the longer those assumptions go unexamined, the more risk accumulates
silently. Reframing a worry as a question does two things at once: it
makes the worry checkable against real evidence gathered later in the
week, and it converts an uncomfortable, diffuse anxiety into a specific,
almost exciting curiosity — the book calls this shift "da incerteza (que
é desagradável) para a curiosidade (que é empolgante)." The pattern —
state the optimistic target, then deliberately pre-mortem your way to a
falsifiable checklist — is portable to any planning process that would
otherwise proceed on assumptions nobody agreed to examine.
