# Design as Utility Plus Significance

## Definition

Pink defines Design, quoting design scholar John Heskett, as "the human nature to shape and make our environment in ways without precedent in nature, to serve our needs and give meaning to our lives" — and reduces it to a working formula: utility plus significance. A chair must hold weight and a brochure must be legible (utility); but at its most effective, the chair also carries aesthetic appeal and the brochure transmits ideas or emotion words alone can't (significance). Pink maps utility to L-Directed Thinking and significance to R-Directed Thinking, and argues that as utility becomes commoditized and easy to achieve, significance becomes the scarce differentiator.

## In the Book

Chapter 4 opens with Gordon MacKenzie's story of asking classrooms "how many artists are there in the room" — nearly every hand goes up in kindergarten, none by sixth grade — as a "cautionary tale" for a moment when, Pink argues, "we must all be designers." The chapter's central proof-of-concept is Target: a mass-market retailer selling Karim Rashid's Garbo wastebasket (nearly three million units sold) and a $5.99 Michael Graves-designed toilet brush — mundane, purely functional objects reframed as objects of desire once basic utility is assumed and cheap. Pink argues this is only possible "against a backdrop of abundance," directly tying the Design chapter back to the Abundance force from Chapter 2: once a product already works and is affordably priced, utility alone no longer wins the sale.

## Why It Matters

The utility-plus-significance formula gives a concrete diagnostic for any product, service, or piece of communication: does it merely work, or does it also carry meaning the audience will pay a premium for? It explains why, in saturated markets, functionally identical offerings diverge sharply in price and demand — the gap is significance, not utility — and it's exportable to any domain where baseline competence has become table stakes and differentiation has to come from somewhere else.
