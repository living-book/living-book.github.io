# Context Beats Facts

## Definition

Pink argues that as facts become "ubiquitous, nearly free, and available at the speed of light" via search engines and databases, the value of knowing or citing any single fact drops toward zero, while the value of the aptitude he calls Story — "context enriched by emotion" — rises. He frames Story as sitting at the intersection of high concept (it sharpens understanding of one thing by placing it in the context of another) and high touch (it carries emotional weight facts alone don't). As cognitive scientist Roger Schank is quoted: "Humans are not ideally set up to understand logic; they are ideally set up to understand stories."

## In the Book

Chapter 5 opens with a demonstration rather than an assertion: a "pop quiz" asking readers to recall a specific statistic from Chapter 2 (dollars in wages shifting to Asia) versus asking who "the John Henry of the Conceptual Age" is (Garry Kasparov). Nearly everyone misses the isolated statistic and remembers the story. Pink cites cognitive scientist Mark Turner's claim that "narrative imagining — story — is the fundamental instrument of thought," and quotes Don Norman: "Stories capture the context, capture the emotions... Logic tries to generalize, to strip the decision making from the specific context." He contrasts a bare fact ("The queen died and the king died") with the same fact turned into a story ("The queen died and the king died of a broken heart") to show what narrative adds beyond information. The chapter then traces the same shift into medicine (Columbia's narrative-medicine curriculum) and business (using story for persuasion once "someone somewhere will inevitably track down a counterpoint" to a bare argument).

## Why It Matters

This is a specific, testable claim about what happens to the value of information under abundance: once retrieval is free, the scarce resource shifts from possessing facts to arranging them into something memorable and motivating. It applies anywhere information has become commoditized — journalism, teaching, sales, internal reporting — where the differentiator is no longer what you know but how compellingly you connect it to what already matters to the listener.
