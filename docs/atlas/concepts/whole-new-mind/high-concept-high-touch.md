# High Concept, High Touch

## Definition

Pink names the two clusters of ability he claims are displacing pure analytic competence as the basis of professional and personal reward. High concept is "the capacity to detect patterns and opportunities, to create artistic and emotional beauty, to craft a satisfying narrative, and to combine seemingly unrelated ideas into a novel invention." High touch is "the ability to empathize, to understand the subtleties of human interaction, to find joy in one's self and to elicit it in others, and to stretch beyond the quotidian, in pursuit of purpose and meaning." Together they define what Pink calls the "whole new mind" the book's title promises — not a replacement for analytic ("L-Directed") thinking, but a required supplement to it.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 stages the shift as a three-act economic drama — Industrial Age (mass-production worker), Information Age (knowledge worker, L-Directed Thinking), Conceptual Age (creator and empathizer, high concept and high touch) — and grounds it in unlikely institutions adopting it: Columbia and Yale medical schools teaching narrative medicine and observational drawing; UCLA's Hospital Overnight Program, where students role-play as patients to build empathy; Japan's Education Ministry pushing "education of the heart" despite leading the world in math scores; and GM veteran Bob Lutz declaring the company is "in the art business." Pink also tracks the shift statistically: graphic designers up tenfold in a decade and now outnumbering chemical engineers four-to-one, MFA applications outstripping Harvard MBA admission rates, and McKinsey's MBA hiring share falling from 61% to 43% as the firm values other disciplines.

## Why It Matters

The pairing separates what's being displaced from what's replacing it, giving a vocabulary for capability that isn't just "soft skills" or "creativity" but two distinct kinds of leverage: high concept (seeing patterns, inventing, telling a story others will remember) and high touch (reading people, connecting, supplying meaning). It's a lens for auditing any role, product, or organization for which of the two — or neither — it actually offers once its routine, analyzable core has been commoditized.
