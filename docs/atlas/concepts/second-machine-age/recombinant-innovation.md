# Recombinant Innovation

## Definition

Against economist Robert Gordon's "innovation-as-fruit" view — that big
inventions get picked and their benefits gradually exhausted, so growth must
eventually slow — the book argues innovation is mainly recombination:
"building blocks" (technologies, techniques, pieces of knowledge) get
combined into new configurations, and each new combination becomes an
additional building block for future combinations. Because combinatorial
possibilities explode, building blocks are never used up; the binding
constraint becomes the capacity to search the space of combinations for the
valuable ones, not the supply of raw ideas.

## In the Book

The chapter grounds the idea in Kary Mullis's Nobel Prize-winning invention
of PCR, which Mullis himself described as pure recombination of existing
biochemistry techniques ("there was not a single unknown in the scheme"),
and in economist Paul Romer's "new growth theory," including his concept
of "meta-ideas" — ideas about how to support the production of other ideas.
The book then argues digital technology is recombinant innovation in its
purest form: Waze recombines a location sensor, a phone, GPS, and a social
network, inventing none of them; the Web recombines TCP/IP, HTML, and
the browser; Instagram recombined mobile photo-sharing with filters.
Economist Martin Weitzman's mathematical model of "recombinant growth"
is used to show that as the number of "seed ideas" grows, the number of
possible combinations explodes even faster — so the limiting factor shifts
from generating ideas to processing them, which the book argues digital
tools (and more connected human "eyeballs") directly address.

## Why It Matters

This reframes stagnation debates: if progress in a field feels like it's
running out of "big ideas," the recombinant view says look instead at whether
the number of combinable building blocks and the capacity to search among
them are still growing — because if they are, the supply of valuable new
combinations is not close to exhausted. It's a domain-general lens for any
system where value comes from combining existing components (research,
product design, business models, even culture) rather than from de novo
invention.
