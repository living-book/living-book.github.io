# Information-Bonded Systems — Culture as the Operating System

## Definition

Mechanical systems are "energy-bonded": once assembled, their parts stay integrated passively until an external force breaks them, the way two nailed boards stay joined until the wood rots. Sociocultural systems — organizations, families, communities — are "information-bonded": their members are purposeful actors with minds of their own, and holding them together is an ongoing achievement, not a completed act. What bonds them is a "shared image": a partially overlapping mental model of reality, values, and desired future that Gharajedaghi treats as culture's true content, functioning as a social equivalent of DNA.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 develops this from Buckley's distinction between energy transmission (mechanical systems) and information linkage (sociocultural systems), and from Gharajedaghi's own account (echoing Maturana/Varela's "to live is to know" and Boulding's "image") of how a shared worldview forms: isolated environmental observations are not enough for humans to act on, so people synthesize them into a unified mental image, and the portion of that image held in common with others is what constitutes a community's culture. The chapter's operational payoff is that culture behaves as a default decision system — "if you do not decide explicitly what kind of parent you want to be, the culture makes this decision for you" — and default values, once repeatedly used, get mistaken for "realities out there" that nobody questions. Gharajedaghi ties this to a deeper claim about self-organization (3.1): open systems are neg-entropic and move toward a predefined order only if they possess "a means of knowing" — DNA for biological systems, culture for social ones — which is why sociocultural systems pursue an existing pattern with tenacity even against active pressure to change it, and why bonding requires "second-degree agreement" (agreement on the *why*, not just the *what* of a shared course of action).

## Why It Matters

This reframes "culture change" from a communications or incentives problem into an information problem: you are not rewiring energy linkages, you are trying to rewrite a shared image that actively filters incoming signals to preserve itself. It explains a common failure mode — declaring new values without touching the tacit defaults that fire whenever no one explicitly decides — and gives a concrete test for real alignment (second-degree agreement on the *why*) versus superficial agreement on an action for incompatible reasons.
