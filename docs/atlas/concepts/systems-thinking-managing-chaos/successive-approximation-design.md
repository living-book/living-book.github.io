# Successive Approximation — Realizing an Idealized Design

## Definition

Interactive design has two phases: idealization (redesigning the system from a clean slate, as if it had been destroyed overnight, constrained only by technological feasibility, operational viability, and the capacity to learn and adapt) and realization, which Gharajedaghi treats as equally iterative. Realization proceeds by successive approximation: identify everything currently blocking the ideal design, sort those blockers into three constraint types with distinct removal strategies, implement the best design achievable now (a "first approximation"), and keep re-approximating as constraints fall away — rather than either freezing at the achievable compromise or waiting for permission to implement the whole ideal at once.

## In the Book

Chapter 7.5 lays out the three constraint types (Figure 7.7): Type I constraints cannot be removed within the existing framework at all, and require improvising a target design — a first approximation — that is implemented now while the constraint is continuously monitored so the design can move closer to ideal once it lifts. Type II constraints are removable but require extensive preparation — time, money, and knowledge — and their removal plan constitutes the strategic plan, described as usually the most resource-intensive part of the whole change effort. Type III constraints are self-imposed and behavioral — fear of the unknown, mistrust, alienation, entrenched interests that benefit from the existing order — and are addressed through candor, participation, and building trust rather than technical planning. Gharajedaghi is explicit that this is not a one-time proposition: "successive approximations of the desired state make up the evolutionary process by which the transformation from current reality to desired future is affected," and it may take several rounds before the design is actually reached. This directly operationalizes Ackoff's idealized design (starting from a clean-slate ideal rather than incremental patching) by adding the machinery for closing the gap between the ideal and the constrained present without abandoning either the ideal or the present.

## Why It Matters

Idealized design is often criticized as an ivory-tower exercise disconnected from what an organization can actually do this quarter. Successive approximation is the missing bridge: it makes the ideal design a moving target to be tracked and re-approached, and it gives a triage — technically impossible now, requires a resourced plan, or requires trust-building — for exactly what kind of work is needed to close each remaining gap, instead of treating "implementation constraints" as one undifferentiated pile of reasons nothing changes.
