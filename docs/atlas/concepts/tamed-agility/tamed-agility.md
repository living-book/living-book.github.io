# Tamed Agility

## Definition

Tamed agility is a deliberate middle path for software development that combines the flexibility and responsiveness of agile methods with the planning certainty and risk management required by enterprise complexity, contractual relationships, and organizational risk tolerance. Rather than choosing between strict waterfall planning or pure agility, tamed agility accepts uncertainty while establishing clear agreements and synchronization points that allow all stakeholders to work toward measurable outcomes.

## In the Book

The book positions tamed agility as a response to what Book, Gruhn, and Striemer call the "New School of IT"—characterized by three forces: mobility (users and applications becoming mobile), agility (need for rapid delivery and adaptation), and elasticity (infrastructure that scales with demand). These forces render pure plan-driven approaches "too rigid" for complex projects where boundaries blur between strategy and execution, and pure agility approaches leave clients unable to understand risk and cost.

The authors illustrate this tension through Boehm and Turner's dimensions: scale (large projects need documentation), criticality (safety-critical systems need verification), dynamism (uncertain contexts benefit from agility), personnel qualifications (domain knowledge deficits), and culture (different organizations have different risk tolerances). Pure agility doctrine fails at scale and criticality; pure plan-driven fails at dynamism.

Tamed agility is manifested in two concrete instruments: the Interaction Room (for collaborative scoping) and the adVANTAGE contract model (for fair risk distribution). The approach recognizes that software development is fundamentally a cognitive process subject to irreducible uncertainty, yet must satisfy business expectations for cost control and measurable delivery.

## Why It Matters

Tamed agility matters because it rejects a false binary. Most real organizations cannot operate on either extreme—they need to respond to change (which agility enables) while maintaining accountability to budgets, contracts, and strategy (which plan-driven methods attempt to ensure). By explicitly naming uncertainty as a design constraint rather than a failure of planning, tamed agility allows teams to build structures and contracts that work *with* uncertainty instead of against it, making collaboration more honest and outcomes more predictable than either pure approach achieves in practice.
