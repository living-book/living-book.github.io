# Interaction Room: Collaborative Scoping Through Shared Modeling

## Definition

The Interaction Room is a facilitated method and physical space where business domain experts, technologists, users, and project stakeholders collaboratively create and annotate visual models of business processes, data structures, system architectures, and user interactions. Sketched on walls in free (not formal) notation, these models are immediately visible and continuously refined during discussion, creating a shared mental model without the overhead of traditional specification documents. Different variants (IR:scope, IR:digital, IR:mobile, IR:tech, IR:agile) target different project contexts.

## In the Book

The Interaction Room method comprises several canvases: feature canvases (what features matter), process canvases (how business operations work), object canvases (what data structures exist), interaction canvases (how users navigate systems), and integration canvases (how systems connect). Each uses minimal, non-formal notation (rectangles for concepts, lines for relationships) deliberately avoiding UML and other formal languages to lower the barrier for non-technical stakeholders.

Stakeholders annotate elements with symbols indicating business value, user value, effort, complexity, uncertainty, risk, and other dimensions. These annotations are discussed face-to-face, creating explicit agreement on what matters and why. The finite walls force prioritization—no room for 140 object types, so 40 essential ones are chosen. Discussion recorded during annotation rounds becomes project memory.

The book illustrates this with the Cura Health Insurance case study (Part IV), where an Interaction Room:scope workshop brought together business domain experts, operations staff, architects, and IT contractors to model the benefit system's features, processes, and data without needing hundreds of pages of upfront specification. Later, IR:agile workshops tracked sprint planning, risk, and progress using the same collaborative model-based approach.

## Why It Matters

The Interaction Room solves a coordination problem: how to create a genuinely shared understanding of a complex system across people with different expertise and vocabularies (business, IT, operations). It does this in two ways: by making models visible and tangible rather than hiding them in documents, and by insisting on discussion and compromise rather than allowing each stakeholder to interpret text differently. Face-to-face negotiation of annotations ("Why is that feature high effort? What makes it valuable?") exposes disagreements early when they can be addressed, not late when they become costly change requests. The method works at any scale because walls impose natural limits on scope.
