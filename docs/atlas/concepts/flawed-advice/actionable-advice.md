# Actionable Advice

## Definition

Actionable advice must meet four criteria: it specifies the detailed, concrete behaviors required to achieve intended consequences; it is crafted in the form of designs containing explicit causal statements; people have or can be taught the concepts and skills needed to implement it; and the context does not prevent its implementation. Importantly, valid advice must also be testable—if implemented correctly, it produces the predicted consequences, persists when no unforeseen conditions interfere, and can be tested in everyday practice.

## In the Book

Argyris critiques much contemporary management advice as fundamentally unactionable. He examines well-known books like Steven Covey's "Seven Habits" and identifies gaps: when Covey describes delegating to his son, he uses abstract language about "trust" and "responsibility" but fails to specify what words to say, how to respond when the son doesn't perform, or how to handle the conflicting emotions that arise. When the son doesn't act, Covey must suppress his frustration while maintaining his positive stance—but the advice provides no specific language or behavior to navigate this contradiction.

Similarly, Argyris examines change consultants' advice to "be responsive" and "understand survival issues"—which sounds wise but offers no concrete dialogue, no specific utterances, no testable claims about what "responsive" looks like in action. Advice that relies on abstract end-states ("generate commitment," "empower employees," "create urgency") without specifying the exact behaviors to produce those states is unactionable because practitioners cannot reliably implement it or test whether they are doing it correctly.

## Why It Matters

The difference between actionable and unactionable advice separates advice that creates lasting change from advice that creates fads. Unactionable advice allows practitioners to avoid responsibility for failure—they can always claim they didn't implement it well enough. More importantly, without concrete behavioral specifications and testable causal reasoning, practitioners cannot learn from failure or improve. The ability to specify and test is what allows advice to be refined through practice rather than remaining trapped in the realm of good intentions.
