# Directions, Not Descriptions

## Definition

A hat is a direction to think in right now, never a description of what a person has already said or a category of person they permanently are. "I want your red hat on this" asks for feelings about to happen; it is categorically different from later saying "you were being emotional," which only labels what already occurred. Once people are sorted into a box — "she is black hat," "he is a green-hat person" — de Bono argues they tend to stay there, and any effort to compensate only reminds them of the label, sinking them deeper into it.

## In the Book

De Bono draws the direction/description line with paired examples: "I want you to look to the east" versus "you have been looking to the east"; "I want you to cook some scrambled eggs" versus "I see that you have cooked some scrambled eggs." He then applies it to capability itself with a race between a thin man and a fat man — the thin man wins on "what is," but if the fat man learns to ride a bicycle he wins on "what can be," and fixed descriptive categories only capture the first. He traces this to a direct contrast between Freud and Confucius: psychology since Freud has emphasized analysis — dig out the underlying personality and motivation — while Confucius, by contrast, laid out correct behavior toward colleagues, subordinates, superiors, and family and asked people simply to follow it, unconcerned with their inner psychological makeup. The Six Hats method, he states explicitly, follows the Confucian route: "If you are aggressive, no one is going to try to make you less aggressive. But if the yellow hat is in use, then you are to use your aggression in that direction."

## Why It Matters

Diagnosing and labeling a person's disposition tends to entrench the very trait being labeled, because the described identity becomes something to live up to (or resentfully live down to) rather than something to simply set aside for the next ten minutes. Instructing a concrete behavior for right now sidesteps that trap entirely — it asks nothing about who someone is, only what they do next — which is why it can redirect a chronically negative critic or a habitually scattered thinker far faster than feedback aimed at their character ever could. The same logic underlies any intervention that swaps "you are the kind of person who..." for "for the next five minutes, do this": performance reviews, coaching conversations, classroom management, and habit design all work better when they specify the next action instead of narrating the actor.
