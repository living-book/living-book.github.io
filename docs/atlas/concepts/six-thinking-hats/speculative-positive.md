# Speculative-Positive Thinking

## Definition

Being positive is framed as a choice, not a mood or a reaction: the thinker deliberately searches for benefit in a plan or situation before knowing whether that benefit exists. De Bono calls yellow hat thinking "speculative-positive" because plans and actions play out in an uncertain future, so a positive assessment of them is necessarily a speculation about value, not a report of a known fact. Crucially, the positive stance comes first and is put on like a color, rather than following naturally from having already spotted something appealing.

## In the Book

De Bono contrasts this with the ordinary way people become positive — most people are positive about an idea when they immediately see personal benefit in it, which he calls a weak, self-interest-driven basis that "does not have to await such motivations" under deliberate yellow hat use. He gives the example of a manager asked to find the upside in Kodak's move into the instant-camera market despite it being a competitor's decision: the resulting advertising will raise public awareness of instant photography generally, which could help their own sales too. He is explicit that the search sometimes comes up empty — "I am wearing my yellow thinking hat but I cannot find anything positive to say" is presented as a legitimate, honestly-reported outcome, not a failure of the method. And he ties the skill directly to entrepreneurship: entrepreneurs are people who "see the value that those around them have not yet spotted," because value and benefit are frequently not obvious at first sight.

## Why It Matters

Treating optimism as a switchable discipline rather than a personality trait or a byproduct of liking an idea means it can be deployed exactly when it's least natural — evaluating a competitor's move, a setback, or a proposal you already dislike — precisely the situations where genuine upside is most likely to be overlooked. It also legitimizes an honest null result: the search can be run in good faith and still find nothing, which distinguishes real speculative-positive thinking from forced cheerfulness or spin. This reframes "finding the opportunity" as a repeatable skill exercised on demand rather than a trait some people happen to have and others don't.
