# First Principles Design

## Definition

Expert designers approach problems by going back to first principles—fundamental physical laws, functional requirements, or engineering fundamentals—rather than relying on "how things are normally done." This means treating each design challenge as if solving it for the first time, not as the nth variation of an existing pattern. It is a deliberate strategy to avoid locking into conventional solutions and to enable genuine innovation.

## In the Book

Gordon Murray explicitly emphasizes this in his design philosophy. When designing a component like a suspension wishbone, he warns against the ease of saying "this is how it's going to look because that's what wishbones look like." Instead, he insists: "If you want to make a step forward, if you're looking for ways of making it much better and much lighter, then you have to go right back to engineering analysis. He says it is like always designing things as though for the first time, rather than the n-th time."

This appears throughout Murray's work. For the city car's suspension, he started with first principles: a tall, narrow car rolls on corners, so the suspension must counter that roll. From there he designed "a suspension with the least number of components and moving parts; this has only three parts in the whole suspension system." The "frog eye" headlight design didn't come from styling precedent but from functional thinking: how to integrate multiple functions (headlamp, parking lamp, indicators, heating element for the mirror) onto "one sub-loop with one plastic plug."

Kenneth Grange similarly grounds his designs in functional first principles, applying "form follows function"—approaching design problems "by trying to sort out just the functionality, just the handling of it, and by-and-large out of that comes a direction." In his High Speed Train design, he questioned the established, underlying engineering requirements (like traditional radiator placement) that had constrained previous solutions, going back to aerodynamic and thermal principles instead.

## Why It Matters

First principles thinking counteracts path-dependent lock-in. Organizations that inherit "the way we've always done it" miss the opportunity to redesign from fundamentals when constraints change. This concept applies beyond product design: in process redesign, organizational structure, or technology choices, going back to "why do we need this at all?" and "what are the fundamental trade-offs?" often reveals simpler, more innovative solutions than incremental improvement.
