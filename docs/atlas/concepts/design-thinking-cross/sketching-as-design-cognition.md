# Sketching as Design Cognition

## Definition

For expert designers, sketching with paper and pencil is not a means to document finished ideas—it is a primary thinking tool. Sketches enable designers to operate at multiple levels of detail simultaneously, shift between concrete and abstract views, maintain a searchable record of ideas and variations, and most importantly, provide interactive "talk-back" that stimulates further thinking. In team contexts, sketching becomes a language for non-hierarchical collaboration.

## In the Book

Cross identifies drawing as "the best cognitive aid for supporting and maintaining parallel design thinking." Paper and pencil sketching "gives the flexibility to shift levels of detail instantaneously; allows partial, different views at different levels of detail to be developed side by side, or above and below and overlapping; keeps a record of previous views, ideas and notes that can be accessed relatively quickly and inserted into the current frame of reference; and permits and encourages the simultaneous, non-hierarchical participation of co-workers, using a common representation."

Gordon Murray's design process exemplifies this. He begins with "a quick sketch of the whole idea, then if there's one bit that looks good, instead of rubbing other bits out, I'd put that bit to one side; I'd do it again and expand on the good bit, and drop out the bad bit, and keep doing it, doing it; and end up with all these sketches, and eventually you end up throwing ninety percent of these away." He also talks to himself through the sketches, writing notes such as "rubbish," "too heavy," or "move it this way 30mm." The sketches serve as a conversation with the emerging design. His team would eventually work at a five-metre-long drawing board so "several team members at once could gather round and contribute," using the full-size drawing as a shared thinking space.

For the city car, sketching revealed new possibilities. When a simple mock-up of wire and cardboard was built, "it began to suggest some new possibilities"—the team discovered they could seat three people instead of two, and that the rear engine could be accessed from inside the car, avoiding the need for a boot lid. The physical sketch generated insights the initial concepts didn't contain.

## Why It Matters

In digital-first work environments, the power of physical sketching—rapid iteration, ambiguity-tolerant representation, embodied collaboration—is often lost to high-fidelity mockups that close thinking rather than open it. Understanding sketching as a cognitive tool, not a preliminary documentation step, reveals why some teams maintain their problem-solving fluidity while others get locked into first-draft solutions.
