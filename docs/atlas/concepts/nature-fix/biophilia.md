# Biophilia

## Definition

Biophilia is the hypothesis, popularized by Harvard entomologist E. O. Wilson (the term itself coined earlier by psychologist Erich Fromm), that humans feel most "at home" in nature because our nervous systems evolved over hundreds of thousands of years attuned to natural cues that once signaled safety, food, and water. It treats the pull toward greenery, water, and open landscapes as a partly heritable disposition shaped by survival value for our ancestors, not simply a cultural taste that could just as easily have gone another way.

## In the Book

Williams opens the book with Wilson's hypothesis and the evidence for its evolutionary logic: preferences for certain habitat features (canopy cover, water, sightlines) show up consistently across cultures and eras rather than tracking any single culture's aesthetics. She notes that while no single "biophilia gene" has been isolated, related affinities are well documented, including evidence that the window for conditioned learning of biophilic responses is strongest before adolescence. The book uses biophilia as the evolutionary backdrop for everything that follows — it's the reason nature exposure has a physiological signature at all, distinguishing it from Attention Restoration Theory's more purely cognitive account of why nature helps.

## Why It Matters

Biophilia converts "nature feels nice" from a preference into a testable evolutionary claim with a mechanism: traits that oriented early humans toward resource-rich, safe terrain would have been selected for, and could persist in modern brains even in environments (offices, cities) those traits were never designed for. That framing is exportable to any question about mismatch between evolved dispositions and modern built environments — food, screens, or social structures, not just landscapes.
