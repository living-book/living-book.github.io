# The Phytoncide Effect

## Definition

Phytoncides are the turpenes, pinenes, limonenes, and other aromatic essential oils that evergreens and many other trees emit into the air — over a hundred identified in Japanese forests, virtually absent from city air away from parks. Qing Li, chairman of the Japanese Society of Forest Medicine, found that exposure to these compounds raises the count and activity of natural killer (NK) cells, an immune cell type that fights tumors and viral infection — a measurable physiological effect, not just a subjective sense of calm.

## In the Book

Williams reports Li's 2008 study sending middle-aged Tokyo businessmen on three days of forest hikes: their NK cell counts rose 40 percent, an effect that lasted seven days and remained 15 percent elevated a month later, while equivalent urban walking produced no change. To isolate the mechanism, Li locked thirteen subjects in hotel rooms for three nights, some with humidifiers vaporizing hinoki cypress oil and some with no scent; the cypress group showed a 20 percent NK increase and reported less fatigue. He replicated the effect on NK cells exposed to phytoncides directly in a petri dish, where the compounds also boosted anticancer proteins (granulysin, granzymes, perforin). The book treats this as evidence that at least part of nature's benefit runs through a genuinely chemical/olfactory pathway — closer to a "miracle drug," in Li's words, than a metaphor — distinct from the purely visual, cognitive route that American researchers like the Kaplans emphasize.

## Why It Matters

The phytoncide research shows that an environmental effect can be simultaneously psychological and biochemical — the same forest walk that calms the mind is also literally changing measurable immune markers through inhaled compounds. That matters for any domain trying to locate whether an intervention's benefit is "in the head" or in the body: sometimes the honest answer is that the distinction is the wrong question, and a single exposure operates through both channels at once.
