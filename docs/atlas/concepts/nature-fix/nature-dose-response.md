# Nature's Dose-Response Curve

## Definition

The book's overall structure — Part Two "The First Five Minutes," Part Three "Five Hours a Month," Part Four "Backcountry Brain" — encodes a claim: nature is not a single on/off exposure but a dose-response relationship, where different amounts and intensities of contact unlock qualitatively different effects, not just more of the same effect. Brief exposure changes mood; repeated moderate exposure changes immune and stress markers; sustained multi-day immersion appears to cross a further threshold into altered attention and cognition.

## In the Book

Qing Li's forest-bathing research supplies the low end of the curve: a single hour in a city park raised natural killer cell activity, though the boost didn't last as long as three days of forest walking, which raised NK counts 40 percent for up to a month. At the high end, Williams joins cognitive scientists David Strayer and Paul Atchley backpacking in Utah to test what they call the "three-day effect" — the claim, informal but consistently reported by wilderness researchers, that something shifts qualitatively after several consecutive days away from directed-attention demands and novelty (new gear, new terrain) has worn off: "After three days, there's just this feeling, ooh, something changes," says Atchley, with Strayer describing day four as when people "notice details" and access something like flow. Li's own practical advice at the end of his chapter — visit a park weekly, take a natural vacation monthly — is itself an explicit dosing recommendation, treating nature contact like a health regimen with a minimum effective dose rather than an occasional treat.

## Why It Matters

Framing an intervention as dose-dependent rather than binary changes what counts as "enough": it makes minimal daily exposure worth doing even though it isn't equivalent to a wilderness trip, and it makes the case for protecting longer, deeper exposures (vacation time, wilderness therapy, sabbaticals) as qualitatively different from — not just bigger versions of — a lunchtime walk. It's a useful frame for any resource whose benefit doesn't scale linearly with time invested.
