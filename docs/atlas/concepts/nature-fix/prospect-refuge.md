# Prospect and Refuge

## Definition

Prospect-refuge theory holds that humans have an inherited preference for spaces that combine "prospect" (a wide, unobstructed view for spotting opportunity or danger) with "refuge" (a sheltered, defensible spot to retreat to) — the layout of the African savanna where the species evolved, "fully, physically alive," in Williams's phrase. Experts note these habitat preferences hold remarkably consistent across cultures and eras, showing up wherever people have a choice.

## In the Book

Williams introduces the idea early, in the book's opening survey of biophilic habitat preference: children building tree houses and forts that feel safe yet open onto a run-around area; adults paying a premium for homes on a beach, a fairway, or a tree-lined street — "we all want our starter castles on the corner of Prospect and Refuge." She frames this as evidence that habitat taste is not arbitrary fashion but a legible, cross-cultural pattern with a plausible evolutionary origin, setting up the book's broader argument (developed through E. O. Wilson's biophilia hypothesis) that human environmental preference is a trait shaped by survival value, not learned from scratch by each generation.

## Why It Matters

Prospect-refuge gives a concrete, testable shape to "good design instinct" — it predicts which layouts will feel safe and appealing before anyone builds them, in architecture, urban planning, or even interface design (a wide overview plus a defensible home base). It is a useful minimal case of a broader pattern: preferences that feel like taste are sometimes decision rules inherited from an ancestral environment very different from the one they're now applied to.
