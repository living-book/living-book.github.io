# Accidental Nature

## Definition

"Accidental nature" is Williams's term for the nature exposure people get without trying — a tree glimpsed on the way to the laundry, a courtyard seen from a window — as distinct from a deliberate hike or forest immersion. The book argues this passive, low-effort category produces real, measurable effects on its own, meaning nature doesn't need to be sought out to matter.

## In the Book

The chapter centers on Roger Ulrich's foundational 1984 study of gallbladder-surgery patients in a Pennsylvania hospital: those randomly assigned rooms with a window view of trees needed fewer postoperative days, requested less pain medication, and were rated by nurses as having better attitudes than patients facing a brick wall — a result Williams credits with popularizing nature photography in dentist's offices and exam rooms ever since. She then follows Frances Kuo's studies at Chicago's Robert Taylor housing project, where residents facing barren asphalt reported more psychological aggression and mild-to-severe violence than those facing modest lawns and trees, and children with barren views showed weaker impulse control. A follow-up study of the Ida B. Wells housing project, using two years of police records across 98 buildings with varying courtyard greenery, found buildings with the most green views had 48 percent fewer property crimes and 56 percent fewer violent crimes than the least green — with Kuo's explanation that greener courtyards drew residents outside where they could see and know each other, not that greenery itself was pacifying.

## Why It Matters

This concept separates the effect of an intervention from the effort required to obtain it: a health or behavior benefit doesn't require sustained attention or intention if the environment itself supplies it passively and continuously. That reframes questions of design equity — who has "accidental" access to a beneficial exposure by default, without needing the time, money, or motivation deliberate immersion requires — as a lever distinct from persuading people to seek out more of something.
