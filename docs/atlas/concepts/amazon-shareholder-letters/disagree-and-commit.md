# Disagree and Commit

## Definition

"Disagree and commit" is a phrase and practice that decouples decision speed from consensus. It allows leaders to hear dissenting views without requiring unanimity before action. The person with conviction says, "I know we disagree on this, but will you gamble with me on it? Disagree and commit?" If the answer is yes, both parties move forward as partners, not as a minority who lost a debate. The person in the minority explicitly commits—not performatively, but genuinely—to the direction, and the decision cycle completes immediately instead of waiting for either consensus or exhaustion to resolve the disagreement.

## In the Book

Bezos introduces the phrase in 2015 as one of four pillars of high-velocity decision-making: "Use the phrase 'disagree and commit.' This phrase will save a lot of time. If you have conviction on a particular direction even though there's no consensus, it's helpful to say, 'Look, I know we disagree on this but will you gamble with me on it? Disagree and commit?' By the time you're at this point, no one can know the answer for sure, and you'll probably get a quick yes."

The practice applies to leadership too: "This isn't one way. If you're the boss, you should do this too. I disagree and commit all the time." He gives an example: he disagreed with the Amazon Studios team on an original series ("debatable whether it would be interesting enough, complicated to produce, the business terms aren't that good"), but the team disagreed with him. He responded: "I disagree and commit and hope it becomes the most watched thing we've ever made."

He emphasizes what the practice is not: "It's not me thinking to myself 'well, these guys are wrong and missing the point, but this isn't worth me chasing.' It's a genuine disagreement of opinion, a candid expression of my view, a chance for the team to weigh my view, and a quick, sincere commitment to go their way."

## Why It Matters

Disagree and commit solves a classic organizational problem: if decisions require consensus, you're slow; if decisions are unilateral, dissent goes underground and execution falters. This protocol makes dissent visible and heard while allowing rapid movement. It requires psychological safety (real disagreement must be safe to voice) and integrity (commitment must be genuine, not performative). Applied across organizations, it enables both decision velocity and authentic buy-in, reducing the exhaustion-based resolution of disagreements that wastes time and erodes trust.
