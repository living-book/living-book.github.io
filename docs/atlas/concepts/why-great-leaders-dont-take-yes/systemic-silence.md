# Systemic Silence

## Definition

When candor is missing from an organization, the instinctive explanation is individualistic — replace the timid or unskilled people, and the problem goes away. Roberto argues the instinct is usually wrong: silence is typically systemic, produced by hierarchical structures, status differences, rules of protocol, and cultural norms that would produce the same silence in a fresh set of people. He contrasts an "individualistic perspective" (which blames skill deficiencies and prescribes discipline or personnel changes) with a "systemic perspective" (which looks at organizational and historical patterns and prescribes structural changes — new forums, altered reward systems, new ground rules).

## In the Book

The chapter's central case is NASA's Columbia disaster. Engineer Rodney Rocha and the ad-hoc Debris Assessment Team grew alarmed about the foam strike during launch, but chose not to petition Mission Management Team chair Linda Ham directly for satellite imagery, out of concern it would violate the chain of command. Rocha wrote a scathing email about management's refusal to seek more data — and shared it only with colleagues at his own level, later explaining "Engineers were often told not to send messages much higher than their own rung in the ladder." At a follow-up meeting, despite deep reservations, Rocha again stayed silent while Ham repeatedly affirmed that the strike was not a "safety of flight" issue. Roberto opens the chapter with Jack Welch's diagnosis of 1981 GE, where planning sessions had become "rubber stamp" rituals, to establish the same pattern in a business setting before showing its lethal version at NASA.

## Why It Matters

The individualistic-versus-systemic distinction changes what a leader actually fixes. Firing or coaching the "quiet" people leaves the hierarchy, protocol, and reward system intact — and the next cohort inherits the same silence. Recognizing silence as a designed (if unintended) feature of the structure redirects effort toward the actual levers that work: who can bypass whose rank, what forums exist for raising concerns, and what gets rewarded when someone does speak up.
