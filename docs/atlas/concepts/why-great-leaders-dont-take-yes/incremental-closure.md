# Incremental Closure

## Definition

Roberto describes two pathologies groups fall into when trying to reach a decision: premature convergence (settling on one option too fast, without enough critical debate) and polarizing divergence (generating plenty of alternatives but never resolving the resulting conflict). His alternative to forcing one all-at-once vote is incremental closure — decompose the decision into its component parts, find the sub-question where common ground already exists, close it explicitly, and move to the next, using each closed point as a foundation the group won't reopen later.

## In the Book

The chapter's central case is Eisenhower's five-month process for settling the D-day invasion strategy among strong-willed Allied leaders (Churchill, Roosevelt, Marshall, Brooke, Montgomery, Patton, and others) with real authority and conflicting priorities. Rather than deciding the whole invasion plan in one sitting, Eisenhower sequenced the debate — the landing date, the bombing strategy, the use of airborne troops, and whether to simultaneously invade southern France were each resolved as separate questions. On the bombing-target dispute (strategic German targets versus French rail networks), historian Stephen Ambrose notes Eisenhower "began by acknowledging those points on which everyone agreed" before tackling what remained contested. When the group couldn't agree directly on invading southern France, Eisenhower first secured agreement on the resources needed for the main cross-channel attack — after which the case for delaying the southern invasion (a conclusion Eisenhower hadn't personally endorsed at the outset) became evident to everyone without him having to impose it. Once he declared a sub-question closed, he did not let the group reopen it.

## Why It Matters

Complex decisions often stall because people are trying to reach agreement on everything simultaneously, which multiplies the number of live disagreements a group has to resolve at once. Sequencing a decision into parts — closing the easy or already-agreed pieces first — reduces what's actually in dispute at each stage and builds momentum and trust that carries into the harder remaining questions. It also gives a leader a legitimate way to prevent endless relitigating: once a sub-decision is closed on the record, it becomes the floor the next debate stands on rather than another item up for grabs.
