# The Culture of No, Yes, and Maybe

## Definition

Roberto argues that a "curious inability to decide" is usually a cultural pattern, not a single leader's indecisiveness, and identifies three recurring forms. A culture of no gives any lone dissenter effective veto power over a proposal, regardless of whether their objection serves the whole organization — dissent becomes a tool for blocking rather than improving ideas. A culture of yes produces surface agreement that dissolves once people leave the room, with objections raised covertly through backchannel sabotage instead of open debate. A culture of maybe never quite closes on anything, endlessly reopening settled questions. Each looks different but produces the same outcome: decisions that are made in name but never survive to implementation.

## In the Book

The primary case is Lou Gerstner's diagnosis of IBM in 1993, where he discovered a formally institutionalized "culture of no": cross-functional teams could spend months on a proposal only to have it killed by a single manager's "nonconcur," and some business units had literally appointed a "nonconcur coordinator" whose job was to block proposals conflicting with the unit's interests. Gerstner's own words capture it — "a culture in which no one would say yes, but everyone could say no." Roberto draws a sharp contrast (Table 6-1) between this and legitimate devil's advocacy: real dissent aims to open new lines of inquiry and is judged on the quality of its argument, while culture-of-no dissent aims to protect parochial interests and rewards whoever has the most power or "pounds the table hardest." The chapter also opens with Paul Levy's diagnosis of Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center — where "everyone knew what had to be done" yet the organization still couldn't execute, which Levy called simply "a curious inability to decide."

## Why It Matters

It reframes chronic indecision as a culture-level pathology with identifiable, distinct mechanisms rather than a vague complaint about weak leadership. Knowing which of the three cultures you're in changes the fix: a culture of no needs its veto power dismantled and dissent redirected toward argument-based scrutiny; a culture of yes needs objections surfaced openly instead of relitigated in the hallway after the meeting; a culture of maybe needs a mechanism that actually closes questions once decided. Treating "we can't seem to decide anything" as one problem, rather than diagnosing which pattern is actually operating, leads leaders to apply the wrong remedy.
