# Fair Process

## Definition

Roberto builds on Thibault and Walker's procedural-justice research (originally about legal disputes) to argue that people care about how a decision gets made independent of whether they like what it produces. If a process is perceived as fair and legitimate — participants had real opportunity to be heard, biases were checked, the outcome wasn't predetermined — people are far more likely to commit to implementing the decision even when they personally disagree with it. Tom Tyler's research adds the "cushion of support" effect: under fair procedures, people who receive an unfavorable ruling report satisfaction levels nearly as high as those who won, a cushion that largely disappears when the process is seen as unfair.

## In the Book

The chapter opens with Mark Ager, a business-development executive who identified a genuinely strong alliance partner for his firm's software but ran the process "close to the vest" — informal conversations, no briefings, no shared documents. Colleagues later reported few objections to the substance of his recommendation, but deep resentment about how it was decided; several felt the choice was "preordained" before they had a chance to weigh in, and consensus-building took far longer than Ager expected as a direct result. Roberto uses this to argue that Ager's problem was never persuasion — it was that he skipped building a fair process before trying to build buy-in. He is explicit that fair process is not the same as consensus, unanimity, or workplace democracy: leaders retain the prerogative to decide, and people ultimately "just want their positions heard. Then, they really want a choice to be made."

## Why It Matters

It separates two things leaders often collapse into one: winning the argument and earning cooperation. A leader who is right on the merits can still lose implementation if the process felt rigged, secretive, or predetermined — and conversely, a leader can make an unpopular call and still retain support if people believe they were genuinely heard first. This gives leaders a lever independent of substantive persuasion: they don't have to change everyone's mind to get everyone's cooperation, they have to run a process people recognize as legitimate.
