# Values-Based Governance vs Rules-Based Control

## Definition

Traditional organizations use rules-based control: detailed budgets, narrow job descriptions, procedure manuals, approval hierarchies. Values-based governance sets direction through principles and values, then empowers people to act within clear boundaries. The difference is between "what you must do" and "what we stand for, now decide."

## In the Book

Bogsnes uses the traffic metaphor (Chapter 2) to contrast these approaches. A traffic light is rules-based: red means stop, green means go, no discretion, managed by a distant programmer based on historical patterns. A roundabout is values-based: "give way to those already in the roundabout" is a principle, not a procedure; drivers apply local real-time information; control emerges from shared values of courtesy and safety. Traffic lights are easier to learn but less efficient; roundabouts require mature driving skills but self-regulate better in complex conditions.

Handelsbanken (Chapter 2) embodies this: the bank has no budgets, no sales targets, no job descriptions. Instead, it has strong values ("the branch is the bank," customer satisfaction, cost consciousness) and clear boundaries (branches have wide lending authority and freedom on staffing and marketing, but financing is centralized). Wallander's vision was "simplicity, transparency, and self-regulation as key principles." Head office provides one message to low-performing branches: "We can see you have a problem. You know your market and your people best. We trust you to solve it."

Borealis (Chapter 3) structured this as "mandate authorities"—wide enough for most business decisions but clear enough that larger strategic investments still went to top management. The explicit philosophy was centralized on common processes (IT, finance operations) but decentralized on business decisions.

## Why It Matters

Rules work when conditions are stable and predictable. VUCA (volatility, uncertainty, complexity, ambiguity) conditions make pre-written rules obsolete before they're implemented. Values-based systems require more trust and more mature decision-makers, but they adapt automatically to new circumstances because the people closest to the situation have authority to decide. This is the foundation of agility without chaos.
