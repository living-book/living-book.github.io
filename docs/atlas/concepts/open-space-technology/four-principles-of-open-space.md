# The Four Principles — Descriptive, Not Prescriptive

## Definition

Before a self-organizing group starts work, it is told four things: "Whoever comes is the right people," "Whatever happens is the only thing that could have," "Whenever it starts is the right time," and "When it's over, it's over." Owen is explicit that these are not commands to be enforced but descriptions of how open, self-organized gatherings actually behave — offered "in an offhanded way," never with "total seriousness," so the group stops fighting its own natural conditions (turnout, surprises, timing, duration) instead of trying to control them.

## In the Book

Chapter VI walks through each principle's practical function. "Whoever comes is the right people" reframes low or unexpected turnout at a session: what matters is the quality of conversation among however many people actually cared enough to show, not headcount or status — and even a group of one, talking to themselves, is legitimate. "Whatever happens is the only thing that could have" is aimed at the group's tendency to mourn a plan that didn't survive contact with reality — the "should-have-beens, could-have-beens" — instead of working with what is actually in front of them now. "Whenever it starts is the right time" pushes back against clock-driven expectations, noting that creativity and spirit "pay little attention to the clock." "When it's over, it's over" (with its corollary, "when it's not over, it's not over") releases groups from stretching a discussion to fill a scheduled block once its useful business is done, or from being cut off before it's actually finished.

## Why It Matters

These principles function as a pre-installed defusing mechanism for the anxieties that would otherwise cause a facilitator or group to re-impose control the moment reality departs from expectation — a no-show, an off-topic tangent, a late start, an early finish. Naming the deviation as normal in advance, rather than as a failure to be corrected, is a transferable move for any process meant to run on emergent, self-directed behavior rather than a fixed script.
