# The Self-Organizing Agenda

## Definition

A large, diverse group can build a complete, complex, multi-track agenda in under an hour with zero advance planning, using four mechanisms borrowed from ordinary human gathering: sitting in a circle, a community bulletin board, and a village marketplace. Anyone who has a real issue they care about writes it on a sheet, posts it on the wall, and thereby volunteers to convene and report on that session; everyone else then walks up and signs on to whatever sessions call to them. "Come to a circle, take a deep breath, create a bulletin board, open a marketplace, and go to work. It really is that simple."

## In the Book

Owen traces the mechanism to a rite of passage he witnessed in the West African village of Balamah, where five hundred people ran a four-day ceremony with no visible planning committee, organized around a circular village layout with an open center. He extracts four "mechanisms of meeting" from that experience — the circle (open, face-to-face communication with no head or foot), breath (a rhythm of gathering and dispersing), the bulletin board (a low-tech way to surface what people care about), and the marketplace (a way of matching those interests to time and space) — and rebuilds them into Open Space Technology. The book's opening case study is a 1992 Denver meeting of 225 Native Americans, federal officials, and state/local government representatives, historic adversaries convened to allocate $1.5 billion in highway funds: within ninety minutes of walking into a room with two concentric circles of chairs and a blank wall, the group had self-organized fifty-two task groups and, by the next morning, produced 150 pages of proceedings. The mechanism itself, described in Chapter VI, is simply: state the theme, invite anyone with passion to write their issue on a sheet and post it (accepting responsibility to convene and report), then send the whole group to the wall en masse to sign up.

## Why It Matters

This is a working counterexample to the assumption that large-group coordination requires a planning committee, an agenda drafted in advance, or a hierarchy to arbitrate what gets discussed. The mechanism separates two things usually bundled together — surfacing what matters (the bulletin board) and allocating time/space to it (the marketplace) — and lets each run on voluntary self-selection rather than authority. Anywhere a group needs to convert scattered individual concern into a coordinated structure without a central planner deciding the structure in advance, this pattern is the transferable move.
