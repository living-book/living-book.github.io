# The Four-Skill EQ Model: Self/Social × Awareness/Management

## Definition

Emotional intelligence comprises four distinct skills organized in a 2×2 matrix. Personal competence covers self-awareness (recognizing your own emotions) and self-management (controlling your behavior despite those emotions). Social competence covers social awareness (reading others' emotions) and relationship management (influencing interactions successfully). Each skill is learnable and measurable independently, though they build on each other—self-awareness enables self-management, and both enable social competence skills.

## In the Book

The book structures EQ as a four-quadrant model where the top row is "personal competence" and the bottom is "social competence," with the left column "awareness" and right column "management." Self-awareness is foundational; the book notes that 83% of people high in self-awareness are top performers versus 2% of bottom performers. Self-management builds on that awareness—it is what happens when you act or do not act, managing your emotional reactions to situations and people. Social awareness is the ability to "accurately pick up on emotions in other people and understand what is really going on with them." Relationship management uses awareness of both your own and others' emotions to manage interactions successfully. The book includes profiles of high-performing individuals in each skill (e.g., Dave T. with a 95 self-awareness score, Lane L. with 93 self-management) to show what each skill looks like in practice, and contrasts them with lower scorers to show what deficit looks like.

## Why It Matters

This framework operationalizes EQ as a set of distinct, measurable competencies rather than an abstract trait. By breaking it into four skills, the model enables targeted development—you can focus on your weakest skill without assuming all four must improve together. The 2×2 structure also reveals a key insight: self-awareness and social awareness are both prerequisite to their management counterparts, so the order of skill development matters. For organizations, this decomposition makes EQ trainable and defensible in performance conversations, not just a personality label.
