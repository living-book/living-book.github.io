# Emotional Hijacking—The Limbic Override of Rational Thought

## Definition

Emotional hijacking occurs when the limbic system (emotional brain) takes control of behavior before the prefrontal cortex (rational brain) can intervene. This happens because sensory signals enter the brain at the spinal cord base and must travel to the frontal lobe to reach rational thinking centers, but they pass through the limbic system first. The result: you experience emotions before you can reason about them. Under high stress or intense emotion, the hijacking is complete—you react without thinking, and reason cannot stop the emotion, only influence it afterward.

## In the Book

The book opens with a vivid example: surfer Butch Connor encounters a 14-foot great white shark. Paralyzed by fear, "his muscles locked up, and he lay there in a panic." This is hijacking—emotion controls behavior. The book explains the neurological pathway: "everything you see, smell, hear, taste and touch travels through your body in the form of electric signals... They enter your brain at the base near the spinal cord, but must travel to your frontal lobe (behind your forehead) before reaching the place where rational, logical thinking takes place. The trouble is, they pass through your limbic system along the way—the place where emotions are produced." The book emphasizes that intensity matters: "typically, the more intense your emotions are, the greater the likelihood that they will dictate your actions." Butch eventually regains control by consciously reasoning with himself, demonstrating that while you cannot prevent the initial emotional reaction, you can—through effort—regain rational control.

## Why It Matters

Understanding hijacking explains why willpower and logic alone fail under stress. It reveals that emotional outbursts and panic are not character flaws but a feature of human neurology. For individuals, this validates the need for EQ strategies like "count to ten" or "sleep on it"—they work by giving the rational brain time to catch up. For teams and organizations, it explains why people behave irrationally under deadline pressure or conflict; interventions should expect hijacking and design around it (e.g., cool-off periods before high-stakes conversations, structured decision-making to bypass emotional reactivity).
