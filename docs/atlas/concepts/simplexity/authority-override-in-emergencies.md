# Authority Override in Emergencies

## Definition

When people face an unprecedented threat, they don't act on what they directly perceive — they wait for social or institutional confirmation, and will override clear physical evidence (a plane crash, a second explosion) in favor of an announcement or the behavior of the crowd around them. This isn't irrationality so much as a heuristic that works well in ordinary conditions and fails badly in genuinely novel ones.

## In the Book

Chapter Two's spine is Ed Schmitt's evacuation from the World Trade Center's Tower Two on September 11. Schmitt froze at a window watching a plane approach and "did precisely nothing" until a coworker, Doris Torres, stood up to leave — her visible action, not his own judgment, is what moved him. Partway down the stairwell, an announcement telling Tower Two employees "the situation is confined to Tower One... stay at your desks" caused many evacuees to turn back; Torres herself later chose the elevator over the stairs and died from the burns she sustained when the last car out was delayed. Kluger frames mass evacuation as sitting on a "complexity arc" between two simple extremes — the frozen, unmoving crowd and the disordered, air-molecule scatter of everyday movement — with genuine complexity, and genuine danger, emerging in between: evacuation simulations that modeled people as fluid particles failed because real evacuees gravitate toward whichever exit others have already chosen, regardless of its actual safety, each additional person increasing the pull on the next.

## Why It Matters

Any system that must move people, information, or capital under uncertainty — a fire drill, a bank run, an organizational crisis — will see individuals defer to official signals and to the visible herd rather than to their own read of the situation, so the design lesson is to make the correct signal louder and more visible than the crowd's default path, not to assume people will reason it out independently.
