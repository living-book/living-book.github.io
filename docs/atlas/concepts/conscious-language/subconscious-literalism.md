# The Subconscious Mind Is Literal

## Definition

Stevens's central claim is that the subconscious mind does not interpret speech idiomatically or figuratively — it executes the literal semantic content of what is said, word by word, including buried puns and embedded negatives inside common phrases. A person who habitually says a phrase is, on this account, handing the subconscious a literal instruction that it will "obediently" carry out into thought, feeling, and eventually the body or circumstances.

## In the Book

The concept is grounded in a specific origin story in Chapter 2 ("Remembering Conscious Language"). During a Body Electronics healing session, Stevens hears a friend repeating "I don't understand" while working through a memory, and realizes the phrase can be parsed literally as "I don't stand under myself" — and the friend's presenting physical complaint tracked that literal meaning. He generalizes this into "THE SUBCONSCIOUS MIND IS LITERAL": "the subconscious mind heard him LITERALLY and brought about his words exactly as he used them." He then reports a pattern from his alternative-health practice (the "Research and Development" section): clients who said "I can't stand my roommate" showed challenges standing; "I hate my mother's anger" tracked to thyroid/energy complaints; "I don't see how I can forgive them" tracked to vision complaints; "It is so hard to handle the stress" tracked to hand/muscular complaints. His conclusion: "Limiting language shapes reality by shaping our Consciousness," and correcting the spoken pattern — not just the "secondary symptom" — is offered as the higher-leverage intervention.

## Why It Matters

This is the mechanism that makes every other technique in the book non-optional rather than decorative: if the subconscious executes literal content, then casual idioms ("it kills me," "I don't get it") are not harmless figures of speech but standing instructions. The generalizable claim — that a system executing instructions will act on their literal content regardless of the intended meaning — is also a recognizable pattern outside psychology (a compiler, a rules engine, or a bureaucracy that enforces the letter of a policy over its spirit), which is what makes it exportable: the fix in every domain is the same — audit for literal content, not intended tone.
