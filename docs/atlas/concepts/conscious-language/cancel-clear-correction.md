# Cancel Clear (Real-Time Correction Protocol)

## Definition

Stevens prescribes a fixed three-step verbal ritual to run immediately after catching oneself saying a limiting statement: neutralize it ("Cancel clear," "In the past," "That has no power"), then requalify it by stating the chosen replacement in the same breath ("...and now I choose..."). The point is timing — the correction has to happen at the moment of the slip, not as a later reflection, because (per the book's literal-subconscious mechanism) the original statement is already being executed.

## In the Book

The technique is named early — a footnote in Chapter 2 defines "'In the past, cancel clear' is a declaration I utter every time I choose to neutralize the power of a[n unwanted] statement" — and formalized in Chapter 18's "Decree a Thing" section, which gives the full protocol: "To Neutralize a statement, yours or someone else's, say silently: Cancel clear! / In the past! / That has no power!" followed by "To Neutralize and requalify a statement: Cancel Clear and now I choose! / In the past and now I… / That has no power and I give power to…" Worked examples pair a caught slip with an immediate scripted replacement: "'You are stupid.'—'Cancel clear, I AM brilliant.'"; "'I feel sick'—'in the past and now I choose to feel great!'"; "'We are going to have a wreck'—'Cancel clear, that has no power, I AM God driving this car and we are always safe and secure.'" The chapter's closing instruction generalizes it into a habit: "Make every limited statement bow before your I AM and Decree what you choose until you get it."

## Why It Matters

This is the book's answer to the gap between diagnosing a bad pattern and actually interrupting it in the moment it occurs — a scripted, low-latency correction routine rather than a general intention to "be more positive." The same shape — catch the error signal, void it explicitly, immediately substitute the intended output — is recognizable as a generic self-correction pattern (a compiler catching and flagging an error before continuing, a pilot's read-back-and-correct radio protocol, cognitive-behavioral thought-stopping) wherever real-time interrupt beats after-the-fact review.
