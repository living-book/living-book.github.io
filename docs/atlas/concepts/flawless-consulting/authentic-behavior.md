# Authentic Behavior

## Definition

Authentic behavior is the simple, repeatable act of putting into words what you are actually experiencing with a client, instead of responding to their words with technique, procedure, or diplomatic evasion. It is not a personality trait or a therapeutic stance — it is a specific communication move: describe the interaction as you see it happening, right now, even when doing so is uncomfortable or risks friction.

## In the Book

Peter Block builds the case through paired examples: a client statement, what the consultant privately experiences, a "nonauthentic" response, and an "authentic" one. When a client says an audit "shouldn't take you too long" and offers only a secretary's time, the nonauthentic move is to justify the audit's importance with facts about head-office scrutiny; the authentic move is to say directly, "You are treating this audit as though it is unimportant and small... I would like you to treat it with more importance." When a client rambles through thirty-five years of company history, the authentic response names the experience — "I am having trouble staying with your narrative" — rather than sitting in polite silence. Block argues these nonauthentic responses "deal indirectly and impersonally with the resistance" and let the client stay distant, while direct statements about the consultant-client interaction rebalance control between them. He treats authentic behavior as the foundation the rest of the book builds on: it resurfaces as the core mechanism for defusing resistance in Chapters 8 and 9, where the surgeon Paul Uhlig's team credits their turnaround in patient outcomes to showing "unfiltered uncertainties" instead of maintaining a facade of expertise.

## Why It Matters

Most professional training pushes people toward controlled, role-appropriate responses — the "correct" thing to say. Block's mechanism inverts that: the honest description of what you're experiencing in an interaction, stated plainly, does more work than any crafted argument, because it forces the other party to engage with the relationship itself rather than hide behind procedure. This generalizes past consulting to any situation where one party is nominally in service to another and status differences make direct statements feel risky — the move is always the same: say what you see happening, and let that statement rebalance the interaction.
