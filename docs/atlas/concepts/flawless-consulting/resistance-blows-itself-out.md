# Resistance Blows Itself Out

## Definition

When someone resists help, arguing them out of it intensifies the resistance, because resistance is "an emotional process, not a rational or intellectual one" — you cannot logic someone out of a feeling. The alternative Block proposes is a three-step move: notice the cues of resistance, state what form it's taking in neutral, unpunishing language ("naming the resistance"), and then go quiet and let the other person respond. Named and given room, the feeling passes on its own, "like a storm."

## In the Book

Block illustrates the mechanism with a physical exercise: press your right palm hard against your left. If the left palm pushes back, both arms lock in a straining stalemate — this is what happens when you defend your position against resistance, e.g., a client objecting to your methodology while you keep citing references and prior results, which only makes the client "more committed to finding holes in your method." But if the left palm instead yields and moves with the push, the right arm reaches its own natural limit and "gets tired and drops of its own weight" — contact without opposition. Chapter 8 catalogs the underlying fears driving resistance (helplessness, alienation, confusion) and pairs them with the flip-side potential (choice, engagement, clarity) that a client moves toward once the resistance is expressed rather than fought. Block also names two failure modes on the consultant's side: taking resistance personally (it's a response to the discomfort of being dependent, not to the consultant's own style) and the "heroic" impulse to power through a bad contract rather than naming the resistance to the terms themselves.

## Why It Matters

This reframes objection-handling: the instinct to counter-argue harder against pushback is usually the wrong move when the pushback is emotional rather than evidentiary, because arguing treats a feeling as though it were a claim that can be defeated with better claims. The transferable skill — describe what you're observing in neutral language, then stop talking — applies anywhere someone has to accept unwelcome help or change: sales, management, therapy, even debugging a defensive teammate's code review pushback.
