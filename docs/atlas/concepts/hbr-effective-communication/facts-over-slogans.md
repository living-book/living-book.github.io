# Facts Over Slogans in Organizational Change Communication

## Definition

When organizations announce change, executives often wrap announcements in slogans ("Trust, Teamwork, and Tomorrow"; "Delighting Customers"; "Working Together as Family") distributed via video, publications, and large gatherings. Frontline employees interpret these slogans through a lens of skepticism. If actual behavior contradicts the slogan, cynicism deepens and trust collapses. The antidote is radical simplicity: articulate in the fewest words possible what the organization plans to do, set it on paper as a "change booklet," and deliver it face-to-face in small groups where supervisors can explain and discuss. No slogans, no threats, no pep talks—only facts.

## In the Book

Larkin and Larkin describe a manufacturing company launching a "Trust, Teamwork, and Tomorrow" campaign. Executives unveiled the slogan on a huge banner, distributed branded pens and mugs, and broadcast via satellite. Simultaneously, the union posted a letter accusing the company of hiring private investigators to watch employees for suspected theft, drug use, and fraudulent disability claims. The contradiction was catastrophic. Executives scrambled to have public relations consultants "explain to employees that hiring private investigators does not necessarily imply a lack of trust"—an absurd assignment that only deepened cynicism.

Research on communication during mergers and acquisitions reveals three consistent findings: (1) In periods of high stress and uncertainty, people fill communication voids with rumors. (2) Rumors attribute the worst possible motives to those in control. (3) Direct communication lowers employee stress and anxiety even when the news is bad—because uncertainty is more painful than bad news. The Larkins' prescription: cut out every unnecessary word, avoid mission statements and proclamations, tell employees straight up what the organization plans to do. Change booklets should contain only facts, no punctuation, no slogans. They are meant to be discussed and explained face-to-face by frontline supervisors, not read silently. This approach signals respect for employee intelligence and grounds communication in reality rather than aspiration.

## Why It Matters

In organizations experiencing change, employee cynicism spreads faster than facts. Slogans amplify cynicism because they highlight the gap between stated values and actual behavior. Conversely, organizations that communicate change through simple facts delivered conversationally, without marketing gloss, are seen as honest. Supervisors become the delivery mechanism, which also ensures questions can be answered in real time. This approach is scalable only when the underlying changes are themselves simple and clear; if change is genuinely complex, it must be simplified before communication begins. The practice thus serves as a filter: if leadership cannot explain a change in plain language to a frontline supervisor, the change may not be clear enough to implement.
