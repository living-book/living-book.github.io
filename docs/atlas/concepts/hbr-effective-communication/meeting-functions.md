# The Six Functions of Meetings

## Definition

Face-to-face meetings perform six distinct functions that no technological medium—phone, email, video, memo—can fully replace. These go far beyond exchanging information. Meetings define who belongs to the group, update the collective knowledge pool that all members know they all possess, align individual understanding with collective aims, create commitment to decisions, improve ideas through collective wisdom, and satisfy a fundamental human need for membership and belonging.

## In the Book

Jay identifies six irreplaceable functions: (1) **Group definition**—those present belong; those absent do not. The meeting makes group identity tangible and allows individuals to perceive their place in the collective. (2) **Shared knowledge update**—the pool of knowledge that all members know all others know. This is distinct from individual knowledge; it allows rapid, efficient communication using shorthand that only the group understands. New information, experiences, and judgments are added to this pool, digested through discussion, and filtered. This process greatly speeds communication and improves collective judgment. (3) **Collective aim alignment**—helping each member understand both the group's goal and how their individual work contributes to group success. (4) **Commitment building**—once something is decided in a meeting, even those who argued against it develop commitment to the decision through their participation in making it. (5) **Idea improvement**—when multiple minds with combined experience, knowledge, and imagination are brought to bear on problems, original ideas are tested, refined, shaped by argument and discussion until they satisfy far more requirements and overcome more objections than the solo version. (6) **Satisfying human connection**—man is a social species; organized humans create regular, frequent meetings to satisfy the need for belonging.

## Why It Matters

Over-reliance on asynchronous or broadcast communication to "save time" often destroys group coherence and shared understanding. Remote teams that skip regular face-to-face meetings lose the ability to build a shared mental model, and miscommunication multiplies. Paradoxically, the perceived time savings from avoiding meetings is often lost to rework, misalignment, and repeated clarifications. Understanding what meetings fundamentally provide—not just information exchange, but group identity, collective sense-making, and commitment—justifies their existence and improves how we design and conduct them.
