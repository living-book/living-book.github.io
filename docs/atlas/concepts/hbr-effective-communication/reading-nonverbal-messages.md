# Reading Nonverbal Messages and Hidden Communication

## Definition

Nonverbal messages—facial expression, tone of voice, body language, physical space, paralinguistics (pace, rhythm, sound qualities)—carry the majority of meaning in face-to-face communication. Words alone account for only about 10% of communication; facial expression and tone of voice together account for over 90%. Yet because nonverbal messages are inherently ambiguous, they are both safer and truer for expressing ideas that cannot be said aloud. A manager reading signals of disapproval during a meeting can pivot strategy without anyone having to explicitly say "we don't support this." A subordinate sending nonverbal distress signals (closed door, withdrawn manner) alerts an observant boss to an underlying problem before a crisis. The ambiguity of nonverbal channels is a feature, not a bug: it allows people to communicate real concerns without the risk of direct confrontation.

## In the Book

McCaskey illustrates with an aerospace company project director sensing disapproval through facial expressions and body postures early in a meeting. Rather than push for an explicit decision (which would have been negative), he pivots his argument and avoids requesting a decision at that moment. Both the director and management have their reasons for keeping communication ambiguous; the nonverbal signal allows adjustment without public disagreement. Similarly, an executive notices his chief of Asian operations working with his door closed during visits—unusual behavior for a normally gregarious person. The director invites him to lunch for a nonthreatening conversation and learns the younger man has heard rumors about promotion opportunities and felt confused about his standing. The closed door was a signal; the lunch was a way to decode it without accusation. McCaskey warns against simplistic body-language "translations" (e.g., crossed arms always means resistance). Meaning depends on cultural norms, personal style, physical setting, what has gone before, and what both parties anticipate. Interpretations should be tentative and tested.

Physical space also sends messages. Locked files, closed-off offices, status-marked hierarchies all signal organizational values. Even a manager's personalization of their office space—family photos, tactile fabrics, orderliness—reveals preferred styles of interaction. McCaskey suggests touring your own organization "as if it were another company" to read the nonverbal messages embedded in physical design.

## Why It Matters

Managers who learn to read nonverbal channels gain access to information that explicit communication cannot safely carry. Problems become visible before they become crises. Conversely, managers who ignore nonverbal signals miss critical data and appear inattentive even when they are hearing words. Understanding that nonverbal communication is not a supplementary channel but the primary channel of meaning changes how leaders pay attention during meetings, conversations, and routine interactions. It also highlights the limitation of broadcast media (video, email, memos) that strip away tone, body language, and spatial context—explaining why face-to-face meetings remain irreplaceable for high-stakes conversations.
