# Organizational Defensive Routines

## Definition

Skilled incompetence occurs when people apply sophisticated social skills—hedging language, softening language, diplomatic tone, careful framing—to avoid upsetting someone with bad news or a contrary opinion. These techniques are skillful; the person executes them well. But they are incompetent at the actual goal: honest, clear communication. The result is the listener cannot understand the speaker's actual concern, and the speaker appears to have less conviction than they actually possess. This creates suspicion. When neither party discusses what just happened, the suspicion metastasizes into a defensive routine: an undiscussable organizational pattern that perpetuates because discussing it is seen as too risky or uncomfortable.

## In the Book

Argyris describes a top-management group where division heads receive mixed messages from headquarters about decentralization and autonomy. Instead of asking headquarters to clarify the contradiction, division heads generate explanatory hypotheses ("Corporate doesn't really mean it"; "Corporate trusts us only when times are good"). These hypotheses are never tested publicly because doing so would be embarrassing. Meanwhile, headquarters senses that division heads are suspicious and covering up, but doesn't make this impression known, fearing the division heads might get upset. The result is a double bind: if division heads go along unquestioningly, they lose autonomy and appear to have little influence. If they resist, they are seen as recalcitrant and disloyal. Both sides cover up their predicament to maintain good relations. Over time, further explanations compound the problem. People eventually conclude that openness is a strategy designed to hide unwillingness to be influenced. Since this conclusion is built on an assumption of covering-up, nobody tests it either.

The self-sealing loop is complete. Mixed messages create defensive routines, which become undiscussable, which makes them self-perpetuating. Argyris's remedy is to teach people new skills in a structured setting: write cases describing key organizational problems. Split the page into two columns—right side for actual words spoken, left side for withheld thoughts. This vivid illustration of the gap between skilled behavior and incompetent outcomes is itself a learning device. Once people see their patterns in writing, they can practice alternative approaches and learn to ask "the questions behind the questions."

## Why It Matters

Defensive routines are the immune system of dysfunctional organizations. They protect people from discomfort in the short term but corrupt organizational learning in the long term. Major failures and near-misses often reveal hidden defensive routines only after damage is done. Recognizing skilled incompetence as a learnable pattern—not a character flaw—makes it addressable. Teams that practice making undiscussable issues discussable unlock insight, speed decision-making, and prevent surprise crises.
