# Active Listening and Spare Thinking Capacity

## Definition

People think much faster (400+ words per minute) than they speak (125 words per minute). This creates "spare thinking time" during listening. The difference between good and poor listeners is how they use this spare capacity. Good listeners direct it toward the speaker's message—anticipating conclusions, weighing evidence, reviewing points, and reading between the lines. Poor listeners let their minds wander onto mental sidetracks, missing portions of what is said.

## In the Book

Nichols and Stevens describe extensive testing at the University of Minnesota showing that immediately after listening to a talk, the average person remembers only half of what they heard. Within two months, retention drops to 25%. Their research identified a clear mechanism: the listener's brain, having received a few opening phrases, suddenly has unused thinking capacity and begins pursuing private thoughts. The listener dashes away to a mental sidetrack ("I should mention that success I had yesterday"), then returns to the speaker. Eventually, the listener strays too far and misses a crucial connection. The private sidetracks become more inviting, and comprehension deteriorates.

The remedy is deliberate. Good listeners engage in four concurrent mental processes: (1) thinking ahead of the speaker to anticipate where the argument leads, (2) weighing the evidence offered to support claims, (3) periodically reviewing and summarizing what has been said, and (4) listening between the lines for nonverbal cues and implied meaning. These mental activities require practice to become habit, but they fill the spare capacity productively and keep focus on the speaker's line of thought.

## Why It Matters

Most communication failures in organizations trace to listening breakdowns. Managers miss critical information, misunderstand intentions, or fail to hear problems before they grow critical. Recognizing that poor listening is not a character flaw but a skill that can be taught—and that the mental mechanism causing it is universal—makes it addressable. Training in spare-capacity management directly improves comprehension and retention, with documented gains of 25–40% in ability to understand spoken communication.
