# Emotional Filters in Listening

## Definition

Our emotions function as aural filters. When we hear something opposing our deeply held convictions, our brain becomes overstimulated and listening stops. We mentally plan rebuttals, formulate embarrassing questions, or retreat into thoughts that reinforce our existing views. Conversely, when we hear something that aligns with our inner feelings, we drop our mental barriers and accept everything uncritically, abandoning our critical faculties. In both cases, emotion prevents genuine listening and comprehension.

## In the Book

Nichols and Stevens illustrate with a concrete example: a general manager, hearing "Bureau of Internal Revenue" from his accountant, feels a surge of anger ("That blasted bureau!") and begins mentally rehearsing grievances about government taxation. While his accountant continues—potentially with good news about tax savings—the manager's emotional flare-up has shut down listening. He may hear the words if pressed, but comprehend nothing. Conversely, when someone confirms our existing beliefs, we become intellectually lazy; we ask few questions and our critical faculties shut down. We enjoy having our biases reinforced. The authors offer two practical countermeasures: (1) **Withhold evaluation** during listening. Reserve judgment until the speaker finishes; only then assess their main ideas. This requires self-control but becomes habit with practice. (2) **Hunt for negative evidence.** Deliberately search for ideas that might prove you wrong, not just those that prove you right. This demands breadth of outlook but is essential to accurate listening.

## Why It Matters

Organizations are filled with situations where emotional filters prevent critical information from reaching decision-makers. A subordinate reports a problem but frames it ambiguously because they fear their boss's reaction. The boss, already skeptical of the source, hears through a filter of doubt and dismisses the concern. Problems fester. Conversely, groupthink flourishes when leaders surround themselves with people who confirm their existing views; everyone's emotional filters align, and contradictory signals are simply not heard. Teaching people to recognize and suspend their emotional filters—to "hear the man out"—is foundational to building organizations where bad news surfaces quickly and diverse perspectives are genuinely considered.
