# The Guiding Coalition

## Definition

No single leader, however capable, can drive large-scale change alone; a small team must be pulled together deliberately, chosen for a mix of credibility, skill, and standing rather than seniority alone, and then given time and shared experience to actually become a team before it can guide anything. Selecting the right individuals is necessary but not sufficient — the group also has to be turned into a cohesive unit.

## In the Book

Louis, the Head Penguin, tells Fred, Alice, Buddy, and Jordan ("the Professor") that "the colony needs a team of birds to guide it through this difficult period. I cannot do the job alone," deliberately including Fred — a junior, low-status penguin — alongside senior council members because of what each contributes: Louis's experience and respect, Alice's decisiveness and closeness to the colony, Buddy's likability and trustworthiness, Fred's curiosity and creativity, and the Professor's logic. The Professor even calculates it explicitly as "A+B+C+D+E," noting the group is additive only if the members can actually work as a team, not merely as five individuals reporting to Louis. Rather than lecture them into cohesion, Louis takes the group squid-hunting together — a shared, uncomfortable, cooperative task — and afterward steers dinner conversation toward their lives and hopes rather than the iceberg problem, deliberately building trust before asking them to work.

## Why It Matters

This separates picking the right people from building the right team — an organization can assemble individually excellent members and still fail if it treats team formation as automatic once titles are assigned. It generalizes to any effort that needs a small group with combined credibility, skill diversity, and genuine mutual trust to drive something the formal hierarchy alone cannot.
