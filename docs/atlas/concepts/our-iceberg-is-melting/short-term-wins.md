# Manufacturing Short-Term Wins

## Definition

Long change efforts lose volunteers and morale in the gap between the initial excitement of a new vision and the eventual, distant payoff, as fatigue, side-conflicts, and doubt accumulate. A short-term win — a genuine, visible, unambiguous success staged and celebrated as a public event, not left to be noticed on its own — restores flagging conviction and re-recruits the wavering.

## In the Book

After the vision is set, the planning group shrinks from thirty-five to eighteen birds as obstacles pile up: NoNo's fearmongering, parents' anxiety over their kindergartner's nightmares, infighting over who should lead the scouts, and a food-sharing tradition that leaves scouts underfed. Louis and the team deliberately clear each obstacle (eliminating irrelevant meetings, ending NoNo's leadership bid, having Buddy calm the kindergarten teacher), and the numbers begin to recover. The turning point is "Heroes' Day," scheduled for the scouts' return: games, a band, and food build anticipation all day, and when every scout returns safely — some hurt, one gravely — the colony erupts, children award them ribbons reading HERO, and Louis publicly hands the now-legendary broken bottle to the child who first proposed the freezing experiment. The text names it explicitly: "Fred and the Scouts had succeeded in creating (what one MBA-sounding bird called) 'a short-term win.' It was a big win" — and NoNo, unable to compete with it, effectively disappears from view.

## Why It Matters

This clarifies that momentum in a long effort is not a byproduct of steady progress but something that must be actively engineered and publicly marked at the right moment — the same success, left unannounced, would not have reversed the exodus from the planning group the way a staged, celebrated event did.
