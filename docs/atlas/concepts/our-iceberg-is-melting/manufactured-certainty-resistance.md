# Manufactured Certainty as Resistance

## Definition

The most durable opponent of change is rarely someone who flatly denies the problem — it is someone who demands a standard of proof no evidence can meet ("I am not 100 percent sure"), stacks a chain of "but maybe not" hypotheticals to make any claim sound like "fast-and-loose speculation," and, when losing that ground, pivots to invoking catastrophic outcomes and blame ("the gods are very mad") to paralyze the group with fear instead of moving it to act.

## In the Book

NoNo, a council member historically responsible for weather forecasting and long "accustomed to being blamed for being wrong," responds to Fred's evidence by strafing it with a chain of qualifiers — "maybe the canal will freeze this winter... but maybe not... but maybe it will not... And even if all he turns out to be true, is our iceberg really so fragile...how do we know what he says is not just... wild speculation?" — designed to sound rigorous while demanding proof that cannot exist before the fact. Later, as the colony commits to the plan, NoNo shifts tactics entirely, telling crowds "the gods are very mad. They will send a gigantic killer whale... it will crush our children in its awful jaws," manufacturing dread rather than data to stop the "nomad nonsense." The book resolves this not by out-arguing NoNo but by neutralizing him structurally: the Professor is assigned to follow him everywhere, boring him into irrelevance with unrelated technical monologues, while a firm, direct refusal ends his separate bid to become "President of the Scouts."

## Why It Matters

Naming this archetype helps a change leader recognize that surface skepticism about evidence and sudden appeals to catastrophic fear can be the same resistance strategy wearing two faces, not two different objections requiring two different rebuttals — and that engaging it on its own terms (more data, more argument) often fails where structurally isolating or out-communicating it succeeds.
