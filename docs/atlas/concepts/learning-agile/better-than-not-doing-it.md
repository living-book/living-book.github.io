# "Better-Than-Not-Doing-It" Results

## Definition

When a team adopts agile practices piecemeal — each person picking up the tools that appeal to their own role — the team gets measurably better, but only in the areas its members already paid attention to. The authors name this ceiling "better-than-not-doing-it" results: real improvement over the old way of working, but far short of the "hyper-productive," "astonishing" outcomes agile literature promises. The team goes from dysfunctional to functional and stops there, because nobody addressed the areas nobody was already watching.

## In the Book

Chapter 2 introduces this through the "jukebox team" — Bruce (team lead), Dan (developer/architect), Joanna (project manager), and Tom (product owner) — who each adopt agile practices that flatter their own job function: Dan writes better code, Joanna gets more visibility into status, Tom gets more say over what's built, Bruce improves team skills and communication. The project genuinely improves — daily standups happen, retrospectives run, the team is happier — yet each person also feels something is missing: Joanna feels reduced to a status-taker, Tom feels dumped on for answers he doesn't have, Bruce senses the results are "unsatisfying and incomplete." The book backs this with VersionOne's 2013 State of Agile survey (88% of respondents "practicing agile," 92% reporting year-over-year improvement) alongside the same survey's top-cited failure causes — lack of experience with agile methods, and company philosophy at odds with agile values — to argue that most teams stall at exactly this level, a state Forrester's Dave West separately dubbed "Water-Scrum-Fall."

## Why It Matters

This names a specific failure mode of partial, self-interested adoption that shows up anywhere a change initiative is rolled out person-by-person rather than as a team: everyone gets locally better at what they already do, total output rises, and yet the organization never crosses into the qualitatively different mode of working that was promised. It's a diagnostic for "why isn't this transformation working when everyone says things are better" — the answer is often that the areas nobody claimed ownership of never got touched.
