# The DQ Appraisal Cycle

## Definition

The DQ Appraisal Cycle is a lightweight, five-step loop for reaching decision quality on everyday "significant" decisions — the frequent, medium-stakes choices that don't warrant the full multi-meeting Dialogue Decision Process but are too consequential for gut instinct. The steps: develop an initial frame; make a rapid first pass through the other five DQ requirements; rate each requirement on the DQ slider scale, where 100% means further effort isn't worth it; improve whichever link is weakest and re-rate everything (since requirements are "dynamically linked" and improving one often changes another); and only commit to action once every requirement clears 100%.

## In the Book

The book illustrates the cycle through Caroline, a sales-team lead turned chief marketing officer, who describes applying it to routine million-dollar-range decisions — a promotion budget, a product rollout, a pricing call. Her process, in her own words: "I deal with them by going around the chain. Are we solving the right problem? Do we have good alternatives?... If we have all of that, then I say, 'Let's decide and get it done.' But if any item is missing, I stop the meeting and say, 'Let's close that gap before we decide.'" The book notes this single habit — refusing to let a meeting reach agreement while any requirement is still visibly weak — is what her colleagues mistake for unusual intelligence, when it's actually a repeatable checklist habit anyone can install. The cycle explicitly builds in refinement: an early tornado diagram or rough decision tree might reveal that alternatives need to be revisited after information is gathered, so the loop keeps cycling rather than running once and stopping.

## Why It Matters

This turns "make a good decision" from a vague aspiration into a repeatable five-step routine cheap enough to run on decisions that happen weekly, not just on once-a-decade strategic bets — the gap the book is explicitly trying to close, since most organizational value is lost across many small-to-medium decisions, not the rare mega-decision that gets a formal process. It's a template for any domain needing a fast, personal quality-assurance loop: frame, rough first pass, score the weakest dimension, fix only that, repeat until nothing's below threshold.
