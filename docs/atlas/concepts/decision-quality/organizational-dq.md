# Organizational Decision Quality

## Definition

Organizational decision quality (ODQ) is what happens when DQ practice is embedded across an organization's structure rather than depending on individual leaders who happen to favor it. It requires four aligned components: a decision agenda (proactively identifying which decisions matter, at every level of the org, instead of reacting late); clear roles for decision makers, decision staff, and content experts/implementers, with the decision maker's core job being to ensure the six DQ requirements are met — since "DQ cannot be inspected in at the point of approval"; and a matched set of decision processes and tools, from the fast DQ Appraisal Cycle for significant decisions to the full Dialogue Decision Process for strategic ones. Adoption follows a maturity curve: demonstration project, then project DQ (periodic, ad hoc use), then islands of ODQ (pockets of organizational competence, e.g. one R&D team), then full ODQ, and finally sustained enterprise ODQ, where DQ is "in our DNA" and survives leadership turnover.

## In the Book

The book uses Chevron as its flagship case: former CEO David O'Reilly required DQ principles for all major capital decisions and, when asked what created the most value, pointed to framing — "attributing it with more than half the value." Chevron became the first recipient of the Society of Decision Professionals' Raiffa-Howard Award in 2014, named for decision analysis founders Howard Raiffa and Ron Howard, recognizing enterprise-wide sustained ODQ. The book contrasts ODQ's adoption difficulty with earlier quality movements like Total Quality Management, where "top management merely had to wave the flag" without changing its own core functions — ODQ instead requires leaders to change how they personally make decisions, which one Asian industrial group's chairman refused outright: "If we adopt ODQ, I will lose most of the source of my power." The book also flags that most ODQ failures happen at leadership transitions, when an incoming leader who wasn't the original champion reasserts a personal decision style instead of the institutionalized process.

## Why It Matters

This distinguishes a genuinely durable capability from a leadership-dependent habit: if a practice only survives because one person enforces it, it isn't organizational yet — it's personal, and it evaporates at the next transition. The maturity-curve framing (demonstration → islands → full → sustained) also gives a diagnostic for any capability-building effort — quality programs, safety culture, agile adoption — to locate where an organization actually sits rather than assuming a training session or executive mandate has already finished the job.
