# The Four Core Values of Mutual Learning

## Definition

Schwarz names four values that, taken together, define effective behavior for a facilitator
and a group alike: valid information (sharing all relevant information, including your
reasoning and feelings, in a way others can independently check); free and informed
choice (people define their own objectives and methods, uncoerced, based on valid
information); internal commitment (people feel personally responsible for a choice because
it is compelling to them, not because of reward or punishment); and compassion
(temporarily suspending judgment and holding basic concern for others' and your own
well-being). The first three come from Chris Argyris and Donald Schön's Model II; Schwarz
adds the fourth.

## In the Book

Chapter 3 presents the values as a reinforcing cycle, not a checklist: people need valid
information to make an informed choice; compassion creates the psychological safety that
makes people willing to share valid information in the first place; free and informed choice
produces internal commitment, which removes the need for over-the-shoulder monitoring;
and internal commitment drives people to keep seeking new information to check whether
their earlier decisions still hold. Schwarz is explicit that compassion is not pity — pity-based
"compassion" protects people in ways they don't want to be protected and undermines
accountability, while the compassion he means holds people (including the facilitator)
accountable while suspending blame. He uses the Challenger space shuttle disaster as the
central illustration of what happens when valid information is withheld and choices are
made without it. The values are explicitly framed as ideals to strive for, not always fully
achievable — a group sometimes must decide without complete information, and the
facilitator's job is to notice the gap and keep working the group back toward the ideal.

## Why It Matters

Most facilitation and management advice tells you what to do (ask this question, run this
exercise) without a test for whether the behavior actually helps. These four values function
as that test: any technique, ground rule, or intervention can be checked against whether it
increases valid information, protects free choice, builds internal commitment, or is
delivered with compassion — and any technique that violates one, however well-intentioned,
predictably produces the opposite of what it was meant to achieve. The reinforcing-cycle
structure also explains why partial fixes fail: giving people choice without giving them valid
information just produces uninformed commitment that collapses the first time reality
contradicts the decision.
