# The Diagnosis-Intervention Cycle

## Definition

Schwarz breaks every facilitator intervention into six parallel steps split across two phases.
The private diagnosis phase: (1) observe behavior, (2) infer meaning, (3) decide whether, how,
and why to intervene. The public intervention phase mirrors it exactly: (4) describe the
observable behavior you privately noted and test whether the group agrees with it, (5) share
the inference you privately made and test whether the group agrees with it, and (6) help the
group decide whether and how to redesign its behavior. The two halves are deliberately
parallel — every private judgment gets surfaced and checked with the group before it is
acted on.

## In the Book

Chapter 8 walks through an extended case (Exhibit 8.1) of three executives arguing about a
performance-management redesign, where the conversation visibly drifts off-topic. The
facilitator privately tracks the drift (steps one through three), then makes it public: first
restating exactly what was said and checking whether Ronald and Andrew agree that's an
accurate description (step four), then naming the private inference — "I don't see the
relationship between your comments and the conversation about the forced distribution" —
and checking whether the group sees it differently (step five), then asking the group directly
whether it wants to return to the original topic or continue on the new one (step six).
Schwarz shows explicitly how each step embeds a specific ground rule: step four uses
specific examples, step five tests an inference, step six jointly designs next steps, and every
step's "test whether they see it differently" clause is the ground rule of combining advocacy
with inquiry. Chapter 7 adds that intervention types can target different diagnostic frames —
ground rules for a narrow single behavior, the group effectiveness model for a broader
structural issue, or a group's theory-in-use for the deepest, most consequential kind of
intervention.

## Why It Matters

The cycle solves a specific credibility problem: any observer who intervenes based on a
private inference risks imposing an interpretation the other party never agreed to, which
breeds resentment rather than change. By forcing the observation and the inference each to
be stated separately and checked before acting on them, the cycle makes the intervener's
reasoning auditable in real time rather than asserted from authority — the group can correct
a wrong inference before it becomes the basis for a decision. The pattern — separate what
you saw from what you concluded, and check both before proposing a change — applies
anywhere feedback is delivered, from performance reviews to incident postmortems to
technical code review.
