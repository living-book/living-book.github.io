# Facilitative Leadership

## Definition

A facilitative leader runs a group using the same core values, ground rules, and diagnostic
models as a neutral facilitator, but without a facilitator's constraint of staying out of the
content. Unlike a facilitator, the facilitative leader shares a point of view on the substance
of the discussion, is often a stakeholder in the decision, and may hold real decision
authority — while still taking responsibility for the group's process. Schwarz argues this role
does not require formal authority: an informal leader with no supervisory power can lead
this way purely through mindset and skill.

## In the Book

Chapter 16 opens by rejecting two conventional alternatives. Unilaterally controlling
leadership — even when relabeled through empowerment programs, TQM, or
reengineering — keeps failing because the new practices are still designed and imposed
top-down, reproducing the "flavor of the month" cycle. Visionary heroic leadership does
better at inspiring commitment, but Schwarz argues (citing Peter Senge on genuinely shared
vision and Peter Block on the limits of strong leadership) that its power still concentrates in
one person, so it cannot build the kind of durable, self-managing capability organizations
say they want. Table 16.1, previewed in Chapter 3's discussion of facilitative roles, spells out
the practical differences: the facilitative leader shares opinions and can be a decision maker,
where the facilitator must not. What holds the role together, and prevents it from collapsing
into ordinary unilateral leadership, is that the leader still uses the mutual learning core
values and ground rules to conduct the conversation — sharing valid information, testing
inferences aloud, and inviting the group into free and informed choice even while personally
advocating a position.

## Why It Matters

Most leadership advice treats "having an opinion and stake in the outcome" and
"facilitating an open, learning conversation" as mutually exclusive — you either run the
meeting or you're a participant in it. Facilitative leadership names the harder, more common
real situation: someone with authority and content investment who also has to manage how
the group thinks together, and shows that the values-and-ground-rules toolkit built for
neutral facilitators works just as well, with adjusted boundaries, for a leader who is not
neutral. It reframes what "empowering" a team actually requires — not less involvement from
the leader, but involvement conducted through valid information and free choice instead of
unilateral control.
