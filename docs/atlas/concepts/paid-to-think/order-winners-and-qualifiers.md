# Order Winners and Order Qualifiers

## Definition

Order Qualifiers are the baseline attributes or capabilities that a customer requires to even consider buying from you (e.g., acceptable quality, reasonable service, competitive pricing). Order Winners are the specific, differentiated reasons why a customer chooses you over competitors. The critical insight is that most organizations confuse the two: they invest heavily in Order Qualifiers thinking they are creating competitive advantage when, in fact, every competitor has similar qualifiers. True differentiation comes from identifying and developing authentic Order Winners that are specific enough to matter.

## In the Book

Goldsmith illustrates this through the "Five Reasons Why" exercise in Chapter 3-4, where he asks leaders to name five reasons why anyone would choose to do business with their organization. When he conducts this exercise with audiences from different industries, he finds strikingly similar responses: "excellent service," "high quality," "competitive pricing," "great people." These are Order Qualifiers that any organization could claim. The real Order Winners are specific: "We own seventeen unique patents for chemical processing that increase product strength by 22%," or "Deliveries made in four hours," or "90% graduation rate." Without this specificity, an organization operates in what Goldsmith calls "vanilla"—indistinguishable from the competition.

He emphasizes that Order Winners decay over time: once an Order Winner becomes standard in the market, it regresses to an Order Qualifier. This means organizations must continuously identify new Order Winners and refresh existing ones to maintain competitive advantage. The distinction guides project selection, product development focus, and whether to invest in "me-too" improvements or pursue genuine differentiation.

## Why It Matters

This concept prevents wasted investment in competitive parity (Order Qualifiers) and focuses organizational energy on authentic differentiation. It also clarifies that competitive advantage is not built through generic excellence but through specific capabilities that matter to customers. Leaders who can distinguish Order Winners from Qualifiers make better resource-allocation decisions and avoid the trap of competing on dimensions where everyone is similar.
