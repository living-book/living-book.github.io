# Goldsmith Productivity Principle

## Definition

The Goldsmith Productivity Principle (GPP) asserts that 80% of organizational results derive from systems, structures, and processes—the organizational design—while only 20% derive from individual capability, talent, and effort. This inverts the typical leadership tendency to focus on "finding the right people" or "motivating better performance." Instead, the principle directs leaders to ask: What systems and structures should we build or improve to move the organization forward? The GPP becomes the operational lever for rethinking and priority management.

## In the Book

Goldsmith introduces the principle early and applies it throughout the book. In the Sheila story, her initial rethinking—from "this employee is not performing" to "this employee lacks the right equipment"—is an application of GPP. By fixing the system (the computer), a persistent performance gap closed immediately without hiring, training, or motivation programs. In the Priority Management section (Chapter 3), Goldsmith returns to GPP explicitly: the priority-management system itself is the 80% (the structure that ensures focus on high-impact activities), while the leader's discipline to use it daily is the 20% (the person).

He emphasizes this principle when discussing why most organizations fail to complete projects: not because of weak people, but because the systems (strategy clarity, project planning, daily prioritization, execution discipline) are absent or broken. A well-designed system makes individual excellence more likely; a broken system defeats excellence.

## Why It Matters

This principle redirects problem-solving energy away from personnel-focused solutions (hire smarter, train harder, incentivize better) toward systemic solutions (redesign workflow, clarify strategy, improve feedback loops). It is more cost-effective, more scalable, and more durable than relying on exceptional individuals. It also explains why replacing one underperforming person with another often fails: if the system is broken, the new person will likely underperform too. Leaders who internalize GPP shift from a blame-oriented mindset ("we have the wrong people") to a design-oriented mindset ("we have the wrong systems") and unlock significantly more organizational leverage.
