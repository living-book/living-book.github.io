# Cyclonic Strategic Thinking: Five-Phase Strategic Model

## Definition

The Cyclonic Strategic Thinking (CST) Model is a five-phase strategic planning approach that structures how leaders think about and develop strategies. Rather than the vague two-phase "strategy then tactics" model used traditionally, it explicitly separates: Desired Outcome (what you want to achieve), Strategy (the approach), Macro Tactics (broad implementation paths), Tactics (specific detailed actions), and Execution (deployment and follow-through). The model gets its name from the natural cyclonic motion of the mind—ideas spiral in from multiple sources, are tested and refined against each phase, and the decision-maker moves fluidly between phases to generate better options before committing.

## In the Book

Goldsmith introduces the CST Model as a response to the failure of traditional two-phase strategizing, where ambiguous terminology (strategy and tactics mean different things to different people) leaves organizations unaligned. In Chapter 3, he walks through the model using a vacation-planning example, then shows how PreQuip, Inc., a precision equipment manufacturer, used it to escape from rushing to action too soon.

The five phases work as an interconnected system: your Desired Outcome sets the starting reference point for all strategy; Cyclonic Thinking—the natural spiraling of ideas—happens throughout all phases as the mind pulls in options and tests them; Macro Tactics are broad approach-level decisions; Tactics are the detailed executable steps; and Execution is separated out deliberately because "execution is often blended into tactics when people do their strategizing" but is critical for converting thoughts to reality.

Goldsmith emphasizes that the model is universal—a TV producer, medical office manager, or public works commissioner can all use it—and teaching it to staff creates a common vocabulary that minimizes errors and accelerates decision-making.

## Why It Matters

The CST Model solves a widespread problem: organizations build "strategies" without agreement on what they've decided, leaving projects incomplete, resources wasted, and people moving in different directions. By making strategy-thinking explicit and moving it through five distinct phases, the model forces leaders to address all components of planning before moving forward. This prevents the "we had a great idea but it never materialized" problem. Because it is universal and teachable, every level of an organization can use the same language and the same thinking process—from the CEO to frontline managers—which compounds the benefit across an entire organization.
