# Priority Management

## Definition

Priority Management is distinct from traditional time management (which merely helps people remember tasks). It is a structured approach where leaders identify items requiring completion, rank them by organizational impact (A items are highest-value, B items are lower but should still be done, C items are new additions), allocate realistic time budgets using the 60% rule (plan only 60% of available time to create buffer for interruptions), and work from this plan throughout the day as a navigation tool. The Escalator Theory—people naturally accelerate effort as they approach completion—is harnessed by making daily goals achievable so that this motivational curve triggers.

## In the Book

Goldsmith introduces Priority Management in Chapter 3 as essential for converting strategy to results. He contrasts it with traditional time management systems (which treat all tasks equally and lead to carryover lists that demoralize leaders) and shows how a "Catch as You Catch Can" approach produces frustration and incomplete projects. His priority-management system has two phases: Conversion (consolidating all lists, tasks, and thoughts into one system) and Daily Routine (a nightly planning ritual where the leader moves meetings to the calendar, adds task items, ranks by A/B, allocates time, and ensures the day is 60% full).

He emphasizes that priority management is not about doing everything or achieving perfection; it is about ensuring that the right things get done and that high-impact strategic work actually happens instead of being perpetually deferred for "urgent" but low-impact items. The system also applies to personal life: leaders should schedule personal priorities (family time, health, community) the same way they schedule work, making work-life balance deliberate rather than accidental.

## Why It Matters

Most strategic plans fail not because the strategy is flawed but because daily execution is disconnected from strategy. Leaders think strategically once a year but then spend 365 days responding to tactical noise. Priority Management bridges this gap by forcing daily reconnection to strategy through a visible, executable plan. This small structural change—making strategic tasks visible and time-allocated at the daily level—is what allows organizations to actually achieve their Desired Outcomes instead of perpetually planning for outcomes they never reach.
