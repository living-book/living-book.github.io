# Pentality of Forecasting

## Definition

The Pentality of Forecasting is a five-sided tool (pentagon) that directs leaders' attention to five critical domains when thinking about organizational futures. Rather than unfocused speculation, it systematically asks: What is the future of You/Your Group/Your Organization? What about your Customers/Clients? What about your Competition? What about your Suppliers/Vendors? And what about the World/Universe (geopolitical, economic, technological, environmental context)? Leaders establish Forecasting Horizon Markers—time windows (e.g., one year, three years, five years, ten years) and apply cyclonic thinking to each category in turn.

## In the Book

Goldsmith introduces the Pentality in Chapter 14 as a response to leaders who, despite good intentions, consistently overlook opportunities for forecasting and remain mentally stuck in the present or past. He emphasizes that living "mentally in the future" requires knowing what to look for and where to look. For each of the five categories, he provides concrete examples: a nanomembrane technology company must look not just at product cycles (Horizon Marker: five years) but also at population shifts and demand for desalination plants (Horizon Marker: five to ten years). The tool works for any organization type—nonprofit, military, government, business—by adapting language (member, patron, sponsor instead of customer).

Goldsmith stresses that forecasting accuracy is not the goal; the goal is "improved decision-making." Leaders establish these categories to avoid the rut of ignoring certain domains and to fine-tune decisions "cyclonically"—as you approach year three of a five-year forecast, you measure progress and adjust years four and five based on new data.

## Why It Matters

Most leaders forecast only a narrow slice of their environment—perhaps their direct competitors or customer demand—missing signals from adjacent industries, supply-chain vulnerabilities, geopolitical shifts, or internal succession challenges. The Pentality forces breadth of attention and creates a systematic discipline for forward thinking that can be taught to teams and integrated into strategic planning cycles. Because external change increasingly threatens organizational survival, the ability to detect threats and opportunities across all five domains early—rather than reacting when crises arrive—is a core leadership capability.
