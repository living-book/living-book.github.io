# Succession: Maintaining Organizational Spirit

## Definition

The paradox that succession decisions must focus on preserving the organization's animating spirit rather than simply finding a qualified replacement. Strong founders and leaders create a gravitational field around themselves that can either suffocate their successor or provide the foundation for continuity. The task of succession is to dissolve that gravity while keeping the mission alive.

## In the Book

Drucker observed a historical pattern: strong leaders in universities and institutions rarely develop internal successors strong enough to thrive in the role. They either suppress potential rivals or continue to exercise influence long after departure—like Alfred Sloan's decades-long shadow over General Motors. The book contrasts this with Stanford University's model: bringing in a president from outside while promoting the provost from within, creating a balance that avoided both suppression and chaos.

The example of Herman Miller is instructive: when Max DePree's successor J. Kermit Campbell failed after two years (1992–1995), the problem was not lack of competence but inability to maintain DePree's organizational spirit. Methodism's decline offers another lesson: the Methodist Church abandoned the radical innovation of using women lay ministers once it grew successful and sought "respectability"—and within twenty years collapsed into denominational bureaucracy. The spirit departed before the founder did.

Drucker emphasizes that "the spirit of an organization is created from the top," and succession must ask: How do we give the founder/predecessor a meaningful sphere where they can contribute without undermining the successor? Some organizations banish predecessors entirely (General Electric's policy), others leave them like "the grin of the Cheshire cat" in constant influence—both extremes damage organizational continuity.

## Why It Matters

Most succession failures are framed as leadership mismatches (wrong person picked). But this concept reframes it: the problem is organizational design—creating structures where the predecessor can remain productive without paralyzing the successor, and where the organization's fundamental values and performance standards survive the transition. This applies beyond corporations to any institution where founding vision is at risk: churches, nonprofits, family businesses, universities. Recognizing that succession is about preserving spirit, not just competence, changes how you prepare people, design governance, and measure success.
