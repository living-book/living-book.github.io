# Developing Leaders, Not Functionaries

## Definition

The distinction between developing people who take responsibility for results and decisions versus developing people who follow rules and procedures. A leader gets things done and earns trust through integrity and performance; a functionary executes procedures. Organizations can deliberately design work and delegation to develop leaders or inadvertently create bureaucracies of functionaries.

## In the Book

Drucker begins the coaching year with a consultation to World Vision International on "What Do Effective Leaders Do to Create High-Performing Organizations?" His premise is stark: "The only definition of a leader is someone with followers." This is not about title or role but about whether people follow because they trust you to deliver results and act with integrity.

The book quotes Drucker's World War II experience: he was given responsibility without line authority (he could not be ordered around by a general, which was "very important") and made decisions that earned respect and trust. General Marshall's response—"You are doing what you are supposed to be doing; forget about it. I'll take care of it"—exemplified leadership: clarity about responsibility, and trust that it would be exercised well.

The concept extends to organizational design. Drucker taught that "a self-governing plant community" requires delegating managerial responsibility to individual employees and work teams—for decisions about job structure, task completion, shift scheduling, and community affairs. But this requires training and genuine empowerment, not just exhortation. Conversely, when management views employees as subordinate wage labor requiring external control, people naturally become functionaries: they do what they're told and nothing more.

The book emphasizes that building trust requires two things: (1) you get the right things done, and (2) people can expect what you say—integrity. Effective leadership is mundane and learnable, not tied to charisma or personality type.

## Why It Matters

This concept reframes the leadership development problem: you don't create leaders through training programs and assessments. You create them by structuring work so people must make real decisions, take responsibility for outcomes, and experience the consequences of their judgment. It explains why organizations with strong customer service or innovation often promote from within while rule-bound organizations struggle to develop internal talent. It also clarifies what "empowerment" actually means—not feeling heard, but having real authority over decisions that affect your work, paired with accountability for results. For individuals, it asks: Am I being developed as a leader (trusted with real responsibility) or managed as a functionary (told what to do)?
