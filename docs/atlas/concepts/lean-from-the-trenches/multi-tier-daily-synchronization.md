# Multi-Tier Daily Synchronization

## Definition

Run three overlapping daily meetings in sequence: (1) Feature team standups where each team discusses their work and blockers (2) Specialty syncs where all analysts, all testers, and all developers meet separately to coordinate across teams (3) A cross-team project sync where one person from each specialty and team meets to surface and resolve cross-cutting issues. Each tier feeds information upward; the cross-team layer makes decisions that ripple down. All meetings happen in quick succession (30 minutes total), with embedded team members attending both their team standup and their specialty sync.

## In the Book

At PUST, before 10:15 a.m., the project floor looked like a cocktail party—people standing in groups in front of boards, moving sticky notes, debating, then breaking apart and reforming in different groups. This wasn't chaos; it was a highly structured rhythm. Feature teams met at 9:15 or 9:30 (each chose their time) to plan their day and raise blockers. At 9:45, testers gathered in front of one board and discussed how to allocate testing effort that day; analysts met in front of another discussing requirements coherence and handoffs; developers discussed build and integration issues. A person could attend their feature team standup with fresh context about what's happening, then immediately carry that context to their specialty sync to coordinate with peers in other teams. By 10:15, most people were back at desks working. The tiered structure meant that a 60-person project could coordinate without a single 60-person meeting (which would be useless) and without leaving people siloed in their own team bubble.

## Why It Matters

This pattern solves the scaling problem of daily standups: as teams grow, a single standup becomes either too large to be useful or so long it's a waste. Multi-tier structure keeps each ceremony small and focused (8–12 people per specialty sync, 10–15 people per team standup) while ensuring information flows across boundaries. The symmetry—everyone attends their team sync, plus everyone in each specialty attends the specialty sync—creates natural information highways without needing complex routing. This applies to any organization scaling beyond 20–30 people where informal corridor conversation stops working but a single all-hands standup becomes theater.
