# Physical Kanban Board as System of Record

## Definition

Make the physical board (tape, columns, sticky notes) the single source of truth for work state, not a nice-to-have visualization of what's in an electronic tool. The board shows columns, WIP limits, blocked cards, and what each person is working on. Electronic systems can exist (for searching history, generating reports) but are strictly secondary—they are "shadows" that replicate information from the board, never the master. If the board and a tool disagree, the board wins.

## In the Book

The PUST team considered building or adopting an electronic Kanban system. They decided against it. The physical board—a wall with tape, cards, magnets—sat in the middle of the project floor, visible to everyone passing by. When a blocker appeared, you moved a card. When a bottleneck formed (too many cards in one column), everyone could see it without opening a tool. The board showed WIP limits in red, bugs in red markers, tech stories in green spots. New people, managers, anyone walking by could instantly see the state of the system without logging in or navigating menus. More importantly, the physicality made pulling work feel real: a developer would walk to the board, see what came next, and pull a card. With an electronic board, people tend to ask "What should I work on?" and wait for assignment. With a physical board, people pull their own work, and the pull behavior is stronger. The team did consider an electronic shadow for upper management to see high-level status remotely, but kept the physical board as the source of truth, with any digital version updated from it, never the reverse.

## Why It Matters

This is a choice about what makes work visible and what drives behavior. A physical board creates a shared reality that's harder to ignore—you walk past it, you see your card blocked, and you feel compelled to unblock it. Electronic tools are convenient for history and search but can become a graveyard of stale data; they don't create the same pull behavior. The physical board is also a conversation starter—people stand in front of it, point, argue, decide. An electronic tool is consumed privately. This isn't anti-technology; it's about matching the tool to the need. For coordination on a co-located team, tangible beats digital. For distributed teams, the calculation changes. But the principle holds: the source of truth should be whatever creates the most honest visibility and strongest pull behavior.
