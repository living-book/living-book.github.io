## Agile & Software Delivery (12)

- `cadence-decoupling` (kanban) — Cadence Decoupling — Letting delivery, prioritization, and development each run on their own natural rhythm instead of forcing all activities into one shared iteration.
- `flow-distribution` (project-to-product) — Flow Distribution — The deliberately tuned ratio of work-item types a value stream commits to, set by business need rather than left as an unconscious byproduct of daily prioritization.
- `flow-items` (project-to-product) — The Four Flow Items — A MECE taxonomy of everything that competes for capacity in a value stream — features, defects, risks, and debts — each pulled by a different stakeholder.
- `product-oriented-management` (project-to-product) — Product-Oriented Management — Funding, staffing, and measuring durable value streams against business outcomes, instead of funding time-boxed projects against fixed scope and budget.
- `rock-breaking` (user-story-mapping) — Rock Breaking — Big stories break into smaller stories at every scale, the way rocks break into smaller rocks — and the tool that breaks them is always conversation, moving through opportunity, discovery, and delivery.
- `decentralized-control` (product-development-flow) — Decentralized Control — Push decision authority to the front line for problems that age poorly, keep it centralized for rare, large-scale, economy-of-scale problems — never all-or-nothing.
- `multi-process-handling` (toyota-production-system) — Multi-Process Handling — One worker running several different machines across different processes (lathe, mill, drill), not several identical machines — a distinct skill that requires becoming multi-skilled, not just fast.
- `last-responsible-moment` (lean-software-development) — The Last Responsible Moment — Delay a commitment until the point where failing to decide would eliminate an important alternative — any later is procrastination, any earlier is a guess.
- `two-kinds-of-integrity` (lean-software-development) — Perceived Integrity and Conceptual Integrity — A system needs two distinct kinds of coherence — matching what the customer actually values, and holding together internally as an architecture — and each fails differently.
- `context-switching-tax` (making-work-visible) — The Context-Switching Tax — Juggling multiple tasks at once imposes a hidden cognitive overhead cost on humans far higher than the CPU-scheduling metaphor suggests.
- `failure-demand` (making-work-visible) — Failure Demand — Unplanned work arriving as fires and rework consumes capacity in a way that is fundamentally different from — and more corrosive than — a spike in ordinary demand.
- `time-thief-o-gram` (making-work-visible) — The Time Thief O'Gram — Measure the named causes of delay directly (WIP, dependencies, unplanned work, conflicting priorities, neglect) instead of lagging output metrics that don't explain why work is slow.

## Business & Management (46)

- `business-model-environment` (business-model-generation) — Business Model Environment — Mapping a business model's external context — market forces, industry forces, key trends, and macroeconomic forces — as a design space that constrains and provokes model redesign.
- `free-as-a-business-model` (business-model-generation) — FREE as a Business Model — Giving a product or service away for free is a deliberate pattern in which one customer segment is subsidized by revenue from another — advertisers, premium upgraders, or a tied consumable.
- `drum-buffer-rope` (theory-of-constraints) — Drum-Buffer-Rope — A production scheduling method that paces the whole system to its constraint (drum), protects it with time buffers, and limits material release (rope) to prevent overproduction elsewhere.
- `innovation-proficiency` (end-of-competitive-advantage) — Innovation as a Managed Proficiency — Innovation must run as a continuous, funded, governed organizational process with its own budget and career paths, not an episodic side activity people do on top of their day jobs.
- `continuous-performance-management` (measure-what-matters) — Continuous Performance Management (CFRs) — Replacing the annual performance review with ongoing Conversations, Feedback, and Recognition gives quantitative goals like OKRs the human context numbers alone can't carry.
- `goal-transparency` (measure-what-matters) — Goal Transparency — Making everyone's goals publicly visible, from the CEO down, drives higher attainment than private goals and lets alignment form bottom-up as well as top-down.
- `gospel-of-10x` (measure-what-matters) — The Gospel of 10x — Deliberately splitting goals into "committed" (achieved in full) and "aspirational" (expected to fail ~40% of the time) baskets extracts more from teams than uniformly safe targets.
- `forcing-functions` (design-of-everyday-things) — Forcing Functions — A physical or procedural constraint that makes an error impossible by preventing the next step until a required condition is met, trading convenience for safety.
- `human-centered-design-process` (design-of-everyday-things) — The Human-Centered Design Process — A double diverge-converge cycle — first finding the right problem, then finding the right solution — repeated through observation, ideation, prototyping, and testing.
- `discovery-delivery-split` (inspired) — Discovery and Delivery as Two Tracks — Fast, cheap, disposable experiments to learn whether an idea is worth building run continuously alongside — not before — the slow, careful work of building it right.
- `disposable-prototypes` (inspired) — Disposable Prototypes — Fake, throwaway simulations built at the lowest fidelity that will still answer the question, used to learn before committing to real engineering.
- `empowered-product-teams` (inspired) — Empowered Product Teams — Cross-functional teams given problems and objectives to solve, not features to build, so they act as missionaries rather than order-takers.
- `opportunity-assessment` (inspired) — Opportunity Assessment — A four-question, few-minutes-long technique that forces a team to state the business objective, success measure, customer problem, and target market before starting discovery.
- `product-vision-and-strategy` (inspired) — Product Vision and Product Strategy — A multi-year vision inspires and gives teams shared context, while a strategy narrows that vision into one target market at a time so effort never fragments.
- `roadmap-commitment-trap` (inspired) — The Roadmap Commitment Trap — Any list of ideas placed on a document called a roadmap gets read by the organization as a commitment, locking teams into building things before anyone knows they'll work.
- `customer-development-interviews` (running-lean) — Get Out of the Building: Structured Customer Interviews — Face-to-face Problem and Solution interviews with real prospects, not surveys or intuition, are how you learn whether a problem is real before writing any code.
- `lean-canvas` (running-lean) — The Lean Canvas — A one-page, nine-block business model diagram that puts Problem before Solution, letting founders document and revise assumptions in an afternoon instead of a business plan.
- `problem-solution-fit` (running-lean) — The Three Stages of a Startup — A startup moves through three qualitatively different stages — Problem/Solution Fit, then Product/Market Fit, then Scale — each with its own question, tactics, and definition of success.
- `unique-value-proposition` (running-lean) — The Unique Value Proposition — A single honed statement of why you are different and worth paying attention to, aimed at early adopters and built from the finished-story benefit, not the feature list.
- `build-for-the-few` (four-steps-to-the-epiphany) — Build for the Few, Not the Many — A startup's first release should satisfy only its small circle of visionary customers, not the aggregated feature wish-list of a mainstream market that doesn't exist yet.
- `customer-development-model` (four-steps-to-the-epiphany) — The Customer Development Model — A parallel, iterative process to Product Development that tests customer and market hypotheses outside the building before scaling sales and marketing.
- `iterate-or-exit` (four-steps-to-the-epiphany) — Iterate or Exit — Each Customer Development step ends with a formal, written checkpoint that forces an honest verdict — recycle to an earlier phase, or advance — rather than letting momentum decide.
- `market-type` (four-steps-to-the-epiphany) — Market Type — Whether a startup is entering an existing, new, or resegmented market determines its sales cycle, cash needs, launch strategy, and timeline — not the product itself.
- `mission-centric-organization` (four-steps-to-the-epiphany) — The Mission-Centric Organization — Between an informal, learning-oriented startup team and a rigid, process-driven large company sits a third stage — departments organized around a shared written mission, not headcount or job titles.
- `premature-scaling` (four-steps-to-the-epiphany) — Premature Scaling — Hiring and spending against a business plan's execution documents, rather than against verified customer facts, front-loads a burn rate that guarantees a death spiral if any assumption is wrong.
- `repeatable-sales-roadmap` (four-steps-to-the-epiphany) — The Repeatable Sales Roadmap — Before scaling marketing or sales headcount, a startup must field-test a sales process to the point that a second and third customer buy the same way the first one did.
- `four-zones-model` (zone-to-win) — The Four Zones Model — Splitting an enterprise into performance, productivity, incubation, and transformation zones, each with its own playbook and investment horizon, so disruption doesn't cross-contaminate execution.
- `performance-matrix-governance` (zone-to-win) — Performance Matrix Governance — Cross-functional resource commitments become real only when jointly owned in a row-by-column grid where every cell is co-signed by both a product owner and a channel owner.
- `zone-offense-defense` (zone-to-win) — Zone Offense vs. Zone Defense — An enterprise must declare exactly one stance each year — proactively catching a new wave (offense) or reactively fending one off (defense) — never both, since each demands opposite resource moves.
- `customer-success-criteria` (jobs-to-be-done) — Success Criteria: The Customer's Win Condition — The specific, customer-defined metrics for what counts as successfully completing a job — distinct from the job itself — that must be uncovered before a product can be designed to hit them.
- `job-drivers` (jobs-to-be-done) — Job Drivers — The underlying attitudes, background, and circumstances that make certain jobs more or less important to a given customer, distinct from the jobs themselves.
- `jobs-to-be-done` (jobs-to-be-done) — The Job to Be Done — Customers "hire" products to accomplish an underlying functional and emotional job, so the job — not purchase history — is the real unit of market analysis.
- `pain-point-process-mapping` (jobs-to-be-done) — Pain-Point Process Mapping — Chart a customer's step-by-step path toward one specific job, occasion by occasion, to surface every stakeholder involved and the exact point where friction occurs.
- `value-based-pricing` (jobs-to-be-done) — Value-Based Pricing — Price a solution by how much of the customer's job it satisfies, not by production cost or competitors' prices — letting price itself signal and reinforce the job being done.
- `businesslike-investing` (intelligent-investor) — Businesslike Investing — Treat every security purchase as if you were buying into an actual business you must understand, supervise, and justify by arithmetic — not by optimism.
- `defensive-vs-enterprising-investor` (intelligent-investor) — Defensive versus Enterprising Investor — Two legitimate, equally respectable investing paths distinguished by how much time and effort you're honestly willing to give it — not by how smart or wealthy you are.
- `dollar-cost-averaging` (intelligent-investor) — Dollar-Cost Averaging — Invest a fixed sum on a fixed schedule regardless of price, so you automatically buy more shares when they're cheap and fewer when they're dear, without having to decide.
- `investment-vs-speculation` (intelligent-investor) — Investment versus Speculation — An operation is an investment only if thorough analysis promises safety of principal and an adequate return; everything else is speculation, however dressed up.
- `superinvestors-of-graham-and-doddsville` (intelligent-investor) — The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville — A cluster of independent winners tracing to one shared method, rather than to one shared lucky call, is statistical evidence of real skill, not chance.
- `cascading-communication` (the-advantage) — Cascading Communication — Clarity spreads through an organization most reliably as informal, consistent word-of-mouth from leaders down through direct reports, not through polished broadcast messaging.
- `first-team` (the-advantage) — First Team (Team Number One) — An executive's primary loyalty must be to the peer leadership team, not to the department they run, or cross-functional decisions default to turf protection.
- `four-meetings-rhythm` (the-advantage) — The Four Meetings Rhythm — Different kinds of organizational decisions need structurally different meetings — daily, weekly, monthly, and quarterly — rather than fewer, longer, all-purpose ones.
- `human-systems-reinforcement` (the-advantage) — Human Systems as Reinforcement — Hiring, performance management, rewards, and every other people-facing process should exist to remind employees what the organization has decided matters, not to be sophisticated in their own right.
- `smart-vs-healthy` (the-advantage) — Smart Versus Healthy — Organizations need both smart decisions (strategy, finance, marketing) and health (minimal politics, high morale); most invest in smart alone.
- `thematic-goal` (the-advantage) — The Thematic Goal (Rallying Cry) — An organization needs exactly one top priority for a given period, modeled on how emergency responders spontaneously drop silos under a single crisis-driven focus.

## Leadership & Coaching (24)

- `the-hidden` (creativity-inc) — The Hidden — Every organization has problems it structurally cannot see, and believing you have no blind spots is itself the most dangerous blind spot.
- `five-step-process` (principles) — The 5-Step Process — A sequential, one-step-at-a-time loop — goals, problems, diagnosis, design, execution — for turning ambition into results without blurring the steps.
- `pain-plus-reflection` (principles) — Pain + Reflection = Progress — Psychological pain is a signal pointing at exactly the weakness or mistake worth examining; reflecting on it (rather than avoiding it) converts it into learning.
- `catalyst-factor` (progress-principle) — The Catalyst Factor — Seven project-directed supports — clear goals, autonomy, resources, time, help, learning from problems, and idea flow — that instantly lift inner work life by enabling progress.
- `inner-work-life-effect` (progress-principle) — The Inner Work Life Effect — People perform measurably better on creativity, productivity, commitment, and collegiality when their inner work life is positive, not when they are under stress.
- `inner-work-life` (progress-principle) — Inner Work Life — The continuous, mostly hidden stream of perceptions, emotions, and motivation that employees experience while working, driving how they perform.
- `nourishment-factor` (progress-principle) — The Nourishment Factor — Person-directed interpersonal events — respect, encouragement, emotional support, and affiliation — that nourish inner work life, versus the toxins that poison it.
- `create-a-customer` (essential-drucker) — Create a Customer — A business's purpose lies outside itself, in society — to create a customer — so its only two functions are marketing and innovation, not profit-making.
- `focus-on-contribution` (essential-drucker) — Focus on Contribution — Effective people ask "what can I contribute to the results of the whole?" instead of focusing on effort, authority, or what the organization owes them.
- `knowledge-worker-as-volunteer` (essential-drucker) — The Knowledge Worker as Volunteer — Knowledge workers own their means of production — their knowledge — and can leave, so they must be managed like volunteers who need mission and challenge, not like subordinate wage labor.
- `leadership-as-work` (essential-drucker) — Leadership as Work, Not Charisma — Effective leadership is mundane, learnable work — defining and holding to a mission — with no correlation to charisma or a fixed set of personality traits.
- `picking-people` (essential-drucker) — Picking People Is a Discipline, Not a Gift — Staffing decisions can approach reliability if treated as a rigorous process with fixed steps, not left to an executive's intuitive "judgment of people."
- `clarity-over-consensus` (five-dysfunctions-of-a-team) — Clarity Over Consensus — Teams commit to decisions not by reaching agreement but by ensuring everyone's view was genuinely heard, then aligning behind one clear call even under uncertainty.
- `peer-based-accountability` (five-dysfunctions-of-a-team) — Peer-Based Accountability — The most effective enforcement of standards is teammates calling out teammates directly, not a leader policing performance from above.
- `status-vs-results` (five-dysfunctions-of-a-team) — Status vs. Results — Teams drift from collective outcomes toward two substitute rewards — team status (prestige of belonging) and individual status (personal career advancement) — that feel like success but aren't.
- `authenticity-trap` (act-like-a-leader) — The Authenticity Trap — Insisting on staying "true to yourself" during a role transition anchors you to the old self you're supposed to be outgrowing.
- `competency-trap` (act-like-a-leader) — The Competency Trap — Overinvesting in the skills that made you successful narrows your job just as the role's real demands are expanding past it.
- `connective-advantage` (act-like-a-leader) — Connective Advantage — Leadership requires deliberately built operational, personal, and strategic networks, not the narrow, self-selected ties that accumulate by default.
- `do-it-yourself-transition` (act-like-a-leader) — The Do-It-Yourself Transition — Today's leadership transitions rarely arrive with a new title — the job's expectations shift underneath you while you sit in the same role.
- `stepping-up-process` (act-like-a-leader) — The Stepping-Up Process — Leadership transitions unfold as five predictable stages — disconfirmation, addition, complication, course correction, internalization — not a straight line or a single conversion moment.
- `culture-of-no-yes-maybe` (why-great-leaders-dont-take-yes) — The Culture of No, Yes, and Maybe — Three distinct dysfunctional decision-making cultures — where lone dissenters can veto, where everyone superficially agrees, and where nothing is ever quite decided — each producing chronic organizational indecision.
- `fair-process` (why-great-leaders-dont-take-yes) — Fair Process — People judge a decision by the fairness of the process that produced it, not only by the outcome — and a genuinely fair process buys commitment even from those who lose the argument.
- `incremental-closure` (why-great-leaders-dont-take-yes) — Incremental Closure — Break a large, contentious decision into a sequence of smaller sub-decisions, closing each one on common ground before moving to the next, rather than seeking one comprehensive agreement.
- `leading-with-restraint` (why-great-leaders-dont-take-yes) — Leading with Restraint — A leader can take charge of how a decision gets made — process-oriented leadership — without immediately taking charge of what the answer should be, and this is not weakness or indecision.
